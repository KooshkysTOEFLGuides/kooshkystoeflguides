## MODE RULES

### AUTO mode

Select a realistic everyday training situation connected to a university, workplace, public facility, community activity, store, workshop, visitor attraction, or volunteer program.

### GUIDED mode

Use the supplied GENERAL SUBJECT as the basis of the set.

Do not merely insert the subject into generic sentences. Convert it into a realistic micro-scenario by deciding:

* where the activity takes place;
* what temporary role the student has;
* who is training the student;
* what the student is learning to explain, demonstrate, or do;
* which concrete objects, locations, and actions naturally belong in the situation.

When the supplied subject is broad, make reasonable decisions yourself. Do not ask follow-up questions.

Example:

Possible interpretation: The student is a park volunteer being trained to guide community members as they assemble and install wooden nest boxes.

## SET-TYPE RULES

### ORIENTATION

Use this for settings in which the student is learning to assist customers, students, tourists, or visitors.

A typical progression is:

1. Identify a basic place, feature, product, or service.
2. Identify another nearby feature.
3. Give a slightly fuller description.
4. Explain a location, function, or available service.
5. Give a practical recommendation or policy.
6. Explain where to obtain assistance or additional information.
7. End with a longer conditional or situational instruction.

### PROCEDURE

Use this for activities involving construction, preparation, repair, operation, cleaning, arrangement, maintenance, or demonstration.

A typical progression is:

1. Give the first short action.
2. Give another preparatory action.
3. Introduce a more detailed physical action.
4. Continue the central procedure.
5. Give an important positioning, checking, or completion step.
6. Add a safety, quality-control, maintenance, or storage instruction.
7. End with a longer final or conditional instruction.

## DIFFICULTY PROGRESSION

### Sentences 1–2

Use:

* one clear clause;
* one concrete idea;
* common vocabulary;
* little or no embedding.

### Sentences 3–5

Gradually add:

* descriptive modifiers;
* location phrases;
* purpose phrases;
* practical topic-specific nouns;
* two closely related objects or actions;
* a short passive construction where natural.

### Sentence 6

Use a somewhat longer instruction or description containing one of the following:

* coordination;
* a purpose phrase;
* a short time phrase;
* an added condition or result.

### Sentence 7

Use one natural complex structure, commonly involving:

* if;
* when;
* before;
* after;
* once;
* so;
* or an infinitive of purpose.

Do not use more than two finite clauses. Do not create complexity through awkward padding.

## COHERENCE REQUIREMENTS

The seven sentences must have global coherence but local independence.

Global coherence means that all seven sentences clearly belong to the same training situation.

Local independence means that each sentence remains understandable when presented separately.

Avoid sentences such as:

“Put it beside the other one.”

Prefer:

“Place the wooden panel beside the two finished frames.”

Procedural sets should follow a workable chronological sequence.

Orientation sets should move logically through the setting, its features, practical instructions, and available assistance.

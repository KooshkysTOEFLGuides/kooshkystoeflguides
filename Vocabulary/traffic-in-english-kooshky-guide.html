What Does Traffic Actually Mean?

Persian speakers often use ترافیک only when roads are crowded and cars are barely moving. In English, however, traffic does not mean “traffic jam.”

Definition
Traffic is the movement or flow of vehicles, people, aircraft, ships, information, data, or visitors through a place or system.

Traffic may be heavy, light, fast, slow, smooth, or completely stopped. It does not have to involve congestion.

Not a jam

Traffic was light, so we arrived early.

رفت‌وآمد خودروها کم بود، بنابراین زود رسیدیم.

Traffic was flowing smoothly despite the rain.

با وجود باران، جریان خودروها روان بود.

Heavy or stopped

Traffic was heavy, but it was still moving.

حجم خودروها زیاد بود، اما هنوز حرکت می‌کردند.

Traffic had come to a complete standstill.

ترافیک کاملاً متوقف شده بود.

Key distinction

A traffic jam is only one possible traffic condition. English traffic describes the flow itself, whether it is smooth or congested.

The Variety of Things Traffic Can Describe

Road traffic

The movement of cars, buses, motorcycles, trucks, and other vehicles.
  • Traffic is usually light on this road after midnight.
  • Heavy traffic delayed us by nearly forty minutes.

Pedestrian traffic

The movement of people walking through an area.
  • The square receives a lot of pedestrian traffic during the summer.
  • The new crossing was built to protect pedestrian traffic near the school.

Foot traffic

The number or movement of people walking into or through a place, especially a shop or commercial area.
  • The café gets a lot of foot traffic because it is next to the train station.
  • Closing the street to cars increased foot traffic in the shopping district.

Air traffic

The movement of aircraft through an area or airport.
  • Air traffic was disrupted by the severe storm.
  • The airport handles a large amount of international air traffic.

Maritime or shipping traffic

The movement of ships and boats.
  • Shipping traffic through the canal has increased significantly.
  • The accident temporarily blocked maritime traffic in the area.

Website or web traffic

The visits, users, or requests received by a website.
  • The article generated a huge amount of traffic for the website.
  • Our web traffic usually increases when we publish a new guide.

Network or data traffic

Information moving through a computer network.
  • The system slows down when network traffic becomes unusually heavy.
  • The company encrypts all data traffic between its servers.

Customer traffic

The movement or number of customers entering a business.
  • The store experiences heavier customer traffic on weekends.
  • The promotion brought more customer traffic into the restaurant.

Traffic Is Normally Uncountable

In its ordinary meanings, traffic is an uncountable noun. We treat it as a mass or flow, not as separate countable objects.

Avoid

Use

Singular Verb Agreement

Because traffic is uncountable, it takes a singular verb.

Examples
  • Traffic is getting worse.
  • The traffic was moving slowly.
  • Internet traffic has increased.

Using Articles with Traffic

Traffic without the

Use this when speaking generally.
  • Traffic tends to be heavier on rainy days.
  • Traffic is a major source of air pollution.
  • Web traffic has increased this month.

The traffic

Use this for a particular flow of traffic that is clear from the context.
  • The traffic outside our apartment is becoming unbearable.
  • The traffic on the northern route was moving slowly.
  • I left early, but the traffic was still terrible.
Compare

Traffic is usually heavy in large cities. This refers to traffic in general.

The traffic in this part of the city is terrible. This refers to the specific traffic in that area.

In Traffic vs. In the Traffic

In traffic is the normal expression when you mean that someone is among vehicles on the road.

  • I was stuck in traffic for an hour.
  • She spends nearly two hours a day sitting in traffic.

In the traffic is possible when the speaker means a particular, previously identified flow of traffic, but it is less common.

  • We got separated in the traffic outside the stadium.
  • I could barely see the motorcycle in the heavy traffic ahead of us.

Common Persian-Speaker Mistakes

Most errors come from treating traffic as countable or from translating a Persian sentence pattern too directly.

✗ Traffic is much today.

✓ Traffic is heavy today.

✓ There is a lot of traffic today.

✗ There are many traffics in Tehran.

✓ There is a lot of traffic in Tehran.

✓ Tehran has heavy traffic.

✓ There are many cars on the roads in Tehran.

✗ I got stuck in traffics.

✓ I got stuck in traffic.

✓ I got stuck in a traffic jam.

✗ The city has many traffic.

✓ The city has a lot of traffic.

✓ The city experiences heavy traffic.

✗ There were a lot of traffic.

✓ There was a lot of traffic.

✗ There is fewer traffic at night.

✓ There is less traffic at night.

✓ Fewer cars are on the road at night.

✗ The traffic was crowded.

✓ Traffic was heavy.

✓ The roads were crowded.

✓ The streets were congested.

✗ The traffic was very much.

✓ There was a lot of traffic.

✓ Traffic was extremely heavy.

✗ I was stuck in a traffic.

✓ I was stuck in traffic.

✓ I was stuck in a traffic jam.

✗ The traffic is full.

✓ The road is full of cars.

✓ Traffic is extremely heavy.

✓ The road is completely congested.

The “There Was a Terrible Traffic” Error

Crucial pattern

Persian speakers often begin with there was and then produce a terrible traffic. The adjective is fine, but the article a is impossible because traffic is uncountable.

Wrong

✗ There was a terrible traffic.

✗ There was a heavy traffic near the airport.

Natural English

✓ The traffic was terrible.

✓ There was terrible traffic near the airport.

✓ There was a lot of traffic near the airport.

✓ Traffic was very heavy near the airport.

The structure there was + adjective + traffic is possible without an article:

  • There was terrible traffic on the motorway.
  • There was unusually heavy traffic around the stadium.

However, when the speaker is commenting on a specific journey or situation, the traffic was terrible is often the cleanest and most natural choice.

Quantifiers Used with Traffic

A lot of traffic

The most natural everyday expression for a large amount of traffic. ترافیک زیاد / حجم زیاد رفت‌وآمد
  • There was a lot of traffic near the stadium after the match.
  • This road carries a lot of traffic during the morning rush hour.

Lots of traffic

An informal equivalent of a lot of traffic.
  • We encountered lots of traffic on the way home.
  • The festival brought lots of traffic into the town centre.

Much traffic

Grammatically correct, but most natural in questions, negative sentences, comparisons, and formal writing—not ordinary positive statements.
  • Was there much traffic on the motorway?
  • There wasn’t much traffic when we left.

Some traffic

An unspecified but limited amount of traffic.
  • We hit some traffic outside the city, but the rest of the journey was easy.
  • There may be some traffic around the airport this evening.

A little traffic

A small amount of traffic.
  • We encountered a little traffic near the bridge.
  • There was a little traffic at first, but the road soon cleared.

Little or very little traffic

Almost no traffic.
  • There is very little traffic in the village after dark.
  • We made excellent time because there was little traffic on the road.

More traffic

A greater amount or volume of traffic.
  • The new shopping centre will bring more traffic into the neighbourhood.
  • There is usually more traffic on Fridays than on other weekdays.

Less traffic

A smaller amount or volume of traffic.
  • We left early because there would be less traffic.
  • The bypass has resulted in less traffic in the town centre.

Too much traffic

More traffic than is desirable or manageable.
  • There is too much traffic for such a narrow residential street.
  • We moved away because the area had too much traffic and noise.

A certain amount of traffic

An unspecified or expected volume.
  • Any popular tourist attraction will generate a certain amount of traffic.
  • The server can handle a certain amount of traffic before it slows down.

A large amount of traffic

A more formal version of a lot of traffic.
  • The bridge carries a large amount of traffic every day.
  • The website received a large amount of traffic after the announcement.

Describing the Amount and Condition of Road Traffic

Everyday descriptions

Heavy traffic

A large number of vehicles, often causing slow movement. ترافیک سنگین
  • We were delayed by heavy traffic on the motorway.
  • Heavy traffic is expected around the stadium before the concert.

Light traffic

A small number of vehicles, allowing easy movement. ترافیک سبک
  • Traffic was light, so we reached the airport in twenty minutes.
  • The roads usually have light traffic early on Sunday mornings.

Moderate traffic

Neither particularly heavy nor particularly light. ترافیک متوسط
  • Drivers should expect moderate traffic throughout the afternoon.
  • Traffic remained moderate despite the public holiday.

Bad traffic

Traffic that is unpleasant, slow, or heavily congested. ترافیک بد
  • The traffic was bad near the city centre this morning.
  • We left before six because traffic gets bad after that.

Terrible, awful, or horrendous traffic

Very heavy or severely congested traffic. ترافیک افتضاح / بسیار سنگین
  • Traffic was absolutely terrible after the football match.
  • We encountered horrendous traffic on the way to the airport.

Dense traffic

A large number of vehicles travelling close together; slightly more formal than heavy traffic. ترافیک متراکم
  • Drivers must maintain a safe distance in dense traffic.
  • The accident occurred in dense traffic near the tunnel entrance.
Descriptions of movement

Slow-moving traffic

Traffic that is still moving, but very slowly. ترافیک کندرو
  • We spent nearly an hour in slow-moving traffic.
  • Slow-moving traffic has been reported near the eastern exit.

Fast-moving traffic

Traffic moving at relatively high speed.
  • It can be dangerous to cross a road with fast-moving traffic.
  • Cyclists need extra protection from fast-moving traffic on this route.

Free-flowing traffic

Traffic moving continuously and without significant obstruction. ترافیک روان
  • The new junction was designed to keep traffic free-flowing.
  • Traffic was free-flowing again once the damaged vehicle had been removed.

Smooth-flowing traffic

Traffic moving regularly and without delays.
  • The wider lanes allow smooth-flowing traffic during most of the day.
  • Police reopened the road, and traffic soon became smooth-flowing again.

Stop-and-go traffic

Traffic that repeatedly moves and stops. ترافیک حرکت و توقف
  • My car uses much more fuel in stop-and-go traffic.
  • We spent forty minutes in stop-and-go traffic near the city centre.

Bumper-to-bumper traffic

Extremely dense traffic in which vehicles are very close together. ترافیک سپر‌به‌سپر
  • The highway was packed with bumper-to-bumper traffic.
  • We crawled through bumper-to-bumper traffic for several kilometres.

Stationary traffic

Traffic that is not moving at all. ترافیک متوقف
  • Drivers were trapped in stationary traffic after the accident.
  • The camera showed several kilometres of stationary traffic.

Gridlocked traffic

Traffic that is completely blocked, often across several streets or intersections. ترافیک قفل‌شده
  • Central streets were gridlocked during the demonstration.
  • The entire area became gridlocked after the traffic lights failed.
Usage note

Congested roads is generally more natural than congested traffic.

  • The city’s roads become heavily congested during rush hour.
  • We avoided the congested streets around the market.

Traffic at Particular Times and Places

Rush-hour traffic

Heavy traffic when large numbers of people travel to or from work. ترافیک ساعت شلوغی
  • I leave home early to avoid rush-hour traffic.
  • Rush-hour traffic can double the length of the journey.

Peak-hour traffic

Traffic during the busiest period; slightly more formal than rush-hour traffic.
  • Additional buses operate during peak-hour traffic.
  • The junction cannot cope with current peak-hour traffic levels.

Morning traffic

Traffic during the morning, often associated with commuting.
  • Morning traffic is usually worst between seven and nine.
  • We left before the morning traffic began to build up.

Evening traffic

Traffic during the late afternoon or evening commuting period.
  • Evening traffic was heavier than usual because of the rain.
  • It takes me nearly an hour to get home in evening traffic.

Weekend traffic

Traffic occurring during the weekend, often near shopping areas or tourist destinations.
  • Weekend traffic can be heavy near the coast.
  • The road was quiet despite the usual weekend traffic.

Holiday traffic

Traffic caused by large numbers of people travelling during a holiday period.
  • Holiday traffic created long delays on the main motorway.
  • Drivers were advised to travel early to avoid holiday traffic.

Commuter traffic

Traffic created by people travelling between home and work. ترافیک رفت‌وآمد شاغلان
  • The railway expansion is intended to reduce commuter traffic.
  • Commuter traffic begins to increase shortly before seven.

City or urban traffic

Traffic within a city or urban area.
  • Electric scooters are becoming increasingly common in urban traffic.
  • She is not confident enough to drive in heavy city traffic.

Highway, motorway, freeway, or interstate traffic

Traffic on a major high-speed road. Motorway is mainly British English; freeway and interstate are common in American English.
  • Motorway traffic was moving normally by midday.
  • The crash caused major delays for highway traffic.

Downtown or city-centre traffic

Traffic in the central commercial area of a city.
  • I avoid downtown traffic by taking the train.
  • City-centre traffic is restricted during the festival.

Residential traffic

Traffic passing through a residential neighbourhood.
  • Residents are concerned about increasing residential traffic.
  • The new barrier prevents heavy traffic from entering this residential street.

Traffic According to Direction or Purpose

Oncoming traffic

Vehicles travelling toward you from the opposite direction. خودروهای روبه‌رو
  • Do not overtake unless you have a clear view of oncoming traffic.
  • The driver moved into the path of oncoming traffic.

Opposing traffic

Traffic travelling in the opposite direction.
  • A central barrier separates opposing traffic.
  • The vehicle crossed the line and entered opposing traffic.

Same-direction traffic

Traffic moving in the same direction as you or another stated stream of traffic; the opposite of opposing traffic.
  • The express lane is separated from same-direction traffic by flexible barriers.
  • Before changing lanes, check for faster traffic moving in the same direction.

Traffic going in your direction

A natural everyday alternative to same-direction traffic.
  • The lane beside us was carrying traffic going in our direction.
  • Oncoming traffic was heavy, but traffic going in our direction was moving freely.

Cross traffic

Traffic moving across your path, particularly at an intersection. ترافیک عبوری از عرض مسیر
  • Watch for cross traffic before entering the junction.
  • The sign warns drivers that cross traffic does not stop.

Merging traffic

Traffic entering and joining another stream of traffic. ترافیک در حال ادغام
  • Drivers should make space for merging traffic.
  • Merging traffic caused delays near the motorway entrance.

Through traffic

Traffic passing through an area without stopping there. خودروهای عبوری
  • The town centre is closed to through traffic.
  • The bypass keeps through traffic away from residential streets.

Local traffic

Traffic belonging to people who live, work, or have business in the immediate area.
  • The road remains open to local traffic.
  • Only local traffic is permitted beyond this point.

Inbound traffic

Traffic travelling into a city, area, airport, or other destination. ترافیک ورودی
  • Inbound traffic is extremely heavy this morning.
  • Police closed one lane to improve the flow of inbound traffic.

Outbound traffic

Traffic travelling out of a city, area, airport, or other destination. ترافیک خروجی
  • Outbound traffic usually increases on Friday afternoons.
  • The accident affected outbound traffic from the city.

Northbound, southbound, eastbound, or westbound traffic

Traffic travelling in a stated direction.
  • Northbound traffic has been diverted onto another road.
  • The collision blocked two lanes of westbound traffic.

One-way traffic

Traffic permitted to travel in only one direction.
  • This street is restricted to one-way traffic.
  • The council introduced a one-way traffic system around the square.

Two-way traffic

Traffic travelling in both directions.
  • The bridge has reopened to two-way traffic.
  • The road is too narrow to carry two-way traffic safely.

Diverted traffic

Traffic sent along a different route.
  • Diverted traffic caused congestion in nearby villages.
  • Signs will guide diverted traffic around the construction site.

Passing traffic

Traffic moving past a particular place.
  • The shop depends heavily on passing traffic.
  • The house is protected from the noise of passing traffic by a high wall.

Experiencing Traffic

Be stuck in traffic

To be unable to move normally because traffic is slow or stopped. در ترافیک گیر کردن
  • I’m sorry I’m late; I was stuck in traffic.
  • We were stuck in traffic for nearly an hour after the concert.

Get stuck in traffic

To enter a situation in which traffic prevents normal movement.
  • Leave now, or you’ll get stuck in traffic.
  • We got stuck in traffic just outside the city.

Get caught in traffic

To unexpectedly encounter heavy or slow traffic. ناخواسته به ترافیک خوردن
  • We got caught in heavy traffic on the way home.
  • She left early but still got caught in rush-hour traffic.

Hit traffic

To reach or encounter an area of heavy or slow traffic. به ترافیک خوردن
  • We hit traffic shortly after leaving the airport.
  • Everything was going well until we hit traffic near the bridge.

Run into traffic

To encounter traffic unexpectedly. It can also literally mean running into a road among moving vehicles, so context determines the meaning.
  • We ran into some traffic near the city centre.
  • You may run into heavy traffic if you leave after five.

Sit in traffic

To spend time inside a vehicle while traffic moves very slowly or not at all.
  • I’m tired of sitting in traffic every morning.
  • She would rather take the train than sit in traffic for two hours.

Wait in traffic

To remain in slow or stationary traffic.
  • We waited in traffic for almost forty minutes.
  • An ambulance was forced to wait in traffic behind several cars.

Spend time in traffic

To use a substantial amount of time travelling in congested conditions.
  • He spends nearly three hours a day in traffic.
  • I do not want to spend my entire evening in traffic.

Battle or fight traffic

To make a difficult journey through heavy traffic. با ترافیک دست‌وپنجه نرم کردن
  • I have to battle traffic every morning on my way to work.
  • Thousands of drivers fought heavy traffic to reach the stadium.

Brave the traffic

To travel despite expecting unpleasant or heavy traffic.
  • We decided to brave the traffic and drive into the city.
  • Thousands of shoppers braved the holiday traffic.

Avoid traffic

To choose a route, time, or method of transportation that prevents encountering heavy traffic.
  • I take the metro to avoid traffic.
  • We left before sunrise to avoid the holiday traffic.

Beat the traffic

To travel before traffic becomes heavy. قبل از شروع شلوغی راه افتادن
  • Let’s leave at six so we can beat the traffic.
  • She starts work early to beat the morning traffic.

Miss the traffic

To travel at a time when traffic has not begun or has already ended.
  • If we leave after eight, we may miss the evening traffic.
  • We delayed our departure so that we could miss the rush-hour traffic.

Drive through traffic

To travel through an area containing traffic.
  • She remained calm while driving through heavy traffic.
  • It took us an hour to drive through the city traffic.

Navigate traffic

To move carefully and successfully through traffic.
  • Motorcyclists can often navigate traffic more easily than cars.
  • The driver skillfully navigated the heavy traffic around the station.

Weave through traffic

To move repeatedly between vehicles, often quickly or dangerously. لایی کشیدن میان خودروها
  • The motorcyclist was weaving dangerously through traffic.
  • The ambulance carefully weaved through the stationary traffic.

Merge into traffic

To enter and join the existing flow of vehicles.
  • Use your mirror before merging into traffic.
  • She waited for a safe opportunity to merge into traffic.

Pull out into traffic

To move from a parked position or side road into the stream of traffic.
  • He pulled out into traffic without checking his mirror.
  • Wait until the road is clear before pulling out into traffic.

Cut through traffic

To move through traffic, often by changing lanes or using a quicker route.
  • The taxi driver cut through traffic to get us to the station.
  • Emergency vehicles need enough space to cut through traffic safely.

How Traffic Moves

Traffic flows

Traffic moves continuously. جریان داشتن ترافیک
  • Traffic is flowing normally again after the accident.
  • The new roundabout allows traffic to flow more efficiently.

Traffic flows smoothly

Traffic moves without major delays or interruptions.
  • Traffic was flowing smoothly despite the wet weather.
  • Once the broken vehicle was removed, traffic began to flow smoothly.

Traffic moves slowly

Traffic continues moving, but at a low speed.
  • Traffic is moving slowly near the tunnel.
  • We could see that traffic ahead of us was moving very slowly.

Traffic crawls

Traffic moves extremely slowly. ترافیک لاک‌پشتی حرکت کردن
  • Traffic crawled along the motorway for several kilometres.
  • We watched the traffic crawl past the construction site.

Traffic backs up

A line of slow or stationary vehicles begins to form.
  • Traffic began to back up after one lane was closed.
  • If the signal fails, traffic quickly backs up into the next intersection.

Traffic is backed up

Traffic has formed a long queue.
  • Traffic is backed up for nearly five kilometres.
  • The news reported that traffic was backed up beyond the airport exit.

Traffic builds up

The amount of traffic gradually increases. ترافیک کم‌کم سنگین شدن
  • Traffic starts to build up around four in the afternoon.
  • We left before traffic had a chance to build up.

Traffic piles up

Vehicles accumulate because the road is blocked or moving slowly. This often suggests a problem.
  • Traffic piled up behind the broken-down truck.
  • Cars began to pile up when the traffic lights stopped working.

Traffic comes to a standstill

Traffic stops completely. ترافیک کاملاً متوقف شدن
  • Traffic came to a complete standstill after the crash.
  • Within minutes, traffic in the entire district had come to a standstill.

Traffic grinds to a halt

Traffic gradually or dramatically stops completely. The past tense in this expression is usually ground.
  • Traffic ground to a halt when protesters entered the road.
  • A sudden snowstorm caused traffic to grind to a halt.

Traffic queues

Vehicles form a line while waiting to move. This is especially common in British English.
  • Traffic was queuing for several kilometres near the border.
  • Drivers should expect traffic to queue near the temporary lights.

Traffic eases

Traffic becomes less heavy or congested. ترافیک سبک‌تر شدن
  • Traffic usually eases after seven in the evening.
  • We waited at a café until the traffic had eased.

Traffic clears

The vehicles or congestion disappear, allowing normal movement again.
  • Traffic should clear once the event finishes.
  • The accident was removed, and traffic gradually cleared.

Traffic thins out

The number of vehicles gradually decreases. حجم ترافیک کم شدن
  • Traffic begins to thin out after the evening rush hour.
  • We continued driving as the traffic slowly thinned out.

Traffic picks up

The amount of traffic begins to increase. It can also mean that the speed of traffic increases; context tells you which meaning is intended.
  • Traffic tends to pick up around lunchtime.
  • Traffic finally picked up after the damaged car was removed.

Traffic resumes

Traffic begins moving again after an interruption.
  • Traffic resumed after police reopened the bridge.
  • Normal traffic resumed shortly before noon.

Traffic passes

Vehicles move past a place.
  • Heavy traffic passes our house throughout the day.
  • Very little traffic passes through the village in winter.

Causing, Controlling, or Changing Traffic

Cause traffic

To create traffic or make existing traffic heavier. More precise alternatives include cause traffic congestion, cause delays, and cause a traffic jam.
  • The accident caused heavy traffic throughout the area.
  • Poorly timed roadworks can cause serious traffic during rush hour.

Create traffic

To produce additional road use or vehicle movement.
  • The new stadium will create more traffic in the neighbourhood.
  • Large public events often create heavy traffic around the venue.

Generate traffic

To produce traffic; especially common in planning, business, websites, and computer systems.
  • The shopping centre is expected to generate thousands of additional car journeys.
  • The advertisement generated a large amount of website traffic.

Add to traffic

To increase the amount of existing traffic.
  • Parents driving their children to school add to morning traffic.
  • The new office buildings will add to traffic in the city centre.

Hold up traffic

To delay or obstruct the movement of traffic. موجب معطلی ترافیک شدن
  • A broken-down bus was holding up traffic.
  • The police stopped the vehicles without holding up traffic for too long.

Block traffic

To prevent vehicles from passing.
  • A fallen tree was blocking traffic in both directions.
  • Do not park here; your car may block traffic.

Disrupt traffic

To interfere with the normal movement of traffic.
  • The demonstration disrupted traffic throughout the city centre.
  • Heavy snow is expected to disrupt traffic tomorrow morning.

Interrupt traffic

To temporarily stop or interfere with traffic.
  • Construction vehicles may occasionally interrupt traffic.
  • The parade interrupted traffic for approximately twenty minutes.

Divert traffic

To send traffic along a different route. تغییر مسیر ترافیک
  • Police diverted traffic away from the damaged bridge.
  • Traffic will be diverted through nearby streets during construction.

Redirect traffic

To direct traffic toward another road or route.
  • Officers redirected traffic around the accident scene.
  • The navigation system redirected traffic to less congested roads.

Reroute traffic

To establish a different route for traffic.
  • The council rerouted traffic while the tunnel was being repaired.
  • Emergency services rerouted traffic through the industrial district.

Direct traffic

To tell drivers when and where to move, often using hand signals.
  • A police officer was directing traffic at the intersection.
  • Volunteers helped direct traffic into the festival car park.

Control traffic

To regulate or organize the movement of traffic.
  • Traffic lights are used to control traffic at busy junctions.
  • Police struggled to control traffic outside the stadium.

Regulate traffic

To control traffic through rules, signals, or systems.
  • The new signals regulate traffic entering the tunnel.
  • Authorities introduced restrictions to regulate heavy vehicle traffic.

Manage traffic

To plan, organize, or control traffic efficiently.
  • The city needs a better system to manage traffic during major events.
  • Smart sensors can help authorities manage traffic more effectively.

Monitor traffic

To observe traffic conditions.
  • Cameras are used to monitor traffic throughout the city.
  • The control centre monitors traffic twenty-four hours a day.

Reduce traffic

To decrease the amount of traffic.
  • Better public transport could reduce traffic in the city centre.
  • The congestion charge was introduced to reduce traffic.

Ease traffic

To make traffic less heavy or congested. کاهش دادن فشار ترافیک
  • The new bridge is expected to ease traffic on the old route.
  • Staggered working hours could help ease morning traffic.

Relieve traffic congestion

To reduce the seriousness of congestion.
  • The bypass was built to relieve traffic congestion in the town.
  • Additional train services may relieve traffic congestion around the airport.

Improve traffic flow

To help vehicles move more smoothly or efficiently.
  • The junction was redesigned to improve traffic flow.
  • Removing roadside parking could significantly improve the flow of traffic.

Keep traffic moving

To prevent traffic from stopping or becoming congested.
  • Police closed one lane but managed to keep traffic moving.
  • Drivers should leave the intersection clear to keep traffic moving.

Calm traffic

To reduce vehicle speeds and make streets safer, particularly in residential areas. The related noun is traffic calming.
  • Speed bumps were installed to calm traffic near the school.
  • The council has introduced several traffic-calming measures.

Important Traffic Compounds

Traffic jam

A line or mass of vehicles that cannot move normally because the road is congested. Unlike traffic, this is countable. راه‌بندان
  • We were stuck in a huge traffic jam for over an hour.
  • Several traffic jams formed after the motorway was closed.

Traffic congestion

A condition in which too many vehicles prevent efficient movement; normally uncountable. ازدحام ترافیکی
  • Traffic congestion is a serious problem in many large cities.
  • The government is investing in public transport to reduce traffic congestion.

Traffic flow

The movement of vehicles through a road or transport system. جریان ترافیک
  • The accident interrupted the normal flow of traffic.
  • The new signals have significantly improved traffic flow.

Traffic volume

The amount or number of vehicles using a road during a particular period. حجم ترافیک
  • Traffic volume increases sharply during the morning rush hour.
  • The bridge was not designed to handle the current volume of traffic.

Traffic density

An accident involving road vehicles. Car accident, road accident, crash, and collision are often more common.

A more formal term that may refer to a traffic light or another signal controlling movement.

The system or activity of controlling vehicle movement.

Traffic management

The planning and control of traffic systems.

Traffic camera

A camera used to monitor traffic or detect violations.

Traffic police

Police officers responsible for road safety and traffic laws.

Traffic lane

A marked section of road intended for a line of vehicles. Usually, simply lane is more natural when the context is clear.

Traffic island

A raised or marked area in a road that separates traffic or gives pedestrians a safe place to wait.

Traffic circle

A circular road junction. Roundabout is more common in British English.

Traffic noise

Noise produced by vehicles.

Traffic pollution

Pollution produced by road vehicles.

Visitor traffic

The number or movement of people visiting a place.

A road used by a large number of vehicles. Here, trafficked means “used by a lot of traffic.”

Air traffic

The movement of aircraft.

Illegal Trade: A Separate Meaning of Traffic

The noun and verb traffic can also refer to the illegal or dishonest buying, selling, or movement of goods or people. This meaning is separate from ordinary road or internet traffic.

Traffic in something

The illegal buying or selling of something.
  • Police uncovered an international traffic in stolen artwork.
  • The report describes the illegal traffic in endangered animals.

Traffic in something as a verb

To deal illegally in goods or people.
  • The group was accused of trafficking in stolen weapons.
  • Several individuals were arrested for trafficking in illegal drugs.

Do not confuse

A heavily trafficked road means a road used by many vehicles.

Trafficking in drugs means illegally buying, selling, or transporting drugs.

For people, human trafficking is the normal expression—not human traffic.

Remember

Traffic is an uncountable flow, not a countable collection of traffic jams.

You can count the things moving within traffic—three cars, twenty pedestrians, five aircraft, ten thousand website visitors, or a million data packets—but the flow itself remains uncountable: heavy traffic, a lot of traffic, less traffic, some traffic.

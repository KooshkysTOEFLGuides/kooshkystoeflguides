Kooshky’s TOEFL Guides

Advanced Transition Words & Phrases for High-Scoring TOEFL Responses

This guide is for students who already know the basic connectors and want to use more sophisticated transitions accurately in TOEFL Writing and Speaking. Advanced transitions are powerful only when the logic, grammar, punctuation, and tone are correct.

Important: High-level transitions do not automatically improve a response. They improve a response only when they show the correct relationship between ideas. A simple transition used correctly is better than an advanced transition used incorrectly.

Advanced Addition, Extension, and Reinforcement 11 items

These transitions add another layer to an argument. They are useful when the second point is not merely extra information, but a stronger, wider, or more refined development of the previous idea.

beyond this Adds a further point that goes past the previous idea.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the start of a sentence: Beyond this, + complete sentence.

Nuance & implication

More polished than besides that. It suggests that the new point expands the discussion rather than simply adding a random detail.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it when the new point is unrelated. It should feel like a natural extension.

Model examples

  • Beyond this, smaller classes allow teachers to notice students’ weaknesses more quickly.
  • Beyond this, the policy may improve public trust in local government.
apart from this Adds another point while slightly separating it from the previous one.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning of a sentence: Apart from this, + complete sentence. It can also be followed by a noun phrase: Apart from cost, convenience is important.

Nuance & implication

Often means “in addition to this.” It is useful when you want to introduce another factor without making it sound stronger than the previous one.

Common mistake / caution: In American academic writing, “aside from this” is often more natural than “apart from this,” but both are acceptable.

Model examples

  • Apart from this, online learning can help students who live far from campus.
  • Apart from salary, job satisfaction should also be considered.
aside from this Adds another relevant factor or exception.

Grammar & punctuation

Use before a full sentence or noun phrase: Aside from this, + sentence; Aside from X, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Slightly more conversational than apart from this but still polished. Good for TOEFL speaking and writing.

Common mistake / caution: Do not confuse it with an exception. In some contexts, aside from can mean except for.

Model examples

  • Aside from this, students need emotional support during exam periods.
  • Aside from the cost, the plan has few serious disadvantages.
not to mention Adds a point that is almost too obvious or important to ignore.

Grammar & punctuation

Use before a noun phrase or clause-like phrase: not to mention + noun/gerund. Full clause use is common in speech but less formal.

Nuance & implication

It often creates a persuasive tone: the new point feels like extra evidence that strengthens the argument.

Common mistake / caution: Useful in speaking and persuasive writing, but a little informal for very formal academic essays.

Model examples

  • The schedule is exhausting, not to mention unfair to students who work part-time.
  • The city needs better buses, not to mention safer sidewalks.
let alone Adds a stronger or more extreme point after a weaker one.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: negative/limited statement + let alone + stronger noun/verb phrase. Example: cannot afford X, let alone Y.

Nuance & implication

It means “and certainly not the stronger thing.” It is excellent for emphasizing scale or difficulty.

Common mistake / caution: Usually used after a negative idea. Do not use it as a simple synonym for also.

Model examples

  • Many students cannot afford private tutoring, let alone expensive international programs.
  • The town lacks basic bus service, let alone a modern subway system.
coupled with this Adds a second factor that works together with the first.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the start of a sentence: Coupled with this, + sentence. Or inside a sentence: X, coupled with Y, creates Z.

Nuance & implication

Strong for cause-effect arguments because it suggests two factors combine to produce a result.

Common mistake / caution: More formal than together with this. Avoid overusing it in speaking.

Model examples

  • Coupled with this, rising housing costs make life especially difficult for young workers.
  • Limited funding, coupled with poor planning, weakened the project.
in the same vein Adds a point with a similar spirit, logic, or direction.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: In the same vein, + complete sentence.

Nuance & implication

This does not simply mean “also.” It means the next idea follows the same type of reasoning or belongs to the same line of thought.

Common mistake / caution: Best for writing and formal speaking; too polished for casual conversation.

Model examples

  • In the same vein, schools should teach students how to evaluate online information.
  • In the same vein, governments can encourage recycling through public education.
along similar lines Introduces a similar argument, example, or policy.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the start of a sentence: Along similar lines, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

A flexible academic phrase for moving from one related idea to another without sounding repetitive.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it for contrast. It signals similarity.

Model examples

  • Along similar lines, universities could offer workshops on financial planning.
  • Along similar lines, teachers can use peer review to improve students’ writing.
by the same token Introduces a parallel point based on the same logic.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning of a sentence: By the same token, + complete sentence.

Nuance & implication

It means “for the same reason.” It is more logical than similarly and more advanced than also.

Common mistake / caution: Use only when the same reasoning truly applies to both points.

Model examples

  • Students should receive feedback on essays. By the same token, they should receive feedback on presentations.
  • If cities invest in roads, by the same token, they should invest in public transportation.
more to the point Adds a point that is more directly relevant than the previous one.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: More to the point, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

This can politely signal that the previous idea was useful but not central; the next idea is closer to the main argument.

Common mistake / caution: Can sound slightly corrective, so use it carefully.

Model examples

  • The app is attractive. More to the point, it helps students practice every day.
  • The building is old. More to the point, it is unsafe.
taken together Shows that several points create a combined conclusion.

Grammar & punctuation

Use before a conclusion: Taken together, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Very useful for synthesis. It tells the reader not to view the previous points separately but as a combined pattern.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use before introducing the separate points. Use it after several points have already been given.

Model examples

  • Taken together, these factors show that the policy is realistic.
  • Taken together, the examples suggest that motivation depends on both freedom and structure.
Advanced Comparison and Parallel Logic 9 items

These transitions compare ideas, show similarity, or build parallel reasoning. They help students sound more analytical, especially in TOEFL Academic Discussion and Integrated Writing.

similarly Shows that the next idea is similar to the previous one.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning of a sentence: Similarly, + complete sentence.

Nuance & implication

Cleaner and more academic than also when comparing two cases, groups, or situations.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it for simple addition unless there is a real similarity.

Model examples

  • Many adults learn better through practice. Similarly, children often understand ideas more deeply when they apply them.
  • Public libraries support students. Similarly, community centers can provide safe learning spaces.
likewise Shows that the same thing is true in another case.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: Likewise, + complete sentence.

Nuance & implication

Slightly more formal and compact than similarly. It often means “the same is true here.”

Common mistake / caution: Can sound formal in speaking; good in essays.

Model examples

  • Parents influence children’s habits. Likewise, teachers shape students’ attitudes toward learning.
  • Technology can save time. Likewise, it can make communication easier.
correspondingly Shows that one thing changes or exists in relation to another.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning of a sentence or before an adjective/adverb.

Nuance & implication

More precise than similarly. It suggests a matching relationship, often between cause and measured effect.

Common mistake / caution: Best for analytical writing. Do not use it just to mean also.

Model examples

  • As demand increased, prices rose correspondingly.
  • Students received more feedback, and their confidence correspondingly improved.
in parallel Shows that two things happen or develop at the same time in a similar way.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning or middle: In parallel, + sentence; X developed in parallel with Y.

Nuance & implication

Useful in academic explanations, especially for social, historical, or scientific developments.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it if the ideas are merely similar but not developing side by side.

Model examples

  • In parallel, universities began offering more online support services.
  • Public concern grew in parallel with the rise in pollution.
in much the same way Shows a close similarity in process or manner.

Grammar & punctuation

Use before or after explaining the second case.

Nuance & implication

Useful when two different examples work through the same mechanism.

Common mistake / caution: Use when the similarity is in how something happens, not just in the final result.

Model examples

  • In much the same way, regular writing practice helps students become more fluent.
  • Children learn social rules through observation; in much the same way, language learners absorb patterns through repeated exposure.
the same holds true for Shows that the same idea applies to another person, group, or situation.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: The same holds true for + noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

A polished way to avoid repeating a full explanation.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it unless the previous point clearly applies to the new subject.

Model examples

  • The same holds true for international students, who often need both academic and social support.
  • The same holds true for public health campaigns.
this is equally true of Shows that a claim applies just as strongly to another case.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: This is equally true of + noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

Stronger than the same holds true for because it emphasizes equal force.

Common mistake / caution: Best when comparing two domains or groups.

Model examples

  • This is equally true of speaking skills, which require regular practice.
  • This is equally true of rural communities, where internet access may be limited.
a similar pattern can be seen in Introduces another example that follows the same pattern.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: A similar pattern can be seen in + noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

Very useful for academic writing because it sounds analytical rather than conversational.

Common mistake / caution: Make sure there really is a pattern, not just a single shared detail.

Model examples

  • A similar pattern can be seen in workplace training programs.
  • A similar pattern can be seen in countries that invested early in public transportation.
in a comparable way Shows that two things are similar in a relevant but not identical way.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning or middle of a sentence.

Nuance & implication

More cautious than in the same way. It admits that the examples are not exactly the same.

Common mistake / caution: Good for nuanced academic comparisons.

Model examples

  • In a comparable way, online forums can create a sense of community among remote students.
  • Libraries support independent learning; museums can do so in a comparable way.
Advanced Contrast, Concession, and Counterargument 15 items

These transitions help students present complexity: one idea may be true, but another idea limits, challenges, or balances it. This is one of the fastest ways to sound more advanced in TOEFL writing.

by contrast Introduces a clear difference from the previous idea.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: By contrast, + complete sentence.

Nuance & implication

Stronger and cleaner than on the other hand when comparing two different cases.

Common mistake / caution: Use it for direct comparison, not for a small unrelated difference.

Model examples

  • Urban schools often have more resources. By contrast, rural schools may struggle to hire enough teachers.
  • Some students enjoy competition. By contrast, others feel anxious and perform worse.
in contrast Introduces an opposite or clearly different point.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning of a sentence or before a noun phrase: In contrast, + sentence; in contrast to + noun.

Nuance & implication

Very similar to by contrast. In contrast can also be used with to: In contrast to X, Y...

Common mistake / caution: Do not write “in contrast with” as often in TOEFL; “in contrast to” is safer for students.

Model examples

  • In contrast, face-to-face classes allow more immediate interaction.
  • In contrast to traditional lectures, project-based learning requires students to solve real problems.
conversely Introduces the opposite relationship or reverse situation.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: Conversely, + complete sentence.

Nuance & implication

More precise than however. It often means “the opposite is true when the situation is reversed.”

Common mistake / caution: Do not use conversely for any contrast. Use it when there is a real reverse relationship.

Model examples

  • Students who receive support may become more confident. Conversely, students who feel ignored may stop participating.
  • A clear schedule can reduce stress. Conversely, a disorganized schedule can increase it.
on the contrary Corrects a previous idea by saying the opposite is true.

Grammar & punctuation

Use after a negative statement or after rejecting an assumption.

Nuance & implication

This is not the same as on the other hand. It means “no, the opposite is true.”

Common mistake / caution: Common mistake: using on the contrary to introduce a second side. Use it only to correct or refute.

Model examples

  • The policy did not reduce student independence. On the contrary, it helped students manage their time better.
  • Technology has not made teachers unnecessary. On the contrary, it has made their guidance more important.
that said Introduces a limitation, contrast, or balancing point after agreeing with something.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: That said, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Natural and advanced in discussion. It is less formal than nevertheless but very useful for TOEFL speaking and Academic Discussion.

Common mistake / caution: Good for speaking and modern written discussion; slightly less formal than nevertheless.

Model examples

  • Online learning is convenient. That said, students still need regular interaction with teachers.
  • The plan is expensive. That said, it may save money over time.
having said that Introduces a balancing point after a previous claim.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: Having said that, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

More conversational than however, but sophisticated when used well. It means “although what I just said is true, I must add this.”

Common mistake / caution: Better for speaking and semi-formal writing than very formal essays.

Model examples

  • Universities should encourage independence. Having said that, first-year students still need guidance.
  • Grades can motivate students. Having said that, they can also create unnecessary pressure.
admittedly Concedes that a point against your argument is true.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: Admittedly, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Very useful for counterargument. It makes the writer sound fair and mature before defending their position.

Common mistake / caution: After admittedly, usually follow with a contrast such as still, nevertheless, or this does not mean.

Model examples

  • Admittedly, online courses require strong self-discipline.
  • Admittedly, public transportation can be inconvenient in some neighborhoods.
to be sure Acknowledges a valid point before limiting it or moving beyond it.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: To be sure, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Formal and balanced. It often prepares the reader for a concession.

Common mistake / caution: Best for formal writing; may sound too formal in casual speaking.

Model examples

  • To be sure, exams can measure some forms of knowledge.
  • To be sure, technology has made communication faster.
granted Accepts a point as true before presenting a stronger point.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: Granted, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

More conversational than admittedly. It means “I accept this, but...”

Common mistake / caution: Usually needs a follow-up contrast. Do not leave it unsupported.

Model examples

  • Granted, group projects can be difficult to organize.
  • Granted, city life can be expensive.
be that as it may Means even if that is true, the main point still stands.

Grammar & punctuation

Use as a sentence transition: Be that as it may, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Very formal and somewhat old-fashioned, but still useful in high-level writing if used sparingly.

Common mistake / caution: Use rarely. It can sound dramatic if overused.

Model examples

  • Be that as it may, schools still have a responsibility to support struggling students.
  • Be that as it may, the evidence points to a serious problem.
all the same Means despite that; the following idea remains true.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning or end of a sentence.

Nuance & implication

Less formal than nevertheless. It is natural in speaking and polished enough for many TOEFL responses.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it for simple contrast; it carries a concession meaning.

Model examples

  • The method is not perfect. All the same, it helps many students.
  • The trip was tiring, but it was valuable all the same.
despite this Introduces something that happens or remains true although the previous fact might prevent it.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: Despite this, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

A concise alternative to nevertheless. It is clear and academic.

Common mistake / caution: Do not write “despite of this.” The correct form is despite this or in spite of this.

Model examples

  • The program is costly. Despite this, many schools have adopted it.
  • The results were limited. Despite this, the study raised important questions.
notwithstanding Means despite, but in a very formal style.

Grammar & punctuation

Use before a noun phrase or after a noun phrase: Notwithstanding these concerns, + sentence; These concerns notwithstanding, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

High-level but formal. Useful for academic writing, not natural in TOEFL speaking.

Common mistake / caution: Avoid using it too often. One strong use can sound impressive; repeated use sounds unnatural.

Model examples

  • Notwithstanding these concerns, the policy has several clear benefits.
  • These limitations notwithstanding, the research remains valuable.
for all its advantages Introduces a limitation after acknowledging benefits.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: For all its/their + positive noun, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

A sophisticated concession phrase. It means “even though it has many advantages.”

Common mistake / caution: Make sure the noun after for all is positive or beneficial.

Model examples

  • For all its advantages, remote work can make employees feel isolated.
  • For all their convenience, smartphones can seriously damage concentration.
while this is a valid concern Acknowledges an opposing view before responding to it.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning of a sentence, followed by a contrast.

Nuance & implication

Very useful in TOEFL Academic Discussion because it sounds respectful and balanced.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it if no concern has been mentioned.

Model examples

  • While this is a valid concern, it does not outweigh the benefits of the proposal.
  • While this is a valid concern, schools can reduce the problem through careful planning.
Advanced Cause, Result, and Logical Inference 15 items

These transitions connect reasons, consequences, and conclusions. They are powerful in TOEFL writing because they make arguments feel logical rather than merely opinion-based.

accordingly Introduces an action or conclusion that logically follows from the previous point.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: Accordingly, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

More formal than therefore. It often suggests that a response or adjustment is appropriate.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it for a random result; it should feel like a reasonable response.

Model examples

  • Many students struggle with time management. Accordingly, universities should offer planning workshops.
  • The evidence was incomplete. Accordingly, the researchers avoided strong conclusions.
in turn Shows the next step in a chain of effects.

Grammar & punctuation

Use inside or at the beginning of a sentence: X causes Y, which in turn causes Z.

Nuance & implication

Excellent for explaining multi-step cause and effect.

Common mistake / caution: Use when one effect becomes the cause of another effect.

Model examples

  • Better feedback increases confidence, which in turn encourages students to participate more.
  • Higher fuel prices can reduce driving; this, in turn, may lower pollution.
thereby Means by doing so; shows the result of an action.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: clause + thereby + -ing verb phrase.

Nuance & implication

Very concise and academic. It connects an action directly to its result.

Common mistake / caution: Grammar warning: thereby is usually followed by an -ing form, not a full sentence.

Model examples

  • Schools can provide free tutoring, thereby helping weaker students keep up.
  • The city expanded bike lanes, thereby reducing short car trips.
as such Means because of this identity/status; therefore in that specific role.

Grammar & punctuation

Use carefully: X is Y. As such, X has the qualities/responsibilities of Y.

Nuance & implication

Often misused as a fancy replacement for therefore. Correct use depends on status or category.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use as such to mean simply therefore. There must be a clear noun identity before it.

Model examples

  • Teachers are not just information providers. As such, they must also guide students emotionally.
  • Libraries are public learning spaces. As such, they should remain accessible to everyone.
to this end Means for this purpose.

Grammar & punctuation

Use after stating a goal: Goal. To this end, + action.

Nuance & implication

Very useful in proposal-style writing. It connects a recommendation to its purpose.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it for cause and effect generally; it is about purpose.

Model examples

  • Schools should help students become independent learners. To this end, they can teach planning and research skills.
  • The city wants to reduce pollution. To this end, it has expanded public transportation.
with this in mind Introduces a recommendation or conclusion based on what has just been said.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: With this in mind, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Balanced and natural. It signals that the next point responds to the previous information.

Common mistake / caution: Good for writing and speaking.

Model examples

  • With this in mind, teachers should give students gradual support rather than complete freedom immediately.
  • With this in mind, governments should invest in both roads and public transportation.
in light of this Means considering this information.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: In light of this, + sentence. Also: in light of + noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

More formal than with this in mind. Excellent before a recommendation or judgment.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it when the previous information is not important enough to justify a conclusion.

Model examples

  • In light of this, schools should not remove arts education from the curriculum.
  • In light of the evidence, the policy seems reasonable.
in view of this Means considering this fact or situation.

Grammar & punctuation

Use like in light of this: In view of this, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Formal and slightly more official than in light of this.

Common mistake / caution: Mostly for writing; may sound stiff in casual speaking.

Model examples

  • In view of this, universities should provide clearer career guidance.
  • In view of these risks, the company changed its safety procedures.
given this Means because this is true; considering this.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: Given this, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Efficient and common in academic argument. It links evidence to a conclusion.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use given this without a clear previous point.

Model examples

  • Given this, students need more than memorization to succeed.
  • Given this, governments should treat internet access as a basic service.
it follows that Introduces a conclusion that logically follows from previous reasoning.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: If/When/Because X, it follows that Y.

Nuance & implication

Very logical and academic. It tells the reader that the conclusion is not just personal opinion.

Common mistake / caution: Use only when the conclusion genuinely follows. Otherwise it sounds overconfident.

Model examples

  • If education prepares people for society, it follows that schools should teach communication skills.
  • If feedback improves learning, it follows that teachers should provide it regularly.
by implication Introduces an indirect conclusion suggested by a previous point.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning or middle: By implication, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Advanced and analytical. It means the idea is not stated directly but logically suggested.

Common mistake / caution: Best for higher-level academic writing; not common in speaking.

Model examples

  • If students need digital skills for most careers, then, by implication, schools must teach them early.
  • The study focuses only on wealthy cities; by implication, its conclusions may not apply everywhere.
for this very reason Emphasizes that the previous point is exactly why the next conclusion is true.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: For this very reason, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Stronger than for this reason. It is useful when the reason may seem to support the opposite view but actually supports your view.

Common mistake / caution: Use sparingly because it is emphatic.

Model examples

  • Some students lack support at home. For this very reason, schools should provide after-class help.
  • Technology can distract students. For this very reason, they need digital discipline training.
the result is that Introduces a consequence in a clear full-clause form.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: The result is that + complete sentence.

Nuance & implication

Good when the effect is complex and needs a full clause.

Common mistake / caution: Less elegant than consequently, but very clear.

Model examples

  • The result is that many students enter university without strong study habits.
  • The result is that public spaces become safer and more welcoming.
this gives rise to Means this creates or produces a new situation, often a problem or question.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: This gives rise to + noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

Academic and useful for problem-solution writing.

Common mistake / caution: Usually followed by a noun phrase, not a full sentence.

Model examples

  • This gives rise to serious concerns about privacy.
  • This gives rise to a difficult question: who should pay for the service?
this raises the question of Introduces an important question created by the previous point.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: This raises the question of + noun/gerund; or This raises the question: + full question.

Nuance & implication

Excellent for moving from evidence to analysis.

Common mistake / caution: Use it when the next part actually discusses or answers the question.

Model examples

  • This raises the question of whether schools should teach media literacy earlier.
  • This raises the question: how can cities grow without becoming less livable?
Evidence, Illustration, and Academic Support 11 items

These phrases are useful when students need to introduce evidence, examples, observations, or support without relying only on for example and for instance.

case in point Introduces a clear example that proves the previous statement.

Grammar & punctuation

Use as a sentence fragment or with a full sentence: Case in point: + example. Or: A case in point is + noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

More forceful than for example. It suggests that the example strongly supports the claim.

Common mistake / caution: Use only for a strong example, not a weak or random one.

Model examples

  • Many public services improve daily life. Case in point: reliable transportation helps people reach school and work.
  • A case in point is Finland, where teacher training is taken very seriously.
take, for example, Introduces a specific example in a more conversational but polished way.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: Take, for example, + noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

Useful in speaking and writing when you want to guide the reader directly to an example.

Common mistake / caution: Punctuation: commas around for example are common in this structure.

Model examples

  • Take, for example, a student who works at night and studies during the day.
  • Take, for example, public libraries, which provide free access to information.
to illustrate this point Introduces an example that explains a previous idea.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: To illustrate this point, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

More formal and purposeful than for example. It tells the reader why the example is being used.

Common mistake / caution: Good for writing; a bit formal for quick speaking.

Model examples

  • To illustrate this point, consider a student who improves after receiving detailed feedback.
  • To illustrate this point, some cities have reduced traffic by expanding bike lanes.
as evidenced by Introduces evidence that supports a claim.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: claim + as evidenced by + noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

Academic and useful in integrated writing. It means the evidence shows the claim is true.

Common mistake / caution: Follow it with evidence, not with a personal opinion.

Model examples

  • Student interest increased, as evidenced by higher attendance at workshops.
  • The program was effective, as evidenced by improved test scores.
as demonstrated by Introduces proof or a clear example.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: claim + as demonstrated by + noun phrase/clause.

Nuance & implication

Slightly stronger than as shown by. It suggests the evidence clearly proves the point.

Common mistake / caution: Best for writing, especially when discussing studies, examples, or results.

Model examples

  • Regular practice improves fluency, as demonstrated by the students’ final presentations.
  • The method can work, as demonstrated by several successful schools.
as illustrated by Introduces an example that makes an idea easier to understand.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: claim + as illustrated by + noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

Softer than as demonstrated by. It focuses more on explanation than proof.

Common mistake / caution: Good when the example clarifies, not necessarily proves.

Model examples

  • This problem affects many families, as illustrated by the rising demand for after-school programs.
  • The value of teamwork is illustrated by successful group research projects.
as reflected in Shows that a larger idea can be seen in a specific sign, result, or behavior.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: claim + as reflected in + noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

Useful for interpreting data, behavior, or social trends.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it unless the example truly reflects the larger idea.

Model examples

  • Students’ anxiety has increased, as reflected in the growing demand for counseling services.
  • Public interest in health is reflected in the popularity of fitness apps.
this is borne out by Means this is supported or confirmed by evidence.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: This is borne out by + noun phrase/evidence.

Nuance & implication

Formal and advanced. Excellent for essays, but probably too formal for TOEFL speaking.

Common mistake / caution: Spelling warning: borne, not born.

Model examples

  • This is borne out by studies showing that feedback improves student performance.
  • This is borne out by the experience of cities that invested in public transportation.
this lends support to Means this supports an argument, but does not necessarily prove it completely.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: This lends support to + noun phrase/the idea that + clause.

Nuance & implication

Cautious and academic. Very good when evidence is suggestive but not absolute.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it for absolute proof. It is intentionally moderate.

Model examples

  • This lends support to the idea that students need regular feedback.
  • The survey lends support to the argument that flexible schedules reduce stress.
this points to Means this suggests or indicates a deeper issue.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: This points to + noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

Analytical and concise. It helps students move from evidence to interpretation.

Common mistake / caution: Follow it with a noun phrase, not usually a full sentence.

Model examples

  • This points to a larger problem in the education system.
  • The increase in stress levels points to the need for better student support.
this underscores Means this emphasizes or shows the importance of something.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: This underscores + noun phrase / the importance of + noun/gerund.

Nuance & implication

High-level and very useful for conclusions and analysis.

Common mistake / caution: Do not overuse; it is emphatic.

Model examples

  • This underscores the importance of teaching students how to manage time.
  • The example underscores how much children depend on stable routines.
Clarification, Precision, and Restatement 10 items

These transitions help students define their meaning more exactly. They are especially useful when discussing abstract ideas in TOEFL writing.

that is to say Restates an idea more clearly or precisely.

Grammar & punctuation

Use between two explanations: sentence. That is to say, + clearer sentence.

Nuance & implication

More formal than in other words. It is useful when the second sentence defines the first.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it to add a new idea. It should restate the same idea.

Model examples

  • Students need autonomy. That is to say, they need some control over how they learn.
  • The policy encourages preventive care; that is to say, it focuses on stopping problems before they become serious.
put differently Restates the same idea in a different way.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: Put differently, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Concise and advanced. It is slightly more formal than to put it another way.

Common mistake / caution: Good for writing and speaking, but use it only for restatement.

Model examples

  • Put differently, grades should guide students, not define them.
  • Put differently, technology is a tool, not a complete solution.
more precisely Corrects or makes a previous statement more exact.

Grammar & punctuation

Use before the more exact statement: More precisely, + sentence/phrase.

Nuance & implication

Useful when the first version was too general. It signals careful thinking.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it if the second idea is not actually more precise.

Model examples

  • The problem is not technology itself. More precisely, the problem is uncontrolled technology use.
  • Students need support, or more precisely, they need timely and specific feedback.
strictly speaking Introduces a technically exact statement.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: Strictly speaking, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

It often corrects a common but imprecise way of speaking. Very useful for nuance.

Common mistake / caution: Can sound formal or corrective. Use carefully in speaking.

Model examples

  • Strictly speaking, online classes are not easier; they simply require different skills.
  • Strictly speaking, a high salary does not guarantee career satisfaction.
in practical terms Explains what an idea means in real life.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: In practical terms, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Excellent for turning abstract points into concrete consequences.

Common mistake / caution: Very useful for TOEFL because it shows clear explanation.

Model examples

  • In practical terms, this means students need fewer lectures and more guided practice.
  • In practical terms, the policy would give families more affordable transportation.
in effect Means in actual result or practical meaning.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning or middle: In effect, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Shows what something really means, even if it is not officially described that way.

Common mistake / caution: Do not confuse it with in fact. In effect means practical result, not simply truth.

Model examples

  • In effect, the rule punishes students who have long commutes.
  • The new system, in effect, gives students more control over their schedules.
for the purposes of this discussion Defines how a term or idea will be used in the current response.

Grammar & punctuation

Use before narrowing a definition or focus.

Nuance & implication

Useful in formal writing when a term could have many meanings.

Common mistake / caution: Very formal; use only when defining scope.

Model examples

  • For the purposes of this discussion, success means long-term learning rather than high test scores.
  • For the purposes of this discussion, technology refers mainly to digital learning tools.
in this context Clarifies that a statement applies within the specific situation being discussed.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: In this context, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Prevents overgeneralization. It helps students sound careful and academic.

Common mistake / caution: Good for writing and academic speaking.

Model examples

  • In this context, independence does not mean leaving students without support.
  • In this context, competition can motivate students rather than discourage them.
viewed this way Introduces an interpretation based on a particular perspective.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: Viewed this way, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Useful for analysis. It shows that the conclusion depends on how we frame the issue.

Common mistake / caution: Use when you have just explained a perspective.

Model examples

  • Viewed this way, homework is not extra work but guided practice.
  • Viewed this way, public parks are not luxuries but health resources.
seen in this light Means understood from this perspective.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: Seen in this light, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

More formal and elegant than viewed this way. Strong for concluding an analytical paragraph.

Common mistake / caution: Best for writing; may sound literary in casual speaking.

Model examples

  • Seen in this light, school uniforms may reduce social pressure rather than limit individuality.
  • Seen in this light, failure can become a useful part of learning.
Qualification, Limitation, and Careful Hedging 16 items

Advanced writers do not make every claim sound absolute. These transitions help students limit claims, avoid overgeneralization, and sound more mature.

to some extent Means partly, but not completely.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning, middle, or end of a sentence.

Nuance & implication

Very useful for balanced arguments. It prevents claims from sounding too extreme.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it when you mean completely.

Model examples

  • To some extent, grades motivate students to study.
  • Online learning can, to some extent, replace traditional lectures.
to a certain extent Means partly, with a moderate degree of truth.

Grammar & punctuation

Use like to some extent.

Nuance & implication

Slightly more formal than to some extent. Good for cautious agreement.

Common mistake / caution: Often followed by but/however to show limitation.

Model examples

  • To a certain extent, competition can improve performance.
  • Parents are responsible to a certain extent, but schools also play a major role.
in many respects Means in many ways or from many points of view.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning or middle of a sentence.

Nuance & implication

Useful when the claim is broadly true but not necessarily true in every way.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it for a single small similarity.

Model examples

  • In many respects, online education has made learning more democratic.
  • The new policy is, in many respects, a practical solution.
in principle Means in theory or according to the basic idea.

Grammar & punctuation

Use before contrasting with real practice.

Nuance & implication

Often implies that something sounds good theoretically but may be difficult in reality.

Common mistake / caution: Often pairs with in practice.

Model examples

  • In principle, giving students more freedom is a good idea.
  • In principle, exams should measure understanding rather than memorization.
in practice Means in real life or actual use.

Grammar & punctuation

Use when contrasting theory with reality.

Nuance & implication

Very useful for TOEFL because many questions involve practical consequences.

Common mistake / caution: Often contrasts with in principle or in theory.

Model examples

  • In practice, however, students may need more guidance than the policy assumes.
  • The rule seems simple, but in practice it is difficult to apply fairly.
at least in theory Means something should be true according to theory, though reality may differ.

Grammar & punctuation

Use after a claim or at the start of a sentence.

Nuance & implication

Signals cautious skepticism. It is excellent for nuanced academic discussion.

Common mistake / caution: Follow with real-world limitation if needed.

Model examples

  • Smaller classes should improve learning, at least in theory.
  • At least in theory, online platforms make education more accessible.
broadly speaking Introduces a general statement without claiming it is true in every case.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: Broadly speaking, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

More academic than generally. Good for overview statements.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it before a very specific detail.

Model examples

  • Broadly speaking, students learn better when they receive clear feedback.
  • Broadly speaking, cities benefit from reliable public transportation.
relatively speaking Compares something to a standard or to other cases.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning, middle, or end.

Nuance & implication

It means the statement is true only in comparison, not absolutely.

Common mistake / caution: Students should make the comparison clear.

Model examples

  • Public transportation is, relatively speaking, inexpensive.
  • Relatively speaking, the first option is easier to implement.
in a sense Means in one way of thinking, but not completely or literally.

Grammar & punctuation

Use before an interpretation.

Nuance & implication

Good for abstract ideas, but can sound vague if overused.

Common mistake / caution: Use when the statement is metaphorical or partially true.

Model examples

  • In a sense, teachers are also coaches because they guide students through difficulties.
  • In a sense, failure is feedback.
if anything Introduces a correction that says the opposite direction may be more accurate.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: not X; if anything, Y.

Nuance & implication

Advanced and concise. It often means “if we must make a stronger claim, it is this opposite one.”

Common mistake / caution: Use after rejecting or questioning a claim.

Model examples

  • Technology has not made teachers less important; if anything, it has made guidance more necessary.
  • The policy did not reduce motivation; if anything, it increased it.
at best Shows the most positive realistic interpretation.

Grammar & punctuation

Use before a limited result or evaluation.

Nuance & implication

Often has a critical tone: even in the best case, the result is limited.

Common mistake / caution: Be careful: it often sounds negative.

Model examples

  • At best, memorization helps students pass short quizzes.
  • The plan is, at best, a temporary solution.
at worst Shows the most negative possible interpretation.

Grammar & punctuation

Use before a serious risk or consequence.

Nuance & implication

Useful for evaluating risks, but can sound dramatic.

Common mistake / caution: Use only when discussing a real potential downside.

Model examples

  • At worst, excessive homework can damage students’ mental health.
  • The policy could, at worst, increase inequality.
at the very least Introduces the minimum conclusion or action that should be accepted.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning or middle of a sentence.

Nuance & implication

Persuasive and useful when arguing for a modest but necessary step.

Common mistake / caution: Good in both writing and speaking.

Model examples

  • At the very least, schools should teach students how to check online sources.
  • The policy should, at the very least, be tested in a few schools first.
this does not necessarily mean that Prevents a reader from drawing an overly strong conclusion.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: This does not necessarily mean that + complete sentence.

Nuance & implication

Very useful for nuance and avoiding logical overclaiming.

Common mistake / caution: Often follows a strong claim and limits its interpretation.

Model examples

  • This does not necessarily mean that exams should be removed completely.
  • This does not necessarily mean that online education is better for every student.
this is not to suggest that Clarifies that your claim should not be misunderstood as a stronger or different claim.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: This is not to suggest that + clause.

Nuance & implication

Formal and very useful for balanced academic writing.

Common mistake / caution: Often followed by your real position.

Model examples

  • This is not to suggest that technology can replace teachers.
  • This is not to suggest that all students dislike group work.
by no means Means absolutely not.

Grammar & punctuation

Use with inversion or before an adjective: By no means is this simple. / This is by no means simple.

Nuance & implication

Strong and formal. Excellent for emphasizing that something is not true.

Common mistake / caution: Grammar warning: if it begins the sentence, use inversion: By no means should we...

Model examples

  • This is by no means an easy problem to solve.
  • By no means should schools ignore students’ mental health.
Purpose, Means, and Method 7 items

These transitions explain why something is done or how a goal can be achieved. They are especially useful in recommendation and solution paragraphs.

with a view to Means with the aim of doing something.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: with a view to + -ing/noun.

Nuance & implication

Formal and elegant. Useful in policy or recommendation contexts.

Common mistake / caution: Grammar warning: to is a preposition here, so use -ing: with a view to reducing, not reduce.

Model examples

  • Schools can redesign assignments with a view to improving critical thinking.
  • The city expanded bike lanes with a view to reducing traffic.
in an effort to Shows the purpose behind an action.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: in an effort to + base verb.

Nuance & implication

Less formal than with a view to and very natural in academic writing.

Common mistake / caution: Good for describing attempts, even if success is uncertain.

Model examples

  • Schools introduced mentoring programs in an effort to support first-year students.
  • The city raised parking fees in an effort to reduce car use.
in an attempt to Shows that an action was done to try to achieve a goal.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: in an attempt to + base verb.

Nuance & implication

Similar to in an effort to, but it can imply uncertainty or possible failure.

Common mistake / caution: Use when the outcome is not guaranteed.

Model examples

  • The government lowered ticket prices in an attempt to increase public transportation use.
  • The teacher changed the assignment in an attempt to make it more practical.
for the sake of Means for the benefit or purpose of something.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: for the sake of + noun/gerund.

Nuance & implication

Often used for values: clarity, fairness, convenience, safety.

Common mistake / caution: Can sound moral or persuasive; use it when a value is being protected.

Model examples

  • For the sake of fairness, teachers should explain grading criteria clearly.
  • For the sake of student health, schools should avoid excessive homework.
toward this end Means in order to achieve this goal.

Grammar & punctuation

Use after stating a goal: Toward this end, + action.

Nuance & implication

Similar to to this end, slightly more formal and process-oriented.

Common mistake / caution: Best for writing; too formal for casual speaking.

Model examples

  • Universities should help students develop career skills. Toward this end, they can offer internship programs.
  • The city aims to reduce pollution. Toward this end, it has invested in electric buses.
by doing so Connects an action to its result or purpose.

Grammar & punctuation

Use after recommending an action: By doing so, + result.

Nuance & implication

Clear and useful. Less formal than thereby, but safer for students.

Common mistake / caution: Make sure doing so clearly refers to the previous action.

Model examples

  • Schools should give students more feedback. By doing so, they can improve both confidence and performance.
  • Cities can build more parks. By doing so, they make neighborhoods healthier.
through this approach Shows that a result can be achieved by using the method just described.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: Through this approach, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Useful in solution paragraphs and speaking responses.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it unless you have already described the approach.

Model examples

  • Through this approach, students can become more independent without feeling abandoned.
  • Through this approach, schools can reduce stress while maintaining academic standards.
Condition, Assumption, and Requirement 7 items

These transitions show when an idea is true, what it depends on, or what must happen first. They help students write more precise arguments.

provided that Means if, but with the sense of a necessary condition.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: provided that + subject + verb.

Nuance & implication

More formal than if. It means the main idea is true only under this condition.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it for ordinary cause and effect. It introduces a condition.

Model examples

  • Online learning can be effective, provided that students receive regular feedback.
  • Group projects can work well, provided that each student has a clear role.
so long as Means as long as.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: so long as + subject + verb.

Nuance & implication

Slightly more formal or emphatic than as long as.

Common mistake / caution: Good for writing and speaking.

Model examples

  • Students can benefit from technology so long as they use it responsibly.
  • Homework is useful so long as it has a clear purpose.
assuming that Introduces a condition that is being treated as true for the argument.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: Assuming that + clause, + main clause.

Nuance & implication

Useful when the argument depends on an assumption.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it if the condition is certain. It signals uncertainty.

Model examples

  • Assuming that the school has enough funding, smaller classes would be beneficial.
  • Assuming that students use the platform regularly, online practice can improve vocabulary.
in the event that Means if something happens, usually a possible future problem.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: in the event that + subject + verb.

Nuance & implication

Formal and often used for planning or precautions.

Common mistake / caution: Too formal for most casual speaking.

Model examples

  • In the event that students miss class, recorded lectures should be available.
  • In the event that the program fails, the school should have a backup plan.
subject to Means depending on or limited by a condition.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: subject to + noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

Formal and concise. Useful for rules, policies, and limitations.

Common mistake / caution: Follow with a noun phrase, not a full clause.

Model examples

  • The program should be expanded, subject to available funding.
  • Students may choose their topics, subject to teacher approval.
contingent on Means dependent on.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: contingent on + noun/gerund.

Nuance & implication

Advanced and formal. Good for academic policy writing.

Common mistake / caution: Not common in speaking; useful in formal writing.

Model examples

  • The success of online learning is contingent on student discipline.
  • The plan is contingent on receiving enough financial support.
failing that Means if that does not happen or if that option fails.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning of a sentence after giving a preferred option.

Nuance & implication

Useful for presenting backup plans.

Common mistake / caution: Mostly for writing and formal speech; a little uncommon but practical.

Model examples

  • Schools should provide free tutoring. Failing that, they should at least offer online practice materials.
  • The city should build more subway lines. Failing that, it should improve bus service.
Topic Shifts, Framing, and Perspective 11 items

These transitions help organize sophisticated responses. They are useful when moving from one aspect of an issue to another or when narrowing the discussion.

with regard to Introduces the topic or aspect being discussed.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: With regard to + noun phrase, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Formal and safe. Useful for shifting to a specific dimension of a topic.

Common mistake / caution: Do not follow it with a full clause directly. Use a noun phrase.

Model examples

  • With regard to cost, online learning is often more affordable.
  • With regard to student motivation, feedback is more useful than punishment.
with respect to Means regarding or concerning.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: With respect to + noun phrase, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Very similar to with regard to, often slightly more formal.

Common mistake / caution: Good for writing; somewhat formal for speaking.

Model examples

  • With respect to public health, parks provide clear benefits.
  • With respect to language learning, daily practice matters more than occasional long study sessions.
in terms of Introduces the specific category or measure you are discussing.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: in terms of + noun/gerund.

Nuance & implication

Useful but often overused. It means “when considering this aspect.”

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it as a vague filler. Name a clear category after it.

Model examples

  • In terms of cost, public transportation is usually better than driving.
  • In terms of long-term learning, feedback is more valuable than grades alone.
as regards Means regarding, in relation to.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: As regards + noun phrase, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Formal and concise, more common in British or formal writing.

Common mistake / caution: May sound too formal for TOEFL speaking.

Model examples

  • As regards student stress, schools should avoid unnecessary testing.
  • As regards transportation, the city needs a more reliable bus system.
in relation to Shows the connection between one topic and another.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: in relation to + noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

Useful when discussing how one idea connects to a larger issue.

Common mistake / caution: Avoid using it when regarding or about would be simpler.

Model examples

  • In relation to academic success, sleep is often underestimated.
  • This problem is especially important in relation to younger students.
from the standpoint of Introduces a perspective or viewpoint.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: From the standpoint of + person/group/field, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Formal and analytical. Excellent for comparing perspectives.

Common mistake / caution: Use when the viewpoint matters.

Model examples

  • From the standpoint of students, flexible deadlines can reduce stress.
  • From the standpoint of employers, communication skills are essential.
from a practical standpoint Introduces a practical rather than theoretical view.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: From a practical standpoint, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Very useful for TOEFL because it moves from abstract opinion to realistic evaluation.

Common mistake / caution: Good in both essays and speaking.

Model examples

  • From a practical standpoint, the plan may be too expensive for small schools.
  • From a practical standpoint, online resources are easier to update than printed textbooks.
against this backdrop Means in the context of the situation just described.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: Against this backdrop, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Advanced and academic. It is excellent after describing a broad social problem or trend.

Common mistake / caution: Use only after giving enough background.

Model examples

  • Against this backdrop, schools must prepare students to evaluate digital information carefully.
  • Against this backdrop, public transportation becomes more than a convenience; it becomes a necessity.
in this regard Means concerning this specific point.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning or end of a sentence.

Nuance & implication

Useful for referring back to the exact aspect being discussed.

Common mistake / caution: Make sure “this” clearly refers to a previous idea.

Model examples

  • Teachers play an important role in this regard.
  • In this regard, smaller classes can make a major difference.
in this respect Means in this particular way.

Grammar & punctuation

Use like in this regard.

Nuance & implication

Often used to compare similarities or differences in one specific aspect.

Common mistake / caution: Slightly more comparison-focused than in this regard.

Model examples

  • In this respect, online courses may be less effective than face-to-face classes.
  • Public libraries and schools are similar in this respect.
as far as X is concerned Limits the discussion to one topic, person, or group.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: As far as + noun phrase + is/are concerned, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Useful for speaking and writing, but it can sound wordy if overused.

Common mistake / caution: Do not confuse this with as far as I’m concerned, which introduces personal opinion.

Model examples

  • As far as student motivation is concerned, feedback is more helpful than punishment.
  • As far as cost is concerned, the second option is clearly better.
Synthesis, Evaluation, and Conclusion 10 items

These transitions help students end with judgment, synthesis, and balance instead of simply repeating their thesis.

ultimately Introduces the final or most important conclusion after considering the issue.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning or middle of a sentence.

Nuance & implication

More sophisticated than in the end. It often signals a deeper final judgment.

Common mistake / caution: Good for both writing and speaking.

Model examples

on balance Introduces a final judgment after weighing both sides.

Grammar & punctuation

Nuance & implication

Excellent for essays with advantages and disadvantages. It means “after considering both sides.”

Common mistake / caution: Use only after you have considered more than one side.

Model examples

all things considered Introduces an overall judgment after considering many factors.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: All things considered, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Natural and polished. Slightly less formal than in the final analysis.

Common mistake / caution: Good for TOEFL writing and speaking.

Model examples

in the final analysis Introduces the deepest or final conclusion.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: In the final analysis, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Formal and elegant. It suggests that after deeper analysis, this is what matters most.

Common mistake / caution: Use once at most. It can sound heavy if repeated.

Model examples

when all is said and done Means after everything has been considered.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: When all is said and done, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

More idiomatic and slightly conversational. Good for speaking, acceptable in less formal writing.

Common mistake / caution: Less formal than in the final analysis.

Model examples

  • When all is said and done, students need both freedom and guidance.
  • When all is said and done, health is more important than convenience.
in sum Means in summary.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: In sum, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

Concise and academic. A good alternative to in conclusion.

Common mistake / caution: Good for writing; slightly formal for speaking.

Model examples

  • In sum, the policy is practical, affordable, and fair.
  • In sum, students benefit most when they receive both challenge and support.
the upshot is that Means the final result or main conclusion is that.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: The upshot is that + complete sentence.

Nuance & implication

Clear and memorable, but less formal than in conclusion. Useful in speaking and discussion.

Common mistake / caution: Not ideal for very formal essays, but strong in speaking.

Model examples

  • The upshot is that schools need to teach both knowledge and life skills.
  • The upshot is that technology helps only when students use it intentionally.
taken as a whole Means considered as one complete thing rather than separate parts.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning or after a noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

Useful when evaluating a policy, argument, course, or system.

Common mistake / caution: Similar to taken together, but often evaluates one larger whole.

Model examples

  • Taken as a whole, the program gives students useful practical experience.
  • The evidence, taken as a whole, supports the need for reform.
collectively Means as a group or together.

Grammar & punctuation

Use before a verb or at the beginning of a sentence.

Nuance & implication

Useful when several factors work together.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it if you are discussing only one factor.

Model examples

  • Collectively, these changes can make schools more supportive.
  • These problems collectively reduce students’ motivation.
in short, then Summarizes a point while showing that it follows from previous reasoning.

Grammar & punctuation

Use at the beginning: In short, then, + sentence.

Nuance & implication

More analytical than in short because then signals logical conclusion.

Common mistake / caution: Good for written analysis; slightly formal for speaking.

Model examples

  • In short, then, the issue is not whether technology is useful, but how it is used.
  • In short, then, feedback should be regular, specific, and practical.

Pattern: This suggests that + complete sentence.

Nuance & implication

Cautious and academic. It is weaker than proves and therefore safer in evidence-based writing.

Model examples

  • This suggests that public transportation can influence both health and the economy.
this indicates that Shows that evidence points toward a conclusion.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: This indicates that + complete sentence.

Nuance & implication

Slightly stronger and more data-oriented than suggests.

Common mistake / caution: Good for data, trends, and observations.

Model examples

  • The increase in attendance indicates that the new schedule is more convenient.
this highlights Shows that an example makes an issue more visible or important.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: This highlights + noun phrase / the fact that + clause.

Nuance & implication

Useful when an example reveals importance rather than proving a claim.

Common mistake / caution: Do not use it for simple cause and effect.

Model examples

  • This highlights the need for better digital literacy education.
  • This highlights the fact that motivation depends on more than grades.
this reinforces Shows that evidence strengthens a previous claim.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: This reinforces + noun phrase / the idea that + clause.

Nuance & implication

Good for adding evidence after a claim has already been made.

Common mistake / caution: Use when the evidence supports something already stated.

Model examples

  • This reinforces the idea that students need regular practice.
  • The professor’s example reinforces the importance of clear communication.
this calls into question Shows that evidence creates doubt about an idea.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: This calls into question + noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

Advanced and useful for critique. It means the idea may not be reliable.

Common mistake / caution: It is critical in tone; use when challenging a claim.

Model examples

  • This calls into question the belief that online learning is always more convenient.
  • The weak results call into question the effectiveness of the new policy.
this casts doubt on Means this makes something seem less certain or believable.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: This casts doubt on + noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

Similar to calls into question, but often slightly softer.

Common mistake / caution: Subject-verb warning: data can be treated as plural in formal academic English: data cast doubt.

Model examples

  • This casts doubt on the claim that exams are the best measure of learning.
  • The inconsistent data cast doubt on the researchers’ conclusion.
this speaks to Means this relates to or reveals something important about a broader issue.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: This speaks to + noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

Modern academic phrase. Useful in discussion, but it can sound trendy if overused.

Common mistake / caution: Use sparingly; not as formal as indicates or suggests.

Model examples

  • This speaks to a larger need for emotional support in schools.
  • The example speaks to the importance of community in education.
this is consistent with Shows that evidence agrees with a theory, claim, or pattern.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: This is consistent with + noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

Very useful in integrated writing and research-based explanations.

Common mistake / caution: It does not mean proves. It means the evidence fits the claim.

Model examples

  • This is consistent with the idea that feedback improves motivation.
  • The results are consistent with earlier research on sleep and memory.
this is incompatible with Shows that evidence does not fit a claim or explanation.

Grammar & punctuation

Pattern: This is incompatible with + noun phrase.

Nuance & implication

Formal and precise. Good for refuting claims.

Common mistake / caution: Strong phrase. Use only when the conflict is clear.

Model examples

  • This is incompatible with the claim that students prefer only online classes.
  • The evidence is incompatible with the idea that the policy was successful.

Common Advanced Transition Confusions

Study these carefully. Many advanced transitions are not interchangeable, even when dictionaries translate them similarly.

by contrast vs in contrast vs conversely

By contrast and in contrast introduce a clear difference between two cases. Conversely is narrower: it should be used when the second relationship is the reverse of the first. If one group benefits and another group is harmed, conversely may work. If the ideas are merely different, use by contrast or in contrast.

  • City students often have access to many libraries. By contrast, rural students may have fewer public resources.
  • A clear schedule can reduce stress. Conversely, a chaotic schedule can increase it.
on the contrary vs on the other hand

On the contrary corrects a false idea and says the opposite is true. On the other hand introduces a different side of an issue. Students often misuse on the contrary when they simply mean however or on the other hand.

  • Technology has not made teachers unnecessary. On the contrary, it has made their guidance more important.
  • Online learning is flexible. On the other hand, face-to-face learning can create stronger relationships.
admittedly vs granted vs to be sure

All three concede a point. Admittedly is polished and common in essays. Granted is more conversational and direct. To be sure is the most formal and often appears before a more nuanced counterargument.

  • Admittedly, the plan is expensive, but it may save money over time.
  • Granted, exams are stressful, but they can provide useful information.
  • To be sure, technology has risks; however, schools can teach students to manage them.
thereby vs by doing so vs in turn

Thereby is concise and formal and is usually followed by an -ing phrase. By doing so is clearer and safer for most students. In turn shows a chain: one result produces another result.

  • Schools can provide feedback, thereby improving student confidence.
  • Schools can provide feedback. By doing so, they help students improve.
  • Feedback improves confidence, which in turn increases participation.
as such vs therefore

As such is often misused. It does not simply mean therefore. It means because something belongs to a certain category or has a certain role. Use therefore for ordinary logical conclusions; use as such only when a noun identity has been established.

  • Libraries are public learning spaces. As such, they should be accessible to everyone.
  • The program is affordable and effective. Therefore, schools should adopt it.
in light of this vs with this in mind vs given this

All three connect previous information to a conclusion. In light of this is more formal. With this in mind is slightly more practical and recommendation-oriented. Given this is concise and logical.

  • In light of this, universities should expand counseling services.
  • With this in mind, teachers should introduce deadlines gradually.
  • Given this, public transportation should be treated as essential infrastructure.
as evidenced by vs as illustrated by vs as demonstrated by

As evidenced by introduces support. As illustrated by introduces an example that clarifies. As demonstrated by is stronger and suggests clearer proof. Students should not use demonstrated by when the example is weak.

  • The course was popular, as evidenced by high enrollment.
  • The problem is common, as illustrated by the experience of many first-year students.
  • The method is effective, as demonstrated by improved performance after training.
to some extent vs in many respects vs in principle

To some extent limits the degree of truth. In many respects means in many ways. In principle means according to the basic idea or theory, often before showing that reality is different.

  • To some extent, homework helps students review.
  • In many respects, online learning is more flexible than traditional learning.
  • In principle, student choice is beneficial; in practice, some students still need structure.
ultimately vs on balance vs in the final analysis

Ultimately gives a final judgment. On balance means after weighing both sides. In the final analysis is more formal and suggests a deeper final evaluation. On balance is especially useful for agree/disagree or advantages/disadvantages tasks.

  • Ultimately, education should prepare students for real life.
  • On balance, the benefits outweigh the drawbacks.
  • In the final analysis, technology matters only if it improves learning.
this suggests vs this indicates vs this proves

This suggests is cautious and safe. This indicates is somewhat stronger and often works with data or observable signs. This proves is very strong and should be used only when the evidence is decisive. In TOEFL, suggests and indicates are usually safer than proves.

  • This suggests that students need more feedback.
  • This indicates that the new schedule is more convenient.
  • This proves that the calculation was incorrect.

How students should study this page

Students should first learn the function of each transition, then study the grammar pattern, then memorize one or two natural example sentences. The most common TOEFL problem is not knowing the word; it is using a transition with the wrong grammar, tone, or logical relationship.

Adding Information 9 items

Use these to add another idea, reason, detail, or explanation.

additionally Best for writing and formal speaking.

Meaning

Introduces one more point in a clear, academic way.

How to use it

Put it at the beginning of a sentence or clause, usually followed by a comma.

Nuance

More formal than also. Very useful in TOEFL essays, but it can sound stiff in casual speaking.

Examples

  • Additionally, online courses allow students to review lessons at their own pace.
  • The plan is affordable. Additionally, it is easy to implement.
also Good for both writing and speaking.

Meaning

Adds another similar idea.

How to use it

Use before the main verb, after be-verbs, or at the beginning of a sentence in less formal writing.

Nuance

Simple, natural, and safe. In academic writing, do not overuse it at the beginning of every sentence.

Examples

  • The program is cheap and also easy to use.
  • Also, students can access the material from home.
in addition Excellent for writing; natural in formal speaking.

Meaning

Adds another point, often after a complete previous point.

How to use it

Usually begins a new sentence: In addition, + sentence.

Nuance

More formal and cleaner than also. It is one of the safest transitions for TOEFL writing.

Examples

  • In addition, public transportation reduces traffic.
  • The museum is educational. In addition, it is free for students.
furthermore Mostly for writing and formal presentations.

Meaning

Adds a stronger or more important supporting point.

How to use it

Use at the beginning of a sentence, followed by a comma.

Nuance

More formal than in addition. It often suggests that the next point strengthens the argument.

Examples

  • Furthermore, strict deadlines teach students responsibility.
  • The policy saves money. Furthermore, it improves public health.
moreover Better for writing than casual speaking.

Meaning

Adds an extra point that reinforces the previous argument.

How to use it

Use at the beginning of a sentence, followed by a comma.

Nuance

Very similar to furthermore, but often feels slightly more formal and argumentative.

Examples

  • Moreover, smaller classes allow teachers to give better feedback.
  • The device is inexpensive. Moreover, it requires little maintenance.
besides that Good for speaking; use in writing only when the style is not very formal.

Meaning

Adds another point, often in a conversational way.

How to use it

Commonly begins a sentence in speaking: Besides that, + sentence.

Nuance

Less academic than in addition. It is useful in speaking but can sound informal in an essay.

Examples

  • Besides that, living on campus helps students make friends.
  • The job is interesting. Besides that, the salary is reasonable.
not only...but also Excellent for writing and speaking, but students must keep the grammar parallel.

Meaning

Connects two related points and emphasizes that both are true.

How to use it

Use parallel grammar after both parts: not only + phrase/clause, but also + matching phrase/clause.

Nuance

A strong structure for showing two benefits or two effects. Be careful with word order when it begins a sentence.

Examples

  • Exercise not only improves physical health but also reduces stress.
  • Not only does the program save money, but it also helps the environment.
in other words Very useful in speaking and writing when explaining complex ideas.

Meaning

Restates an idea in a clearer or simpler way.

How to use it

Use after a sentence that may need clarification.

Nuance

This is not a true addition transition; it is a clarification transition. The photo places it under adding information, but its main function is restating.

Examples

  • The city became more walkable. In other words, people could reach many places without driving.
  • He lacked motivation; in other words, he did not really want to continue.
another point is that Very useful for TOEFL speaking; acceptable in writing.

Meaning

Introduces an additional reason, detail, or argument.

How to use it

Use before a full clause: Another point is that + sentence.

Nuance

Clear and student-friendly. In formal essays, vary it with in addition or furthermore.

Examples

  • Another point is that public libraries provide quiet study spaces.
  • Another point is that children learn social skills at school.
Giving Examples 7 items

Use these to introduce examples, stories, evidence, or personal experiences.

for example Excellent for both writing and speaking.

Meaning

Introduces one example that supports or explains an idea.

How to use it

Use at the beginning, middle, or end of a sentence. At the beginning, use a comma.

Nuance

The safest and most common example transition. Good in both academic and personal contexts.

Examples

  • Many students benefit from technology. For example, they can watch recorded lectures.
  • Some animals, for example dolphins, communicate in complex ways.
for instance Excellent for both writing and speaking.

Meaning

Introduces a specific example.

How to use it

Works almost the same as for example.

Nuance

Slightly more formal or polished than for example, but the difference is small.

Examples

  • Some cities encourage cycling. For instance, they build protected bike lanes.
  • Students can reduce stress in simple ways, for instance by sleeping enough.
a good example of this is Natural in speaking and writing; use singular example with is.

Meaning

Introduces a strong example that clearly shows the point.

How to use it

Use before a noun, situation, or clause.

Nuance

The photo says “a good examples of this is,” but the correct form is “a good example of this is.”

Examples

  • A good example of this is online banking, which saves people time.
  • A good example of this is my high school, where students had to complete group projects.
I remember when Best for speaking and personal writing.

Meaning

Introduces a personal memory as an example.

How to use it

Use before a full clause about a past event.

Nuance

Very useful in TOEFL speaking, especially independent speaking. It is usually too personal and informal for academic essays unless the task invites personal examples.

Examples

  • I remember when my teacher let us choose our own project, and everyone became more motivated.
  • I remember when I first used an online dictionary; it made reading much easier.
this reminds me of Best for speaking.

Meaning

Connects the topic to a memory, example, or similar situation.

How to use it

Use before a noun, person, event, or clause.

Nuance

Conversational. It helps speakers move naturally into an example, but it is not formal enough for most academic essays.

Examples

  • This reminds me of a class project I did last year.
  • This reminds me of my uncle, who learned English by watching movies.
I once heard about Good for speaking; informal for essays.

Meaning

Introduces an example or story that the speaker learned from another source.

How to use it

Use before a noun phrase or clause.

Nuance

Useful when students do not have a personal example. It sounds conversational, so avoid it in formal academic writing.

Examples

  • I once heard about a school that replaced grades with written feedback.
  • I once heard about a city that reduced pollution by limiting cars downtown.
one example is Good for speaking and acceptable in writing.

Meaning

Introduces one supporting example.

How to use it

Use before a noun phrase or a clause.

Nuance

Simple and clear. It is less formal than for instance but very useful for lower-level students.

Examples

  • One example is public transportation.
  • One example is that many students can now attend classes online.
Giving Opinions 7 items

Use these to express a personal view, position, or judgment.

personally Very useful in TOEFL speaking.

Meaning

Shows that the statement is your own opinion or experience.

How to use it

Often used at the beginning of a sentence: Personally, + sentence.

Nuance

Natural in speaking. In formal academic writing, use it only when the task asks for your opinion.

Examples

  • Personally, I prefer studying alone because I can focus better.
  • Personally, I think cities should invest more in public parks.
I believe Good for writing and speaking.

Meaning

Introduces your opinion in a clear and direct way.

How to use it

Use before a full clause: I believe + sentence.

Nuance

A little more formal than I think. Do not repeat it too often in one essay.

Examples

  • I believe universities should offer more practical courses.
  • I believe that children learn better when they feel safe.
in my opinion Good for TOEFL; avoid overusing it.

Meaning

Signals that the statement is your personal view.

How to use it

Usually begins a sentence, followed by a comma.

Nuance

Very common and safe. However, advanced writing often sounds better if the opinion is clear without repeating this phrase.

Examples

  • In my opinion, students should have some freedom to choose their courses.
  • In my opinion, the benefits outweigh the disadvantages.
as far as I’m concerned More natural in speaking than formal writing.

Meaning

Introduces a strong personal opinion or judgment.

How to use it

Use at the beginning of a sentence, followed by a comma.

Nuance

Stronger and more personal than in my opinion. It can sound slightly firm or dismissive, so use it carefully in academic writing.

Examples

  • As far as I’m concerned, students should not be forced to choose a major too early.
  • As far as I’m concerned, the second option is much more practical.
I tend to think that Excellent for nuanced TOEFL speaking and writing.

Meaning

Introduces an opinion in a softer, less absolute way.

How to use it

Use before a full clause.

Nuance

Good when you want to sound balanced or not too extreme. It suggests a preference rather than complete certainty.

Examples

  • I tend to think that homework is useful when it is not excessive.
  • I tend to think that online learning works best for older students.
I strongly feel that Good for speaking; use carefully in formal essays.

Meaning

Introduces a strong personal opinion, often with emotion or conviction.

How to use it

Use before a full clause.

Nuance

Stronger and more emotional than I believe. In academic writing, I strongly believe that is often more polished.

Examples

  • I strongly feel that schools should teach financial literacy.
  • I strongly feel that parents should limit young children’s screen time.
personally speaking Best for speaking.

Meaning

Introduces an opinion based on your personal view or experience.

How to use it

Usually begins a sentence, followed by a comma.

Nuance

Slightly more conversational than personally. It can be useful in speaking but may sound unnecessary in essays.

Examples

  • Personally speaking, I learn better when I write notes by hand.
  • Personally speaking, I would choose the smaller university.
Emphasizing Points 10 items

Use these to make a point stronger, clearer, or more important.

as a matter of fact Useful in speaking; acceptable in writing but not overly academic.

Meaning

Introduces a fact or detail that strengthens, corrects, or surprises.

How to use it

Use at the beginning of a sentence or before a clause.

Nuance

Often used when the next point is stronger than expected or corrects an assumption.

Examples

  • As a matter of fact, many successful entrepreneurs failed several times before succeeding.
  • The solution is not expensive. As a matter of fact, it may save money.
especially Good for both writing and speaking.

Meaning

Highlights one case, group, or reason as particularly important.

How to use it

Use before the item being emphasized, or after a clause.

Nuance

Do not use especially as a full sentence connector by itself in formal writing.

Examples

  • Exercise is important, especially for people who sit all day.
  • This problem affects many students, especially those who work part-time.
indeed Excellent for formal writing.

Meaning

Strengthens or confirms the previous point.

How to use it

Use at the beginning of a sentence or before an adjective/adverb.

Nuance

Formal and academic. In speaking, it may sound a little polished or dramatic.

Examples

  • Indeed, the data suggest that early education has long-term benefits.
  • The task was difficult indeed.
in fact Very useful in writing and speaking.

Meaning

Adds a true detail that strengthens, explains, or corrects the previous idea.

How to use it

Use at the beginning of a sentence, in the middle, or before a correction.

Nuance

More common and natural than indeed. It often means the next statement is more exact or stronger.

Examples

  • Many people think libraries are less important today. In fact, they provide essential internet access.
  • The program did not fail; in fact, it became more popular.
most importantly Good for both writing and speaking.

Meaning

Introduces the most important point in a list or argument.

How to use it

Use at the beginning of a sentence, followed by a comma.

Nuance

Use only when the point really is the most important. It is stronger than importantly.

Examples

  • Most importantly, the policy protects students’ mental health.
  • The course is cheap and flexible. Most importantly, it teaches practical skills.
obviously Natural in speaking; use carefully in writing.

Meaning

Shows that the point seems clear or easy to understand.

How to use it

Use carefully at the beginning or inside a sentence.

Nuance

Can sound too strong or even rude because it suggests the idea should be obvious to everyone. In academic writing, clearly is often safer.

Examples

  • Obviously, students need enough sleep to perform well.
  • This option is obviously cheaper, but it may not be better.
actually Good for speaking; acceptable in writing when used precisely.

Meaning

Introduces a real fact, correction, or surprising contrast.

How to use it

Use before the corrected or surprising information.

Nuance

Very common in speaking. In essays, use it when you are correcting a false assumption.

Examples

  • Many people think online classes are easier, but they can actually require more self-discipline.
  • Actually, the second plan is more realistic.
what I mean is Best for speaking.

Meaning

Clarifies your previous statement.

How to use it

Use before a clearer explanation of what you intended to say.

Nuance

This is a clarification phrase, not exactly an emphasis phrase. It is natural in speaking but too conversational for formal essays.

Examples

  • What I mean is, students need feedback, not just grades.
  • The city should be more livable. What I mean is that it should be safer and easier to walk around.
still Good for speaking and informal-to-neutral writing.

Meaning

Shows that something remains true despite another fact.

How to use it

Use before the main verb, after be-verbs, or at the beginning of a sentence as a contrast marker.

Nuance

When used as a transition, still is close to nevertheless, but it is less formal and more conversational.

Examples

  • The system is expensive. Still, it may be worth the investment.
  • Even after the warning, many people still ignored the rule.
to put it another way Good for writing and speaking.

Meaning

Restates the same idea in different words.

How to use it

Use before a clearer or simpler explanation.

Nuance

This is a restatement transition. It helps students explain difficult ideas without sounding repetitive.

Examples

  • The policy rewards long-term thinking. To put it another way, it encourages people to plan for the future.
  • The skill is transferable; to put it another way, it can be used in many jobs.
Showing Cause and Effect 7 items

Use these to show reasons, results, consequences, or logical conclusions.

as a result Excellent for TOEFL writing and speaking.

Meaning

Shows that one situation happened because of another.

How to use it

Use after explaining the cause: Cause. As a result, effect.

Nuance

Neutral and very useful. It focuses on the result, not the reasoning process.

Examples

  • The city improved its bus system. As a result, fewer people drove to work.
  • Students received regular feedback; as a result, their writing improved.
consequently Mostly for writing.

Meaning

Shows a result or consequence.

How to use it

Use at the beginning of a sentence, followed by a comma.

Nuance

More formal than as a result. It is useful in essays but may sound stiff in casual speaking.

Examples

  • The company ignored customer complaints. Consequently, many people stopped buying its products.
  • The roads were icy; consequently, several schools opened late.
hence Formal writing only; avoid in casual speaking.

Meaning

Means therefore or as a result.

How to use it

Use before a conclusion or result, often in formal writing.

Nuance

Very formal and sometimes old-fashioned. It can sound unnatural in TOEFL speaking.

Examples

  • The evidence is limited; hence, the conclusion should be cautious.
  • The demand increased, hence the rise in prices.
therefore Excellent for academic writing.

Meaning

Introduces a logical conclusion based on previous information.

How to use it

Use after a reason or evidence: Reason. Therefore, conclusion.

Nuance

More logical and argumentative than as a result. Very useful for thesis-related sentences and conclusions.

Examples

  • The program improves health and saves money. Therefore, the city should expand it.
  • Many students work part-time; therefore, flexible deadlines can be helpful.
thus Good for formal writing; use less in casual speaking.

Meaning

Introduces a result, conclusion, or summary.

How to use it

Use at the beginning of a sentence or before a verb phrase.

Nuance

Formal and concise. Similar to therefore, but often more academic.

Examples

  • The experiment was poorly designed. Thus, its results are unreliable.
  • The policy reduces waste, thus helping the environment.
that’s why Best for speaking.

Meaning

Introduces the reason for a result or explains a conclusion in a conversational way.

How to use it

Use after explaining the cause or situation.

Nuance

Very natural in speaking. Too informal for formal academic essays; use therefore or for this reason instead.

Examples

  • Public transportation is cheaper and cleaner. That’s why I think cities should invest in it.
  • He practiced every day. That’s why he improved so quickly.
for this reason Excellent for writing and formal speaking.

Meaning

Introduces a conclusion or action based on the previous reason.

How to use it

Use after explaining the reason: For this reason, + conclusion.

Nuance

Clear, formal, and very safe for TOEFL writing.

Examples

  • Many students cannot afford private tutors. For this reason, schools should offer free academic support.
  • The method is simple and inexpensive. For this reason, it is widely used.
Contrasting Ideas 12 items

Use these to show difference, opposition, limitation, or unexpected contrast.

although Excellent for writing and speaking.

Meaning

Introduces a contrast or concession.

How to use it

Use before a dependent clause: Although + subject + verb, main clause.

Nuance

More formal than though. It cannot stand alone as a sentence connector between two complete sentences without correct punctuation.

Examples

  • Although online learning is convenient, it requires strong self-discipline.
  • The plan is expensive, although it may save money in the long run.
at the same time Good for both writing and speaking.

Meaning

Shows that a contrasting idea is also true.

How to use it

Use at the beginning of a sentence or clause.

Nuance

This phrase can mean literally simultaneously, or it can mean however/also true. Context decides.

Examples

  • The internet gives students more information. At the same time, it can distract them.
  • She was studying at the same time as she was working part-time.
but Good for speaking and writing; vary it in essays.

Meaning

Connects two contrasting ideas.

How to use it

Use between two words, phrases, or clauses.

Nuance

Simple and powerful. In formal writing, do not begin too many sentences with but, though it is not grammatically wrong.

Examples

  • The course is difficult, but it is useful.
  • I agree with the idea, but I think there are some risks.
even so Good for both speaking and writing.

Meaning

Means despite that; introduces an unexpected contrast.

How to use it

Use after a sentence that gives a reason to expect a different result.

Nuance

Less formal than nevertheless but more polished than still.

Examples

  • The program is expensive. Even so, it is worth supporting.
  • The weather was terrible. Even so, many people attended the event.
even though Excellent for writing and speaking.

Meaning

Introduces a strong contrast or surprising condition.

How to use it

Use before a dependent clause: Even though + subject + verb.

Nuance

Stronger than although because it often suggests the contrast is surprising.

Examples

  • Even though the class was challenging, most students enjoyed it.
  • I chose the university even though it was far from home.
however Excellent for academic writing.

Meaning

Introduces a contrasting sentence or clause.

How to use it

Usually starts a new sentence: However, + sentence. It can also appear mid-sentence with commas.

Nuance

More formal than but. Do not use it like but between two independent clauses without proper punctuation.

Examples

  • Online classes are convenient. However, they can make students feel isolated.
  • The policy, however, has several disadvantages.
nevertheless Mostly for writing and formal speaking.

Meaning

Means despite what was just said; introduces an unexpected continuation.

How to use it

Use after a sentence that gives an obstacle or opposing fact.

Nuance

Formal and slightly stronger than however. It emphasizes that the previous point does not stop the next point from being true.

Examples

  • The research has some limitations. Nevertheless, its findings are valuable.
  • The cost is high; nevertheless, the investment may be justified.
nonetheless Mostly for writing and formal speaking.

Meaning

Means despite that; still.

How to use it

Use much like nevertheless.

Nuance

Very close to nevertheless. Nonetheless can feel slightly softer or more modern, while nevertheless can feel slightly more formal and forceful.

Examples

  • The evidence is incomplete. Nonetheless, it points to an important trend.
  • The solution is not perfect; nonetheless, it is a practical first step.
on the other hand Very useful in TOEFL opinion tasks.

Meaning

Introduces the opposite side, a different view, or a contrasting point.

How to use it

Use when comparing two sides or alternatives.

Nuance

Best when there is a clear first side, often introduced with on the one hand. Avoid using it for every simple contrast.

Examples

  • Online learning is flexible. On the other hand, face-to-face classes may encourage more discussion.
  • On the one hand, the city needs more housing. On the other hand, it must protect green spaces.
while Excellent for writing and speaking.

Meaning

Shows contrast or time.

How to use it

Use before a clause. For contrast: While + idea A, idea B.

Nuance

Can mean although, whereas, or during the time that. Make sure the context is clear.

Examples

  • While technology saves time, it can also reduce face-to-face interaction.
  • She listened to music while she studied.
yet Good for writing; natural in speaking when used carefully.

Meaning

Means but; introduces a surprising contrast.

How to use it

Use between clauses or at the beginning of a sentence in formal/literary style.

Nuance

Stronger or more elegant than but. Also has a separate time meaning: not yet.

Examples

  • The idea is simple, yet it is difficult to apply.
  • The evidence is limited. Yet the conclusion is widely accepted.
whereas Excellent for academic writing.

Meaning

Contrasts two facts directly.

How to use it

Use between two parallel clauses.

Nuance

More formal and precise than while. It is ideal for comparing two groups, systems, or facts.

Examples

  • Some students prefer group work, whereas others learn better alone.
  • Urban areas often have more job opportunities, whereas rural areas may offer a quieter lifestyle.
Ordering Information 11 items

Use these to organize events, stages, reasons, or steps.

afterward Good for writing and speaking.

Meaning

Means after that time or event.

How to use it

Use after describing a previous action or event.

Nuance

Common in American English. Afterwards is also common, especially in British English.

Examples

  • The students discussed the problem. Afterward, they wrote a short reflection.
  • I attended the lecture and met the professor afterward.
and then Best for speaking and simple process descriptions.

Meaning

Shows the next step or event in a sequence.

How to use it

Use between actions, especially in stories or spoken explanations.

Nuance

Natural in speaking but can sound too simple or informal if overused in academic writing.

Examples

  • First, students read the article, and then they answer questions.
  • I saved my work, and then I submitted the file.
finally Good for both writing and speaking.

Meaning

Introduces the last point, last step, or final result.

How to use it

Use in ordering, conclusions, or narratives.

Nuance

Can mean last in a list or after a long wait. In essays, it is useful for the final body point but should not replace a full conclusion.

Examples

  • Finally, schools should teach students how to manage stress.
  • After months of practice, she finally passed the exam.
first (second...) Excellent for TOEFL structure.

Meaning

Introduces ordered points or steps.

How to use it

Use first, second, third, finally to organize a list.

Nuance

Firstly and secondly are also possible, but first and second sound cleaner and more modern.

Examples

  • First, the policy is affordable. Second, it is easy to apply.
  • First, mix the materials. Second, heat the solution.
in the past Good for writing and speaking.

Meaning

Refers to an earlier time period.

How to use it

Use when contrasting earlier conditions with present conditions.

Nuance

A time marker rather than a logical transition. Often pairs well with today, now, nowadays, or currently.

Examples

  • In the past, students relied mainly on printed books.
  • In the past, people had fewer opportunities to work remotely.
later Good for both writing and speaking.

Meaning

Means at a time after the previous event.

How to use it

Use in narratives, processes, or historical development.

Nuance

Less formal than subsequently, but very natural.

Examples

  • Later, researchers discovered a more efficient method.
  • I took a short break and later continued studying.
now Very useful in speaking; acceptable in writing.

Meaning

Refers to the present moment or present period.

How to use it

Use to shift from past to present or from background to current point.

Nuance

In essays, nowadays or currently may sound more academic when referring to modern society.

Examples

  • Now, many students use digital textbooks.
  • In the past, few people worked from home. Now, it is common.
soon Good for speaking and narrative writing.

Meaning

Means after a short time.

How to use it

Use in time sequences and predictions.

Nuance

Vague time marker. For academic writing, specify the time if precision matters.

Examples

  • Soon, the new policy began to show results.
  • Students who practice regularly soon become more confident.
subsequently Mostly for writing and formal speaking.

Meaning

Means afterward or later.

How to use it

Use to describe later events in a formal sequence.

Nuance

Formal and academic. It is excellent for history, science, and process descriptions but may sound stiff in casual speaking.

Examples

  • The law was passed in 2010. Subsequently, pollution levels began to fall.
  • The sample was heated and subsequently tested for impurities.
another reason is Very useful for TOEFL speaking and writing.

Meaning

Introduces the next reason in an argument.

How to use it

Use before a noun phrase or full clause.

Nuance

Clear and easy. In stronger writing, vary it with in addition, furthermore, or moreover.

Examples

  • Another reason is that public spaces improve community life.
  • Another reason is the high cost of private transportation.
at the same time Good for writing and speaking.

Meaning

Shows simultaneous timing or introduces a parallel/contrasting point.

How to use it

Use with either literal time or logical balance.

Nuance

This phrase appears in both ordering and contrast categories. Students must decide whether it means simultaneously or however/also true.

Examples

  • The students worked on the project at the same time.
  • The plan is ambitious. At the same time, it is realistic.
Concluding Stance 8 items

Use these to summarize, conclude, or state your final position.

all in all Good for speaking; acceptable in writing.

Meaning

Introduces an overall conclusion after considering the main points.

How to use it

Use at the beginning of a final sentence or paragraph.

Nuance

Natural and slightly informal. Good for speaking and acceptable in TOEFL essays, though overall or in conclusion may sound more academic.

Examples

  • All in all, online learning is useful when students receive enough support.
  • All in all, I think the benefits are greater than the drawbacks.
finally Good for writing and speaking.

Meaning

Introduces the last point or final stage.

How to use it

Use for the final item in a list, not always for the final conclusion.

Nuance

Students often overuse finally in conclusions. In conclusion is clearer when ending an essay.

Examples

  • Finally, governments should encourage people to recycle.
  • Finally, after considering both sides, I support the second option.
in conclusion Excellent for TOEFL writing.

Meaning

Signals the final paragraph or final statement.

How to use it

Use at the beginning of the conclusion paragraph, followed by a comma.

Nuance

Clear and academic, but very common. It is safe for TOEFL, though advanced writers may vary it.

Examples

  • In conclusion, schools should focus on both knowledge and practical skills.
  • In conclusion, the advantages of the plan outweigh its disadvantages.
in short Good for writing and speaking.

Meaning

Summarizes the main idea briefly.

How to use it

Use before a short summary or simplified conclusion.

Nuance

Slightly less formal than in conclusion. It works well when you want to reduce a complex idea to its main point.

Examples

  • In short, the policy is simple, affordable, and effective.
  • In short, technology should support teachers, not replace them.
in the end Good for speaking; acceptable in writing.

Meaning

Means ultimately or after everything is considered.

How to use it

Use for a final result, decision, or judgment.

Nuance

Can be used for stories or conclusions. It is less formal than ultimately.

Examples

  • In the end, students learn more when they are actively involved.
  • The project was difficult, but in the end it was successful.
on the whole Good for writing and formal speaking.

Meaning

Means overall, considering everything.

How to use it

Use before a balanced general judgment.

Nuance

Useful when there are both positive and negative points but your final judgment is general.

Examples

  • On the whole, the new schedule benefits most students.
  • On the whole, I believe group projects are valuable.
to conclude Good for writing and formal speaking.

Meaning

Introduces the final statement or conclusion.

How to use it

Use at the beginning of the final paragraph or final sentence.

Nuance

Slightly more formal and direct than to sum up. It clearly signals closure.

Examples

  • To conclude, universities should prepare students for both careers and civic life.
  • To conclude, I would choose the first option.
to sum up Good for both writing and speaking.

Meaning

Introduces a summary of the main points.

How to use it

Use before restating the main idea briefly.

Nuance

Less formal than in conclusion, but very natural. Good for TOEFL speaking and acceptable in writing.

Examples

  • To sum up, public transportation is cheaper, cleaner, and more practical.
  • To sum up, I think students should have more control over their schedules.

Important Comparisons

These pairs and groups are often confused. Study the difference in force, formality, and grammar.

also vs in addition vs additionally

Also is the simplest and most conversational. In addition and additionally are more academic. In TOEFL writing, use in addition or additionally when beginning a new supporting sentence; use also inside a sentence when you simply add one more detail.

  • The course is affordable. In addition, it is flexible.
  • The course is affordable and also flexible.
furthermore vs moreover

Both add a stronger supporting point. Furthermore often feels like the next point develops the argument. Moreover often feels like the next point is extra evidence that makes the argument harder to deny. In TOEFL, they are usually interchangeable, but both are formal.

  • Furthermore, the policy would help working students.
  • Moreover, it would cost very little to introduce.
for example vs for instance

These are almost identical. For example is more common and neutral. For instance sounds slightly more polished. Students can use either, but they should not use both together.

  • For example, students can use online libraries.
  • For instance, many universities now provide free digital journals.
I think vs I believe vs I tend to think

I think is simple and conversational. I believe sounds a bit more formal and confident. I tend to think is softer and more cautious, which is useful when discussing complex issues.

  • I think group work is useful.
  • I believe group work teaches communication.
  • I tend to think group work is useful when the task is well designed.
in fact vs actually vs indeed

In fact often strengthens or corrects a point. Actually often corrects an assumption and is more conversational. Indeed confirms a point in a formal academic tone.

  • The program did not fail. In fact, it became more popular.
  • Actually, the cheaper option may be better.
  • Indeed, early education has measurable long-term benefits.
therefore vs as a result vs consequently

Therefore emphasizes logic and conclusion. As a result emphasizes an effect caused by a previous situation. Consequently is formal and focuses on consequence.

  • The plan is affordable; therefore, the school should adopt it.
  • The school adopted the plan. As a result, attendance improved.
  • The company ignored safety rules; consequently, accidents increased.
however vs nevertheless vs nonetheless vs even so

However shows general contrast. Nevertheless and nonetheless mean despite that and are stronger when the second idea remains true despite a problem. Even so is a little more conversational. Nevertheless is often slightly more formal and forceful than nonetheless.

  • The plan is useful. However, it is expensive.
  • The plan is expensive. Nevertheless, it is worth considering.
  • The evidence is limited. Nonetheless, it is important.
  • The weather was bad. Even so, many people came.
although vs even though vs whereas vs while

Although introduces a concession. Even though is stronger and more surprising. Whereas directly compares two different facts. While can mean although, whereas, or during the time that, so context matters.

  • Although the class was difficult, it was useful.
  • Even though the class was difficult, most students enjoyed it.
  • Some students prefer tests, whereas others prefer projects.
  • While technology is useful, it can be distracting.
finally vs in conclusion vs to sum up

Finally is mainly for the last point in a list. In conclusion clearly signals the final paragraph of an essay. To sum up introduces a brief summary and sounds natural in speaking.

  • Finally, the plan would help teachers save time.
  • In conclusion, schools should adopt the plan.
  • To sum up, the benefits are greater than the drawbacks.

Effective homework can not only improve academic knowledge but also build self-discipline. A good example of this is a short reading assignment before a history class. If students read a few pages before the lesson, they can understand the lecture more easily and participate in discussion with more confidence. This kind of homework clearly supports learning.

While homework can be beneficial, too much homework may harm students. Young people also need sleep, exercise, family time, and hobbies. If homework becomes excessive, students may complete it quickly without thinking, copy from friends, or develop negative feelings toward school. Nonetheless, the problem is not homework itself but poor homework design.

Hence, teachers should assign work that has a clear purpose and a reasonable length. What I mean is, students should be able to understand why the task matters. To sum up, homework is valuable only when it supports learning rather than replacing rest and healthy development.

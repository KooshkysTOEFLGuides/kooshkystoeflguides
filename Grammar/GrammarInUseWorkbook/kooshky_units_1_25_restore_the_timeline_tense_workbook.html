Evaluate time meaning, sequence, aspect, and genre—not merely whether a form is technically possible.

Important principles:
1. More than one answer may be correct. Accept any alternative that the context genuinely supports.
2. When two forms are possible but convey different meanings, explain the difference instead of automatically rejecting one.
3. Preserve the source text's facts, genre, communicative purpose, register, and point of view.
4. Do not rewrite the text merely to improve vocabulary or style.
5. Pay close attention to time signals such as yesterday, last year, since, for, already, yet, recently, by the time, before, while, this time next week, tomorrow, next month, by Friday, when, after, until, and if.
6. In future time and condition clauses after when, before, after, as soon as, until, and if, check whether a present form is required instead of will.
7. Distinguish among future schedules, arrangements, prior intentions, predictions, actions in progress at a future point, and actions completed before a future point.
8. Do not assume that every highlighted verb must change. A highlighted present-simple form may occasionally be the best answer; explain why.
9. For email responses, preserve all task requirements, the relationship between sender and recipient, and appropriate email tone.
10. For Academic Discussion responses, preserve the writer's position, relevance, support, and concise academic-discussion style.
11. For speaking responses, keep the correction natural and speakable. Do not convert it into a formal essay.
12. For specialized documents, respect genre conventions. For example, schedules often use the present simple; news reports use past and present forms differently; historical analysis often uses the past perfect to establish earlier conditions; literary criticism often uses the literary present; reports may combine present perfect progress with future milestones.
13. For conversation-style texts, preserve natural turn-taking, contractions where appropriate, and the difference between spontaneous decisions, prior intentions, arrangements, offers, suggestions, and predictions.
14. For heavy academic readings, preserve the passage's disciplinary viewpoint. Historical passages may require layered past forms; scientific-method passages often combine general present forms with present perfect research progress; future-oriented passages may require future continuous, future perfect, planned arrangements, evidence-based predictions, and present forms in future time clauses.

The workbook may provide a suggested answer. Treat it as one defensible reconstruction, not an absolute answer key. If the student differs from it, judge the student’s timeline independently and explain whether the alternative is valid.

Text 1 — The Antarctic Field Season

TASK

A climate researcher explains a tightly scheduled expedition, prior intentions, evidence-based predictions, arrangements, future activities, and future completion.

At present, our twelve-person research team prepare for a six-week expedition to Antarctica. We plan the project for nearly a year, and we secure permits for the three coastal sites we intend to study. The main supply ship leaves Cape Town on August 4, but four of us fly to the research station two days earlier because we meet the station engineer and inspect the storage facilities before the equipment arrives.

Several decisions still depend on the weather. Satellite images show a widening area of unstable sea ice, so the southern landing site become difficult to reach. At tonight’s briefing, the director ask whether we should replace that site with a safer one farther north. If the forecast worsens, I contact the helicopter company immediately. One of my colleagues has offered to handle the revised maps, so I think I let her do that while I update the sampling schedule. Do I call the station manager before the meeting, or should we wait until the director has made the final decision?

By this time next month, we cross the Southern Ocean, and the laboratory technicians calibrate the sensors during the voyage. When the ship reaches the station, the first field group unload the drilling equipment while the second group set up the temporary laboratory. We are going to collect ice cores, measure coastal erosion, and compare the results with data that earlier teams recorded.

By the end of the fourth week, we complete the main sampling program and send preliminary measurements to our university. If the instruments operate reliably, we continue farther inland. When we finish the final survey, we return to the station and begin packing. This time in two months, I travel home; by then, the project produce a complete seasonal record that researchers will use for years.

If one team detects a possible biosignature, other telescopes will observe the planet at different times. By the time scientists accept a biological explanation, they will have eliminated many alternative chemical and geological processes.

    Chapter 01

    The big idea behind as … as

    Most students first meet this structure in a sentence such as He is as tall as me. That pattern is important, but it is only the beginning. The words between the two as markers can express a quality, a manner, an amount, a limit, or even a complete description containing several words.

    Key point

    Think of as … as as a comparison frame:

    as + the quality / amount / manner being compared + as + the comparison point

    The comparison point can be a person, a thing, a number, an expectation, or a whole idea.

    This guide uses a few short grammar labels. If words such as modifier, clause, or countable noun are unfamiliar, read the plain-English grammar toolbox before continuing. The same highlighted links appear throughout the lesson.

    Chapter 02

    A very quick review of the familiar patterns

    This chapter is only a reminder. The more useful and challenging forms begin in the next chapter.

    A quality: as + adjective + as

    Use this to compare what two people or things are like.

    • The second option is as practical as the first.
    • The issue is not as simple as it seems.
    • Her explanation was almost as clear as the professor’s.

    The manner of an action: as + adverb + as

    Use this to compare how people do something or how something happens.

    • She speaks as confidently as her classmates.
    • The economy did not recover as quickly as experts expected.
    • Please answer as clearly as you can.

    An amount or number

    Use many/few with plural countable nouns and much/little with uncountable nouns.

    • We interviewed as many students as possible.
    • The report did not include as much evidence as it should have.
    • Use as few words as possible.
    • The change should cause as little disruption as possible.
    • When the noun is already clear, it can disappear: Read as much as you can.

    Small and large differences

    Add a word before the first as to show how close the comparison is.

    • just as useful as = equally useful
    • almost as useful as = close, but not equal
    • not quite as useful as = a small difference
    • not nearly as useful as = a large difference
    Remember

    The middle can contain more than one word. For example, as extremely difficult as, as environmentally harmful as, and as carefully and accurately as all follow the same basic logic. We will not treat every added modifier as a separate structure.

    Chapter 03

    Put a complete description in the middle

    The most important step beyond as tall as is learning that the middle can be a complete description, not just one adjective. This lets you state exactly what the quality relates to and makes the pattern genuinely useful in TOEFL speaking and writing.

    Keep the adjective’s natural partner with it

    Many adjectives naturally connect to extra information with words such as about, for, to, in, and of. Do not leave that information outside the comparison. Keep the whole description between the two as markers.

    as + adjective + its necessary extra information + as
    Examples
    • Students are as concerned about tuition fees as they are about housing costs.
    • The professor was as interested in our research method as in our final conclusion.
    • This policy is as harmful to small businesses as the previous policy.
    • The findings are as relevant to developing countries as they are to wealthy ones.
    • She is as capable of leading the project as anyone else on the team.
    • The instructions were as useful for first-time applicants as they were for experienced students.
    How to build it

    Start with the expression you would normally use: concerned about the cost, useful for beginners, or capable of managing a team. Then place that complete expression after the first as: as concerned about the cost as, as useful for beginners as, and as capable of managing a team as.

    Add to + verb when the quality belongs to an action

    This form is especially useful with words such as easy, difficult, safe, likely, pleasant, and challenging. It allows you to compare how easy, difficult, likely, or safe an action is.

    High-value patterns
    • The software is as easy to use as the company claims.
    • The concept is as difficult to explain as it is to understand.
    • This approach is as likely to fail as the previous one.
    • Young adults may be as willing to volunteer as older people.
    • The material was as challenging to analyze as we had expected.
    • The online form is as safe for applicants to complete as the paper version.

    Use common -ed and -ing descriptions naturally

    Words such as accepted, affected, motivated, convincing, and surprising often behave like ordinary adjectives. They can be modified and expanded in exactly the same way.

    Examples
    • The newer treatment is as widely accepted as the traditional one.
    • Rural communities were as severely affected by the shortage as urban neighborhoods.
    • The students appeared as motivated to improve as their instructors had hoped.
    • The second explanation was as convincing as the first, although it relied on different evidence.
    • The results were as surprising to the researchers as they were encouraging.

    Occasionally, add a whole idea after the adjective

    Some adjectives can be followed by a complete idea: certain that …, confident that …, or aware that …. The result is grammatical but can become heavy, so use it only when the exact idea matters.

    Formal, carefully controlled examples
    • I am as certain that the policy will create new problems as I was when it was first announced.
    • The researchers are as confident that the treatment is safe as they are that it is effective.
    • She was as aware that the schedule might change as anyone else in the department.
    Style warning

    When this form becomes difficult to follow, divide it: The researchers are equally confident about the treatment’s safety and effectiveness. Advanced grammar should make your meaning more exact, not force the reader to reread the sentence.

    Place two or more qualities together

    When the same person, solution, or action needs several qualities, connect them with and or or. The same works with adverbs when you compare how an action is performed.

    Examples for speaking and writing
    • We need a solution that is as simple and affordable as possible.
    • Her response was as clear and concise as the professor’s.
    • The system should be as accurate, efficient, and accessible as possible.
    • The task was not as lengthy or complicated as we had feared.
    • Students should answer as clearly and directly as possible.
    • The team completed the task as quickly and accurately as it could.

    Treat ready-made descriptions as one unit

    Some common descriptions already begin with a short connecting word: at home, in control, under pressure, out of date, and out of touch. Keep the whole expression together.

    Examples
    • She felt as at home in the new city as she did in her hometown.
    • The minister appears as out of touch with ordinary people as his predecessor.
    • The new model is already as out of date as the old one.
    • The students were as under pressure as their instructors.
    • He seemed as in control of the situation as anyone could be.
    Chapter 04

    The advanced noun pattern: as large an audience as

    This formal and highly useful structure allows you to compare a quality and name the person or thing that has that quality in the same expression.

    as + adjective + a/an + singular countable noun + as
    Core examples
    • We want to attract as large an audience as possible.
    • This is as serious a problem as unemployment.
    • She is as capable a leader as anyone in the organization.
    • The researchers conducted as comprehensive a review as time permitted.
    • We need as simple a solution as possible.
    • It was as difficult a decision as I had ever made.
    • They presented as convincing an argument as one could reasonably expect.

    Why does the word order change?

    Normally, English says a large audience. In this comparison, however, the quality must come immediately after the first as. The adjective therefore moves in front of a/an.

    Ordinary order

    a large audience

    Comparison order

    as large an audience as possible

    Common error
    ✗We want to attract as a large audience as possible.
    ✓We want to attract as large an audience as possible.

    Where this pattern works—and where it usually does not

    Crucial limitation

    This special order normally applies to one singular countable noun introduced by a or an: a problem, an audience, a solution, a leader, a study, or an environment.

    Singular countable noun

    as serious a problem as this

    as inclusive an environment as possible

    The special order is natural because the phrase contains a/an + one countable thing.

    Plural, uncountable, or definite noun

    problems as serious as these

    evidence as convincing as this

    the solution that is as practical as the old one

    English normally changes the order instead of forcing the special pattern.

    Avoid these forms
    ✗as serious problems as these
    ✓problems as serious as these
    ✗as convincing evidence as this
    ✓evidence as convincing as this

    How to build it reliably

    1. Start with a normal phrase: an effective solution.
    2. Choose the adjective you want to compare: effective.
    3. Place it after the first as: as effective.
    4. Add a/an + the singular noun: as effective a solution.
    5. Add the comparison point or limit: as effective a solution as the earlier proposal.

    The adjective itself can still be expanded

    Once the word order is correct, the adjective may include a careful modifier or may be joined to another adjective. Keep the result readable.

    Longer but natural versions
    • This represents as potentially serious a threat as climate change.
    • They conducted as methodologically rigorous a study as their budget permitted.
    • We should develop as environmentally sustainable a system as possible.
    • The university hopes to create as inclusive and supportive an environment as possible.
    • The committee produced as clear, concise, and practical a set of recommendations as the available evidence allowed.
    Chapter 05

    Compare how strongly a noun label fits

    Sometimes you are not comparing a simple quality such as large or useful. You are comparing whether something truly counts as a problem, risk, challenge, teacher, or another type of thing.

    Use as much of a … as

    as much of a/an + noun + as

    This structure asks: To what degree is it really this kind of thing?

    Examples
    • The delay was not as much of a problem as we had expected.
    • The assignment was as much of a challenge as the final exam.
    • Social isolation can be as much of a health risk as poor nutrition.
    • The policy became as much of a political issue as an economic one.
    • His statement was not as much of an apology as an excuse.
    • We want the schedule change to be as little of an inconvenience as possible.

    as serious a problem as

    This compares the seriousness of the problem.

    It was as serious a problem as we had expected.

    as much of a problem as

    This compares how strongly the situation counts as a problem at all.

    It was not as much of a problem as we had expected.

    Use as much a X as a Y

    as much a/an + noun + as a/an + noun

    Use this when the same person, thing, or issue belongs equally to two categories.

    Strong academic examples
    • She is as much a researcher as a teacher.
    • The issue is as much a cultural problem as an economic one.
    • The book is as much a political argument as a historical study.
    • TOEFL writing is as much a test of idea development as a test of grammar.
    • The disagreement was as much a matter of values as of evidence.

    Correct the label with not so much X as Y

    This related pattern does not say that X and Y are equal. It says that Y is the more accurate description.

    Examples
    • The problem is not so much financial as political.
    • She was not so much angry as disappointed.
    • The course tests not so much memorization as analytical ability.
    • His response was not so much an explanation as an excuse.
    TOEFL strategy

    These patterns are excellent for showing a careful distinction in Academic Discussion writing. They help you avoid a simple either/or argument:

    The issue is not so much whether technology is useful as whether people use it responsibly.

    Chapter 06

    Express a maximum, minimum, or realistic limit

    In many useful sentences, the second half is not another person or thing. It states the limit created by ability, time, evidence, money, rules, safety, need, or ordinary reasonableness.

    Ask for the greatest or smallest achievable degree

    Maximum and minimum
    • Respond as quickly as possible.
    • Make the explanation as clear as possible.
    • We need to recruit as diverse a group as possible.
    • Students should examine as many sources as possible.
    • The procedure should produce as little waste as possible.
    • Write as clearly as you can.
    • Gather as much evidence as you can.
    • We made as few changes as we could.

    Choose an ending that names the real limit

    Possible is only one option. A more exact ending often produces a more mature sentence because it tells the reader what actually controls the limit.

    Abilityas clearly as you can; as carefully as they could
    Timeas many questions as time allows; as complete a review as the deadline permitted
    Money or resourcesas much support as the budget allows; as broad a program as resources permit
    Evidenceas strong a conclusion as the evidence supports; as specific as the data justify
    Rules or lawas flexible as the regulations permit; as open as the law allows
    Safety or practicalityas quickly as is safely possible; as close as is reasonably practicable
    Needas detailed as necessary; as many changes as needed; as frequent as required
    Appropriatenessas formal as appropriate; as cautious as advisable; as low as medically reasonable
    Reasonable expectationas comprehensive as anyone could reasonably expect; as successful as we could have hoped
    General circumstancesas fair as circumstances permit; as accurate as conditions allow
    Natural full sentences
    • We answered as many questions as time allowed.
    • Universities should offer as much financial support as their budgets reasonably allow.
    • The researchers stated as strong a conclusion as the available evidence supported.
    • The program should remain as flexible as the regulations permit.
    • The damaged building must be reopened as quickly as is safely possible.
    • The final version should remain as close to the original as is reasonably practicable.
    • The committee tried to make the process as fair and transparent as circumstances permitted.

    Maximum is not the same as enough

    as much detail as possible

    Give the greatest amount you can. This may create a very long answer.

    as much detail as necessary

    Give enough detail to achieve the purpose, but do not add unnecessary material.

    Needed, required, appropriate, reasonable
    • Include as much detail as necessary.
    • Make as many revisions as required.
    • The explanation may be as technical as needed, but no more.
    • Meetings should be as frequent as necessary, not automatically weekly.
    • The tone should be as formal as appropriate for the recipient.
    • The dosage should remain as low as medically advisable.
    • The researchers offered as carefully qualified a conclusion as the evidence justified.
    • Her answer was as comprehensive as anyone could reasonably expect in ten minutes.

    Two emphatic ways to describe an extreme

    as … as it gets

    Use this to mean that something is at or very near the greatest possible degree: The final question was as difficult as it gets.

    as … as can be

    This informal emphatic form means “extremely”: Despite the delay, she remained as calm as can be. It is natural in speech but less useful in formal academic writing.

    Usage warning

    As possible often encourages the maximum. In an argument, that maximum may be wasteful or unrealistic. Instead of Governments should spend as much money as possible on education, write Governments should invest as much in education as their budgets and other essential responsibilities reasonably allow.

    Chapter 07

    Show exact size differences with multipliers and fractions

    Place a multiplier or fraction before the first as. This is clearer and safer than many informal expressions with larger than.

    twice / three times / half / one-third + as + quality or quantity + as
    Examples
    • The new building is twice as large as the old one.
    • Housing is three times as expensive as it was twenty years ago.
    • The new processor operates twice as quickly as the old one.
    • The city has twice as many residents as it did in 1990.
    • The new system uses half as much energy as the old one.
    • The rural school has half as many teachers as the urban school.
    • The treatment is only one-third as expensive as the earlier method.
    Precision note

    Twice as large as X normally means two times the size of X. Expressions such as two times larger than are used inconsistently, so the twice as … as form is usually clearer in academic writing.

    Chapter 08

    Highlight a surprising upper or lower point

    Some as … as expressions do not compare two ordinary things. They announce an extreme number, amount, age, date, distance, or duration—often because it is surprising.

    Numbers and amounts

    Equality

    She invited as many students as I did.

    The two people invited equal numbers.

    A surprising upper point

    She invited as many as 200 students.

    The number may have reached 200.

    High and low endpoints
    • As many as 500 students attended the event.
    • The project could cost as much as ten million dollars.
    • The treatment may reduce symptoms by as much as 40 percent.
    • The process can take as few as three days.
    • Some classes contain as few as five students.
    • The device uses as little as two watts of power.

    Age, time, distance, and history

    Useful academic patterns
    • Children as young as five use the application.
    • People as old as ninety participated.
    • The idea appeared as early as the seventeenth century.
    • Similar results were reported as recently as last year.
    • The practice continued as late as the 1990s.
    • Records extend as far back as 1850.
    • Some birds travel as far as 10,000 kilometres.
    • The procedure may take as long as six hours.
    How to use it

    Use these forms when the endpoint itself matters. Do not add them merely to make a sentence look advanced. The number, date, age, or distance should support your point.

    Chapter 09

    Control the strength of the comparison

    The small words before the first as let you show exact equality, near equality, a small difference, or a very large difference.

    just as / every bit as

    Strong equality: Practical experience can be just as valuable as formal education.

    almost as / nearly as

    Close but not equal: The second solution is almost as effective as the first.

    not quite as

    A small difference: The new system is not quite as efficient as expected.

    not nearly as

    A large difference: Online interaction is not nearly as spontaneous as face-to-face conversation.

    nowhere near as

    A very emphatic large difference, more common in speech: The evidence is nowhere near as strong as the author suggests.

    More examples
    • Rural communities need just as much support as urban ones.
    • The two samples were exactly as heavy as each other.
    • The second essay was not quite as persuasive as the first.
    • The university does not provide nearly as many scholarships as it should.
    • The long-term effects may be every bit as serious as the immediate ones.
    • The city has almost as many residents as the capital.

    Use as … as ever and as … as any

    As … as ever says that a quality remains at its previous level. As … as any often has a strong meaning close to among the best.

    Useful emphatic forms
    • The need for reform is as urgent as ever.
    • The professor's lectures are as engaging as ever.
    • She is as capable as any candidate in the field.
    • This explanation is as convincing as any I have encountered.
    Formal alternative

    Not so … as can replace not as … as: The evidence is not so convincing as it first appears. It is correct but usually more formal and less common. Do not confuse it with the different pattern not so much X as Y.

    Common error

    Do not combine not with nowhere near:

    ✗The project did not attract nowhere near as many participants as expected.
    ✓The project attracted nowhere near as many participants as expected.
    ✓The project did not attract nearly as many participants as expected.
    Chapter 10

    What can come after the second as?

    The comparison point can be short, but it can also be a complete idea. Learning these endings will make your sentences more flexible and accurate.

    After learning the possible endings, continue to the conditional-comparison chapter, then move to the repetition-avoidance chapter.

    A noun or pronoun

    Simple endings
    • The new method is as reliable as the old method.
    • He is as tall as me.
    • She is as experienced as him.

    Object forms such as me and him are normal, especially in conversation.

    A complete idea

    Complete comparisons
    • He is as tall as I am.
    • She is as experienced as he is.
    • The result was as important as we had expected it to be.
    • The concept is as difficult to explain as it is to understand.

    The complete version is often clearer in formal writing because it shows exactly what is being compared.

    A shorter ending with a helper verb

    English often avoids repeating the same main verb. Instead, it uses a short helper such as do, can, will, or would.

    Avoiding repetition
    • She works as hard as I do.
    • He can run as quickly as she can.
    • Students did not improve as much as we thought they would.
    • The new system performed as effectively as we had hoped it would.

    A reduced academic ending

    Academic English often removes words that are easy to understand from context.

    Full version

    The task was as difficult as we had expected it to be.

    Reduced version

    The task was as difficult as expected.

    Common reduced endings
    • The results were not as significant as predicted.
    • The treatment proved as effective as anticipated.
    • Costs were twice as high as originally estimated.
    • The policy was not as damaging as initially feared.
    • The system is as reliable as ever.
    • She performed as well as before.

    Short time, place, and descriptive endings

    The words after the second as may also be a short time or place expression when the missing comparison is easy to recover.

    Compressed but natural comparisons
    • The lecture was as engaging as ever.
    • She performed as well as before.
    • The weather was as warm as in early spring.
    • The second experiment was as successful as under laboratory conditions.
    • After the repairs, the equipment was as good as new.

    In a fuller version, the last three examples might say as it is in early spring, as it had been under laboratory conditions, and as good as it was when new.

    Style note

    Reduced endings are concise and natural in academic writing. In speaking, the fuller version may be easier to produce quickly: not as difficult as I expected.

    Chapter 11

    Compare real and imagined conditions with as … as

    An if-condition can interact with as … as in several ways. The whole equality comparison may describe the result of a condition; a conditional situation may come after the second as; or two different conditions may become the two sides of the comparison. These patterns are especially useful when you compare reality with an alternative that is possible, imagined, or no longer possible.

    THE THREE BASIC SHAPES
    • If the connection remains stable, remote students can participate as actively as students in the classroom.
    • The basic plan is not as expensive as it would be if we hired outside consultants.
    • The course would be as useful if it were delivered online as if it were taught in person.

    Compare real, repeated, or genuinely possible conditions

    Use ordinary present forms after if when the condition is real, repeated, or genuinely open. The sentence compares what happens under one condition with what happens under another.

    Real and open conditions
    • The device uses as much power if a video is playing as it does if a game is running.
    • Students can perform as well if they prepare independently as if they prepare in a group, provided that they receive clear guidance.
    • If the internet connection is stable, remote participants can contribute as actively as those in the room.
    • If both applicants meet the language requirement, the international student should receive as fair an evaluation as the local applicant.

    The repeated result can be stated twice for maximum clarity—as much power if X as it does if Y—or shortened when the missing words are obvious.

    Compare reality with a hypothetical present or future alternative

    When the alternative is imagined rather than expected as a real fact, English commonly uses a past form after if and would, could, or might in the imagined result. The past form here creates distance from reality; it does not necessarily refer to past time.

    Present or future hypothesis
    • The current plan is not as expensive as it would be if we hired consultants.
    • I would be as satisfied working from home as I would be working in an office.
    • The course could be just as effective if it were offered online as if it were taught face to face.
    • Public transport would not be as convenient as it is now if the evening service ended at eight.

    In careful hypothetical writing, were is a safe choice with every subject: if I were, if the course were, if the policy were. Was is common in conversation with singular subjects, but were remains the clearer formal choice.

    Compare an actual past result with an unreal past alternative

    To imagine a different past, use if + had + past participle. The imagined result normally contains would/could/might have + past participle. This allows you to judge what actually happened against a past possibility that did not happen.

    Unreal past comparison
    • The result was not as strong as it might have been if the sample had been larger.
    • We completed the project as quickly as we would have if we had hired an additional team.
    • The low-cost method produced results almost as accurate as they would have been if we had used the premium equipment.
    • The event was as successful as it could have been if the organizers had received twice the funding.

    Would have presents the expected imagined result, could have presents ability or possibility, and might have makes the imagined result less certain.

    Mix time: a different past would change the present

    A past decision or event can be compared through its present result. Use a past-perfect condition for the unreal past, but use a present-style result with would, could, or might because the consequence belongs to now.

    Past condition → present result
    • The team is not as prepared now as it would be if training had begun earlier.
    • The neighborhood would be as crowded today as the city center if the festival had not been canceled.
    • I do not have as much flexibility now as I would if I had chosen freelance work.
    • The university is not as financially secure as it might be if it had invested more carefully ten years ago.

    The two times are intentionally different: the condition is in the past, while the result being compared is true now.

    Mix time in the other direction: a continuing condition affected a past result

    Sometimes a present or general characteristic helps explain a past outcome. Use a past-form condition such as if the speaker were more confident, but a past imagined result such as would have been.

    Present/general condition → past result
    • The presentation was not as persuasive as it would have been if the speaker were more confident.
    • The negotiations did not proceed as smoothly as they might have if the two sides trusted each other more.
    • The report was not as precise as it could have been if the writer were more familiar with statistical methods.
    • The class discussion was not as productive as it would have been if the students were generally more willing to challenge one another politely.

    If the condition was true only at that past time, use the past perfect instead: if the speaker had been more confident. Use the past form when you mean a continuing or general characteristic.

    Compare two conditional alternatives directly

    Both sides of the comparison can be imagined conditions. Keep the two if-parts parallel so the reader can see the exact alternatives.

    Two possible or unreal conditions
    • The program would be as valuable if it served rural schools as if it served urban ones.
    • I would be just as comfortable if the interview were online as if it were held in person.
    • The final judgment would have been as fair if the names had been hidden as if independent judges had reviewed the work.
    • The device would use as much energy if the processor were slowed as if the screen brightness were reduced.

    A fuller version may repeat the result: The program would be as valuable if it served rural schools as it would be if it served urban ones. The shorter version is natural when no ambiguity is possible.

    Shorten a conditional comparison—but keep it recoverable

    English can omit the repeated subject and imagined result after the second as. This produces compact sentences, but the missing words must be easy to reconstruct.

    FULLER

    The basic plan is almost as expensive as it would be if we hired consultants.

    COMPRESSED

    The basic plan is almost as expensive as if we hired consultants.

    FULLER

    The results were almost as accurate as they would have been if we had used the premium method.

    COMPRESSED

    The results were almost as accurate as if we had used the premium method.

    The compressed form is natural when the full imagined result is obvious. In formal TOEFL writing, keep the fuller version whenever shortening could make the time or meaning unclear.

    Use as … as if for an imagined standard

    In this pattern, the if-idea describes the imaginary situation that would normally produce the observed manner or quality. It often means “with the same appearance, manner, or intensity that we would expect in this imagined situation.”

    An imagined standard
    • She answered as confidently as if she had prepared for weeks, although she had seen the topic only minutes earlier.
    • The room looked as clean as if it had never been used.
    • He discussed the unfamiliar theory as comfortably as if he worked with it every day.
    • The system responded as smoothly as though it had been tested for months.

    As though has the same basic meaning as as if and is slightly less common. Past and past-perfect forms often signal that the imagined situation is unreal or doubtful.

    Express the condition without if

    Condition-like meanings can also be introduced by unless, provided that, as long as, without, and otherwise.

    Other conditional signals
    • The revised system can be as efficient as the old one, provided that staff receive adequate training.
    • Remote assessment can be as fair as classroom assessment as long as every student has reliable technology.
    • Without the grant, the program could not have been as inclusive as it eventually became.
    • The safety problem was not as serious as it otherwise might have been because the automatic shutdown worked immediately.
    • The results will remain as unreliable as before unless the researchers enlarge the sample.

    Otherwise means “if the situation had been different” or “without the fact just mentioned.” It is a concise way to carry an unstated condition.

    COMMON ERRORS
    • Do not normally put would inside an ordinary hypothetical if-part: as expensive as it would be if we hired consultants, not if we would hire consultants.
    • Match the time carefully: use had + past participle for an unreal past condition, and choose would be or would have been according to the time of the result.
    • Do not compress the ending if the reader may not know what has been omitted.
    • Keep two compared conditions parallel: if it were online … as if it were in person.
    Chapter 12

    Avoid repetition: shorter, more natural comparisons

    Long comparisons can become clumsy when the same noun or verb appears twice. English therefore uses two closely related tools: ellipsis, which leaves predictable words unstated, and substitution, which replaces them with shorter forms such as one, those, ours, or do. These are not decorative shortcuts: they are central to producing fluent comparisons in TOEFL speaking and writing.

    KEY POINT

    Keep enough information for the comparison to remain clear, but do not repeat words that the listener or reader can recover immediately.

    Replace a repeated countable noun with one or ones

    Use one or ones when the repeated item is a countable noun. Use one for a singular noun and ones for a plural noun. Keep any new description after it.

    Singular and plural replacement
    • The debate is as much a discussion about human responsibility as one about technological ability.
    • The final proposal was as practical as the original one, but it was easier to fund.
    • The risks faced by part-time workers can be as serious as the ones faced by full-time employees.
    • We need as clear an explanation as the one the professor gave yesterday.

    One does not replace an uncountable noun. Say as much evidence as the earlier study provided, not as much evidence as the earlier one when one would incorrectly stand for evidence. It can, however, stand for a countable noun such as study: as much evidence as the earlier one provided.

    Use that or those for formal comparisons

    That and those often replace a repeated noun in formal writing, especially before an of-phrase. Use that for a singular or uncountable idea and those for plural items.

    Common academic patterns
    • The effect of social isolation may be as damaging as that of poor nutrition.
    • The quality of the online course was as high as that of the classroom course.
    • The results of the pilot study were as reliable as those of the larger investigation.
    • The challenges facing rural schools are as urgent as those facing schools in crowded urban areas.

    This form is usually cleaner than repeating the effect, the quality, the results, or the challenges. Do not use that for a plural noun or those for a singular noun.

    Use a possessive form when ownership is already clear

    A possessive replacement such as mine, yours, hers, ours, or theirs can stand for a repeated noun phrase.

    Do not repeat the owned noun
    • Her argument is as carefully developed as mine.
    • Their findings were as surprising as ours.
    • Your proposed schedule is as flexible as theirs.
    • The university’s response was not as detailed as the committee’s.

    Let a helper verb carry the repeated action

    When the same action would otherwise be repeated, retain only the appropriate helper verb. Match the helper to the original grammar: do/does/did for ordinary verbs, a form of be for descriptions, have for completed actions, and a modal such as can, will, would, may, or should when one is already present.

    Repeated and heavy

    She works as carefully as her supervisor works.

    The policy may cost as much as experts believe it will cost.

    Natural substitution

    She works as carefully as her supervisor does.

    The policy may cost as much as experts believe it will.

    Choose the helper that preserves the meaning
    • I cannot explain the process as clearly as the technician can.
    • The second group improved as much as the first group did.
    • The new system has performed as reliably as the older one has.
    • The reform was as controversial as its critics said it would be.
    • The final cost was not as high as we had feared it might be.

    Do not choose a helper merely because it sounds short. It must preserve the tense, time, possibility, or completed meaning of the longer version.

    Leave out a repeated description, noun, or connecting phrase

    Sometimes no replacement word is necessary. When both sides clearly share the same description or grammatical frame, the repeated part can simply disappear.

    Ellipsis inside balanced comparisons
    • Students are as concerned about housing costs as about tuition fees.
      Fuller: as concerned about housing costs as they are concerned about tuition fees.
    • The professor was as interested in our research method as in our final conclusion.
    • The debate is as much about responsibility as about technology.
    • The course should be as useful for working adults as for full-time students.
    • The policy is as difficult to defend politically as to implement efficiently.

    Keep matching structures on the two sides. As concerned about X as about Y is balanced; a mixture such as as concerned about X as Y worries them forces the reader to rebuild the sentence and is usually harder to follow.

    Reduce an obvious complete idea

    A whole ending can be shortened when its missing words are obvious. This produces common academic forms such as as expected, as well as compact time- and place-based comparisons.

    From a full idea to a compact ending
    • The treatment was as effective as researchers had predicted it would be → The treatment was as effective as predicted.
    • The task was not as difficult as we had expected it to be → The task was not as difficult as expected.
    • The equipment works as reliably in the field as it does in the laboratory → The equipment works as reliably in the field as in the laboratory.
    • Students performed as well this year as they did last year → Students performed as well this year as last year.
    COMMON ERROR

    Do not shorten a comparison until the missing meaning is unmistakable. The policy affected students as much as teachers can mean either that it affected teachers equally or that it affected students as much as teachers affected students. In a context where both readings are possible, write the fuller version: The policy affected students as much as it affected teachers.

    TOEFL STRATEGY

    Draft the full comparison mentally, then remove only what is repetitive. This keeps your grammar accurate while giving the final sentence a more natural rhythm.

    Chapter 13

    Expressions that look similar but have special meanings

    The following groups contain two instances of as, but they are not always ordinary equality comparisons. Learn each as a useful expression.

    As soon as

    Use it to mean immediately when.

    I will respond as soon as I receive the message.

    The class will begin as soon as the professor arrives.

    Submit the form as soon as possible.

    As long as

    It can compare duration, but it often means provided that or only if this condition is met.

    The meeting lasted as long as the lecture. (duration comparison)

    You may leave early as long as you finish your work. (condition)

    The method is acceptable as long as it produces reliable results. (condition)

    As far as

    It can refer to physical distance or mark the limit of knowledge and discussion.

    We walked as far as the river.

    As far as I know, the results have not been published.

    As far as education is concerned, funding remains inadequate.

    As well as

    It can compare performance, but it can also mean and also.

    Comparison

    She writes as well as her professor.

    Addition

    The course develops speaking as well as writing skills.

    Extra grammar note

    When as well as adds a second subject, the verb usually agrees with the first subject: The professor, as well as the students, is concerned. In most TOEFL sentences, using ordinary and is simpler when both subjects are equally central.

    As much as

    Besides quantity, it can introduce a contrast similar to although.

    She earned as much money as her colleague. (quantity)

    As much as I admire the proposal, I cannot support it. (contrast)

    As much as students value flexibility, they also need structure.

    As good as

    It can show equality, but it can also mean practically or virtually.

    The new version is as good as the old one. (equal quality)

    The project is as good as finished. (practically finished)

    Without additional funding, the plan is as good as dead.

    Keep the meanings separate

    Always ask whether the expression is making a real comparison or acting as a ready-made connector. For example, She writes as well as her professor compares skill, while The course teaches writing as well as speaking simply adds a second item.

    Chapter 14

    Turn these patterns into TOEFL language

    Knowing a structure is not enough. You need a reliable way to produce it under time pressure. The following frames can be adapted to many topics.

    Academic Discussion frames

    Equal importance

    X can be just as important as Y because …

    Practical experience can be just as important as formal education because it helps students apply abstract ideas.

    Careful distinction

    The issue is not so much X as Y.

    The issue is not so much whether technology is useful as whether people use it responsibly.

    Two-sided classification

    X is as much a ___ as a ___.

    Remote work is as much a management challenge as a technological one.

    Realistic limit

    Institutions should ___ as much as their resources reasonably allow.

    Universities should offer as much financial support as their resources reasonably allow.

    Compare reality with an alternative

    X is not as ___ as it would be if ___.

    The current policy is not as expensive as it would be if the university outsourced every service.

    Email frames

    Polite request

    Could you please send me the information as soon as possible?

    This is direct but still polite when the matter is genuinely urgent.

    Reducing inconvenience

    I would like to make this as little of an inconvenience as possible.

    Useful when requesting a change, extension, or special arrangement.

    Clear instructions

    Please include as much detail as necessary.

    Use necessary when you want enough information, not every possible detail.

    Flexible condition

    Any time would work, as long as the meeting ends before 4 p.m.

    This gives flexibility while stating one condition.

    Speaking frames

    Natural spoken language
    • Online classes are not quite as engaging as face-to-face classes, but they are much more flexible.
    • I try to finish important tasks as early as I can because unexpected problems often appear later.
    • Public transportation can be just as convenient as driving if the city has reliable services.
    • The trip was not nearly as expensive as I had expected.
    • For me, choosing a major is as much a personal decision as a financial one.
    • X would be just as ___ if ___ as if ___.

    A five-step production method

    1. Decide the relationship: equal, almost equal, very different, maximum, minimum, or dual category.
    2. Choose the core quality or amount: useful, difficult to manage, many students, much evidence.
    3. Add necessary information: useful for beginners, concerned about cost, difficult to explain.
    4. Choose the ending: another thing, a complete idea, possible, necessary, or a realistic limit.
    5. Read the whole sentence aloud: if the middle is too long, divide the sentence.
    Exam reminder

    An advanced structure earns no credit merely because it looks advanced. Use it when it makes your comparison clearer, more precise, or more balanced.

    Chapter 15

    Common errors worth eliminating

    Error 1 · Wrong word order before a singular noun
    ✗It is as a serious problem as unemployment.
    ✓It is as serious a problem as unemployment.
    Error 2 · Using many/much with the wrong noun
    ✗as much students as possible
    ✓as many students as possible
    ✗as many information as possible
    ✓as much information as possible
    Error 3 · Confusing maximum with necessity

    As much as possible asks for the maximum. As much as necessary asks for enough. Choose the meaning you actually need.

    Error 4 · Incomplete comparison
    ✗The new method is as effective.
    ✓The new method is as effective as the old one.

    Exceptions exist in short conversational answers, but full academic sentences normally need the comparison point.

    Error 5 · Overloading the middle

    Grammatical does not always mean readable. Instead of writing one enormous comparison, split it:

    Heavy: The policy is as economically damaging to small local businesses and socially divisive among low-income communities as many critics had predicted.

    Clearer: The policy is as economically damaging as many critics predicted. It is also highly divisive among low-income communities.

    Error 6 · Forcing the special noun order onto the wrong kind of noun

    The pattern as serious a problem as normally needs one singular countable noun with a/an. With a plural, uncountable, or definite noun, change the order.

    ✗as serious problems as these
    ✓problems as serious as these
    ✗as convincing evidence as this
    ✓evidence as convincing as this
    Error 9 · Mixing conditional times

    Choose the imagined result by time. Say not as prepared now as it would be if training had begun earlier, but not as persuasive yesterday as it would have been if the speaker had prepared more carefully.

    1. We expected the delay to be a major problem. It was less of a problem than that.

    3. Online education is a technological challenge. It is equally a social challenge.

    4. The main difficulty is not a lack of money. Poor planning is the more accurate explanation.

    5. The new platform serves 40,000 users. The old platform serves 20,000.

    9. A highly prepared student may complete the course in only six weeks.

      Use as few as as a low endpoint.

    10. The archive contains records from 1870.

      Use as far back as.

    11. Children who are only four years old can use the application.

      Use as young as.

    12. The second essay is close to the first in persuasiveness, but it is slightly weaker.

      Use not quite as … as.

    1. The article presents the evidence as strong. In fact, the evidence is much weaker than the article suggests.

      Use nowhere near as … as without adding not.

    2. The need for reform was urgent before the election. It remains equally urgent now.

      Use as … as ever.

    3. Mina is among the most capable candidates in the entire applicant pool.

      Use as … as any.

    4. We expected the new system to perform effectively. It performed at exactly the level we had hoped it would.

      Use a helper verb ending and avoid repeating perform.

    5. The committee predicted that the results would be significant. They were equally significant.

      Create one full version and then a reduced academic version.

    6. I admire the policy’s goal. Nevertheless, I cannot support the policy because its cost is unrealistic.

      Use the contrasting meaning of as much as.

    7. The course develops speaking skills. It also develops writing skills.

      Use the additive meaning of as well as.

    8. The department may use the method, but only on the condition that the method continues to produce reliable results.

      Use the conditional meaning of as long as.

    9. Please review the attached documents. Reply immediately after you finish reviewing them.

      Use as soon as.

    10. The final approval has been received, and only a routine signature remains; therefore, the project is practically finished.

      Use the special meaning of as good as.

    11. The committee wants a broad audience, but its advertising budget and the short registration period create limits.

    13. Residents care strongly about the policy’s immediate cost. They care equally strongly about its long-term environmental effects.

    16. The city wants the new transport system to be highly accessible, but it must still preserve safety and service standards.

    12. The equipment works reliably in the field. It works equally reliably in the laboratory.

    Why this works: Two coordinated adjectives share the maximum-limit ending.

    07

    The researchers are as confident that the treatment is safe for older patients as they are that it can reduce the most common symptoms, although they still recommend further study.

    Why this works: Each adjective is followed by a whole idea; this is formal and should be used only when the balance matters.

    08

    The proposal may prove as politically divisive as it is economically expensive, because it asks taxpayers to accept immediate costs for benefits that may not appear for many years.

    Why this works: The sentence compares two different qualities of the same proposal.

    Special noun order

    09

    To give the event a realistic chance of success, the organizers should try to attract as large an audience as the venue, advertising budget, and short registration period reasonably allow.

    Why this works: The singular countable noun audience follows the adjective, and several real limits appear after the second as.

    10

    Before recommending a nationwide change, the committee must conduct as comprehensive a review as the six-month deadline permits, including evidence from both urban and rural schools.

    Why this works: The special noun order combines with a time-based limit.

    11

    The research team designed as methodologically rigorous a study as the available data and ethical restrictions allowed, but the authors still acknowledged several unavoidable weaknesses.

    Why this works: A modifier expands the adjective before a study.

    12

    A university should create as inclusive and supportive an environment as possible without lowering the academic standards that prepare students for demanding professional work.

    Why this works: Two adjectives precede the singular noun environment.

    13

    If governments continue to delay action, water insecurity could become as potentially serious a threat as climate change for communities that already experience long periods of drought.

    Why this works: The modifier potentially remains part of the adjective phrase.

    14

    Choosing whether to leave a stable job for an uncertain educational opportunity was as difficult a decision as Maria had ever faced, particularly because her family depended on her income.

    Why this works: The ending as Maria had ever faced gives the comparison a personal maximum.

    15

    The student presented as convincing an argument as anyone could reasonably expect in ten minutes, supporting the main claim with a clear example and a carefully explained consequence.

    Why this works: The ending sets a reasonable expectation rather than an impossible ideal.

    16

    After examining several conflicting studies, the panel produced as clear, balanced, and practical a set of recommendations as the evidence justified, while openly identifying areas of uncertainty.

    Why this works: The head noun is the singular countable noun set, so the special order remains possible even though recommendations is plural inside the of-phrase.

    Noun labels

    17

    Once the technical problems had been corrected, the delayed launch was not as much of a disaster as the early news reports had suggested, although it still damaged the company’s reputation.

    Why this works: The sentence compares how strongly the event deserves the label disaster.

    18

    Because the department announced the new schedule several weeks in advance, the change caused as little of an inconvenience as could reasonably be expected for students with jobs and family responsibilities.

    Why this works: As little of a … as minimizes the degree to which the label applies.

    19

    The debate over artificial intelligence is as much a discussion about human responsibility as a discussion about technological ability, since tools do not decide how societies choose to use them.

    Why this works: One issue belongs equally to two categories.

    20

    A successful teacher is as much a careful observer of students as a knowledgeable presenter of information, because effective instruction depends on noticing confusion and adjusting explanations.

    Why this works: The same person is described through two equally important roles.

    21

    The central problem is not so much that employees resist change as that managers often fail to explain why the change is necessary or how it will improve daily work.

    Why this works: The second idea replaces the first as the more accurate explanation.

    22

    His statement was not as much of an apology as an attempt to protect his public image, because he focused on the criticism he had received rather than the harm he had caused.

    Why this works: The structure questions whether the statement truly deserves the noun label apology.

    23

    For older adults who live alone, prolonged social isolation can be as much of a health risk as poor nutrition or insufficient exercise, even though its effects are less immediately visible.

    Why this works: The label health risk is compared across different causes.

    Limits

    24

    Students should consult as many reliable sources as time allows, but they should spend enough time analyzing each source rather than collecting references they cannot meaningfully use.

    Why this works: The deadline sets the maximum number.

    25

    Researchers should make as strong a claim as the evidence can support, because exaggerating a result may attract attention in the short term but weaken trust in the long term.

    Why this works: The evidence directly controls the strength of the noun phrase.

    26

    The authors offered as carefully qualified a conclusion as the limited sample justified, clearly separating what the study demonstrated from what future research might discover.

    Why this works: The special noun order combines with an evidence-based realistic limit.

    27

    Considering the severe time restriction, her response was as comprehensive as anyone could reasonably expect, since it stated a clear position, developed two reasons, and included a relevant example.

    Why this works: The ending evaluates performance against a reasonable human expectation.

    28

    The instructions should be as technical as necessary for accuracy, but any specialized term that does not improve precision should be replaced with language ordinary users can understand.

    Why this works: Necessary asks for sufficiency rather than maximum complexity.

    29

    The damaged bridge should reopen as early as is safely and legally practicable, but public pressure must not lead engineers to ignore inspections required by law.

    Why this works: Safety, law, and practicality jointly limit the earliest date.

    30

    When updating a historical building, architects should keep the new design as close to the original as modern safety requirements permit, rather than copying old features that create avoidable risks.

    Why this works: A rule-based limit follows a long descriptive phrase.

    31

    Even when every applicant cannot receive the same outcome, the university should make the selection process as fair, transparent, and consistent as circumstances permit.

    Why this works: Three qualities share one circumstances-based limit.

    Multipliers and endpoints

    32

    After the city introduced dedicated bus lanes, the new route carried twice as many passengers as the previous service while completing the journey in less time.

    Why this works: The multiplier compares plural countable quantities.

    33

    The redesigned cooling system uses half as much electricity as the older equipment, which reduces operating costs without lowering the building’s safety or comfort standards.

    Why this works: The fraction modifies an uncountable quantity.

    34

    Because the generic medicine is only one-third as expensive as the branded alternative, more patients can continue treatment without sacrificing other essential expenses.

    Why this works: The fraction compares price directly.

    35

    The international survey included as many as 12,000 participants from thirty countries, giving the researchers an unusually broad view of public attitudes.

    Why this works: As many as announces a noteworthy upper number.

    36

    With intensive support and recognition of earlier coursework, some students can complete the program in as few as eighteen months rather than the usual three years.

    Why this works: As few as highlights a surprisingly low number.

    37

    Historical records show that versions of the practice existed as early as the seventeenth century, although its modern form developed much later.

    Why this works: The phrase marks an early historical endpoint.

    Strength and endings

    38

    Online seminars are not quite as spontaneous as face-to-face discussions, but carefully designed breakout activities can still encourage meaningful interaction among students.

    Why this works: Not quite signals a small difference, not a complete failure.

    39

    The evidence is nowhere near as conclusive as the article’s confident title suggests, because the study involved a small sample and measured participants for only two weeks.

    Why this works: The phrase expresses a very large difference without an extra not.

    40

    For many students who cannot move to another city, a well-designed online program can be every bit as valuable as traditional classroom instruction.

    Why this works: Every bit as strongly emphasizes full equality.

    41

    Despite repeated reforms, the need to improve access to affordable education is as urgent as ever, especially for students from rural and low-income communities.

    Why this works: The quality remains at its earlier high level.

    42

    With her research experience, communication skills, and careful understanding of the project, Leila is as capable as any candidate the committee is likely to interview.

    Why this works: This gives a strong, nearly superlative evaluation.

    43

    The pilot program proved as effective as anticipated, reducing waiting times without increasing staff workload or lowering the quality of service.

    Why this works: The reduced ending compresses a fuller comparison such as as experts had anticipated it would be.

    Special meanings

    44

    Please examine the revised contract carefully and contact me as soon as you have identified any section that remains unclear, so we can resolve the issue before the deadline.

    Why this works: As soon as means immediately when, not equal speed.

    45

    Students may choose any research topic they find meaningful as long as the topic can be investigated with reliable evidence and completed within the available time.

    Why this works: As long as introduces a condition.

    46

    As far as the available records show, the town had never experienced a flood of comparable size before the storm that destroyed several bridges.

    Why this works: As far as marks the limit of the speaker’s evidence.

    47

    The revised course develops students’ ability to evaluate evidence as well as their ability to present a clear argument under strict time limits.

    Why this works: As well as adds a second ability; it is not an equality comparison here.

    48

    As much as I appreciate the convenience of remote work, I do not think organizations should ignore the isolation that some employees experience when they rarely meet colleagues in person.

    Why this works: At the beginning of the sentence, as much as introduces a concession similar to although.

    49

    Once the safety inspection has been completed and the final permit has been signed, the new community center will be as good as ready to open.

    Why this works: As good as means practically or virtually.

    Extreme forms

    50

    For a beginning student who has never studied statistics, interpreting several conflicting data sets under a ten-minute deadline is as difficult as it gets.

    Why this works: As … as it gets places the quality at an extreme maximum.

    51

    Although everyone else became visibly anxious when the presentation equipment stopped working, Sara remained as calm as can be and continued with a clear verbal explanation.

    Why this works: This emphatic spoken form means extremely calm.

    Reasonable ideals

    52

    The revised guide is as practical and easy to navigate as a busy TOEFL student could wish, yet it still provides enough detail for careful independent study.

    Why this works: As … as someone could wish says that the result reaches a desirable realistic maximum.

    Ellipsis and substitution

    53

    The debate over artificial intelligence is as much a discussion about human responsibility as one about technological ability, since even the most powerful tools cannot determine the values, laws, and social priorities that guide their use.

    Why this works: One replaces the second occurrence of the singular countable noun discussion while preserving its new about-phrase.

    54

    Although the current regulations address newer forms of online fraud, they are as difficult for small businesses to interpret as the earlier ones, particularly when legal guidance is available only in highly technical language.

    Why this works: Ones replaces the plural countable noun regulations.

    55

    For older adults who live alone, the long-term effect of social isolation may be as damaging as that of poor nutrition, even though emotional and cognitive decline can be harder to observe than physical weight loss.

    Why this works: Formal that of replaces the effect of and prevents an awkward repetition.

    56

    Despite the pilot study’s smaller sample, its main findings were as consistent as those of several national investigations conducted under more controlled conditions, which gave the researchers confidence that the pattern was not accidental.

    Why this works: Those replaces the plural noun findings and carries a long identifying phrase.

    57

    The opposing team’s evidence was as carefully selected as ours, but our interpretation accounted more fully for the exceptions that appeared in the final data set.

    Why this works: The possessive form ours stands for our evidence.

    58

    A responsible editor checks numerical claims as carefully as a researcher does, because a single misplaced decimal point can completely alter the conclusion readers draw from an otherwise accurate report.

    Why this works: Does replaces the repeated action checks numerical claims.

    59

    Even after studying the manual several times, most new employees cannot explain the emergency procedure as clearly as an experienced technician can, so supervised practice remains essential.

    Why this works: The modal can retains the meaning of ability while the repeated verb phrase disappears.

    60

    The nationwide reform may cost as much as independent analysts warned it would, especially if local governments must replace old equipment before they can introduce the new reporting system.

    Why this works: Would carries the earlier future prediction and replaces would cost.

    61

    By the end of the semester, students who attended the optional workshops had improved as much as those who had received weekly individual feedback had, suggesting that structured peer practice can sometimes provide comparable benefits.

    Why this works: The final had replaces had improved, while those also replaces students.

    62

    First-year students are often as concerned about finding affordable housing as about selecting courses that will satisfy complicated degree requirements, yet universities tend to provide much more guidance on the academic decision.

    Why this works: The second side omits they are concerned; two matching about + -ing expressions remain.

    63

    The revised treatment proved as effective as researchers had predicted it would be in reducing short-term symptoms and, in the final report, was therefore described more compactly as as effective as predicted.

    Why this works: The sentence displays both the full helper-verb version and the conventional reduced academic ending.

    64

    After engineers strengthened the casing and protected the internal sensors from moisture, the monitoring equipment operated as reliably in remote field conditions as in the laboratory, even during several days of unusually heavy rain.

    Why this works: The second place phrase omits the predictable words the equipment operated reliably.

    Conditional comparisons

    65

    If the university can guarantee stable internet access and provide every participant with a working microphone, remote students should be able to contribute as actively as those attending in person, even though the two groups will interact through different physical settings.

    Why this works: The entire equality comparison describes the result of a real and achievable condition.

    66

    The revised schedule is not as disruptive as it would be if every examination were moved into the same week, because the current plan spreads the additional workload across three separate periods.

    Why this works: A real present arrangement is compared with a hypothetical present alternative.

    67

    The low-cost training program could be just as effective if it were delivered through weekly online workshops as if it were taught in an intensive classroom course, provided that participants completed the same guided practice.

    Why this works: Two hypothetical delivery conditions are compared directly and kept parallel.

    68

    Although the researchers had access to only a modest sample, their estimate proved almost as accurate as it would have been if they had surveyed the entire population, largely because the smaller group had been selected with unusual care.

    Why this works: An actual past result is compared with an unreal past alternative.

    69

    Because the department postponed staff training until after the new system had already launched, employees are not as confident using the software now as they would be if the preparation had begun several months earlier.

    Why this works: A past condition is linked to a present result in a mixed-time comparison.

    70

    The final presentation was not as persuasive as it might have been if the speaker were generally more comfortable responding to unexpected objections, even though the prepared sections were clear and well organized.

    Why this works: A continuing present characteristic is used to imagine a different past result.

    71

    The review process would have been as fair if the applicants’ names had been removed as if a second independent committee had evaluated every file, since either safeguard would have reduced the influence of personal familiarity.

    Why this works: Two unreal past conditions share an equal imagined result.

    72

    Leila answered the unfamiliar question as calmly as if she had rehearsed that exact topic for days, pausing only once before presenting a coherent example and a carefully qualified conclusion.

    Why this works: The as if-part supplies an imagined standard for her calm manner.

    73

    The inexpensive sensor performed almost as reliably as if the research team had purchased the premium model, although the compressed comparison would be clearer in very formal writing as almost as reliably as it would have if ….

    Why this works: The sentence demonstrates a recoverable but highly compressed conditional comparison.

    74

    Without the emergency grant, the outreach program could never have become as geographically inclusive as it eventually did, because nearly half of its travel budget came from that single source.

    Why this works: Without introduces an unreal condition, while did substitutes for the repeated result.

    75

    The first technical failure was not as damaging as it otherwise might have been, since the automatic backup preserved every file that had been uploaded before the network went offline.

    Why this works: Otherwise carries the unstated condition “if the backup had not worked.”

    76

    The revised assessment can remain as rigorous as the original one, provided that teachers use the same scoring criteria and review a representative sample of recorded responses before final grades are assigned.

    Why this works: A condition introduced by provided that controls the equality claim.

    Appendix

    A plain-English grammar toolbox

    You do not need to memorize these labels. They simply give us short names for parts of a sentence. Throughout the guide, linked grammar terms are highlighted; select one to jump directly to its explanation here.

    Noun
    A noun names a person, place, thing, group, event, or idea: student, city, method, audience, decision, education. In as serious a problem as, problem is the noun.
    Singular countable noun
    One thing that can be counted and can normally follow a or an: a problem, an audience, a study. The special order as serious a problem as normally needs this kind of noun.
    Plural countable noun
    Two or more separate countable things: students, questions, sources. Use many or few: as many students as, as few errors as.
    Countable noun
    A noun whose individual units can be counted: one source, three sources. Singular countable nouns often use a/an; plural ones can use many/few.
    Uncountable noun
    A noun normally treated as a mass rather than separate units: information, evidence, money, time, water. Use much or little, not many or few.
    Adjective
    A word that describes a person or thing: useful, expensive, difficult, serious. It usually follows the first as in a quality comparison: as useful as.
    Adjective phrase
    An adjective together with words that complete or modify it: very useful for beginners, difficult to explain, concerned about cost. The whole phrase can sit between the two as markers.
    Adverb
    A word that commonly describes how an action happens: quickly, clearly, carefully. In She answered as clearly as she could, clearly describes the answering.
    Adverb phrase
    An adverb together with its modifiers or a coordinated partner: remarkably quickly, clearly and directly. It gives a fuller description of how an action is performed.
    Modifier
    A word or group that changes another word’s strength, viewpoint, or exact meaning. Almost changes equality in almost as useful as; potentially changes serious; scientifically explains the kind of reliability in scientifically reliable.
    Quantifier
    A word that expresses number or amount, such as many, few, much, little. Choose it according to the noun: many/few students but much/little information.
    Preposition or connecting word
    A short word such as about, for, in, to, of, with that links a description to extra information: concerned about cost, useful for beginners, capable of leading.
    Complement or necessary extra information
    Words that complete a meaning that would otherwise feel unfinished. In concerned about tuition, about tuition completes concerned. In easy to use, to use completes easy.
    To + verb form
    A form such as to use, to explain, to complete. It often names an action after an adjective: easy to use, difficult to explain, safe to enter.
    Subject
    The person or thing that the sentence is about and that normally performs or experiences the action: students in Students are concerned; the system in The system works.
    Verb
    A word expressing an action, event, or state: work, improve, become, is, can. A complete idea normally contains a subject and a verb.
    Clause, or “complete idea” in this guide
    A group of words built around a subject and a verb: I am, she can, experts predicted, we had expected it to be. It may be a whole sentence or one part of a longer sentence.
    Complete idea
    The student-friendly name used here for a clause. After the second as, it makes the comparison exact: He is as tall as I am; It was as difficult as we expected.
    Pronoun
    A short word that replaces a noun: I, me, she, her, they, them, it. After the second as, conversation often uses me/him/her, while formal writing may prefer a complete version such as as I am.
    Helper verb
    A short verb such as do, can, will, would, have that supports another verb or prevents repetition. She works as hard as I do means as I work; as we hoped it would avoids repeating the main verb.
    Participle used as an adjective
    An -ed or -ing form that describes someone or something: widely accepted, severely affected, convincing, surprising. In these uses, treat the whole expression like an adjective.
    Reduced ending
    A shorter comparison ending in which obvious repeated words disappear. As expected may stand for as we had expected it to be; as originally estimated may stand for a much longer passive idea.
    Comparison point
    The person, thing, number, limit, or idea introduced by the second as: the old method, I am, possible, time allows, or the evidence supports.
    Ellipsis
    Leaving out words that the reader or listener can recover easily. In Students are as concerned about housing as about tuition, the second side leaves out they are concerned. Ellipsis makes a sentence shorter, but it should be used only when the missing meaning remains clear.
    Substitution
    Replacing repeated words with a shorter form instead of simply deleting them. English uses forms such as one/ones, that/those, mine/ours, and helper verbs: The new plan is as practical as the old one; She works as carefully as I do.
    One and ones
    Replacement words for countable nouns. One replaces one countable thing; ones replaces plural things: the original proposal → the original one; the earlier problems → the earlier ones. They can carry a new description: one about technology.
    That and those as replacements
    Formal replacements used especially in academic comparisons. That replaces a singular or uncountable noun phrase, while those replaces a plural one: the impact of X → that of X; the results of X → those of X.
    Possessive replacement
    A form that expresses ownership without repeating the owned noun: mine, yours, his, hers, ours, theirs, or a noun ending in ’s. Her evidence is as strong as ours means as strong as our evidence.
    Conditional
    A structure in which one situation depends on another. It often uses if, but it can also use expressions such as unless, provided that, as long as, without, or otherwise: If the price falls, more people will buy it.
    Real or open condition
    A condition presented as a real possibility, a repeated fact, or something whose result is still genuinely open. It normally uses an ordinary present form after if: If students receive feedback, they improve.
    Hypothetical present or future condition
    An imagined or less-real situation now or later. English commonly uses a past form after if and would, could, or might in the result: If the course were cheaper, more students would join.
    Unreal past condition
    An imagined change to a past event that did not happen. It normally uses if + had + past participle and an imagined result such as would have changed: If we had acted earlier, the result would have been better.
    Mixed-time conditional
    A conditional whose condition and result belong to different times. A past event may explain a present result, or a continuing present characteristic may be used to imagine a different past result.
    Otherwise
    A word that can carry an unstated condition. In The grant made the study larger than it otherwise would have been, otherwise means “without the grant” or “if the grant had not existed.”
    Final reminder

    The central question is always simple: What exact quality, manner, amount, or label am I comparing—and what is the comparison point or limit? Once those parts are clear, even a long as … as sentence becomes manageable.

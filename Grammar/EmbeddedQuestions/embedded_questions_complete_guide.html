Embedded Questions: A Complete Guide

Learn how English places questions inside larger sentences, why the word order changes, how whether and if work, and how to use these structures accurately in TOEFL speaking and writing.

Chapter 01

The central idea

An embedded question is a question placed inside a larger sentence. The most important first step is recognizing where the larger sentence ends and where the question inside it begins.

Before you begin

This guide occasionally uses terms such as clause, main clause, and subordinate clause. They are explained when they first appear. You do not need to become an expert in grammatical terminology. The terms simply help us point to different parts of a sentence clearly.

1.1 What is an embedded question?

Direct question

Where does Lina live?

This question stands by itself and directly asks for information.

Embedded question

I wonder where Lina lives.

The question Where does Lina live? has been placed inside a larger sentence.

A clause is a group of words organized around a subject and a verb. In I wonder where Lina lives, there are two clauses:

Sentence map

I wonder = the main clause

where Lina lives = the embedded question

The main clause carries the main structure of the complete sentence. The embedded question is a subordinate clause: it is placed inside or attached to the larger structure and normally does not stand alone in that form.

1.2 Three common names

The below 3 names are all used to describe embedded questions. You just have to familirize yourself so that you can recognize them in other texts.

Embedded interrogative clause

A precise grammatical name. Interrogative means “related to a question.”

Subordinate interrogative clause

This emphasizes that the question-like clause is subordinate to a larger clause.

Indirect question

A broad, student-friendly classroom term. It often refers to the same structure.

What matters

All three labels point toward the practical idea of a question contained inside a larger sentence.

Key point

The terminology is less important than the structure. Focus on recognizing the embedded question and giving it the correct word order.

1.3 Statements and larger direct questions

An embedded question can appear inside a statement:

I do not know where the office is.

She explained why the schedule had changed.

We need to determine whether the method is reliable.

It can also appear inside a larger direct question:

Do you know where the office is?

Could you explain why the schedule changed?

Can you tell me whether the method is reliable?

In the second group, the complete sentence is a question because expressions such as Do you know...? and Could you explain...? directly ask the listener for something. However, the question inside the sentence still uses embedded-question word order.

1.4 The two basic types

Wh-embedded questions

These begin with a question word or phrase:

whowhatwhichwherewhenwhyhowhow long

Example: I wonder why she left.

Yes/no embedded questions

These usually begin with whether or if.

Example: I wonder whether she left.

The missing answer is basically yes, no, or one of two alternatives.

Chapter 02

The central word-order rule

Direct questions often use special question word order. Embedded questions normally use the same order as an ordinary statement: subject before verb. Read on to see the details.

Remember

Direct question order → embedded statement order

Do not keep direct-question inversion inside the embedded question.

2.1 Direct versus embedded order

Direct questionEmbedded question
Where does Maya live?I wonder where Maya lives.
Why did the class end early?Do you know why the class ended early?
Where is the office?Could you tell me where the office is?
What should we bring?Please explain what we should bring.

The embedded question keeps its question word, but the subject normally comes before the verb or auxiliary:

question word + subject + verb

Common error

I wonder where does Maya live.

I wonder where Maya lives.

Where does Maya live? is direct-question order. After I wonder, use subject-before-verb order.

2.2 Removing do, does, and did

In many direct questions, do, does, or did is added only to form the question. When the question becomes embedded, this helper disappears, and the main verb carries the correct tense.

Present simple

Where does he work?

I know where he works.

Past simple

Why did they leave?

I wonder why they left.

What do the students need? → The teacher explained what the students need.

When does registration begin? → Can you tell me when registration begins?

How did the accident happen? → Investigators are trying to determine how the accident happened.

Do not simply move the helper

I wonder where he does work.

I wonder where he works.

He does work can express special emphasis, but it is not the normal embedded-question pattern.

2.3 Questions with be

A direct question with be usually places be before the subject. In an embedded question, the subject comes first.

Where is the station? → Do you know where the station is?

Why was the door locked? → I wonder why the door was locked.

How serious are the delays? → Could you explain how serious the delays are?

2.4 Modal, perfect, and continuous forms

Modal auxiliaries include can, could, may, might, should, will, would, and must. In an embedded question, the modal follows the subject. The same rule applies to have/has/had and continuous forms.

What should we do? → I do not know what we should do.

When can she arrive? → Please tell me when she can arrive.

Why has the price increased? → Economists are studying why the price has increased.

What was the student doing? → The professor asked what the student was doing.

2.5 When the question word is the subject

Some direct questions already have subject-before-verb order because the question word itself is the subject.

Question word as subject

Who called Sara?

Who is the unknown caller and therefore the subject of called.

Question word as object

Who did Sara call?

Sara is the subject. Who is the unknown person receiving the call.

Who called Sara? → I wonder who called Sara.

Who did Sara call? → I wonder who Sara called.

What happened? → Please explain what happened.

Which student won? → Do you know which student won?

Useful test

Ask: “Who or what performs the verb?” In Who called Sara?, the answer is who, so who is the subject. No extra do is needed.

2.6 Complex question phrases

See some more examples of phrases that become embedded questions.

how many studentshow much timehow longhow oftenwhat kind of evidencewhich of the two optionswhose responsibilityat what stage

How many students have registered? → Do you know how many students have registered?

How much time do we have? → Please tell me how much time we have.

Which of the two options is cheaper? → We need to determine which of the two options is cheaper.

What kind of evidence did they collect? → The article explains what kind of evidence they collected.

How long will it take to get from Toronto to New York?? → Check the map to see how long it will take to get from Toronto to New York.

At what stage should the dancers enter the stage? → They asked the directory at what stage the dancers should enter the stage.

Whose responsibility is it to ensure that kids cross the road safely? → The guidelines clarify whose responsibility it is to ensure that kids cross the road safely.

Which of the two options has the customer chosen? → The waiter is asking which of the two options the customer has chosen.

2.7 Punctuation follows the whole sentence

Some students think that just because a sentence contains an embedded question, they should punctuate it with a question mark. This is WRONG! An embedded question does not automatically change the containing sentence into a question. They can be inside statements too. See below:

Whole sentence: statement

I wonder where the station is.

She asked whether the class had started.

Whole sentence: direct question

Could you tell me where the station is?

Do you know whether the class has started?

Common error

I wonder where the station is?

I wonder where the station is.

I wonder... is grammatically a statement, so it normally ends with a period.

Chapter 03

Yes/no questions with whether and if

When the original direct question can be answered mainly with yes or no, use whether or if to introduce the embedded question.

3.1 The basic pattern

Is the office open? → I wonder whether the office is open.

Did Sara receive the email? → Do you know if Sara received the email?

Can the deadline be extended? → I would like to know whether the deadline can be extended.

The word-order rule does not change: after whether or if, use subject-before-verb order.

Common error

I wonder whether is the office open.

I wonder whether the office is open.

3.2 When both are possible

In many cases, it doesn't really matter if you choose "if" or "whether" to introduce the embedded question. These are some examples:

I do not know whether she is coming.

I do not know if she is coming.

Please check whether the file was uploaded.

Please check if the file was uploaded.

Whether is often slightly more formal and makes the “two possibilities” meaning especially clear. It is therefore common in academic and professional writing.

3.3 When whether is required or strongly preferred

Before an infinitive

We must decide whether to continue.

We must decide if to continue.

Immediately before or not

I do not know whether or not the office is open.

I do not know if or not the office is open.

When or not comes at the end, if is often possible: I do not know if the office is open or not.

After a preposition

Remember that prepositions include words like about, for, in, at, etc.

They argued about whether the policy was fair.

They argued about if the policy was fair.

When the embedded question is the subject

Remember clauses? Well embedded questions are clauses, so they can function as the subjects of sentences. In such cases, we can only use whether.

Whether the plan will succeed remains uncertain.

If the plan will succeed remains uncertain.

With explicit alternatives

If you want to present two options and mention both directly, it's best to use whether.

We need to determine whether the change is temporary or permanent.

The committee discussed whether to approve or reject the proposal.

Safe TOEFL strategy

When you are unsure, whether is usually the safer choice in formal TOEFL writing. It works in all the special positions above.

3.4 Embedded-question if versus conditional if

Don't confuse these two! Sometimes if is used to create a conditional. While both use if, conditionals and embedded questions are two separate structures in grammar.

Embedded question

I do not know if the office is open.

I do not know the answer to “Is the office open?”

Conditional

If the office is open, I will go inside.

Going inside depends on the office being open.

Chapter 04

Where embedded questions can appear

Embedded questions can perform several jobs inside a sentence. You do not need to memorize every technical label, but recognizing the patterns will help you read and write longer sentences accurately.

4.1 After a verb

I wonder where she went.

We need to determine whether the method is reliable.

The professor explained why the theory had failed.

Please tell me when the office closes.

Grammar books often call the embedded question a complement of the verb. A complement is simply a word or group of words that completes the meaning required by another word. You do not need this label to form the sentence correctly.

4.2 After an adjective

I am unsure whether the information is accurate.

It is unclear why the system failed.

We are not certain how long the repairs will take.

4.3 After a noun or preposition

The question is whether the plan will work.

There is some uncertainty about how the data should be interpreted.

Researchers debated the issue of whether the species was native.

Success depends on how the plan is implemented.

4.4 As the subject of the sentence

Why the population declined remains unknown.

Whether the treatment is safe has not been established.

How the structure was built continues to puzzle researchers.

These forms are common in formal and academic English. Conversation often uses introductory it: It has not been established whether the treatment is safe.

4.5 After be: identifying the main issue

The main question is whether the policy will work.

The problem is how the project should be funded.

What we need to discover is why the temperature changed.

4.6 Common introductory expressions

These can go before embedded questions. Familiarize yourself with them. Note that this is a limited list and there are many other phrases that can be followed by embedded questions.

Polite requests

  • Could you tell me...?
  • Would you mind explaining...?
  • Do you happen to know...?
  • Could you let me know...?

Uncertainty

  • I wonder...
  • I am not sure...
  • It is unclear...
  • Nobody knows...

Investigation

  • find out
  • determine
  • investigate
  • examine

Knowledge and explanation

  • know
  • remember
  • understand
  • explain
Verb pattern warning

She asked to me where I lived.

She asked me where I lived.

She explained to me why the system had failed.

Use ask someone, but explain something to someone.

Chapter 05

Reported questions

A reported question tells the reader what someone asked without repeating the exact words as a direct quotation. Reported questions are an important type of embedded question.

5.1 Direct quotation versus reported question

Direct quotation

She asked, “Where is the laboratory?”

Reported question

She asked where the laboratory was.

The reported version may involve three separate changes:

  1. Change direct-question order to embedded statement order.
  2. Decide whether a tense shift is appropriate.
  3. Change pronouns, place words, or time expressions when the viewpoint changes.

5.2 Word order in reported questions

“Where does he live?” → She asked where he lived.

“Is the office open?” → She asked whether the office was open.

“What should I bring?” → He asked what he should bring.

Do not add that

She asked me that where I lived.

She asked me where I lived.

He asked that if the office was open.

He asked whether the office was open.

5.3 Tense backshift

When the reporting verb is in the past, English often moves the tense of the original question one step back. This is called backshift.

Original questionCommon reported form
“Where is the room?”She asked where the room was.
“Why are they leaving?”She asked why they were leaving.
“When did he arrive?”She asked when he had arrived or arrived.
“Has she finished?”She asked whether she had finished.
“Will they attend?”She asked whether they would attend.
“Can he help?”She asked whether he could help.

5.4 Backshift is not always automatic

A speaker may keep the original present tense when the information is still true or is being presented as a current fact.

Backshifted

She asked where the university was.

This presents the question from the earlier past viewpoint.

Still-current fact

She asked where the university is.

This is possible when the location is still current.

The safest basic pattern is to learn normal backshift after a past reporting verb, then remember that English can keep a present tense when there is a clear reason.

5.5 Pronouns, place, and time

When reporting a question, you need to adjust the pronouns and adverbs of place and time accordingly. Look at the examples:

Mina asked Ali, “Did you submit your form?”

Mina asked Ali whether he had submitted his form.

He asked, “Will the office open tomorrow?”

He asked whether the office would open the following day.

Common changes include here → there, today → that day, tomorrow → the following day, and yesterday → the previous day. Do not change them mechanically if the original reference is still correct.

5.6 Reporting verbs and patterns

ask someone where/why/whether...

want to know where/why/whether...

wonder where/why/whether...

inquire whether/about...

Ask, tell, and say

She said me where the office was.

She told me where the office was.

She asked me where the office was.

Tell reports information. Ask reports a question or request. Say does not normally take a person directly.

Chapter 06

Embedded questions with infinitives

English can sometimes shorten an embedded question by using a question word plus to and a verb: what to do, where to go, how to begin.

6.1 The basic pattern

question word + to + base verb

I do not know what to do.

She explained how to use the machine.

We need to decide where to meet.

Please tell me who to contact.

They are considering whether to expand the program.

6.2 Relationship to a fuller clause

Fuller embedded question

I do not know what I should do.

Infinitive form

I do not know what to do.

In the shorter version, the subject is not written, but it is understood from context. In I do not know what to do, the understood person is normally I.

6.3 Common combinations

what to dowhere to gowhen to beginhow to respondwho to contactwhich option to choosewhether to continue

6.4 Use whether to, not if to

Common error

I am not sure if to apply.

I am not sure whether to apply.

6.5 Not every question can be shortened

Natural

I do not know how to solve the problem.

The understood problem-solver is clear.

Needs a full clause

I do not know why the machine stopped.

Why to stop would not express the same meaning.

Similarly, I wonder who arrived cannot normally become I wonder who to arrive. The infinitive pattern does not replace every finite embedded question.

Chapter 07

Structures that look similar

Not every clause beginning with what, where, how, or if is an embedded question. Meaning matters. This chapter gives simple tests for the most important distinctions.

7.1 Embedded questions versus that-clauses

A that-clause presents information or a statement. An embedded question contains an unknown answer, choice, reason, method, time, place, or person.

Information

I know that she left.

The clause presents the fact “she left.”

Embedded question

I know why she left.

The clause gives the answer to “Why did she leave?”

Information

The report confirms that the method is safe.

Unresolved question

The report examines whether the method is safe.

Answer test

Ask whether the clause can be understood as “the answer to the question...”. If yes, it is probably an embedded question.

7.2 Embedded questions versus relative clauses

A relative clause describes a noun. An embedded question fills an information gap.

Embedded question

I know where he works.

I know the answer to “Where does he work?”

Relative clause

I know the company where he works.

Where he works describes company.

7.3 “The thing/place that” meanings

Clauses such as what she bought sometimes refer directly to a thing rather than asking for an answer. Some grammar books call these free relatives or fused relatives. You do not need to memorize those names.

Embedded question

I wonder what she bought.

I wonder about the answer.

Thing meaning

I liked what she bought.

I liked the thing that she bought.

Embedded question

I know where she lives.

I know the location.

Place meaning

I visited where she lives.

I visited the place where she lives.

7.4 Conditional if

I wonder if the class is canceled. → embedded question

If the class is canceled, I will stay home. → conditional

7.5 Exclamative meanings

Some clauses express surprise or degree rather than a real information question.

It is amazing how quickly she recovered.

You would not believe how expensive the tickets were.

I was surprised by what a difficult task it was.

These forms still use subject-before-verb order. The important point is their meaning: they emphasize a surprising degree or quality.

Chapter 08

Advanced patterns and clarity

The same basic rules continue to work in formal, long, or nested sentences. The main challenge is seeing which words belong together.

8.1 Prepositions at the end or before the question word

When dealing with prepositions in embedded questions, you have two options. First is to put the preposition at the end of the embedded question. This is the method used in conversation. The second option is called a fronted preposition where you use the preposition at the beginning of the embedded question. This is the more formal option used in writing. See the below examples:

Do you know who she spoke to?

I wonder which department the funding came from.

Do you know to whom she spoke?

I wonder from which department the funding came.

The first style is normal in conversation. The second is more formal. After a fronted preposition, careful formal English normally uses whom rather than who.

8.2 More than one level of embedding

Layered sentence

Could you tell me why she thinks the experiment failed?

Main direct question: Could you tell me X?

Embedded question: why she thinks Y

Clause inside it: the experiment failed

I wonder whether the professor knows why the results changed.

Main clause: I wonder X

First embedded question: whether the professor knows Y

Second embedded question: why the results changed

Reading strategy

Work from the outside inward. First find the main clause. Then identify the first question word. Finally, look for any additional clause inside it.

8.3 Inserted reporting phrases

Where do you think the meeting will be held?

Why do researchers believe the species disappeared?

The whole sentence is a direct question, and the inserted phrase comments on the answer. The main question word belongs logically to the clause after the inserted phrase.

Word-order warning

Where do you think will the meeting be held?

Where do you think the meeting will be held?

After do you think, use subject-before-modal order.

8.4 Avoiding ambiguity

I asked the professor when the class ended.

This could mean either “I asked what time the class ended” or “I asked something at the time the class ended.”

I asked the professor what time the class ended.

When the class ended, I asked the professor a question.

8.5 Avoiding unclear pronouns

Unclear: Sara asked Mina whether her form had been accepted.

Clear: Sara asked Mina whether Sara’s form had been accepted.

Clear: Sara asked Mina whether Mina’s form had been accepted.

8.6 Negative embedded questions

Why didn’t she attend? → I wonder why she didn’t attend.

Hasn’t the office replied? → Do you know whether the office hasn’t replied?

The committee investigated why the safety check had not been completed.

Chapter 09

Using embedded questions on the TOEFL

Embedded questions help you ask politely, report uncertainty, describe research questions, and summarize what speakers want to know.

9.1 Write an Email

Embedded questions are especially useful when requesting information politely.

Useful email patterns

Could you tell me when the replacement will arrive?

I would like to know whether I can receive a refund.

Could you clarify which documents I need to submit?

I was wondering if the deadline could be extended.

Please let me know whether any additional information is required.

Tone strategy

The polite opening does not change the order inside the embedded question. Write Could you tell me when the package will arrive?, not Could you tell me when will the package arrive?

9.2 Academic writing

It is unclear whether...Researchers investigated why...The study examined how...One important question is whether...The evidence does not explain why...The outcome depends on how...

The study examined whether regular feedback improved performance.

Researchers investigated why some participants abandoned the task.

It remains unclear how the material reached the upper atmosphere.

The success of the policy depends on how consistently it is enforced.

9.3 Speaking

The student wants to know whether the fee can be refunded.

I am not sure which option would be more practical.

The professor explains why the original theory was rejected.

I would first find out how much the program costs.

Do not force an embedded question into every answer. Use it when you genuinely need to express uncertainty, report a question, or name missing information.

9.4 Reading and listening

Embedded questions can hide the main structure of a long sentence. Find the main clause first.

What remains unclear is how the structure was built without modern machinery.

Main structure: What remains unclear is X

Embedded question identifying X: how the structure was built without modern machinery

The professor discusses why certain species migrate even when food remains available.

Main subject: The professor

Main verb: discusses

Embedded question: why certain species migrate...

Chapter 12

Final review

The five rules to keep
  1. An embedded question is a question placed inside a larger sentence.
  2. Use subject-before-verb order inside the embedded question.
  3. Remove question-forming do, does, and did; put the tense on the main verb.
  4. Use whether or if for yes/no embedded questions, but use whether in the special positions from Chapter 3.
  5. Choose punctuation according to the whole sentence.

Final self-check

  1. What is the main clause?
  2. Where does the embedded question begin?
  3. Is it a wh-question or a yes/no question?
  4. Did I use subject-before-verb order?
  5. Did I remove unnecessary do, does, or did?
  6. Does the whole sentence need a period or question mark?
  7. Does whether work better than if here?
  8. Is the meaning truly an embedded question, or is it a relative clause, conditional, or thing/place meaning?

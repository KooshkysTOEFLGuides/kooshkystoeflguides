    Introduction

    What This Part Adds

    Relative clauses do much more than begin with who, which, or that. In real English, some are shortened, some disappear partly, and some look almost identical to other kinds of clauses even though they do a different job.

    This guide focuses on the patterns that often confuse advanced learners. You will learn how to recognize them, how to use them naturally, and how to avoid making a sentence shorter at the cost of changing its meaning.

    Key point

    A shorter structure is not automatically better. The best form is the one that expresses the intended meaning clearly and sounds natural in its context.

    Chapter 01

    Reductions Beyond -ing and Past Participles

    A full relative clause contains a clause with its own verb:

    Example

    We need a room that is available this afternoon.

    English often removes that is or who are when the remaining words can describe the noun directly:

    We need a room available this afternoon.

    Teachers often call this a reduced relative clause. After the reduction, however, the remaining description may technically be only an adjective phrase or a prepositional phrase. You do not need the technical label to use the pattern correctly.

    1.1 Removing who/which/that + be Before an Adjective

    noun + who/which/that + be + adjective → noun + adjective
    Full
    The students who are interested in the workshop should register today.
    Reduced
    The students interested in the workshop should register today.
    Full
    The only rooms that are available are on the third floor.
    Reduced
    The only rooms available are on the third floor.
    Full
    Anyone who is responsible for the equipment must sign this form.
    Reduced
    Anyone responsible for the equipment must sign this form.
    Remember

    Some adjectives naturally come after the noun in these patterns: the people responsible, the information available, the students present, and something useful.

    1.2 Removing who/which/that + be Before a Place or Other Prepositional Phrase

    A prepositional phrase begins with a word such as in, on, at, near, with, or within. Such a phrase can describe a noun directly.

    noun + who/which/that + be + prepositional phrase → noun + prepositional phrase
    Full
    Are you looking for something that is within walking distance?
    Reduced
    Are you looking for something within walking distance?
    Full
    The books that are on the top shelf belong to the professor.
    Reduced
    The books on the top shelf belong to the professor.
    Full
    The students who are in the hallway are waiting for an interview.
    Reduced
    The students in the hallway are waiting for an interview.
    Full
    The woman who is with the blue backpack can help you.
    Reduced
    The woman with the blue backpack can help you.

    The same pattern works with short place words such as inside, outside, upstairs, and nearby:

    The people who are outside → the people outside

    The office that is upstairs → the office upstairs

    1.3 Infinitive Descriptions: to do, to be done, and More

    An infinitive is the form to + verb, such as to read, to contact, or to be completed. Infinitives can describe nouns in several different ways.

    A. The noun performs the action

    Maria was the first student to finish.

    ≈ Maria was the first student who finished.

    This pattern is especially common after:

    • ordinal numbers: the first, the second, the next, the last;
    • superlatives: the best, the easiest, the most useful;
    • words such as only and next.

    She was the only person to notice the mistake.

    This is the easiest route to follow.

    Who is the next speaker to present?

    B. The noun receives the action

    I need something to read.

    ≈ I need something that I can read.

    Do you have any questions to ask?

    ≈ Do you have any questions that you want to ask?

    The person who performs the action is often understood from the situation. In I need something to read, the understood reader is I.

    C. The noun follows a preposition in meaning

    The necessary preposition usually stays at the end of the infinitive phrase.

    We need a chair to sit on.

    She is looking for someone to talk to.

    This is a difficult topic to write about.

    They found a safe place to store the equipment in.

    Common error

    Do not remove a preposition that the verb needs. We sit on a chair, talk to a person, and write about a topic.

    D. Passive infinitives: the action must be done to the noun

    There are several forms to be completed.

    ≈ There are several forms that must be completed.

    The committee identified three issues to be discussed.

    We still have a number of decisions to be made.

    E. Naming the person who performs the infinitive action

    Use for + person + to + verb when the performer is not obvious or when you want to state it clearly.

    I have a document for you to sign.

    We need a quiet room for the students to work in.

    Is there anyone for me to contact?

    The professor suggested several articles for us to read.

    1.4 Negative and Perfect Participial Descriptions

    A negative participial description uses not before the participle:

    Students not wishing to participate may leave.

    Any document not submitted by Friday will receive a late penalty.

    People not familiar with the building should follow the signs.

    A perfect participle uses having + past participle. It emphasizes that one action was completed before another. This form is correct but formal and relatively uncommon.

    Applicants having completed the first stage will receive an interview invitation.

    Students having already submitted the form do not need to submit it again.

    Natural alternative

    In ordinary writing, a full relative clause is often clearer: Applicants who have completed the first stage...

    1.5 When a Reduction Sounds Unnatural or Changes the Meaning

    Reduction is not a mechanical operation that works equally well with every verb. Some reduced forms are possible but sound unnatural. Others remove an important idea such as future time, ability, obligation, or completed action.

    Natural reduction

    Students who live off campus must register.

    Students living off campus must register.

    Possible but awkward

    Students who know the answer should raise their hands.

    Students knowing the answer should raise their hands.

    Clear future meaning

    The people who will attend tomorrow must register today.

    Meaning becomes less exact

    The people attending tomorrow must register today.

    Often acceptable, but the future meaning now depends more heavily on tomorrow.

    Ability is explicit

    Students who can solve the problem should explain it.

    Different meaning

    Students solving the problem should explain it.

    This refers to students currently engaged in solving it, not everyone who has the ability.

    Decision rule

    Reduce a relative clause only when the shorter wording keeps the intended meaning and sounds natural. When in doubt, keep the full clause.

    1.6 Commas with Reduced Descriptions

    Reduced descriptions follow the same basic meaning distinction as full relative clauses.

    No commas: identifies a group

    Students living off campus must register with the housing office.

    Only the students who live off campus are being discussed.

    Commas: adds a side comment

    Mina, living off campus, had to leave early.

    We already know who Mina is; the middle phrase adds extra information.

    The documents submitted before noon have already been processed.

    The documents, submitted only yesterday, have already been processed.

    Practice 1

    Reduce the Description Where It Sounds Natural

    1. We need a hotel that is near the conference center.
    2. The students who are interested in the exchange program should attend.
    3. She was the first researcher who identified the cause.
    4. I need a quiet place where I can study.
    5. There are several safety rules that must be followed.
    6. The people who can speak Japanese should translate the document.
    7. Any applicant who is not available on Monday should contact us.
    8. The only seats that are available are at the back.
    Show answer key
    1. We need a hotel near the conference center.
    2. The students interested in the exchange program should attend.
    3. She was the first researcher to identify the cause.
    4. I need a quiet place to study / to study in. The second version emphasizes the place relationship.
    5. There are several safety rules to be followed.
    6. Keep the full clause: The people who can speak Japanese... Changing it to people speaking Japanese changes the meaning.
    7. Any applicant not available on Monday should contact us.
    8. The only seats available are at the back.
    Chapter 02

    Omission Is Not the Same as Reduction

    Sometimes English simply leaves out an object relative word. The rest of the clause remains a complete clause with its own subject and verb.

    The book that I bought was expensive.

    The book I bought was expensive.

    In both versions, I is the subject of bought. The missing word represents the object: I bought the book.

    2.1 When the Object Relative Word Can Disappear

    You may usually omit who, whom, which, or that when all of these conditions are true:

    1. The missing relative word is the object, not the subject.
    2. The relative clause identifies the noun and therefore has no commas.
    3. No preposition has been moved in front of the relative word.
    Object of verb
    The article (that) I read was useful.
    Object of verb
    The professor (whom) we invited accepted.
    Object of preposition
    The person (who) I spoke to was helpful.
    Object of preposition
    The topic (that) they argued about was controversial.

    2.2 When the Relative Word Must Remain

    It is the subject

    The student who answered received a prize.

    Not: The student answered received a prize.

    The clause has commas

    My laptop, which I bought last year, is already broken.

    Not: My laptop, I bought last year, is already broken.

    A preposition comes first

    The person to whom I spoke was helpful.

    Not: The person to I spoke...

    Stranded preposition allows omission

    The person I spoke to was helpful.

    The preposition remains at the end.

    2.3 Compare Omission and True Reduction

    Full relative
    The students who live nearby usually walk.
    Reduction
    The students living nearby usually walk.
    Full relative
    The students whom I interviewed were prepared.
    Omission
    The students I interviewed were prepared.
    Simple test

    If the shorter version still contains a subject and a normal tense-marked verb, it is probably object-pronoun omission. If the verb becomes -ing, a past participle, an adjective, a prepositional phrase, or an infinitive, it is a reduction.

    Practice 2

    Can the Bold Relative Word Be Omitted?

    1. The book that I borrowed is overdue.
    2. The book that explains the theory is overdue.
    3. My advisor, whom I contacted yesterday, replied quickly.
    4. The advisor whom I contacted yesterday replied quickly.
    5. The organization for which she works is expanding.
    6. The organization which she works for is expanding.
    Show answer key
    1. Yes: The book I borrowed...
    2. No. That is the subject of explains.
    3. No. A relative word cannot normally be omitted from a clause set off by commas.
    4. Yes: The advisor I contacted...
    5. No. The preposition comes before which.
    6. Yes: The organization she works for...
    Chapter 03

    Where, When, and Why: Choose by Meaning, Not by the Noun Alone

    A place noun does not automatically require where, and a time noun does not automatically require when. You must look at the missing relationship inside the relative clause.

    3.1 Where Versus That/Which

    Use where when the meaning is in, at, or on that place.

    Place relationship

    The city where I lived was crowded.

    I lived in the city.

    The place is the direct object

    The city that I visited was crowded.

    I visited the city.

    Common error

    Do not say the city where I visited. The verb visit directly takes the place as its object: I visited the city.

    The restaurant where we ate was quiet. — We ate at the restaurant.

    The restaurant that the critic reviewed was quiet. — The critic reviewed the restaurant.

    The room where the meeting took place was small.

    The room that we reserved was small.

    3.2 When Versus That/Which

    Use when when the meaning is at, on, or during that time.

    I remember the day when we first met. — We met on that day.

    I remember the day that changed my life. — The day itself caused the change.

    I remember the day that I described in my essay. — I described the day.

    In defining clauses, when can sometimes be replaced by that or omitted:

    The year when I graduated

    The year that I graduated

    The year I graduated

    3.3 Why, Reason, and Unnecessary Repetition

    Why usually follows the noun reason and means for that reason.

    The reason why I left was simple.

    The reason that I left was simple.

    The reason I left was simple.

    The reason for which I left was simple. Very formal.

    Avoid heavy repetition

    The reason why I left is because I was tired is common in speech, but it repeats the same cause relationship. In careful writing, prefer:

    The reason I left is that I was tired.
    I left because I was tired.

    3.4 The Special Pattern with the way

    Standard English normally uses one of these patterns:

    I like the way she explains difficult ideas.

    I like the way that she explains difficult ideas.

    I like the way in which she explains difficult ideas.

    Common error

    Avoid the way how she explains difficult ideas. Both the way and how express manner, so the combination is usually unnecessary.

    Practice 3

    Choose the Best Relative Word

    1. The laboratory ___ we conducted the experiment has been renovated.
    2. The laboratory ___ the university renovated has reopened.
    3. I remember the semester ___ I took three science courses.
    4. I remember the semester ___ changed my career plans.
    5. The reason ___ the machine stopped is still unclear.
    6. The reason ___ the technician gave was not convincing.
    7. I admired the way ___ she handled the complaint.
    Show answer key
    1. where — we conducted the experiment in the laboratory.
    2. that/which — the university renovated the laboratory.
    3. when/that, or no relative word — the action happened during that semester.
    4. that/which — the semester is the subject of changed.
    5. why/that, or no relative word.
    6. that/which, or no relative word — the technician gave the reason.
    7. No word, that, or in which. Do not use how after the way.
    Chapter 04

    Relative Clauses Versus Clauses That Explain the Content of a Noun

    Some clauses beginning with that describe a noun in the usual relative-clause way. Others tell us the content of an idea, claim, fact, belief, or possibility. The two structures may look almost identical.

    4.1 The Missing-Word Test

    In a relative clause, the noun has a missing job inside the clause. Imagine an empty space:

    The claim that she made ___ was surprising.

    She made the claim. The noun claim fills the missing object position.

    In a content clause, nothing is missing. The clause is already complete and tells us what the claim says:

    The claim that she was innocent was surprising.

    She was innocent is a complete idea. It gives the content of the claim.

    4.2 The Meaning Test

    Relative clause: identifies or describes

    The evidence that we examined was incomplete.

    We examined the evidence.

    Content clause: states the idea

    The evidence that the treatment works is strong.

    The clause tells us what the evidence supports.

    Relative clause

    The idea that he proposed was expensive.

    He proposed the idea.

    Content clause

    The idea that education should be free is controversial.

    The clause tells us what the idea is.

    4.3 Nouns Commonly Followed by Content Clauses

    These nouns frequently introduce a clause that states their content:

    Statements and beliefs

    claim, belief, argument, opinion, assumption, conclusion

    Information and evidence

    fact, news, evidence, indication, discovery, finding

    Possibility and intention

    possibility, hope, suggestion, proposal, decision, plan

    The fact that the course is free attracts many students.

    There is a possibility that the event will be cancelled.

    Her suggestion that we meet earlier was practical.

    The discovery that the material was toxic changed the project.

    Quick question

    Ask: “Does the clause describe which noun I mean, or does it tell me what the idea, claim, fact, or possibility actually is?”

    Practice 4

    Relative Clause or Content Clause?

    1. The report that the committee published was extremely detailed.
    2. The report that unemployment is falling surprised the committee.
    3. The possibility that we discussed worried the manager.
    4. The possibility that the project may fail worried the manager.
    5. The argument that the student presented was well organized.
    6. The argument that public transport should be free is difficult to defend.
    Show answer key
    1. Relative. The committee published the report.
    2. Content. Unemployment is falling states what the report says.
    3. Relative. We discussed the possibility.
    4. Content. The clause states what the possibility is.
    5. Relative. The student presented the argument.
    6. Content. The clause states the argument itself.
    Chapter 05

    Many of Whom, Several of Which, and Similar Patterns

    This useful pattern adds information about part or all of a previously named group. It is common in formal and academic English.

    noun group, + quantity/number + of whom (people) / of which (things) + clause

    The students, many of whom were tired, left early.

    Ten books were examined, three of which contained major errors.

    The proposals, none of which were practical, were rejected.

    The committee has twelve members, two of whom are students.

    5.1 Common Words Before of Whom/Which

    Quantities

    all, both, many, most, some, several, few, none

    Numbers and fractions

    two, five, half, one-third, 20 percent

    Superlative groups

    the largest of which, the most important of which

    The university offers eight language courses, two of which are available online.

    She interviewed thirty participants, half of whom were first-year students.

    The experiment produced four results, the most important of which was unexpected.

    5.2 Possession Inside the Pattern

    Use whose when the people or things in the group possess something.

    The researchers, all of whose findings were published, attended the conference.

    The buildings, several of whose windows were broken, were closed for repairs.

    Punctuation

    These structures normally add extra information about an already identified group, so they are usually separated by commas.

    5.3 Build the Sentence Step by Step

    Separate ideas: The department hired six assistants. Two of the assistants had previous experience.

    Combined: The department hired six assistants, two of whom had previous experience.

    Separate ideas: We considered five solutions. None of the solutions was affordable.

    Combined: We considered five solutions, none of which was affordable.

    Practice 5

    Combine the Sentences

    1. The class has twenty students. Eight of the students are international students.
    2. The article discusses six causes. Three of the causes are economic.
    3. She interviewed four scientists. All of the scientists supported the proposal.
    4. The company owns several buildings. Two of the buildings have solar panels.
    5. The researchers submitted their reports. Most of their reports included charts.
    Show sample answers
    1. The class has twenty students, eight of whom are international students.
    2. The article discusses six causes, three of which are economic.
    3. She interviewed four scientists, all of whom supported the proposal.
    4. The company owns several buildings, two of which have solar panels.
    5. The researchers submitted their reports, most of which included charts.
    Chapter 06

    Subject–Verb Agreement Inside Relative Clauses

    When who, which, or that is the subject of the relative clause, the verb agrees with the noun that the relative word refers to.

    Singular noun

    The student who works in the laboratory is absent.

    Plural noun

    The students who work in the laboratory are absent.

    6.1 One of the Students Who...

    Look carefully at what who refers to:

    Leila is one of the students who work in the laboratory.

    The meaning is: Leila is one member of a group of students, and those students work in the laboratory. Therefore, who refers to plural students.

    6.2 The Only One Who...

    Leila is the only one of the students who works in the laboratory.

    Here, the meaning is that only Leila works there. The clause describes the only one, so the singular verb is natural.

    Meaning controls agreement

    Do not choose the verb only by looking at the nearest word. Decide which noun the relative clause actually describes.

    6.3 Other Useful Agreement Examples

    She is one of the few employees who understand the entire system.

    This is the only device that works without electricity.

    These are the only devices that work without electricity.

    The number of students who need assistance has increased.

    A number of students who need assistance have contacted us.

    Practice 6

    Choose the Correct Verb

    1. She is one of the researchers who studies / study climate change.
    2. She is the only one of the researchers who studies / study this particular species.
    3. The course that requires / require the most reading is history.
    4. The courses that requires / require the most reading are history and biology.
    5. The number of applicants who needs / need financial aid is growing.
    Show answer key
    1. study — who refers to plural researchers.
    2. studies — the intended meaning is that she alone studies this species.
    3. requires — singular course.
    4. require — plural courses.
    5. need — the relative clause describes plural applicants; the main verb is agrees with the number.
    Chapter 07

    Free Relatives, Embedded Questions, and -ever Clauses

    A free relative contains its own noun meaning. For example, what can mean the thing that, and whoever can mean any person who.

    7.1 The Same Words Can Introduce Different Structures

    Free relative

    I ate what she cooked.

    Meaning: I ate the thing that she cooked.

    Embedded question

    I wonder what she cooked.

    Meaning: I want to know the answer to the question “What did she cook?”

    Free relative

    Take whichever book you prefer.

    Meaning: take any book that you prefer.

    Embedded question

    I do not know which book you prefer.

    The speaker does not know the answer.

    7.2 Noun-Like -ever Clauses

    Whoever arrives first should unlock the room.

    You may choose whichever topic interests you.

    I will accept whatever you decide.

    Give the form to whoever is at the front desk.

    These clauses act like nouns: they can be the subject, object, or object of a preposition.

    7.3 -ever Clauses Meaning “No Matter...”

    The same words can introduce a side clause meaning no matter who, what, where, when, or how.

    Whatever you choose, I will support you.

    Whoever calls, do not share the password.

    Wherever you study, try to remove distractions.

    However difficult the task becomes, do not give up.

    Noun-like

    Whatever you choose will be acceptable.

    The clause is the subject of will be.

    “No matter” meaning

    Whatever you choose, I will support you.

    The main message is I will support you.

    7.4 A Simple Meaning Test

    • If you can replace the clause with the thing/person/place that..., it is probably a free relative.
    • If the main verb expresses knowing, asking, wondering, discovering, or deciding, the clause may be an embedded question.
    • If the clause means no matter... and the main sentence can stand without it, it is a concessive -ever clause.
    Practice 7

    Identify the Meaning

    1. I cannot remember what the professor recommended.
    2. I bought what the professor recommended.
    3. Whoever finishes early may leave.
    4. Whoever finishes first, the team will celebrate together.
    5. Please tell me which route is shorter.
    6. Choose whichever route is shorter.
    Show answer key
    1. Embedded question. The speaker cannot remember the answer.
    2. Free relative. It means I bought the thing that the professor recommended.
    3. Noun-like free relative. The clause is the subject.
    4. “No matter who” clause. The celebration will happen regardless of the winner.
    5. Embedded question. The speaker asks for information.
    6. Free relative. It means choose any route that is shorter.
    Chapter 08

    Keep the Reference Clear

    A relative clause should normally appear close to the noun it describes. When it is placed too far away, readers may connect it to the wrong noun.

    8.1 Misplaced Relative Clauses

    Ambiguous

    I discussed the proposal with the professor that had been rejected.

    Grammatically, the clause appears to describe the professor.

    Clear

    I discussed the proposal that had been rejected with the professor.

    Unclear

    She gave the report to the manager which contained several errors.

    Clear

    She gave the report, which contained several errors, to the manager.

    8.2 Make Whole-Situation Which Clear

    A comma + which clause can refer to an entire previous event:

    The university cancelled the exam, which surprised the students.

    Here, which refers to the cancellation, not simply to the noun exam. This is useful, but the previous statement must be clear enough to create only one reasonable interpretation.

    Potentially unclear

    Ali criticized Reza during the meeting, which upset the manager.

    Did the criticism, Reza, or the meeting upset the manager?

    Clearer rewrites

    Ali’s criticism of Reza during the meeting upset the manager.

    Ali criticized Reza during the meeting. This public criticism upset the manager.

    8.3 Do Not Overload a Sentence with Nested Clauses

    A relative clause can appear inside another relative clause, but too much nesting makes the reader hold several unfinished ideas in memory.

    Heavy

    The policy that the committee that the university created proposed was controversial.

    Clearer

    The university created a committee that proposed a controversial policy.

    Heavy

    The book that the professor who won the award wrote became famous.

    Clearer

    The professor who won the award wrote a book that later became famous.

    Writing strategy

    Nested clauses are useful to understand while reading. In your own writing, use them only when the sentence remains easy to follow. Clarity is more valuable than visible complexity.

    Practice 8

    Rewrite for Clear Reference

    1. I showed the photograph to my teacher that had been damaged.
    2. The company changed its schedule, which annoyed the customers, after several complaints.
    3. The article that the researcher who joined our team last month published attracted attention.
    4. The assistant placed the files beside the computer which were confidential.
    Show sample answers
    1. I showed the photograph that had been damaged to my teacher.
    2. After several complaints, the company changed its schedule, a decision that annoyed the customers.
    3. The researcher who joined our team last month published an article that attracted attention.
    4. The assistant placed the confidential files beside the computer. / The assistant placed the files, which were confidential, beside the computer.
    Chapter 09

    A Helpful Note About That

    Traditional grammar often calls that a relative pronoun. Some modern grammar books describe it mainly as a connector that introduces the relative clause. You do not need to choose one analysis for ordinary English study.

    What matters is that that behaves differently from who, whom, and which.

    No fronted preposition
    the method by which we measured it — not by that
    No comma clause
    My phone, which I bought last year, is broken — not , that I bought...
    No possessive form
    the student whose laptop was stolen — there is no possessive form of that
    Often omissible as object
    the book that I bought → the book I bought
    Practical conclusion

    Learn the environments in which that is possible. The label used by a particular grammar book is less important than using the word correctly.

    Comprehensive Practice

    Exercise A

    Choose the Most Natural Form

    1. We need a hotel (that is near / near) the train station.
    2. The students (who can use / using) the statistical software should help the group.
    3. She was the last person (who left / to leave) the building.
    4. I need someone (who I can talk to / to talk to).
    5. The files (that were stored / stored) in this folder are confidential.
    6. The people (who know / knowing) the password should not share it.
    7. There are three questions (that must be answered / to be answered).
    8. The only room (that is available / available) has no windows.
    Show answers
    1. near is concise and natural.
    2. who can use preserves the meaning of ability.
    3. Both are correct; to leave is especially natural after the last person.
    4. Both are correct; to talk to is more compact.
    5. Both are correct and natural.
    6. who know is more natural.
    7. Both are possible; to be answered is compact and formal.
    8. available is natural.
    Exercise B

    Correct the Error

    1. The museum where we visited was extremely crowded.
    2. My laptop, I bought last year, has stopped working.
    3. The person to I sent the message never replied.
    4. I admire the way how she explains grammar.
    5. The reason why the event was cancelled is because the speaker was ill.
    6. Students can speak French should report to the front desk.
    7. We need a table to put the equipment.
    8. The two buildings, one of who was damaged, are closed.
    Show answers
    1. The museum that/which we visited was extremely crowded.
    2. My laptop, which I bought last year, has stopped working.
    3. The person to whom I sent the message never replied. / The person I sent the message to never replied.
    4. I admire the way she explains grammar. Other possibilities: the way that... or the way in which...
    5. The reason the event was cancelled is that the speaker was ill. / The event was cancelled because the speaker was ill.
    6. Students who can speak French should report to the front desk.
    7. We need a table to put the equipment on.
    8. The two buildings, one of which was damaged, are closed.

    Identify the Clause Type

    1. The claim that the newspaper printed was false.
    2. The claim that the mayor had resigned was false.
    3. I cannot decide which course I should take.
    4. I will take whichever course fits my schedule.
    5. Whatever happens, remain calm.
    6. What she suggested solved the problem.
    7. The office where we submitted the form is closed.
    8. I wonder where we should submit the form.
    Show answers
    1. Relative clause. The newspaper printed the claim.
    2. Content clause. The clause states the claim.
    3. Embedded question.
    4. Free relative.
    5. “No matter” clause.
    6. Free relative. It means the thing that she suggested.
    7. Relative clause.
    8. Embedded question.
    Exercise D

    Combine or Rewrite

    1. The program accepted fifty applicants. Twelve of the applicants received scholarships.
    2. The laboratory owns six microscopes. Two of the microscopes are currently broken.
    3. She is one of the teachers. The teachers use project-based learning.
    4. She is the only one of the teachers. She uses project-based learning.
    5. I sent the document to the assistant that needed a signature. Rewrite clearly.
    6. The committee that the department that hired a new director created met yesterday. Rewrite naturally.
    Show sample answers
    1. The program accepted fifty applicants, twelve of whom received scholarships.
    2. The laboratory owns six microscopes, two of which are currently broken.
    3. She is one of the teachers who use project-based learning.
    4. She is the only one of the teachers who uses project-based learning.
    5. I sent the document that needed a signature to the assistant.
    6. The department hired a new director and created a committee that met yesterday. Another possible meaning: The department that hired a new director created a committee, and the committee met yesterday.
    Final Review

    A Compact Decision Guide

    Want a shorter adjective or place description?
    Remove who/which/that + be: students interested, books on the desk.
    Want an infinitive?
    Check who performs the action and whether a preposition is needed: someone to call, a chair to sit on, forms to be completed.
    Want to omit the relative word?
    It must be an object in a defining clause, with no fronted preposition.
    Choosing where/when/why?
    Look for a missing place, time, or reason relationship inside the clause.
    Clause after claim, fact, or idea?
    Ask whether it describes the noun or states its content.
    Using many of whom/which?
    Use whom for people and which for things; commas are normally needed.
    Choosing singular or plural verb?
    Find the noun that who/which/that actually refers to.
    Sentence feels hard to follow?
    Move the relative clause closer to its noun or divide an overloaded sentence.
    Final principle

    Advanced grammar is not the art of making every sentence complicated. It is the ability to choose from several structures and use the one that expresses your meaning most clearly.

Advanced Relative Clauses

Part of Kooshky's TOEFL Guides. Collected and compiled by Amir Kooshky.

This workbook helps you use advanced relative structures naturally in TOEFL writing and speaking: formal preposition structures, reduced clauses, free relatives, of which, stacked clauses, quantifier clauses, and to-infinitive relatives.

Contents

Overview

What this workbook trains

These exercises are for students who already know basic relative clauses and now need better control, compactness, and style. The goal is not to sound complicated. The goal is to choose advanced structures only when they make your meaning clearer, tighter, or more academic.

Writing focus: compact academic sentences, formal tone, precise group claims, and clean error repair.
Speaking focus: natural choices, not stiff forms like the chair on which I sat in ordinary answers.

Exercise 1 · Formal vs Natural Transformation

Advanced relative clauses often give you two correct choices: a natural spoken form and a more formal academic form. Your job is to control the tone, not just the grammar.

Part A · Natural → Formal

Rewrite each sentence in a more formal academic style by moving the preposition before the relative pronoun. Use which for things and whom for people.

  1. The theory that the argument is based on is not convincing.
  2. The professor who I sent my draft to replied within an hour.
  3. The method that the researchers relied on produced inconsistent results.
  4. The issue that the lecture focused on was urban pollution.
  5. The committee that the proposal was submitted to rejected it.
  6. The principle that the policy depends on is fairness.
  7. The author who the article refers to is known for her work on migration.
  8. The economic model that the discussion started with is now outdated.
Answer Key · Part A
  1. The theory on which the argument is based is not convincing.
  2. The professor to whom I sent my draft replied within an hour.
  3. The method on which the researchers relied produced inconsistent results.
  4. The issue on which the lecture focused was urban pollution.
  5. The committee to which the proposal was submitted rejected it.
  6. The principle on which the policy depends is fairness.
  7. The author to whom the article refers is known for her work on migration.
  8. The economic model with which the discussion started is now outdated.

Note: These are formal. In speaking, the original versions are often more natural.

Part B · Formal → Natural

Rewrite each sentence in a more natural style. This is useful for TOEFL speaking and for writing that should not sound stiff.

  1. The university at which I studied has a strong engineering program.
  2. The advisor to whom I spoke suggested a different course.
  3. The topic about which we argued was not important.

  6. The research to which the professor referred was published recently.
  7. The condition under which the experiment succeeded was very specific.

Part C · Where/When vs In/At Which

Rewrite each sentence twice if possible: once with where/when and once with preposition + which.

  1. The lab is the place. We conducted the experiment there.
  2. 2019 was the year. The policy changed then.
  3. The lecture hall was the room. The debate took place there.
  4. The period was difficult. Many families moved to cities during that period.
  5. The website is the platform. Students submit their assignments there.
  6. The stage was the point. The project became too expensive at that stage.
Answer Key · Part C
  1. The lab is the place where we conducted the experiment. / The lab is the place in which we conducted the experiment.
  2. 2019 was the year when the policy changed. / 2019 was the year in which the policy changed.
  3. The lecture hall was the room where the debate took place. / The lecture hall was the room in which the debate took place.
  4. The period when many families moved to cities was difficult. / The period during which many families moved to cities was difficult.
  5. The website is the platform where students submit their assignments. / The website is the platform on which students submit their assignments.
  6. The stage when the project became too expensive was difficult to manage. / The stage at which the project became too expensive was difficult to manage.

Style: where/when are usually more natural. In which / at which / during which sound more formal and precise.

Part D · Whose vs Of Which

Rewrite each sentence using the structure given. Remember: whose is natural for people; of which is formal and mainly for things, studies, systems, theories, policies, and institutions.

  1. The professor has published influential research. Use whose.
  2. The study had unexpected results. Use the results of which.
  3. The policy has serious consequences. Use the consequences of which.
  4. The student lost her laptop. Use whose.
  5. The theory has several limitations. Use the limitations of which.
  6. The organization has grown quickly. Use whose or of which, then choose the more natural one.
  7. The research paper had a weak methodology. Use the methodology of which.
  8. The company has declining revenue. Use the revenue of which.
Answer Key · Part D
  1. The professor whose research is influential has published widely.
  2. The study, the results of which were unexpected, changed the discussion.
  3. The policy, the consequences of which are serious, should be reconsidered.
  4. The student whose laptop was lost asked for an extension.
  5. The theory, the limitations of which are clear, is still widely cited.
  6. The organization, whose membership has grown quickly, needs a larger office. This is usually more natural than the membership of which.
  7. The research paper, the methodology of which was weak, received criticism.
  8. The company, the revenue of which has declined, is restructuring.

Warning: Do not use of which for people: the professor, the research of which... is wrong.

Part E · Tone Choice

Choose the best version for the situation: formal writing, ordinary writing, or speaking. More than one may be grammatically correct.

  1. Situation: TOEFL Academic Discussion. Topic: the basis of a policy.
    A. The principle on which the policy is based is fairness.
    B. The principle the policy is based on is fairness.
  2. Situation: Speaking answer about a friend.
    A. The friend with whom I went to the library helped me a lot.
    B. The friend I went to the library with helped me a lot.
  3. Situation: Formal essay about research design.
    A. The method by which the data were collected was unreliable.
    B. The method the data were collected by was unreliable.
  4. Situation: Email to a professor.
    A. The assignment to which I referred in my last email is attached.
    B. The assignment I referred to in my last email is attached.
  5. Situation: Speaking answer about a chair in a classroom.
    A. The chair on which I sat was uncomfortable.
    B. The chair I sat on was uncomfortable.
  6. Situation: Academic paragraph about a study.
    A. The study, the findings of which were surprising, received media attention.
    B. The study whose findings were surprising received media attention.
  7. Situation: Casual speaking about a city.
    A. The city in which I lived as a child was crowded.
    B. The city where I lived as a child was crowded.
  8. Situation: Academic essay about online learning.
    A. Many students who lack self-discipline struggle online.
    B. Many students, who lack self-discipline, struggle online.
Answer Key · Part E
  1. A is stronger for formal academic writing; B is also correct but less formal.
  2. B is much better for speaking. A is correct but stiff.
  3. A is cleaner and more academic.
  4. B is usually better for an email. A is correct but may sound unnecessarily formal.
  5. B is natural. A sounds comically formal for a simple chair.
  6. A is more formal and precise. B is also possible, but whose may sound less academic for a study.
  7. B is better for casual speaking.
  8. A. It means only students who lack self-discipline struggle online. B implies many students in general lack self-discipline, which changes the logic.

Exercise 2 · Reduce or Don’t Reduce

Reduced relative clauses make sentences shorter and more academic. But they only work when the grammar relationship is right. This exercise trains you to reduce accurately and reject impossible reductions.

Part A · Active Reductions

Reduce the relative clause to an -ing phrase.

  1. Students who participate in internships often gain practical experience.
  2. Researchers who study urban development often focus on transportation.
  3. People who live near busy roads may experience more noise pollution.
  4. Companies that invest in employee training often keep workers longer.
  5. Universities that offer flexible schedules attract more adult learners.
  6. Children who grow up in bilingual homes may develop strong language awareness.
  7. Scientists who examine ancient fossils can learn about past climates.
  8. Students who use feedback effectively usually improve faster.
Answer Key · Part A
  1. Students participating in internships often gain practical experience.
  2. Researchers studying urban development often focus on transportation.
  3. People living near busy roads may experience more noise pollution.
  4. Companies investing in employee training often keep workers longer.
  5. Universities offering flexible schedules attract more adult learners.
  6. Children growing up in bilingual homes may develop strong language awareness.
  7. Scientists examining ancient fossils can learn about past climates.
  8. Students using feedback effectively usually improve faster.

Logic: The noun before the phrase performs the action, so the -ing reduction works.

Part B · Passive Reductions

Reduce the relative clause by removing the relative word and be. Keep the past participle.

  1. The data that were collected during the survey were carefully analyzed.
  2. The article that was published last year challenged the traditional view.
  3. The proposal that was submitted by the student received strong support.
  4. The materials that were provided by the university were useful.
  5. The device that was developed by the engineers reduced energy use.
  6. The paintings that were discovered in the cave attracted public attention.
  7. The policy that was introduced in 2020 changed the admission process.
  8. The examples that were discussed in the lecture clarified the theory.
Answer Key · Part B
  1. The data collected during the survey were carefully analyzed.
  2. The article published last year challenged the traditional view.
  3. The proposal submitted by the student received strong support.
  4. The materials provided by the university were useful.
  5. The device developed by the engineers reduced energy use.
  6. The paintings discovered in the cave attracted public attention.
  7. The policy introduced in 2020 changed the admission process.
  8. The examples discussed in the lecture clarified the theory.

Logic: The noun receives the action, so the past participle carries passive meaning.

  3. The policy being discussed by the committee affects first-year students.
  4. The students being trained for the competition meet twice a week.
  5. The software being tested by the department has several problems.
  6. The research being carried out at the university focuses on water quality.

Logic: Being shows that the passive action is ongoing.

Part D · Do Not Reduce

These sentences contain relative clauses that should not be reduced in the simple way. Explain why.

  1. The student whom I met after class is from Brazil.
  2. The article that we read yesterday was difficult.
  3. The professor who I emailed last week responded today.
  4. The book that the library recommended is not available.
  5. The city where I lived as a child has changed dramatically.
  6. The year when I started university was stressful.
  7. The person to whom the message was addressed was absent.
  8. The issue that the speaker referred to was complex.
Answer Key · Part D
  1. Do not reduce to the student meeting after class. The student is the object of met, not the person doing the meeting.
  2. Do not reduce to the article reading yesterday. The article is the object of read.
  3. Do not reduce to the professor emailing last week. That would mean the professor emailed someone.
  4. Do not reduce to the book recommending.... The library recommended the book.
  5. Where shows place. It is already compact and should not become an -ing phrase.
  6. When shows time. It is already compact and should not become an -ing phrase.
  7. The phrase is formal and passive-like in meaning; a simple reduction would not preserve the relationship.
  8. This can become the issue the speaker referred to, but not a participle reduction like the issue referring to....

Part E · Mixed Reduction Challenge

For each item, write Reduced or Do not reduce. If it can be reduced, rewrite it.

  1. Students who complete the practice tests usually feel more confident.
  2. The practice test that I completed yesterday was harder than expected.
  3. The solution that was proposed by the lecturer was simple.
  4. The seminar that is being offered this semester focuses on public health.
  5. The professor whom we interviewed gave a detailed answer.
  6. Researchers who work with large data sets need strong statistical skills.
  7. The evidence that was presented in the article supports the claim.
  8. The museum where the paintings were displayed is now closed.
  9. The plan that the students designed was approved.
  10. The plan that was designed by the students was approved.
  11. The scientist who discovered the pattern received an award.
  12. The pattern that the scientist discovered changed the field.
Answer Key · Part E
  1. Reduced: Students completing the practice tests usually feel more confident.
  2. Do not reduce. The test is the object of completed. Natural: The practice test I completed yesterday...
  3. Reduced: The solution proposed by the lecturer was simple.
  4. Reduced: The seminar being offered this semester focuses on public health.
  5. Do not reduce. The professor is the object of interviewed.
  6. Reduced: Researchers working with large data sets need strong statistical skills.
  7. Reduced: The evidence presented in the article supports the claim.
  8. Do not reduce. Where gives place.
  9. Do not reduce with a participle. The plan is the object of designed. Natural: The plan the students designed...
  10. Reduced: The plan designed by the students was approved.
  11. Usually do not reduce. The scientist discovering the pattern received an award can sound as if the discovery was happening at the same time as receiving the award. The full version is clearer: The scientist who discovered the pattern received an award.

Exercise 3 · Choose the Best Compact Structure

Advanced grammar gives you options. This exercise trains you to choose the structure that is compact, clear, and appropriate for TOEFL.

Part A · To-Infinitive Relatives

Rewrite each sentence with a to-infinitive relative where natural.

  1. She was the first student who submitted the assignment.
  2. This is the best method that we can use for the project.
  3. There is nothing that we need to change in the introduction.
  4. He was the last person who left the laboratory.
  5. This is the most effective strategy that students can follow.
  6. The committee selected the second proposal that would be tested.
  7. Do you have anything that you want to add?

  1. She was the first student to submit the assignment.
  2. This is the best method to use for the project.
  3. There is nothing to change in the introduction.
  4. He was the last person to leave the laboratory.
  5. This is the most effective strategy to follow.
  6. The committee selected the second proposal to be tested.
  7. Do you have anything to add?
  8. The article provides several issues for researchers to examine. / The article provides several issues to examine.

Part B · Free Relative Clauses

Rewrite the underlined idea using a free relative structure: what, whoever, whomever, whatever, whichever, wherever, whenever, however.

  1. The thing that the speaker emphasized was the need for reliable evidence.
  2. Any person who finishes the form early can leave.
  3. You may choose any topic that you want.
  4. Choose any one of the two schedules that works better for you.
  5. At any place where the river slows down, sediment begins to collect.
  6. At any time when the temperature drops suddenly, the reaction slows.
  7. In any way that students organize their notes, they should review them weekly.
  8. No matter how difficult the reading is, students should identify the main claim first.
  9. The university will interview any person whom the department recommends.
  10. The result depends on the choice that the committee makes.
Answer Key · Part B
  1. What the speaker emphasized was the need for reliable evidence.
  2. Whoever finishes the form early can leave.
  3. You may choose whatever topic you want. / You may choose whatever you want.
  4. Choose whichever schedule works better for you.
  5. Wherever the river slows down, sediment begins to collect.
  6. Whenever the temperature drops suddenly, the reaction slows.
  7. However students organize their notes, they should review them weekly.
  8. However difficult the reading is, students should identify the main claim first.
  9. The university will interview whomever the department recommends. In modern English, whoever is also common, but whomever is the formal object form.
  10. The result depends on whatever choice the committee makes. If the committee is choosing from a limited set, use whichever choice.

Part C · Quantifier + Relative Clause

Rewrite each weak claim so it defines the group more precisely with a quantifier + relative clause.

  1. Many students struggle in online courses. Make the group more precise: lack time-management skills.
  2. Some books are difficult to use for research. Make the group more precise: were published many decades ago.
  3. Anyone must register. Make the group more precise: wants to attend the workshop.
  4. Few solutions are practical. Make the group more precise: were proposed in the discussion.
  5. Those argue that the policy is unfair. Make the group more precise: oppose the new rule.
  6. Most articles support the claim. Make the group more precise: were assigned for the class.
  7. Several problems delayed the project. Make the group more precise: the team identified in the first week.
  8. Everyone received the slides. Make the group more precise: attended the lecture.
Answer Key · Part C
  1. Many students who lack time-management skills struggle in online courses.
  2. Some books that were published many decades ago are difficult to use for research.
  3. Anyone who wants to attend the workshop must register.
  4. Few solutions that were proposed in the discussion are practical.
  5. Those who oppose the new rule argue that the policy is unfair.
  6. Most articles that were assigned for the class support the claim.
  7. Several problems that the team identified in the first week delayed the project.
  8. Everyone who attended the lecture received the slides.

Logic: These clauses identify a subgroup. Do not use commas.

Part D · Stacked Clause or Split Sentence?

Combine the ideas into one sentence only if the result remains readable. Otherwise, write two sentences. Use stacked relative clauses carefully.

  1. A researcher conducted a study. The researcher specializes in climate models. The study received international attention.
  2. A committee proposed a policy. The university created the committee. The policy was controversial.
  3. A student wrote a report. The student interviewed several local business owners. The report won an award.
  4. A professor recommended an article. The article summarized a theory. The theory changed modern psychology.
  5. A team developed software. The software detects errors. The errors appear in long essays.
  6. A museum displayed artifacts. Archaeologists discovered the artifacts in a coastal cave. The exhibit attracted many visitors.
  7. A lecture explained a method. The method is used by researchers who study memory. The lecture was difficult to follow.
  8. A book became popular. A journalist wrote the book. The journalist interviewed families affected by the policy.
Answer Key · Part D
  1. Good stacked version: The study that the researcher who specializes in climate models conducted received international attention.
  2. Possible but heavy: The policy that the committee that the university created proposed was controversial. Better: The university created a committee, and the policy it proposed was controversial.
  3. Good: The report that the student who interviewed several local business owners wrote won an award. Also natural: The student interviewed several local business owners, and the report she wrote won an award.
  4. Better split: The professor recommended an article that summarized a theory. That theory changed modern psychology.
  5. Good: The team developed software that detects errors that appear in long essays.
  6. Good: The museum displayed artifacts that archaeologists discovered in a coastal cave, and the exhibit attracted many visitors. Fully stacked would be possible but less smooth.
  7. Better split: The lecture explained a method used by researchers who study memory. However, the lecture was difficult to follow.
  8. Good but dense: The book that the journalist who interviewed families affected by the policy wrote became popular. A split version may be clearer in TOEFL writing.

Rule: Stacking is useful when the relationship is a clear chain. Split the sentence when the reader has to hold too many nouns in memory.

Part E · Best Compact Version

Choose or write the best compact version. Avoid both wordiness and over-compression.

  1. Wordy: The thing that surprised the researchers was the speed of the reaction.
  2. Wordy: The students who were selected by the department will receive scholarships.

Exercise 4 · Advanced Error Repair in Full TOEFL Texts

This is a high-value exercise. Each text contains realistic advanced relative-clause problems. Find the problems, fix them, and make the whole response smoother.

Part A · TOEFL Email Repair

Each email contains advanced relative-clause errors, awkward formal structures, or unclear compression. Correct the response while keeping the tone appropriate for an email.

Email 1 · Problem with a workshop

Dear Professor Allen,

I am writing about the research workshop at which I attended last Friday. The session introduced several methods by that students can organize sources for academic essays. I especially liked the part in which the instructor explained citation software, which many students are not familiar with them. However, the room in which the workshop was held in was too small, and several students who their names were on the registration list had to stand near the door. Also, the handout being printed by the department last month did not match the examples that the instructor was showing on the screen. Could you please send us the updated version, which would help us review what we learned?

Sincerely,
Daniel

Email 2 · Request about a group project

Dear Dr. Mason,

I am writing to ask about the group presentation that our team is preparing for next week. One of the articles, the main argument of which we are basing our presentation on, is difficult to understand because it uses several terms what we have not studied before. We also found two charts, the results of which seem to contradict each other, but we are not sure however we should explain this difference. The student with whom I am working with suggested that we focus only on the second chart, but I think we should mention both. Could you tell us which approach to use and whether there is anything that we should add to the conclusion?

Best regards,
Mina

Email 3 · Course registration issue

Dear Academic Office,

I am contacting you about a registration problem which happened yesterday, which prevented me from enrolling in the statistics course. The course, whose instructor is Professor Hall, is required for students which plan to apply for the research track. The system showed an error message, the reason of which was not clear, and then it removed the course from my schedule. I spoke to an advisor to who the department referred me, and she said that there may be a technical issue that is being fixed by the IT team. Since this is the last course to complete my requirement, I would appreciate any help that you can provide.

Sincerely,
Arman

Answer Key · Part A

Email 1 · Main issues

  • at which I attended is wrong because attend is transitive. Use which I attended or that I attended.
  • by that is impossible after a preposition. Use by which.
  • which many students are not familiar with them repeats the object. Remove them.
  • in which the workshop was held in repeats the preposition. Use either in which...was held or which...was held in.
  • students who their names should be students whose names.
  • handout being printed last month suggests an ongoing action last month, which is illogical. Use printed last month.

Possible corrected version:

Dear Professor Allen,

Final Reminder

Advanced grammar should serve meaning

A high-scoring TOEFL response does not need to be packed with complex structures. Use advanced relative clauses when they make your sentence more precise, more compact, or more natural. If a structure makes the sentence harder to read or harder to say, simplify it.

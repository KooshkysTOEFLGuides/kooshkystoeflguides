Chapter index

Relative clauses are everywhere in English. You use them when you say which person you mean, describe an object, add a fact about a place, or connect two ideas without repeating the same noun. This guide begins with meaning and examples. The grammar labels come later, after the pattern already feels familiar.

Chapter

An Intuitive First Look at Relative Clauses

Before learning any rules, look at what relative clauses do. In the examples below, the highlighted words give us more information about a person, thing, place, time, or situation.

The basic idea

A relative clause is a group of words that comes after a noun and helps us understand exactly who or what we are talking about.

Describing a person

Example

The woman who answered the phone was very helpful.

Which woman was helpful? The woman who answered the phone. The highlighted part tells us which woman we mean.

Example

I spoke to a student who had taken the course before.

The highlighted part gives us information about the student. It tells us that this student had previous experience with the course.

Example

The professor I emailed yesterday has replied.

We are not talking about just any professor. We mean the professor whom I emailed yesterday. In everyday English, the word whom is often left out here.

Describing a thing

Example

The laptop that I bought last month has stopped working.

The highlighted part tells us which laptop has stopped working.

Example

We need a solution that does not cost too much.

The highlighted part describes the kind of solution we need.

Example

She showed me the article which changed her opinion.

The article changed her opinion. The highlighted part tells us something important about it.

Example

This is the book you were looking for.

The highlighted part identifies the book. It means: “You were looking for this book.”

Describing a place

Example

That is the café where we first met.

The meeting happened in that café. The highlighted part tells us what happened there.

Example

The neighborhood where I grew up has changed completely.

The highlighted part means “I grew up in that neighborhood.”

Describing a time

Example

I still remember the day when I received my TOEFL score.

The highlighted part tells us what happened on that day.

Example

There was a period when I studied English for four hours a day.

The highlighted part describes that period of time.

Showing possession

Example

I met a student whose brother studies in Canada.

The brother belongs to the student. Whose connects the student to the student’s brother.

Example

They live in a house whose windows face the sea.

The windows belong to the house. Whose can be used for things as well as people.

Adding an extra comment

Sometimes the relative clause does not identify the noun. The noun is already clear, and the clause simply adds another fact.

Example

My sister, who lives in Tabriz, is visiting us next week.

We already know who my sister is. The highlighted part adds the extra information that she lives in Tabriz. Notice the commas.

Example

The meeting was cancelled, which surprised everyone.

Here, which refers to the whole situation: the cancellation of the meeting surprised everyone.

The same pattern in different contexts

Everyday English

The bus that goes downtown stops here.

The clause identifies the bus.

University English

Students who miss the deadline must contact the registrar.

The clause identifies which students must act.

Academic English

The evidence that supports this theory remains limited.

The clause describes the evidence.

Do not worry about the labels yet

At this stage, simply notice the pattern: a noun comes first, and then a group of words gives us more information about that noun. Later chapters will explain exactly how the pattern works.

Quick recognition practice

In each sentence, find the words that describe the bold noun.

  1. The man who fixed our refrigerator was very polite.
  2. I finally found the file that you sent me.
  3. The library where I usually study closes at nine.
  4. Do you remember the year when we first met?
  5. She is the student whose presentation won the competition.
  6. The exam was postponed, which gave us more time to prepare.
Show the answers
  1. who fixed our refrigerator
  2. that you sent me
  3. where I usually study
  4. when we first met
  5. whose presentation won the competition
  6. which gave us more time to prepare refers to the entire previous situation: the exam was postponed.

What to remember

  • A relative clause gives us information about a noun or, sometimes, about an entire situation.
  • It usually comes immediately after the noun it describes.
  • Common opening words include who, which, that, whose, where, when, and why.
  • Sometimes the opening word can be left out, as in the book I bought.

The Minimum Grammar You Need

You can use many relative clauses by intuition. However, to build them accurately, you need a few basic ideas: phrase, clause, subject, verb, object, preposition, and noun phrase.

Strong recommendation

This guide explains the necessary basics, so you can still understand it on its own. However, relative clauses become much easier when you already understand how clauses work. Read the separate lesson before continuing, especially if terms such as subject, verb, independent clause, and dependent clause are not completely clear to you.

Words, phrases, and clauses

A word

A single unit such as:

student, useful, study, carefully

A phrase

A unit of grammar organized around a central word, called its head. A phrase may be very short or may contain smaller phrases and even clauses.

in the library

the student near the door

Phrase example

The student near the door is my friend.

Near the door is a prepositional phrase. It is organized around the preposition near and describes the student.

Clause example

The student who is standing near the door is my friend.

The highlighted words contain a predication: someone—who—is presented as standing near the door. Therefore, the highlighted part is a clause.

The more technical definitions

A clause is a grammatical unit organized around a predication: it presents someone or something as doing something, experiencing something, or being in a particular state. A clause normally contains a subject and a predicate built around a verb.

A phrase is a grammatical unit organized around a head word, such as a noun, verb, adjective, or preposition. Importantly, a phrase can contain another phrase or even a complete dependent clause inside it.

A phrase can contain a relative clause

This point can initially feel strange. Look at the complete subject in the following sentence:

A phrase containing a clause

[The student who sits by the window] is absent today.

The smaller highlighted unit, who sits by the window, is a clause because it contains the predication “who sits.” However, the larger bracketed unit, the student who sits by the window, is a noun phrase.

The larger unit is a phrase because it is organized around the head noun student. The relative clause is nested inside that noun phrase and gives information about the student.

Whole noun phrase
the student who sits by the window
Head noun / antecedent
student
Nested relative clause
who sits by the window
Predication inside the clause
who sits

In other words, the whole group is organized around the noun student, not around the smaller predication who sits. That is why the whole group is a noun phrase even though it contains a subject and a verb inside its relative clause.

The larger phrase can perform a sentence role

The antecedent and its relative clause usually become part of a larger noun phrase. That whole phrase can then act as a subject, object, or another noun-like part of the sentence.

Subject

The student who sits by the window is absent.

The whole noun phrase is the subject of is absent.

Object

I called the student who sits by the window.

The whole noun phrase is the object of called.

After a preposition

I spoke to the student who sits by the window.

The whole noun phrase comes after the preposition to.

This distinction is not important for using relative clauses

Do not let these labels slow you down. You do not need to worry whether the larger unit is technically a phrase while the smaller unit inside it is a clause. The distinction simply shows that grammar has layers: a clause can be nested inside a phrase, and that phrase can then function as one part of a larger sentence.

For practical learning, focus on three questions: What noun is being described? What does the relative clause say about it? What job does the whole noun phrase perform in the sentence?

Independent and dependent clauses

Some clauses can stand alone as complete sentences. Others need to be attached to a larger sentence.

Independent clause

The student received a scholarship.

This expresses a complete message and can stand alone.

Dependent clause

who received a scholarship

This is not a complete message by itself. We need to know who this clause describes.

A relative clause is normally a dependent clause. It becomes part of a larger noun phrase:

the student + who received a scholarship
Complete sentence

The student who received a scholarship thanked her professor.

Subjects and verbs

The subject is the person, thing, or idea that the clause is about. The verb tells us what the subject does, what happens to it, or what state it is in.

Clause
The student studies every night.
Subject
the student
Verb
studies
Clause
The course is difficult.
Subject
the course
Verb
is

Objects

An object is usually the person or thing that receives the action of a verb.

Clause
I read the article.
Subject
I
Verb
read
Object
the article

This matters because relative words can be either the subject or the object inside a relative clause.

Prepositions

Prepositions are short words such as in, on, at, to, with, from, for, about, and of. They express relationships.

in the library
speak to the professor
work with a partner
depend on technology
write about climate change

A relative word can represent the noun after a preposition:

Example

The professor whom I spoke to was helpful.

I spoke to the professor. The preposition to is part of the meaning.

Nouns and noun phrases

A noun names a person, thing, place, or idea: student, laptop, city, freedom.

A noun phrase is a noun together with the words that belong with it:

student
the student
the new student
the new student from Turkey
the new student from Turkey who joined our class yesterday

In each example, the main noun is student. The other words add information to it.

Useful term: head noun

The head noun is the central noun in a noun phrase. In the new student from Turkey who joined our class, the head noun is student.

The antecedent

The noun that a relative word refers back to is called its antecedent. This is simply a technical name for “the noun being described.”

Example

The article that I read was useful.

The antecedent of that is the article. In simple terms, that refers back to the article.

Read the clause lesson if this feels difficult

If you are still unsure how to find subjects, verbs, objects, or clause boundaries, pause here and read What Clauses Are. That lesson will make the rest of this guide much easier.

What to remember

  • A clause is organized around a predication, normally expressed through a subject and a verb.
  • A phrase is organized around a head word and may contain smaller phrases or dependent clauses.
  • A relative clause is usually dependent: it cannot stand alone as a complete message.
  • The antecedent and its relative clause normally form part of a larger noun phrase.
  • That larger noun phrase can act as a subject, object, or another noun-like part of the sentence.
  • The head noun is the central noun being described; the antecedent is the noun to which the relative word refers.
  • The phrase-versus-clause label is far less important than understanding the meaning and structure.

What a Relative Clause Does

A relative clause usually performs one of two jobs: it identifies the noun we mean, or it adds more information about a noun that is already clear.

Job 1: identifying the noun

When a relative clause does Job 1, it singles out one member—or one subgroup—from a larger group of possibilities. You can imagine the clause as a funnel or filter.

One student from a group

The student who sits by the window is absent today.

There is a group of students. Before the relative clause appears, the student could refer to any student in that group. The clause who sits by the window funnels the possibilities down to one particular student.

One document from a group

Please send me the document that contains the final schedule.

There may be several possible documents. The clause selects the document containing the final schedule and excludes the others.

The “Which X?” test

Temporarily remove the relative clause. If it becomes natural to ask “Which + antecedent?”, the clause is probably doing Job 1.

The student is absent today. → Which student?

Please send me the document. → Which document?

The question is valid because the shortened sentence no longer tells us which member of the group is meant.

Job 2: adding another fact

When a relative clause does Job 2, the person, thing, place, or situation is already identified. The clause does not narrow a group. It adds a separate comment.

Already identified by name

Professor Green, who teaches linguistics, will lead the workshop.

The name Professor Green already identifies the person. We do not need the clause to answer “Which Professor Green?” The clause simply adds the fact that this professor teaches linguistics.

Comment on an entire event

The meeting was cancelled, which surprised everyone.

The clause does not identify a meeting. It comments on the whole cancellation situation.

Removal test for Job 2

If you remove a Job 2 clause, the same person, thing, or situation normally remains clear:

Professor Green will lead the workshop.

The identity has not become uncertain. We have only lost an extra fact.

Part 2 of this course will examine the punctuation and meaning difference between these two uses in detail. For now, notice the central contrast: Job 1 selects; Job 2 comments.

Combining two sentences

Relative clauses allow us to combine two sentences when both sentences mention the same person or thing.

  1. Start with two sentences.
    I met a student. The student speaks four languages.
  2. Find the repeated noun.
    The repeated noun is student.
  3. Turn one sentence into a description.
    who speaks four languages
  4. Place the description immediately after the noun.
    I met a student who speaks four languages.

Before combining

The article explains the problem.

I read the article yesterday.

After combining

The article that I read yesterday explains the problem.

Before combining

We visited a village.

My grandfather was born in the village.

After combining

We visited the village where my grandfather was born.

The normal position of a relative clause

A relative clause normally comes immediately after the noun it describes. This keeps the meaning clear.

noun + relative clause + rest of the sentence
Sentence pattern

The professor who teaches biology has published a new book.

Noun
the professor
Relative clause
who teaches biology
Rest of sentence
has published a new book
Common clarity problem

Do not separate a relative clause from its noun when another noun in between could confuse the reader.

I discussed the proposal with the professor that had been rejected.

Because that had been rejected comes immediately after the professor, the sentence can be understood to mean that the professor had been rejected—perhaps rejected for a job, application, or position. That is not the intended meaning.

I discussed the proposal that had been rejected with the professor.

Now the relative clause stands directly after the proposal, so the meaning is clear: the proposal had been rejected, and I discussed that rejected proposal with the professor.

Finding the main sentence

A relative clause is often inserted inside a larger sentence. To understand the structure, temporarily remove the relative clause.

Full sentence

The book that I borrowed from the library is on my desk.

Main sentence after removal

The book is on my desk.

This is especially useful when the subject of the main sentence is long:

Long subject

The students who completed the optional research project received extra credit.

Main sentence: The students received extra credit.

What to remember

  • A relative clause can identify a noun or add another fact about it.
  • It often combines two sentences that contain the same noun.
  • It normally appears immediately after the noun it describes.
  • Removing the relative clause often reveals the main sentence.

The Relative Word Does Two Jobs

Words such as who, which, that, whose, where, and when do more than connect ideas. They also have a role inside the relative clause.

Job 1: connecting the clause to the noun

Example

The woman who called this morning left a message.

Who connects the clause called this morning to the noun the woman.

Job 2: replacing the repeated noun inside the clause

Start with two separate sentences:

Two sentences

The woman left a message.

The woman called this morning.

When we combine them, we do not repeat the woman:

Combined sentence

The woman who called this morning left a message.

Inside the smaller clause, who takes the place of the woman:

The woman called this morning. → who called this morning

The missing-position idea

In many relative clauses, the noun being described belongs in a particular position inside the smaller clause. However, English does not repeat the noun—or replace it with an ordinary pronoun—in that position. The position is left unspoken. Grammarians often call this unspoken position a gap.

Step 1: begin with the complete ordinary sentence

I bought the book.

The verb bought normally has an object. Here, that object is the book.

Step 2: make the book the antecedent outside the clause

the book + that I bought ___

The antecedent the book now appears before the relative clause. Inside the clause, the object position after bought is still part of the grammar and meaning, but it is not pronounced. The relative connection tells us that the missing object is the book.

Ordinary clause
I bought the book.
Relative structure
the book that I bought ___
Meaning of the gap
___ = the book

Why learners sometimes add an unnecessary pronoun

Your mind correctly notices that bought normally needs something after it. The mistake is filling the gap with it, even though the antecedent and relative structure have already supplied that object meaning.

Do not repeat the object

The book that I bought it is on the table.

The book that I bought is on the table.

In the incorrect sentence, that connects the clause to the book, and the gap after bought already means the book. Adding it expresses the same object a second time.

The gap can come after a preposition

I talked to the man. → the man that I talked to ___

The preposition to normally has an object: to the man. In the relative clause, that object position becomes a gap connected to the antecedent.

Do not repeat the object after the preposition

The man that I talked to him is here.

The man that I talked to is here.

The man to whom I talked is here.

In the natural version, the gap after to means the man. Adding him repeats the same person.

The gap is not always after the verb

Its position depends on the hidden sentence:

Direct object

the article that she recommended ___

She recommended the article.

After a preposition

the colleague who I work with ___

I work with the colleague.

Place relationship

the room where we met ___

We met in the room.

Time relationship

the day when we met ___

We met on that day.

Subject relatives work slightly differently

In a subject relative clause, the relative word itself occupies the subject position. There is no separate visible subject to add.

Hidden sentence

The student called me.

Relative clause

the student who called me

Do not repeat the subject

The student who he called me is absent.

The student who called me is absent.

Who already represents the student as the subject of called.

A powerful reconstruction question

Ask: “What complete ordinary sentence contains this antecedent?”

the article that she recommended → She recommended the article.

the man that I talked to → I talked to the man.

Then locate the position occupied by the antecedent in that ordinary sentence. That position is the one English does not repeat inside the relative clause.

Different jobs inside the relative clause

Subject

The student who answered was correct.

Who performs the action answered.

Object

The student whom I called was absent.

I called the student. The student receives the action.

After a preposition

The person who I work with is very patient.

I work with the person.

Possession

The student whose essay won is delighted.

The essay belongs to the student.

Place relationship

The room where we met was small.

We met in the room.

Time relationship

I remember the day when we met.

We met on that day.

Subject and Object Relative Clauses

This is one of the most important distinctions in the entire topic. If you understand it well, many other rules become much easier.

Subject relative clauses

In a subject relative clause, the relative word performs the action or is the subject of the verb.

Example

The student who answered the question received extra credit.

Inside the relative clause:

who answered the question

Who is the subject of answered. It means the student answered the question.

Important rule

If the relative word is already the subject, do not add another subject.

The student who he answered the question received extra credit.

The student who answered the question received extra credit.

Person

The woman who lives upstairs is a doctor.

Who lives upstairs? The woman.

Thing

The machine that produces these parts is broken.

What produces the parts? The machine.

Idea

The theory which explains this behavior remains controversial.

What explains the behavior? The theory.

Animal

The dog that followed us home had no collar.

What followed us home? The dog.

Object relative clauses

In an object relative clause, someone or something else performs the action. The noun being described receives the action.

Example

The student whom I interviewed received a scholarship.

Inside the relative clause:

I interviewed the student.

I is the subject. The student is the object of interviewed. In the relative clause, whom represents the student.

Important rule

If the relative word is the object, the relative clause still needs its own subject.

The article that read yesterday was useful.

The article that I read yesterday was useful.

Person

The professor whom we invited accepted our offer.

We invited the professor.

Thing

The book that she recommended was excellent.

She recommended the book.

Idea

The argument which the author presents is not convincing.

The author presents the argument.

No relative word

The film we watched was surprisingly good.

We watched the film. The object relative word has been omitted.

A quick test: what comes immediately after the relative word?

This shortcut can help with simple active clauses, but it is not a complete analysis and should not replace reconstruction of the hidden sentence.

Relative word + active verb

The student who answered...

The machine that broke...

The relative word is probably the grammatical subject.

Relative word + noun/pronoun + verb

The student whom I called...

The machine that the technician repaired...

The relative word is probably an object.

Do not rely on this shortcut with passive clauses

The student who was interviewed yesterday received an offer.

The relative word is followed by a verb, but the student does not perform the interviewing. Who is the grammatical subject of the passive verb was interviewed, while the student receives the action.

Therefore, the “what comes next?” shortcut cannot tell you the active–passive meaning. Reconstruct the complete idea instead:

The committee interviewed the student. / The student was interviewed.

Objects of prepositions

Sometimes the noun is connected to a preposition rather than directly to the verb.

Example

The colleague who I work with is very reliable.

Hidden sentence: I work with the colleague.

Example

The topic that we talked about was complicated.

Hidden sentence: We talked about the topic.

In more formal English, the preposition can come before the relative word:

Natural everyday form

The professor who I spoke to

The theory which the argument is based on

Formal form

The professor to whom I spoke

The theory on which the argument is based

Part 2 will explain these formal patterns fully. For now, the main point is that the preposition must remain somewhere because it is part of the meaning.

Common error

The professor whom I spoke was helpful.

The professor whom I spoke to was helpful.

The professor to whom I spoke was helpful.

Subject or object? Use the hidden sentence

Subject

The student who called me

Hidden sentence: The student called me.

Object

The student whom I called

Hidden sentence: I called the student.

  • In a subject relative clause, the relative word is the subject of the verb.
  • In an object relative clause, another subject performs the action.
  • Do not add a second subject after a subject relative word.
  • Do not forget the subject inside an object relative clause.
  • Use the hidden complete sentence to identify the role accurately.
Chapter

Choosing the Basic Relative Word

The best relative word depends on two questions: what kind of noun are you describing, and what role does the relative word have inside the clause?

Who

Use who for people, especially when the relative word is the subject.

Subject

The teacher who helped me was very patient.

Object in everyday English

The teacher who I asked did not know the answer.

In modern everyday English, who is also common as an object, especially when the preposition remains at the end:

Natural everyday English

The person who I spoke to was helpful.

Whom

Use whom for people when the person is the object. It is more formal than who and is especially common after a fronted preposition.

Object

The applicant whom we interviewed had excellent qualifications.

After a preposition

The applicant to whom we offered the position accepted it.

Practical advice

Use who naturally in speech and ordinary writing. Learn whom so you can understand and use formal structures accurately, especially forms such as to whom, with whom, and for whom.

Which

Use which for things, animals, and ideas. It can also refer to an entire previous event, fact, or situation.

Thing as subject

The device which measures temperature is extremely sensitive.

The device measures temperature.

Thing as object

The device which we tested worked well.

We tested the device.

Idea

The proposal which the committee discussed was rejected.

The committee discussed the proposal.

Which can describe an entire previous situation

Sometimes which does not refer to one noun immediately before it. It refers back to the whole fact or event expressed by the previous clause. This is commonly called a sentential relative clause or sentence-relative clause. More technically, it is a supplementary relative clause with a clause or situation as its antecedent.

Result

The deadline was extended, which gave us more time.

What gave us more time? The entire event of extending the deadline.

Reaction

She refused the offer, which surprised everyone.

Everyone was surprised by her refusal—not by a noun called the offer.

Consequence

The power went out, which stopped the experiment.

The power failure caused the experiment to stop.

Which as an object

The professor resigned without warning, which nobody had expected.

Nobody had expected the professor to resign without warning. Which is the object of expected.

Compare noun reference and situation reference

Which refers to a noun

They bought a new printer, which was expensive.

The printer was expensive.

Which refers to the event

They bought a new printer, which surprised me.

Their buying a new printer surprised me.

Notice the comma

A sentential which-clause is an added comment, so it is separated from the previous clause with a comma. It performs Job 2, not Job 1.

That

Use that for people or things only in identifying relative clauses—Job 1. It helps select the person or thing meant from a group of possibilities.

Person: Job 1

The students that arrived late had to wait outside.

Which students? The students who arrived late.

Thing: Job 1

The information that you provided was useful.

Which information? The information you provided.

That cannot perform Job 2

Do not use that to add a separate fact about an already identified noun, and do not place it after the comma of a supplementary relative clause.

My laptop, that I bought last month, has stopped working.

My laptop, which I bought last month, has stopped working.

Professor Green, that teaches linguistics, will lead the workshop.

Professor Green, who teaches linguistics, will lead the workshop.

The meeting was cancelled, that surprised everyone.

The meeting was cancelled, which surprised everyone.

Job 1: that is possible

The laptop that is on the front desk belongs to Sara.

Which laptop? The clause identifies it.

Job 2: that is impossible

Sara’s laptop, which is on the front desk, needs charging.

The laptop is already identified; the clause adds a fact.

For people in Job 1 clauses, who is often more natural than that, especially in careful writing:

Natural

The professor who teaches the course

Also possible

The professor that teaches the course

Whose

Use whose to show possession or another close relationship. It can refer to people, animals, objects, places, organizations, and abstract ideas. It does not apply only to human owners.

Person

The student whose laptop was stolen reported the incident.

The student’s laptop was stolen.

Animal

The dog whose leg was injured is recovering well.

The dog’s leg was injured.

Place or thing

The school whose walls are red is beside the park.

The walls of the school are red. This is completely standard English.

Building

We visited a museum whose entrance was decorated with tiles.

The museum’s entrance was decorated.

Organization

The company whose products are sold worldwide began as a small workshop.

The products are associated with and produced by the company.

Idea or theory

They rejected a theory whose assumptions could not be tested.

The assumptions belong to or form part of the theory.

Whose expresses a broad relationship

The relationship does not have to be literal legal ownership. A school does not “own” its walls in the same way a person owns a laptop, and a theory does not own its assumptions. Whose can simply connect a whole to one of its parts, features, members, or associated ideas.

Where

Use where when the relative clause expresses a place relationship. It often means in that place, at that place, or to that place.

In that place

The library where I study is open all night.

I study in the library.

At that place

The station where we met has been renovated.

We met at the station.

Do not choose where only because the noun is a place

Use where only when the hidden sentence contains a genuine place relationship such as in the place, at the place, or sometimes to the place. A place noun can also be an ordinary subject or object, in which case you need that, which, or no object relative word.

Place relationship: where

The city where I live is crowded.

Hidden sentence: I live in the city.

Direct object: that/which

The city that I visited was beautiful.

Hidden sentence: I visited the city. We say visit the city, not visit in the city.

The city where I visited was beautiful.

The city that I visited was beautiful.

Place relationship

The university where she teaches is nearby.

She teaches at the university.

Direct object

The university that she attended is nearby.

She attended the university. No at is required after attend.

Place relationship

The restaurant where we had dinner was crowded.

We had dinner at the restaurant.

Direct object

The restaurant that the newspaper reviewed was crowded.

The newspaper reviewed the restaurant.

Place relationship

The house where he was born is now a museum.

He was born in the house.

Direct object

The house that his family bought is now a museum.

His family bought the house.

The reliable question

Reconstruct the hidden sentence. If you naturally say in/at/to + the place, where may be appropriate. If the verb takes the place directly—visit the city, attend the university, review the restaurant, buy the house—use an ordinary relative pronoun or omit an object relative word when allowed.

When

Use when for a time relationship. It often means on that day, in that year, during that period, or at that moment.

Day

I remember the day when the results were announced.

Period

There was a period when few people owned computers.

Why

Use why mainly after the noun reason. It means for that reason.

Example

The reason why I left early was that I felt ill.

In many sentences, why can be omitted:

Also natural

The reason I left early was that I felt ill.

Why what is different

What usually means the thing that. Because it already contains the meaning of a noun, it normally does not follow another noun.

Normal relative clause

The thing that I need is more time.

What-clause

What I need is more time.

What = the thing that

Common error

The thing what I need is more time.

The thing that I need is more time.

What I need is more time.

A practical selection guide

People
who; formal object whom; identifying that
Things or ideas
which or identifying that
Possession
whose
Place relationship
where
Time relationship
when
Reason
why, mainly after reason
The thing that
what, without a noun before it

Building Different Types of Relative Clauses

This chapter brings the pieces together. Instead of memorizing isolated rules, learn the main sentence patterns that relative clauses express.

A person performs an action

person + who/that + verb
Everyday

The neighbor who feeds the stray cats is moving away.

University

Students who complete the survey will enter a prize draw.

Academic

Researchers who study migration often use tracking devices.

Workplace

Employees who handle customer data must follow strict rules.

A person receives an action

person + whom/who/that + subject + verb
Everyday

The mechanic whom my father recommended was honest.

University

The advisor who I contacted explained the process clearly.

No relative word

The student we selected will represent the class.

Formal

The candidate whom the committee selected accepted the offer.

A thing performs an action

thing + that/which + verb
Object

The alarm that detects smoke is not working.

Academic idea

A theory which explains every case is difficult to develop.

A thing receives an action

thing + that/which + subject + verb
Everyday

The package that I ordered arrived damaged.

Academic

The data which the researchers collected were incomplete.

An action happens in a place

place + where + subject + verb
Everyday

This is the room where we keep the tools.

University

The laboratory where the experiment was conducted has been renovated.

An action happens at a time

time noun + when + subject + verb
Moment

That was the moment when everything changed.

Period

The 1990s were a period when internet access expanded rapidly.

A reason explains an action

reason + why + subject + verb
Everyday

The reason why she missed the meeting was simple.

Academic

One reason why the policy failed was poor communication.

Possession or connection

person/thing + whose + noun + verb
Person

The author whose novel won the award gave a speech.

Thing

The company whose products are sold worldwide began as a small workshop.

A whole event causes another result

complete statement, which + verb
Result

The power went out, which stopped the presentation.

Reaction

She received a full scholarship, which delighted her family.

A Reliable Method for Combining Sentences

Sentence combining is not just an exercise. It teaches you how relative clauses are actually built. Use the following method until the process becomes automatic.

Step 1: find the repeated person or thing

Two sentences

The professor gave us an extension.

The professor teaches statistics.

The repeated noun is the professor.

Step 2: decide which sentence carries the main message

Ask what you mainly want to say.

Main message: extension

The professor who teaches statistics gave us an extension.

Main message: teaching

The professor who gave us an extension teaches statistics.

Both sentences are grammatical, but they place different information in the main clause.

Step 3: reconstruct the smaller sentence

Determine the repeated noun’s role in the sentence that will become the relative clause.

Repeated noun is the subject

The professor teaches statistics.

→ who teaches statistics

Repeated noun is the object

I emailed the professor.

→ whom I emailed

Step 4: choose the relative word

person as subject → who
person as object → whom/who/that
thing → which/that
place relationship → where
time relationship → when
possession → whose

Step 5: place the relative clause after the noun

Result

The professor who teaches statistics gave us an extension.

Step 6: check for repetition

Do not repeat the noun or add an unnecessary pronoun inside the relative clause.

Check the combined sentence

The professor who she teaches statistics gave us an extension.

The professor whom I emailed her replied.

The professor who teaches statistics gave us an extension.

The professor whom I emailed replied.

Combining when the repeated noun follows a preposition

Two sentences

The colleague was very helpful.

I worked with the colleague.

Natural

The colleague who I worked with was very helpful.

Formal

The colleague with whom I worked was very helpful.

Combining possession

Two sentences

The student won the prize.

The student’s essay was excellent.

Combined

The student whose essay was excellent won the prize.

Combining a place or time relationship

Place

We visited the university.

My mother studied at the university.

We visited the university where my mother studied.

Time

I remember the year.

We moved abroad in that year.

I remember the year when we moved abroad.

Do not force every pair into one sentence

Sometimes two sentences contain separate important ideas. Combining them can make the result heavy or unclear.

Overloaded

The professor who teaches biology and whose research focuses on insects and whom I met at a conference last year has written a book.

Clearer

The professor teaches biology and studies insects. I met her at a conference last year, and she has now written a book.

Writing principle

A relative clause should connect ideas more clearly. It is not automatically better simply because it makes the sentence longer or more complex.

  1. A. The student received a scholarship.
    B. The student organized the charity event.
  2. A. The documentary won an award.

The six-step method

  1. Find the repeated noun.
  2. Choose the main message.
  3. Find the repeated noun’s role in the smaller sentence.
  4. Choose the relative word.
  5. Place the relative clause after the noun.
  6. Remove repetition and check clarity.

Essential Beginner Errors

Most mistakes with relative clauses are not random. They come from a small number of misunderstandings. Learn the reason behind each error rather than memorizing the correction alone.

Error 1: adding a second subject

Common error

The woman who she works at the bank is my aunt.

The woman who works at the bank is my aunt.

Who already means the woman and is already the subject of works. Adding she creates two subjects.

Error 2: forgetting the subject in an object relative clause

Common error

The book that recommended was useful.

The book that my teacher recommended was useful.

Here, the book does not perform the action. Someone must recommend it. The relative clause therefore needs a subject such as my teacher.

Error 3: repeating the object

Common error

The laptop that I bought it is already broken.

The laptop that I bought is already broken.

That already represents the laptop inside the clause. Do not repeat it with it.

Error 4: using what after a noun

Common error

The information what you gave me was useful.

The information that you gave me was useful.

What you gave me was useful.

What normally means the thing that. It already contains a noun meaning, so it does not normally follow another noun.

Error 5: choosing where simply because the noun is a place

Common error

The restaurant where we visited was expensive.

The restaurant that we visited was expensive.

The restaurant where we ate was expensive.

We visit a restaurant, so the restaurant is the object of visit. We eat at a restaurant, so where works with ate.

Error 6: losing a necessary preposition

Common error

The person whom I spoke was helpful.

The person whom I spoke to was helpful.

The person to whom I spoke was helpful.

The verb pattern is speak to someone. A relative clause does not remove the preposition from the meaning.

Error 7: putting the clause next to the wrong noun

Clarity problem

I showed the photograph to my sister that I had taken in Shiraz.

I showed my sister the photograph that I had taken in Shiraz.

In the incorrect version, that I had taken in Shiraz appears next to my sister, so the sentence briefly sounds as if the speaker took the sister in Shiraz.

Error 8: adding an unnecessary be verb

Common error

The machine that is produces these parts is expensive.

The machine that produces these parts is expensive.

The machine that is producing these parts is expensive.

Use either a simple verb, produces, or a complete continuous form, is producing. Do not combine is with the simple form produces.

Error 9: confusing the main verb with the relative-clause verb

Example

The students who study regularly usually perform well.

Relative verb
study
Main verb
perform

A long subject may contain a verb inside its relative clause, but the entire sentence still needs a main verb.

Incomplete sentence

The students who completed the project on time.

The students who completed the project on time received extra credit.

Error 10: using a relative clause where the reference is unclear

Unclear

The manager spoke to the assistant who was angry.

Grammar suggests that the assistant was angry, but the writer may have meant the manager.

Clear alternatives

The angry assistant spoke to the manager.

The manager, who was angry, spoke to the assistant.

  • I can identify the noun it describes.
  • I can separate the main sentence from the relative clause.
  • I can reconstruct the hidden complete sentence.
  • I can identify subject and object relative clauses.
  • I can use who, whom, which, that, whose, where, when, and why.
  • I understand why what is different.
  • I can combine two sentences without repeating the noun.
  • I can keep necessary prepositions.
  • I can avoid double subjects and repeated objects.
  • I can keep a relative clause near the noun it describes.
  • I can choose clarity instead of unnecessary complexity.

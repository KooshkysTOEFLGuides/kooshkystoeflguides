Chapter

Defining and Non-Defining Relative Clauses

Relative clauses do not all have the same relationship with the noun before them. Some clauses help identify exactly who or what we mean. Others add a separate fact about a noun that is already clear. This difference controls both meaning and punctuation.

A small term before we begin

The structures in this part are sometimes called finite relative clauses. A finite clause contains an ordinary verb form that shows tense or works with a modal: works, worked, is working, has worked, will work, can work. You do not need to memorize the term. It simply separates the full clauses in this part from shorter forms such as students living nearby, which will be studied later.

Defining clauses: the filter

A defining relative clause identifies the person or thing being discussed. Imagine a large group. The relative clause acts like a filter and selects the member or subgroup that the speaker means.

Example

Students who submit the form by Friday will receive an email.

The sentence is not about all students. It is about the students who submit the form by Friday. The clause filters the larger group.

Example

Please bring the book that contains the practice tests.

There may be several books. The clause tells the listener which one to bring.

People

The employees who work remotely use a different system.

Only the remote employees are being discussed.

Things

The files that were damaged cannot be opened.

Only the damaged files cannot be opened.

Punctuation rule

Do not place commas around a defining relative clause. The clause is built into the identification of the noun.

The students who need extra help can stay after class.

The students, who need extra help, can stay after class.

The version with commas normally suggests that all of the already identified students need extra help. That may not be the intended meaning.

Non-defining clauses: the comment

A non-defining relative clause does not identify the noun. The listener already knows which person or thing is meant. The clause adds another fact—almost like a short comment inserted into the sentence.

Example

Dr. Rahimi, who teaches linguistics, will lead the workshop.

The name already identifies the person. The clause adds the fact that Dr. Rahimi teaches linguistics.

Example

Our main library, which was renovated last year, is open until midnight.

The phrase our main library already identifies the building. The relative clause adds information about its renovation.

Punctuation rule

A non-defining relative clause is separated from the rest of the sentence by commas.

My laptop, which I bought only six months ago, has stopped working.

The lecture was cancelled, which disappointed many students.

Why the commas matter

The commas do more than create a pause. They tell the reader how the information should be understood.

No commas: only some brothers

My brothers who live in Canada are doctors.

I may have other brothers elsewhere. The sentence says that the brothers living in Canada are doctors.

Commas: all the brothers

My brothers, who live in Canada, are doctors.

The natural interpretation is that all my brothers live in Canada and all of them are doctors.

No commas: a subgroup

The cars that are electric are parked downstairs.

Only the electric cars are downstairs. Other cars may be elsewhere.

Commas: an added fact

The cars, which are electric, are parked downstairs.

The cars are already identified. The clause tells us that all of them are electric.

Context decides whether identification is necessary

Do not decide only by looking at a word such as my brother, the professor, or a person’s name. Ask whether the listener already knows which person or thing you mean.

Identifying one brother

My brother who lives in Mashhad is an engineer.

This is natural if I have more than one brother and need to identify the one in Mashhad.

Adding a fact

My brother, who lives in Mashhad, is an engineer.

This is natural if the listener already knows which brother I mean, or if I have only one relevant brother.

A particular version of a place

The Paris that tourists usually see is different from the city experienced by many residents.

The clause selects one way of understanding Paris.

An added fact about a named place

Paris, which attracts millions of visitors, has an extensive public transport system.

The name already identifies the city; the clause adds a fact.

The removal test—and its limitation

A useful first test is to remove the relative clause.

If the listener can still identify exactly the same person or thing, the clause is probably non-defining.
If removing the clause leaves the listener asking “Which one?” or changes the group being discussed, the clause is probably defining.
Non-defining

Leila, who works at the university, called me.

Without the clause: Leila called me. The same person is still identified.

Defining

The Leila who works at the university called me.

This could be said when several people named Leila are known. The clause identifies which Leila.

Remember

The removal test is helpful, but meaning and context are more important than a mechanical rule. A sentence may remain grammatical after a defining clause is removed while becoming less precise or changing its meaning.

Defining and non-defining clauses in longer sentences

Defining clause inside the subject

The students who completed the optional assignment received detailed feedback.

Main sentence: The students received detailed feedback. The clause identifies which students.

Non-defining clause inside the subject

The course coordinator, who had already reviewed the complaints, agreed to meet the students.

The coordinator is already identified. The clause adds relevant background.

Non-defining clause at the end

We finally received the replacement laptop, which arrived without a charger.

The relative clause adds a new fact about the replacement laptop.

What to remember

  • A defining relative clause identifies the noun or selects a subgroup.
  • A non-defining relative clause adds a separate fact about an already identified noun.
  • Defining clauses do not take commas.
  • Non-defining clauses are separated with commas.
  • Changing the commas can change which people or things the sentence includes.
  • Context—not simply the type of noun—determines whether identification is necessary.

Relative Words in Defining and Non-Defining Clauses

The defining/non-defining distinction affects which relative words can be used. The most important rules concern that, omitted relative words, and commas.

Who

Who can introduce both defining and non-defining clauses referring to people.

Defining

The student who sits near the door is absent.

The clause identifies the student.

Non-defining

Neda, who sits near the door, is absent.

Neda is already identified; the clause adds a fact.

In ordinary modern English, who is also widely used as the object when the preposition remains at the end:

Natural object use

The consultant who we spoke to recommended a different plan.

Whom

Whom refers to people in object positions. It is formal, especially after a preposition.

Defining

The candidate whom we selected has accepted the position.

Non-defining

Ms. Chen, whom the committee selected, has accepted the position.

After a fronted preposition

My supervisor, to whom I had already explained the problem, supported my request.

Which

Which refers to things, animals, ideas, and sometimes an entire previous situation. It can introduce both defining and non-defining clauses.

Defining

The report which contains the new figures is on my desk.

Non-defining

The annual report, which contains the new figures, is on my desk.

Usage note

In American editing styles, writers often prefer that in defining clauses and reserve which for non-defining clauses. However, defining which is standard English, especially in British and international usage:

The book which I ordered has arrived.

The book that I ordered has arrived.

That

That can refer to people or things in defining relative clauses.

Person

The employees that need new ID cards should contact security.

Thing

The software that controls the system needs updating.

Crucial restriction

Do not use that in a non-defining relative clause.

My laptop, that I bought last month, is already broken.

My laptop, which I bought last month, is already broken.

Amir, that teaches the evening class, is absent.

Amir, who teaches the evening class, is absent.

You also cannot use that immediately after a fronted preposition:

After prepositions

The system on that the company depends is outdated.

The system on which the company depends is outdated.

Whose

Whose can introduce both defining and non-defining clauses. It can refer to people or things.

Defining

The student whose essay won the competition will speak first.

Non-defining

Farah, whose essay won the competition, will speak first.

Thing

The company, whose sales increased sharply, plans to hire more staff.

Where and when

Where and when can also occur in both types of clause.

Defining place

The building where the interviews are held is across the street.

Non-defining place

The West Hall, where the interviews are held, is across the street.

Defining time

I remember a period when I had almost no free time.

Non-defining time

During my first semester, when I had almost no free time, I learned to organize my schedule.

Why

Why most often follows reason in a defining clause:

Typical use

The reason why the experiment failed remains unclear.

Non-defining uses with why are much less common and often sound awkward. A separate explanation is usually clearer.

Relative words cannot disappear from non-defining clauses

Defining object clause

The book (that) I ordered has arrived.

The object relative word may be omitted.

Non-defining object clause

My new book, which I ordered online, has arrived.

The word which cannot be omitted.

Common error

My new book, I ordered online, has arrived.

My new book, which I ordered online, has arrived.

A compact comparison

Who
People; defining or non-defining; subject and commonly object
Whom
People as objects; defining or non-defining; especially formal after prepositions
Which
Things, ideas, animals, or a whole situation; defining or non-defining
That
People or things; defining clauses only; never after a fronted preposition
Whose
Possession or connection; people or things; defining or non-defining
Where/when
Place or time relationships; defining or non-defining

What to remember

  • Who, whom, which, whose, where, and when can appear in both defining and non-defining clauses.
  • That is limited to defining clauses.
  • That cannot follow a fronted preposition.
  • A relative word cannot be omitted from a non-defining clause.
  • Defining which is standard, although some American styles prefer that.

Omitting the Object Relative Word

In many defining relative clauses, an object relative word can be left out. This is one of the most common patterns in natural English: the book I bought, the person we met, the topic they talked about.

When omission is possible

The relative word can normally disappear when all of the following are true:

The relative word is an object, not the subject.
The clause is defining, not non-defining.
No preposition has been moved directly before the relative word.

With the relative word

The article that I downloaded is useful.

The person whom we interviewed was confident.

The topic which they discussed was controversial.

With omission

The article I downloaded is useful.

The person we interviewed was confident.

The topic they discussed was controversial.

Why object relatives allow omission

In an object relative clause, another subject is still visible:

the book (that) I bought

The sequence I bought makes it clear that something is missing after the verb. The listener understands that the missing object is the book.

Hidden sentence

I bought the book.

Subject relative words cannot normally be omitted

Correct subject relative

The book that contains the answers is on my desk.

Incorrect omission

The book contains the answers is on my desk.

If that disappears, the book contains the answers looks like a complete main clause. The structure no longer shows where the relative clause begins.

Core rule

Do not omit a subject relative word:

The student who called me left a message.

The student called me left a message.

Omission with a preposition at the end

An object relative word may be omitted when the preposition stays at the end of the clause.

With who

The person who I spoke to was helpful.

With omission

The person I spoke to was helpful.

With that

The project that we are working on is almost finished.

With omission

The project we are working on is almost finished.

No omission after a fronted preposition

Preposition at the end

The person (whom) I spoke to

Omission is possible.

Preposition at the front

The person to whom I spoke

Whom must remain.

Incorrect forms

The person to I spoke was helpful.

The system on the company depends is outdated.

The person to whom I spoke was helpful.

The system on which the company depends is outdated.

No omission in non-defining clauses

Defining

The report (that) I submitted yesterday needs revision.

Non-defining

My final report, which I submitted yesterday, needs revision.

The commas create an inserted comment. An explicit relative word is needed to connect that comment to the noun.

Omission does not change the tense or structure of the clause

Only the object relative word disappears. The rest of the clause remains a normal clause.

Full object relative

the article that I have been reading

Omitted object relative

the article I have been reading

Omission is not reduction

In the article I read, the clause still contains a subject and a normal verb: I read. Only the object relative word is missing. In a later part, you will study true reductions such as students who live nearby → students living nearby, where the verb structure itself changes.

When keeping the relative word is helpful

Even when omission is grammatical, keeping the relative word may make a long or complicated sentence easier to read.

Short and clear

The book I ordered has arrived.

Longer clause

The policy that several members of the committee had strongly opposed was eventually approved.

Keeping that clearly marks the beginning of the long relative clause.

Omission after there is / there are

Natural English often uses an omitted object relative in expressions such as:

Example

There are several points we need to discuss.

Example

Is there anything I can do?

Example

That is the best explanation I have heard.

Example

She was the first person I contacted.

What to remember

  • Only object relative words can normally be omitted.
  • Omission is limited to defining clauses.
  • Omission is possible when a necessary preposition remains at the end.
  • Omission is impossible after a fronted preposition.
  • Omission removes only the relative word; it does not change the rest of the clause.
  • Keeping the relative word can improve clarity in a long clause.

Prepositions in Relative Clauses

Many English verbs, adjectives, and expressions naturally require a preposition: speak to someone, depend on something, be interested in something, work with someone. When the noun after that preposition becomes the noun described by a relative clause, the preposition must remain in the sentence.

Begin with the hidden sentence

Hidden sentence

I spoke to the professor.

When the professor becomes the noun being described, English offers two main patterns:

Preposition at the end

The professor who I spoke to was helpful.

This is natural in speech and ordinary writing.

Preposition at the front

The professor to whom I spoke was helpful.

This is more formal and common in careful academic or professional writing.

Preposition stranding: the natural pattern

When the preposition stays at the end, grammarians sometimes say it is stranded. This simply means that the preposition is separated from its object.

Person

The colleague who I share an office with is on leave.

I share an office with the colleague.

Thing

The issue that we were arguing about has been resolved.

We were arguing about the issue.

Omission

The chair I was sitting on suddenly broke.

I was sitting on the chair.

Phrasal verb

The child she looks after is five years old.

Look after means take care of. The two words belong together.

Natural English

Ending a relative clause with a preposition is not an error. It is often the most natural choice:

That is the person I told you about.

This is the form you need to sign.

The hotel we stayed at was inexpensive.

Preposition fronting: the formal pattern

In formal English, the preposition may be placed before whom or which.

People

The researcher with whom I collaborated works in Germany.

Things

The principle on which the policy is based is fairness.

Institution

The university from which she graduated is well known.

Purpose

The purpose for which the funds were provided must be respected.

Restrictions after a fronted preposition

Rule 1: no that

The method by that they solved the problem was expensive.

The method by which they solved the problem was expensive.

Rule 2: no omission

The person to I sent the message has replied.

The person to whom I sent the message has replied.

Rule 3: whom for people, which for things

The manager with which I spoke was helpful.

The manager with whom I spoke was helpful.

The system on whom the company depends is outdated.

The system on which the company depends is outdated.

Choosing the correct preposition

Do not select the preposition because it “sounds formal.” Reconstruct the original sentence and use the preposition required by the verb or meaning.

Depend on

The system on which the company depends

The company depends on the system.

Refer to

The study to which the article refers

The article refers to the study.

Benefit from

The program from which many students benefit

Many students benefit from the program.

Be responsible for

The department for which she is responsible

She is responsible for the department.

Useful formal combinations

Some preposition-relative combinations occur frequently in academic and professional English. Learn them as meaningful patterns, not decorative phrases.

by which
the method or means used: the process by which cells divide
on which
the basis or support: the evidence on which the conclusion is based
to which
direction, connection, degree: the extent to which income affects health
under which
conditions or rules: the conditions under which the reaction occurs
through which
a route or process: the mechanism through which the drug works
for which
purpose, reason, or responsibility: the purpose for which the building was designed
in which
place, situation, period, or arrangement: a situation in which no easy solution exists

The extent to which

This pattern expresses degree: how much one factor is true, affects another factor, or explains something.

Question of degree

Researchers disagree about the extent to which social media affects attention.

They disagree about how strongly social media affects attention.

Academic caution

The extent to which these results can be generalized remains uncertain.

It is uncertain how broadly the results apply.

Prepositions in non-defining clauses

Formal non-defining clause

Professor Malik, with whom I worked for two years, has retired.

Natural non-defining clause

Professor Malik, who I worked with for two years, has retired.

Both are grammatical. The first sounds more formal; the second sounds more conversational and direct.

Do not front every preposition

Formal fronting is useful when it suits the context. In an ordinary sentence, it may sound unnecessarily stiff.

Natural everyday sentence

The sofa that I slept on was uncomfortable.

Unnecessarily formal

The sofa on which I slept was uncomfortable.

Grammatical, but unusually formal for this simple situation.

Register choice

Choose the structure that fits the context. Formality is not automatically better. Good writing uses formal structures when they improve precision or suit the subject.

  • A necessary preposition must remain somewhere in the relative clause.

  • Choose the preposition by reconstructing the hidden sentence.
  • Use formal combinations because they express the right relationship, not merely to make a sentence look advanced.

Where, When, and Why

The words where, when, and why do not simply match place nouns, time nouns, and the word reason. They express a relationship inside the relative clause. Understanding that relationship prevents many common errors.

Where expresses a place relationship

Use where when the hidden sentence contains a place expression such as in the place, at the place, or sometimes to the place.

In a place

The town where I grew up is near the coast.

I grew up in the town.

At a place

The desk where I left the keys is near the window.

I left the keys at the desk.

In an institution

The university where she teaches has a strong engineering program.

She teaches at the university.

In a document or site

The section where the rule is explained includes several examples.

The rule is explained in the section.

Where versus that or which

A place noun does not automatically require where. Ask what job the place has inside the hidden sentence.

Place relationship

The city where I studied was expensive.

I studied in the city.

Direct object

The city that I visited was expensive.

I visited the city.

Where

The restaurant where we had dinner was crowded.

We had dinner at the restaurant.

That/which

The restaurant that the newspaper reviewed was crowded.

The newspaper reviewed the restaurant.

Alternatives to where

Natural

the laboratory where the test was conducted

Formal

the laboratory in which the test was conducted

In defining clauses, a stranded preposition is also possible:

Natural alternative

The laboratory that the test was conducted in has closed.

Extended uses of where

English also uses where after certain abstract nouns when the meaning is similar to “in that situation, case, stage, or system.”

Situation

We have reached a situation where no simple solution exists.

Case

There are cases where the usual rule does not apply.

Stage

The project has reached a stage where additional funding is necessary.

System

They want a system where students can track their progress.

In careful formal writing, in which may sound more precise after abstract nouns:

Formal alternative

We have reached a situation in which no simple solution exists.

When expresses a time relationship

Use when when the hidden sentence contains a time expression such as on that day, in that year, during that period, or at that moment.

Day

I remember the day when the results arrived.

The results arrived on that day.

Year

2018 was the year when she began university.

She began university in that year.

Period

There was a period when the company struggled financially.

Moment

That was the moment when I understood the problem.

When versus that or which

Time relationship

The day when we met was rainy.

We met on that day.

Subject

The day that changed my life was rainy.

The day changed my life.

Time relationship

I remember the year when we moved.

Object

I remember the year that historians often discuss.

Historians discuss the year.

Alternatives to when

With when

the year when the company was founded

Formal

the year in which the company was founded

In defining clauses, when can often be omitted:

With when

I remember the first time when I spoke in public.

More natural omission

I remember the first time I spoke in public.

Natural pattern

After expressions such as the first time, the last time, every time, and the next time, English often omits when:

The next time you call, leave a message.

I remember the first time I drove alone.

Why expresses a reason relationship

Why usually follows the noun reason and means for that reason.

Example

The reason why the event was cancelled has not been announced.

The event was cancelled for that reason.

Alternatives to why

With why

The reason why I objected was the cost.

With that

The reason that I objected was the cost.

With omission

The reason I objected was the cost.

Formal

The reason for which I objected was the cost.

The reason why ... is because

This pattern is common in speech, but it repeats the idea of reason: both the reason why and because introduce a cause. In careful writing, choose one clear structure.

Clearer writing

The reason why the project failed was because nobody checked the data.

The reason the project failed was that nobody checked the data.

The project failed because nobody checked the data.

The reason why versus the reason that someone gave

These structures look similar but contain different relationships.

Why: cause

The reason why she left was personal.

She left for that reason.

That: object

The reason that she gave was unconvincing.

She gave the reason. The reason is the object of gave.

The way

After the way, English normally uses one of these patterns:

the way she explained it
the way that she explained it
the way in which she explained it
Avoid this combination

I liked the way how she explained the idea.

I liked the way she explained the idea.

I liked how she explained the idea.

Possession with Whose and Of Which

Relative clauses can connect a person or thing to something associated with it: a student’s essay, a company’s products, a building’s roof, or a theory’s assumptions. The most natural relative word for this job is usually whose.

The basic whose pattern

person or thing + whose + noun + rest of clause
Person

The student whose essay won the prize is in my class.

The student’s essay won the prize.

Organization

The company whose products are sold worldwide began locally.

The company’s products are sold worldwide.

Object

They live in a house whose roof was damaged.

The house’s roof was damaged.

Abstract idea

We examined a theory whose assumptions are difficult to test.

The theory’s assumptions are difficult to test.

Important

Whose is standard for both people and things. It does not mean only human possession.

The noun after whose can have different roles

The word immediately after whose belongs to the antecedent, but its role inside the relative clause may differ.

The possessed noun is the subject

The author whose book became famous lives nearby.

The book became famous.

The possessed noun is the object

The author whose book I reviewed lives nearby.

I reviewed the book.

After a preposition

The researcher whose work we referred to attended the conference.

We referred to the researcher’s work.

Longer noun phrase

The school whose science laboratories need renovation has requested funding.

Defining and non-defining whose clauses

Defining

The student whose ID card is missing should visit the office.

The clause identifies which student.

Non-defining

Reza, whose ID card is missing, should visit the office.

Reza is already identified; the clause adds a fact.

Whose is not who’s

Spelling distinction

Whose shows possession. Who’s is a contraction of who is or who has.

The student whose bag was lost...

Who’s responsible for this room? = Who is responsible?

Formal possession with of which

For things, formal English can use a structure with of which. The possessed feature normally comes before of which.

thing + the/a/its feature + of which + verb

With whose

A theory whose assumptions are questionable

With of which

A theory the assumptions of which are questionable

Defining

We need a system the limitations of which are clearly understood.

Non-defining

The new system, the limitations of which are already apparent, requires revision.

Part or feature

They purchased a building the roof of which needs replacement.

Academic noun

The study, the methodology of which was criticized, was later repeated.

Choosing between whose and of which

Both can be grammatical for things, but they do not always sound equally natural.

Usually clearer and more natural

a company whose profits declined

a building whose windows were broken

a theory whose assumptions are unrealistic

More formal and heavier

a company the profits of which declined

a building the windows of which were broken

a theory the assumptions of which are unrealistic

Practical choice

Use whose when it produces a clear, natural sentence. Use the noun of which when a formal structure suits the context or when the possessed feature needs special emphasis.

Word order with of which

Keep the feature before of which

The report, the conclusions of which were disputed, was revised.

This is generally clearer than placing of which before the feature.

Common possession errors

Do not use which’s

The company which’s profits declined closed two offices.

The company whose profits declined closed two offices.

Do not repeat a possessive

The student whose her laptop was stolen reported it.

The student whose laptop was stolen reported it.

Whose must be followed by a noun

The student whose was selected received a prize.

The student who was selected received a prize.

The student whose essay was selected received a prize.

  • Use the clearer, more natural structure unless the formal pattern serves a real purpose.

Agreement Inside Relative Clauses

When the relative word is the subject, the verb inside the relative clause must agree with the noun that the relative word refers to. In simple terms, a singular noun normally takes a singular verb, and a plural noun takes a plural verb.

The basic pattern

Singular antecedent

The student who works in the library is my cousin.

Who refers to the singular noun student.

Plural antecedent

The students who work in the library are my classmates.

Who refers to the plural noun students.

Singular thing

The device that measures air quality is expensive.

Plural things

The devices that measure air quality are expensive.

Singular person

Anyone who needs help should ask.

Plural people

Those who need help should ask.

The antecedent may not be the nearest noun

Do not automatically make the verb agree with the closest noun. First decide what who or which refers to.

Who refers to students

She is one of the students who work in the laboratory.

The meaning is: she is one member of the group of students who work there. Who refers to students.

Who refers to one

She is the only one of the students who works in the laboratory.

The word only changes the meaning. She alone works there, so who refers to the only one.

One of the people who...

In this very common structure, the relative clause normally describes the plural noun:

Correct agreement

Ali is one of the employees who handle international orders.

Several employees handle these orders; Ali is one of them.

Correct agreement

This is one of the books that explain the theory clearly.

Several books explain it clearly; this is one of them.

The only one who...

Singular reference

Ali is the only one of the employees who handles international orders.

Ali alone handles the orders.

Singular reference

This is the only one of the books that explains the theory clearly.

Anyone, everyone, someone, and nobody

Indefinite pronouns such as anyone, everyone, someone, no one, and nobody are grammatically singular, even though they may refer generally to people.

Anyone

Anyone who wants to participate must register.

Everyone

Everyone who has completed the course will receive a certificate.

Someone

We need someone who understands the software.

Nobody

Nobody who works here has access to that file.

Those who and people who

Plural expressions require plural verbs:

Those

Those who prefer online classes can choose the evening section.

People

People who live near busy roads often report more noise.

Everything, something, and anything

These words are grammatically singular:

Everything

Everything that affects the final result must be recorded.

Something

We need something that works without electricity.

Agreement depends on the intended antecedent

Some sentences can have two possible meanings. The verb helps reveal which noun the relative clause describes.

Plural antecedent

I spoke to the leader of the volunteers who were waiting outside.

The volunteers were waiting outside.

Singular antecedent

I spoke to the leader of the volunteers, who was waiting outside.

The leader was waiting outside. The comma also helps show the intended attachment.

Clarity warning

If the reader might connect the relative clause to the wrong noun, rewrite the sentence rather than relying only on verb agreement.

Object relatives do not take agreement from the antecedent

When the relative word is the object, the visible subject inside the relative clause controls the verb.

Singular visible subject

The students whom the professor teaches are advanced.

The subject inside the clause is the professor.

Plural visible subject

The student whom the professors teach is advanced.

The subject inside the clause is the professors.

Which Referring to an Entire Situation

A relative clause with which can describe a noun, but it can also comment on an entire event, fact, or situation expressed by the previous clause.

Which referring to a noun

Noun reference

They bought a new printer, which was expensive.

Which refers to a new printer. The printer was expensive.

Which referring to the whole previous event

Whole-situation reference

They bought a new printer, which surprised everyone.

The surprising event was the purchase of the printer—not the printer itself.

Noun meaning

She received a scholarship, which was worth $10,000.

The scholarship was worth $10,000.

Whole-event meaning

She received a scholarship, which delighted her family.

Her receiving the scholarship delighted them.

Common meanings of sentential which

A whole-situation which clause often expresses a result, reaction, evaluation, or consequence.

Result

The power failed, which stopped the experiment.

Reaction

The professor extended the deadline, which relieved the students.

Evaluation

The company ignored the warning, which was irresponsible.

Unexpected fact

He completed the task in ten minutes, which I had not expected.

The comma is required

A whole-situation which clause is non-defining. It comments on a complete statement and must be separated with a comma.

Punctuation

The bus arrived forty minutes late which made me miss the interview.

The bus arrived forty minutes late, which made me miss the interview.

That cannot replace sentential which

Relative word

The school cancelled the trip, that disappointed the students.

The school cancelled the trip, which disappointed the students.

Which can be the subject or object inside the comment

Subject

He forgot the appointment, which caused a problem.

Which is the subject of caused.

Object

He forgot the appointment, which I found surprising.

I found the situation surprising. Which is the object of found.

Possible ambiguity

Sometimes the noun immediately before which and the whole previous situation are both possible references.

Potentially unclear

The company replaced the manager, which shocked the staff.

The natural meaning is that the replacement event shocked the staff. However, a reader may briefly wonder whether which refers to the manager.

When the reference is not immediately clear, rewrite:

With sentential which

The company replaced the manager, which shocked the staff.

Explicit rewrite

The company’s decision to replace the manager shocked the staff.

The company replaced the manager. This decision shocked the staff.

Avoid long, unclear backward references

Clarity problem

If several events appear before which, the reader may not know which event the clause comments on.

The team changed the schedule after the manager rejected the first plan, which created confusion.

What created confusion: changing the schedule, rejecting the plan, or the entire sequence?

The manager rejected the first plan, so the team changed the schedule. This change created confusion.

Useful alternatives

You do not need to use sentential which every time. These alternatives can be clearer:

This + noun: This decision caused delays.
As a result: As a result, several students complained.
A noun phrase: The cancellation disappointed everyone.
A coordinating conjunction: The deadline was extended, so we had more time.

One Noun with Multiple Relative Clauses

A noun may need more than one description. English can coordinate information inside one relative clause, attach two relative clauses to the same noun, or add a second comment after an identifying clause. The challenge is to keep the structure clear and balanced.

One relative word with two coordinated verbs

When the same subject performs two actions, one relative word can introduce both verbs.

Person

We need a volunteer who speaks Arabic and understands the registration system.

The volunteer speaks Arabic and understands the system.

Thing

They developed a tool that saves time and reduces errors.

The tool performs both actions.

Parallel structure

Keep the coordinated elements grammatically balanced:

a course that explains the theory and provides practical examples

a course that explains the theory and practical examples are provided

One object shared by two verbs

A single omitted or expressed object relative word may belong to two coordinated verbs.

Expressed object relative

The book that I bought and read during the holiday was excellent.

I bought the book and read the book.

Omitted object relative

The book I bought and read during the holiday was excellent.

Repeating the relative word for clarity

When two descriptions are long, structurally different, or independently important, repeating the relative word may make the sentence easier to follow.

One compact clause

The student who organized the event and collected the donations received an award.

Repeated for emphasis or separation

The student who organized the event and who later presented the results to the board received an award.

The repeated who is not always necessary, but it clearly signals the beginning of the second description.

Two relative clauses describing the same noun

Two defining descriptions

We are looking for applicants who have relevant experience and who can begin immediately.

Both clauses help identify the applicants wanted.

Two non-defining comments

Dr. Chen, who led the original study and who now works in Singapore, will speak first.

The name identifies Dr. Chen; both clauses add information.

A defining clause followed by a non-defining comment

A noun phrase may first be identified by a defining clause and then receive an additional comment.

Layered information

The report that I submitted last week, which included the revised figures, has been approved.

That I submitted last week identifies the report. Once that report is identified, which included the revised figures adds another fact about it.

Another example

The students who represented the university, who had trained for several months, won the competition.

The first clause identifies the students. The second adds background about those identified students.

Clauses describing different nouns

A sentence may contain several relative clauses, each attached to a different noun.

Two different antecedents

The professor who teaches the course recommended a book that explains the theory clearly.

Who teaches the course describes the professor. That explains the theory clearly describes a book.

Object relative inside a larger sentence

The organization that my sister works for supports schools that lack basic equipment.

Light nesting

Sometimes a relative clause contains a noun that has its own relative clause:

Nested structure

The article that the professor who teaches our seminar recommended is difficult.

The larger relative clause is that the professor ... recommended. Inside it, who teaches our seminar describes the professor.

English allows this, but heavy nesting is difficult to process. Later parts will examine it more fully. At this stage, use it mainly as a reading skill.

Heavy

The article that the professor who teaches the seminar recommended contains examples that many students find confusing.

Clearer

Our seminar professor recommended an article. It contains examples that many students find confusing.

Avoid unclear coordination

Common structure problem

We need a student who speaks French and the registration system is familiar.

We need a student who speaks French and is familiar with the registration system.

We need a student who speaks French and who understands the registration system.

Avoid relative-clause overload

A sentence can be grammatical and still be difficult to read.

Overloaded

The company that developed the software that our department uses and that recently opened an office that employs several of our graduates has announced a new product.

Edited for clarity

The company that developed our department’s software has announced a new product. It recently opened an office that employs several of our graduates.

Writing principle

Use multiple relative clauses when they make relationships clearer. Split the sentence when the reader must hold too many unfinished descriptions in mind.

Relative Clauses Versus Content Clauses

The word that can introduce more than one kind of clause. A clause after a noun is not automatically a relative clause. Sometimes the clause tells us the content of an idea, claim, belief, fact, report, or possibility. This distinction is especially important in academic English.

Relative clauses describe a noun

In a relative clause, one part of the clause represents the noun before it. There is a missing position inside the clause.

Relative clause

The claim that she made ___ was surprising.

Hidden sentence: She made the claim. The word that connects the clause to claim, and the claim fills the missing object position.

Content clauses express what a noun says or means

A content clause gives the content of a noun. It answers a question such as “What is the claim?”, “What is the idea?”, or “What fact are we discussing?”

Content clause

The claim that she was innocent was surprising.

The claim was: “She was innocent.” Nothing is missing inside she was innocent. The clause expresses the content of the claim.

Relative clause

The claim that she made ___ was surprising.

She made the claim. The claim is the object of made.

Content clause

The claim that she was innocent was surprising.

The clause states what the claim was.

The missing-position test

Ask whether the noun before the clause has a grammatical role inside the clause.

If the noun fills a missing subject, object, or prepositional position, the clause is probably a relative clause.
If the clause is already complete and states the content of the noun, it is probably a content clause.

Relative: something is missing

the evidence that researchers collected ___

Researchers collected the evidence.

Content: complete message

the evidence that the treatment works

The treatment works. This complete statement is the content of the evidence.

The meaning test

Which one? What kind?

The report that I submitted was incomplete.

The clause identifies the report. Which report? The one I submitted.

What did the report say?

The report that several files were missing caused concern.

The clause tells us the content of the report.

Common nouns followed by content clauses

Academic and formal English often places content clauses after nouns such as:

fact: the fact that the climate is changing
claim: the claim that the treatment is harmless
idea: the idea that education should be free
belief: the belief that talent is fixed
evidence: evidence that the policy reduced accidents
possibility: the possibility that the data are inaccurate
suggestion: the suggestion that the meeting be postponed
hope: the hope that conditions will improve
argument: the argument that public transport should be subsidized
assumption: the assumption that all participants had equal access
news: the news that the university had closed
report: reports that prices are rising

Pairs with the same noun

Idea + relative clause

The idea that changed the field was initially rejected.

The idea changed the field.

Idea + content clause

The idea that language shapes thought remains controversial.

The idea is: language shapes thought.

Evidence + relative clause

The evidence that the police collected was incomplete.

The police collected the evidence.

Evidence + content clause

The evidence that the suspect was elsewhere was convincing.

The evidence supports the complete statement that the suspect was elsewhere.

Report + relative clause

The report that the committee published was criticized.

The committee published the report.

Report + content clause

The report that several students had complained worried the dean.

The reported content was that several students had complained.

Possibility + relative clause

The possibility that the researchers had ignored later proved important.

The researchers had ignored the possibility.

Possibility + content clause

The possibility that the results were inaccurate had to be considered.

The possible situation was that the results were inaccurate.

That has different jobs in the two structures

Relative that

the proposal that the committee rejected ___

That introduces a relative clause connected to a missing object position.

Content-clause that

the proposal that the committee should meet again

The clause is complete. That simply introduces the proposed content.

Which cannot normally introduce a content clause

Common error

The fact which the results were unreliable caused concern.

The fact that the results were unreliable caused concern.

The results, which were unreliable, caused concern.

The first correct sentence contains a content clause. The second contains a non-defining relative clause describing the results.

Content clauses do not use defining/non-defining comma rules

A content clause is closely connected to the noun whose content it expresses. It is not normally placed between commas as an optional comment.

Punctuation

The fact, that the data were incomplete, caused concern.

The fact that the data were incomplete caused concern.

Can that be omitted?

After some verbs, content-clause that is often omitted: I think (that) she is right. After nouns, keeping that is usually clearer and often necessary for smooth reading:

Clear with that

The possibility that the exam will be postponed worries students.

Awkward without that

?The possibility the exam will be postponed worries students.

Some noun patterns allow omission in certain styles, but learners should normally keep that.

Safe learner rule

Keep that when a full clause expresses the content of a noun. It makes the relationship clear and is natural in academic writing.

Relative clause, content clause, or both?

Occasionally, context is needed because a clause may initially allow more than one interpretation.

Compare

We discussed the suggestion that the committee made.

Relative clause: the committee made the suggestion.

Compare

We discussed the suggestion that the committee meet again.

Content clause: the suggested action was for the committee to meet again.

Practice 3: correct the clause-type errors

  1. The fact which the temperature had increased was ignored.
  2. The belief, that intelligence cannot change, is widespread.
  3. The report that several files were missing it worried the manager.
  4. The idea which everyone should work from home is unrealistic.
  5. The evidence that the researchers collected it was incomplete.
Show the corrections
  1. The fact that the temperature had increased was ignored.
  2. The belief that intelligence cannot change is widespread.
  3. The report that several files were missing worried the manager.
  4. The idea that everyone should work from home is unrealistic.
  5. The evidence that the researchers collected was incomplete.

What to remember

  • A relative clause describes or identifies a noun and contains a position connected to that noun.
  • A content clause states what an idea, claim, fact, report, belief, or possibility consists of.
  • Use the missing-position test: relative clauses have a gap; content clauses are internally complete.
  • Use the meaning test: relative clauses answer “which one?”; content clauses answer “what is the claim/idea/fact?”
  • Which does not normally introduce a content clause.
  • Content clauses are not surrounded by relative-clause commas.
  • Keeping content-clause that is the safest and clearest choice after nouns.

Part 2 Comprehensive Practice

These exercises combine the complete finite relative-clause system. Work slowly. For difficult sentences, reconstruct the hidden clause, identify the antecedent, and decide whether the clause identifies, comments, or expresses the content of a noun.

Exercise 1: defining meaning and punctuation

Add commas where necessary. Then explain whether each relative clause identifies the noun or adds information.

  1. The students who have completed all four modules may take the final test.
  2. My evening students who have completed all four modules may take the final test.
  3. My evening class which normally meets on Tuesdays has been moved to Thursday.
  4. The class that normally meets in Room 8 has been moved to Room 12.
  5. Dr. Evans who developed the original test will review the new version.
  6. The researcher who developed this particular test works at another institution.
  7. The two laptops which were purchased last week have already malfunctioned.
  8. The laptops that were purchased from the second supplier are functioning normally.
Show the answers
  1. No commas. The clause identifies which students may take the test.
  2. This depends on context. If only some evening students completed the modules, no commas. If the entire already identified evening group completed them, use commas: My evening students, who have completed all four modules, may take the final test.
  3. My evening class, which normally meets on Tuesdays, has been moved to Thursday. The class is already identified.
  4. No commas. The clause identifies which class has moved.
  5. Dr. Evans, who developed the original test, will review the new version. The name identifies the person.
  6. No commas. The clause identifies which researcher.
  7. The two laptops, which were purchased last week, have already malfunctioned. The exact two laptops are already identified.
  8. No commas. The clause selects the laptops bought from the second supplier.

Complete each sentence with a suitable relative word. Put the word in parentheses if it may be omitted.

  1. The employee ________ manages the evening shift is absent.
  2. The employee ________ I called has not replied.
  3. Our manager ________ I called this morning has not replied.
  4. The system on ________ the company depends is being replaced.
  5. The system ________ the company depends on is being replaced.
  6. The report ________ contains the full results is confidential.
  7. The report ________ the assistant prepared is confidential.
  8. The report ________ the assistant prepared yesterday is now online.
  9. The lecturer ________ office is beside the library is new.
  10. The deadline was extended ________ gave us another week.
Show possible answers
  1. who/that; subject, so no omission
  2. (whom/who/that); object in a defining clause
  3. Our manager, whom/who I called this morning, has not replied; non-defining, so no omission
  4. which
  5. (which/that)
  6. that/which; subject, so no omission
  7. (that/which)
  8. This sentence needs context. If a particular report is being identified: (that/which). If the report is already uniquely identified, use commas and which.
  9. whose
  10. , which

Exercise 4: where, when, why, or a pronoun?

  1. The country ________ she studied attracts many international students.
  2. The country ________ she studied in detail has a complex history.
  3. The month ________ applications close is approaching.
  4. The month ________ includes the final examination is always stressful.
  5. The reason ________ the manager rejected the plan was financial.

  7. This is a case ________ the normal rule does not apply.

Show the answers
  1. where or in which
  2. that/which, or omission
  3. when or in which
  4. that/which
  5. why, that, for which, or omission
  6. that/which, or omission
  7. where or in which
  8. that/which, or omission

Exercise 5: possession and agreement

Choose the correct form and correct any possession error.

  1. The student whose / who’s essay won is one of the finalists.
  2. The company which’s products are imported has opened a new office.
  3. This is one of the policies that (affect / affects) international students.
  4. This is the only one of the policies that (affect / affects) international students directly.
  5. Anyone who (want / wants) to join must register.
  6. Those who (want / wants) to join must register.
  7. The researchers whose work we referred (to / —) attended the meeting.
  8. The building, the windows of which (was / were) replaced, has reopened.
  9. The reports that the assistant (prepare / prepares) contain several errors.
  10. The assistant who (prepare / prepares) the reports is new.
Show the answers
  1. whose
  2. The company whose products are imported has opened a new office.
  3. affect; that refers to plural policies
  4. affects; the meaning is that this one policy alone affects them directly
  5. wants
  6. want
  7. to; the pattern is refer to the work
  8. were; the subject is plural windows
  9. prepares; the subject inside the object relative is the assistant
  10. prepares; who refers to singular assistant

Exercise 6: noun reference or whole-situation which?

State what which refers to. Rewrite any sentence that is unclear.

  1. The team submitted a revised plan, which contained a detailed budget.
  2. The team submitted a revised plan, which pleased the director.
  3. The manager dismissed the complaint from the customer, which was unreasonable.
  4. The university cancelled the examination after the power failure, which caused confusion.
  5. The new software deleted several files, which the technicians had not expected.
Show possible answers
  1. Which refers to the revised plan.
  2. Which refers to the team’s submission of a revised plan.
  3. Unclear: either the complaint or the dismissal may be called unreasonable. Rewrite according to the intended meaning:
    • The manager unreasonably dismissed the customer’s complaint.
    • The manager dismissed the customer’s unreasonable complaint.
  4. Potentially unclear. Better: After the power failure, the university cancelled the examination. This cancellation caused confusion.
  5. Which refers to the fact that the software deleted the files; it is the object of had not expected.

Exercise 7: relative or content clause?

Classify each bold clause.

  1. The conclusion that the researchers reached was controversial.
  2. The conclusion that the treatment was ineffective was controversial.
  3. The assumption that underlies the model is unrealistic.
  4. The assumption that all consumers behave rationally is unrealistic.
  5. The news that surprised everyone spread quickly.
  6. The news that the factory would close spread quickly.
  7. The argument that the author develops in Chapter 3 is complex.
  8. The argument that governments should regulate the market is complex.
Show the answers
  1. Relative: the researchers reached the conclusion.
  2. Content: it states the conclusion.
  3. Relative: the assumption underlies the model.
  4. Content: it states the assumption.
  5. Relative: the news surprised everyone.
  6. Content: it states what the news was.
  7. Relative: the author develops the argument.
  8. Content: it states the argument.

Exercise 8: edit the paragraph

The paragraph contains problems with commas, relative words, omission, agreement, prepositions, and clause type. Rewrite it accurately.

The research center that was opened in 2022, studies urban pollution. Its director, that previously worked for the health ministry has hired several scientists which specialize in air quality. One of the researchers who studies traffic emissions have developed a sensor that the center depends. The sensor, the accuracy of which have been tested repeatedly can detect particles that older devices often miss them. The claim which the device is cheaper than existing models has attracted attention, which several companies have contacted the center. The laboratory where the sensor was tested it is now being expanded.

Show a corrected version

The research center, which was opened in 2022, studies urban pollution. Its director, who previously worked for the health ministry, has hired several scientists who specialize in air quality. One of the researchers who study traffic emissions has developed a sensor on which the center depends. The sensor, the accuracy of which has been tested repeatedly, can detect particles that older devices often miss. The claim that the device is cheaper than existing models has attracted attention, which has led several companies to contact the center. The laboratory where the sensor was tested is now being expanded.

The first clause is non-defining because the research center is presented as an already identified institution. In one of the researchers who study..., who refers to the plural noun researchers. The clause after claim expresses content, so it uses that, not which.

Exercise 9: build a controlled paragraph

Write a short paragraph of four to six sentences about a course, workplace, public service, scientific issue, or community project. Include all of the following:

  • one defining relative clause;
  • one non-defining relative clause;
  • one omitted object relative word;
  • one relative clause containing a preposition;
  • one clause with whose;
  • one content clause after a noun such as fact, idea, claim, possibility, or evidence.
Show a sample paragraph

Our university recently introduced a recycling program that serves all residence halls. The program coordinator, who previously managed a community environmental project, trained the volunteers personally. The bins students use most often are placed near the main entrances. One problem that the organizers are dealing with is contamination from ordinary waste. The volunteers whose shifts begin in the morning check the bins before classes start. There is growing evidence that clear labels improve recycling accuracy.

Before moving to Part 3

Make sure you can distinguish identifying information from added comments, decide when an object relative word can disappear, preserve necessary prepositions, and tell relative clauses apart from content clauses. Part 3 will shorten many of these full clauses into participial, adjective, prepositional, and infinitive structures.

Part 2 final checklist

  • I can distinguish defining and non-defining relative clauses by meaning.
  • I can use commas to show whether information identifies or comments.
  • I know that that cannot introduce a non-defining clause.
  • I can decide when an object relative word may be omitted.
  • I know that subject relative words cannot normally be omitted.
  • I can place a preposition naturally at the end or formally at the front.
  • I know that fronted prepositions require whom or which.
  • I can choose among where, when, why, that, and which by reconstructing the hidden relationship.
  • I can use whose for people and things.
  • I can understand and form formal the noun of which structures.
  • I can make relative-clause verbs agree with their antecedents.
  • I understand one of those who... and the only one who....
  • I can use which to comment on an entire event.
  • I can keep the reference of sentential which clear.
  • I can coordinate or stack multiple relative descriptions without losing clarity.
  • I can distinguish a relative clause from a noun-content clause.
  • I can edit sentences for accuracy, register, and readability.

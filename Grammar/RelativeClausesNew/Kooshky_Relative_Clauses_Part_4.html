Ordinary Relative Clauses and Free Relatives

In an ordinary relative construction, a noun comes first and a relative clause describes it. In a free relative, no separate noun appears before the clause. The relative word itself contains the missing noun meaning.

An ordinary relative has an expressed noun

Ordinary relative clause

I found the book that you recommended.

The noun the book appears before the relative clause. The clause that you recommended describes it.

Noun
the book
Relative clause
that you recommended
Hidden sentence
You recommended the book.

A free relative contains its own noun meaning

Free relative

I found what I needed.

What I needed means approximately the thing that I needed. There is no separate noun before what.

Ordinary relative

I found the thing that I needed.

Free relative

I found what I needed.

Why it is called free

The clause is “free” from an expressed noun before it. It does not attach to a separate antecedent such as the thing, the person, or the place. The noun meaning and the relative connection are fused together.

Other names you may see

Grammar books use several labels for these structures:

free relative clause
fused relative clause
headless relative clause
nominal relative clause in some traditional descriptions

The labels highlight slightly different ideas, but they usually refer to the same central pattern. This guide mainly uses free relative and occasionally fused relative.

A free relative acts like a noun phrase in the larger sentence

Although a free relative contains a clause internally, the whole structure can perform the jobs normally performed by a noun phrase.

Subject

What I need is rest.

≈ The thing that I need is rest.

Object

I understand what you mean.

The entire clause is the object of understand.

After a preposition

We talked about what had happened.

Subject complement

This is what I was looking for.

Object complement-like description

Call the situation whatever you like.

Place expression

Put the chair wherever you want.

The relative word has an internal role and the whole clause has an external role

A free relative has two levels of structure:

Example

I will buy whatever fits the room.

Inside the clause
Whatever is the subject of fits.
In the full sentence
The whole clause whatever fits the room is the object of buy.
Another example

Give the key to whoever arrives first.

Inside the clause
Whoever is the subject of arrives.
In the full sentence
The entire free relative follows the preposition to.

Free relatives do not need a second noun before them

Common error

The thing what I need is rest.

The thing that I need is rest.

What I need is rest.

Use either an ordinary noun followed by a relative clause or a free relative. Do not combine both patterns.

Another common error

The person whoever arrives first will receive the key.

The person who arrives first will receive the key.

Whoever arrives first will receive the key.

Free relatives can refer to more than one thing

The paraphrase the thing that is useful, but what can refer to a collection, an amount, or a situation—not only one countable object.

Several things

Take what you need.

This may mean one item or several items.

Information

What she told me was surprising.

An action or event

I could not believe what he had done.

An amount

He donated what he could afford.

Agreement with a what-clause

A free relative used as a subject is often treated as one unit and therefore takes a singular verb:

Clause treated as one unit

What worries me is the cost.

When a following complement is plural, both singular and plural agreement may occur in natural English:

Singular agreement

What we need is two more chairs.

The subject clause is treated as one need or one answer.

Notional plural agreement

What we need are two more chairs.

The plural complement influences the verb. This is also found in standard usage.

Practical advice

When the complement is singular or uncountable, use singular agreement: What I need is rest. With a clearly plural complement, both forms occur. Rewriting is always possible if the sentence feels awkward: We need two more chairs.

Free relatives are complete units

Do not separate the relative word from the rest of its clause with a comma.

No internal comma

What I need, is a quiet room.

What I need is a quiet room.

Whoever finishes first, will receive a prize.

Whoever finishes first will receive a prize.

What to remember

  • An ordinary relative clause follows an expressed noun.
  • A free relative contains the noun meaning inside the relative word.
  • The whole free relative can act as a subject, object, complement, or prepositional complement.
  • The relative word has a role inside the clause while the whole clause has a role in the larger sentence.
  • Do not put what or an -ever word after a separate noun with the same meaning.
  • Free relatives are complete grammatical units and do not take an internal comma.

What, Whatever, and Whichever

These forms all refer to things, choices, information, actions, or situations, but they do not express exactly the same meaning. What identifies the relevant thing or things. Whatever removes restrictions. Whichever normally selects from a known or limited set.

What means the thing or things that

Subject inside the clause

I will accept what seems reasonable.

What is the subject of seems.

Object inside the clause

I will accept what you recommend.

You recommend something; what represents that object.

After a preposition inside

This is what I was referring to.

Possessive relationship expressed separately

I could not understand what its purpose was.

This is normally an embedded question rather than a free relative; the distinction is covered later.

What does not normally refer to people

People versus things

I spoke to what was in charge.

I spoke to whoever was in charge.

I spoke to the person who was in charge.

What versus that which

The formal expression that which can sometimes replace what, especially in philosophical, legal, or highly formal writing.

Natural

We must distinguish what is possible from what is desirable.

Formal

We must distinguish that which is possible from that which is desirable.

In ordinary English, what is usually more direct and natural.

What versus all that

What

I told her what I knew.

the information that I knew

All that

I told her all that I knew.

everything that I knew; emphasizes completeness

Avoid all what

I told her all what I knew.

I told her what I knew.

I told her all that I knew.

I told her everything I knew.

Whatever as an unrestricted free relative

Whatever can mean anything that or everything that, depending on context.

Anything that

Choose whatever seems useful.

Everything that

She wrote down whatever the witness said.

No restriction

You may use whatever is available.

Object after a preposition

We will adapt to whatever happens.

Whatever as a concessive word

An -ever clause can also mean “no matter what.” In this use, the clause does not act as an ordinary noun phrase; it sets up a condition that does not change the result.

Free relative object

I will buy whatever you recommend.

I will buy anything that you recommend.

Concessive clause

Whatever you recommend, I will make the final decision myself.

No matter what you recommend, the result will be the same.

Position helps

A fronted whatever clause followed by a comma often has the “no matter what” meaning. A clause filling a normal noun-phrase position usually has the free-relative meaning.

Whichever selects from a limited set

Whichever means any one—or sometimes any ones—from a known or limited group.

Two choices

Take whichever route is shorter.

Known options

You may choose whichever of the three topics interests you most.

Followed by a noun

Use whichever method produces clearer results.

Plural selection

Select whichever files you need.

Whatever versus whichever

Whatever: open or unrestricted

Choose whatever topic you like.

Any topic at all; the set is not presented as limited.

Whichever: selection from a set

Choose whichever topic you prefer from the list.

The choice comes from an identified set.

Whatever and whichever followed by nouns

Unlike ordinary what in standard modern English, whatever and whichever can directly modify a following noun.

Take whatever books you need.

Choose whichever book looks most useful.

Whatever decision you make, explain it clearly.

Whichever option you select, the cost will be similar.

What + noun is limited in modern standard English

Modern English normally uses which, what kind of, or a free relative rather than plain what + noun in an ordinary statement.

Standard choices

Take what book you need.

Take whichever book you need.

Take the book that you need.

Take what you need.

You will still see what + noun in questions and exclamations:

Question

What book are you reading?

Exclamation

What a remarkable result!

Those are not the ordinary free-relative pattern studied here.

Whatever you do

The fixed-looking expression whatever you do often introduces strong advice or a warning.

Whatever you do, do not delete the original file.

Whatever you do, keep a copy of your receipt.

The meaning is “no matter what else you do.”

  • What means the thing or things that and normally refers to non-human meanings.

  • Do not use all what; use what, all that, or everything that.
  • Position and context distinguish free-relative and concessive uses.

Whoever and Whomever

Whoever and whomever refer to an unrestricted or unknown person. The most important grammatical principle is that case is determined by the word’s role inside its own clause—not by the role of the whole free relative in the larger sentence.

Whoever as the subject inside its clause

Whole clause as subject

Whoever finishes first will receive a prize.

Inside the clause, whoever performs finishes.

Whole clause as object

We will hire whoever performs best.

The whole clause is the object of hire, but inside it, whoever is the subject of performs.

Whole clause after a preposition

Give the form to whoever asks for it.

The whole clause follows to; internally, whoever is the subject of asks.

A famous trap

In Give it to whoever asks, do not choose whomever merely because the clause follows the preposition to. The preposition governs the whole clause. Inside that clause, the person asks, so whoever is the subject.

Whomever as the object inside its clause

Object of trust

Hire whomever you trust.

You trust that person. Whomever is the object of trust.

Object of choose

The committee may select whomever it considers most qualified.

Object after an internal preposition

Invite whomever you have been working with.

You have been working with that person.

Use the hidden clause, not the larger sentence

Subject internally

Give the award to whoever wins.

Whoever wins. The person is the subject.

Object internally

Give the award to whomever the judges select.

The judges select that person. The person is the object.

Subject internally

We will support whoever needs help.

Object internally

We will support whomever you nominate.

Whomever is highly formal

In contemporary English, many speakers use whoever in both subject and object positions, especially in conversation.

Formal distinction

Choose whomever you prefer.

Common modern usage

Choose whoever you prefer.

Practical advice

Understand the formal distinction for careful writing and grammar analysis. In normal speech, whoever is widely accepted as the natural choice. Avoid forcing whomever into a sentence unless you are confident that it is the object inside its own clause.

Whoever versus anyone who

Free relative

Whoever needs assistance should ask.

Ordinary noun + relative

Anyone who needs assistance should ask.

The meanings are often very similar. Anyone who can sound slightly more transparent to learners, while whoever is more compact.

Whoever as a concessive word

Free relative

I will speak to whoever is responsible.

the person who is responsible, whoever that is

Concessive

Whoever is responsible, the problem must be fixed.

No matter who is responsible

Whoever he is

A pattern such as whoever he is explicitly includes a subject after whoever. Here whoever functions as a complement after is, not as the subject.

Whoever he is, he does not have permission to enter.

I do not trust him, whoever he may be.

These constructions normally have a concessive meaning: no matter who he is.

Whoever versus who ever

The single word whoever introduces the structures in this chapter. The two-word sequence who ever appears when ever adds emphasis to a question.

Free relative

Whoever called left no message.

Emphatic question

Who ever told you that?

More commonly written: Who on earth told you that?

Agreement after whoever

Whoever normally takes a singular verb when it refers to an unknown individual:

Whoever is responsible should report the problem.

When the meaning clearly allows multiple people, English often uses a plural paraphrase rather than forcing plural agreement after whoever:

Individual interpretation

Whoever wants a copy may take one.

Transparent plural alternative

All those who want copies may take them.

  • Whoever is used when the person is the subject inside the free relative.
  • Formal whomever is used when the person is the object inside the free relative.
  • The external role of the whole clause does not determine the form.
  • Whoever is widely used for objects in contemporary everyday English.
  • An initial whoever clause followed by a comma often means no matter who.
  • Use the hidden internal clause to analyze case accurately.

Wherever, Whenever, and Related Forms

English can fuse place and time meanings into structures such as where I live, wherever you go, and whenever you need help. However, not every clause beginning with where or when is a relative clause. Some are embedded questions or ordinary adverbial clauses.

Where as a free relative of place

A clause beginning with where can mean the place where or the place in which.

Expressed place noun

Stay in the place where you feel safest.

Free relative

Stay where you feel safest.

Destination

Put the boxes where they belong.

≈ in the place where they belong

Location

I live where the river meets the sea.

Source

We returned to where the trail began.

Abstract state

The discussion returned to where it had started.

Wherever as an unrestricted place relative

Wherever can mean any place where.

Any place

Sit wherever you like.

Unrestricted destination

I will follow you wherever you go.

Location choice

The equipment can be installed wherever space is available.

Source

Order the materials from wherever you find the best price.

This is possible, though from whichever supplier offers... is often clearer because a supplier is a business, not merely a place.

Wherever with a concessive meaning

Wherever can also mean no matter where.

Place free relative

We will meet wherever you choose.

at any place that you choose

Concessive

Wherever you choose to meet, some participants will have to travel.

no matter where you choose

When as a time expression

A when-clause can have several different analyses. Compare the larger role it plays.

Ordinary relative

I remember the day when we met.

The noun day is expressed.

Embedded question

I cannot remember when we met.

The meaning is: I cannot remember the answer to “When did we meet?”

Adverbial time clause

I was nervous when we met.

The clause tells us when I was nervous. It is not functioning as a noun-like free relative.

Time referred to as an entity

That was when the problem began.

≈ That was the time when the problem began. This is close to a free relative or fused time expression.

Whenever

Whenever can mean at any time when or every time that.

Any suitable time

Come whenever you are ready.

Every time

Whenever the alarm rings, everyone must leave the building.

Time as complement

The appointment can be whenever you prefer.

Repeated situation

She takes notes whenever the professor gives an example.

Many whenever-clauses are best understood as adverbial time clauses rather than noun-like free relatives. The meaning and larger sentence role matter more than forcing every example into a single label.

Whenever as no matter when

Time condition

Call me whenever you arrive.

at the time when you arrive

Concessive emphasis

Whenever you arrive, someone will be available to help.

no matter when you arrive

Why does not normally form a free relative

A clause beginning with why usually expresses an embedded question or follows an expressed noun such as reason.

Ordinary relative

I understand the reason why she left.

Embedded question

I understand why she left.

I understand the answer or explanation to the question “Why did she leave?”

It is not normally useful to analyze why she left as meaning “the reason that she left” in exactly the same fused way as what she bought means “the thing that she bought.”

How is generally not an ordinary relative word

A clause beginning with how usually expresses manner in an embedded question or content clause:

Embedded question

I do not know how she solved the problem.

I do not know the answer to “How did she solve it?”

Manner content

Show me how the machine works.

The way how

I liked the way how she explained it.

I liked the way she explained it.

I liked how she explained it.

I liked the way in which she explained it.

Where and when without an expressed noun do not always have the same grammar

Compare these three functions:

Place complement
Stay where you are. The clause supplies a place.
Embedded question
I know where you are. The clause supplies the answer to “Where are you?”
Adverbial clause
I will call when I arrive. The clause tells when the calling will happen.
Do not classify from the first word alone

To understand a where, when, why, or how clause, examine what the entire clause does in the larger sentence and what question or noun meaning it supplies.

  • Where can supply a place meaning without an expressed place noun.
  • Wherever can mean any place where or no matter where.

  • Why and how usually introduce embedded questions or content-like clauses rather than ordinary free relatives.
  • Never classify a clause from its first word alone; examine its job in the whole sentence.

Free Relatives Versus Embedded Questions

Free relatives and embedded questions can begin with the same words: what, who, where, when, which, or how. Their internal word order may also look identical. The difference lies in meaning and in the role the clause plays.

A free relative refers to an entity

A free relative usually refers to a thing, person, place, or amount. It can often be paraphrased with a noun followed by a relative clause.

Thing

I ate what she cooked.

≈ I ate the food that she cooked.

Person

We hired whoever had the most experience.

≈ We hired the person who had the most experience.

Place

I sat where she sat.

≈ I sat in the place where she sat.

An embedded question refers to an answer

An embedded question reports or refers to a question inside a larger sentence. The clause represents the unknown or known answer.

What question

I wonder what she cooked.

The underlying question is: What did she cook?

Who question

We do not know who had the most experience.

The underlying question is: Who had the most experience?

Where question

I asked where she sat.

The underlying question is: Where did she sit?

The paraphrase test

Free relative paraphrase

I bought what she recommended.

≈ I bought the thing that she recommended.

Embedded question paraphrase

I asked what she recommended.

≈ I asked the question “What did she recommend?”

Two useful questions

Can the clause be replaced by the thing/person/place that...? It is probably a free relative.

Does the clause express the answer to a direct question? It is probably an embedded question.

The selecting verb often guides the analysis

Some verbs naturally take things, people, or places as their objects. Others naturally take information or questions.

Entity-selecting verbs

eat, buy, use, take, hire, visit, put, live

I used what you gave me.

I used the thing that you gave me.

Question/information verbs

wonder, ask, discover, investigate, decide, determine

I discovered what had caused the error.

I discovered the answer to what caused it.

Know, remember, understand, and forget can be less obvious

Some verbs can relate either to an entity or to information. Context determines the interpretation.

Information reading

I know what she bought.

I know the answer to “What did she buy?” This is normally an embedded question.

Entity reading

I recognized what she bought.

I recognized the object that she bought. This is closer to a free relative.

Remembered content

I remember what he said.

This can be understood as remembering the words or information he said. Analyses may differ because the meanings overlap.

Clear entity use

I repeated what he said.

I repeated the words that he said: a clear free-relative interpretation.

Some boundaries are genuinely fuzzy

Grammar categories describe patterns, but meaning does not always divide perfectly. When a clause can be understood both as an entity and as information, different grammatical analyses may be possible. The important skill is recognizing the two central meanings.

Embedded questions use statement word order

A direct question normally uses inversion. An embedded question does not.

Direct question

Where does she live?

What did he buy?

Why was the meeting cancelled?

Embedded question

I know where she lives.

I wonder what he bought.

Do you know why the meeting was cancelled?

Common word-order error

I do not know where does she live.

I do not know where she lives.

Can you tell me what did he buy?

Can you tell me what he bought?

Question marks depend on the whole sentence

Statement

I wonder what happened.

Whole sentence is a question

Do you know what happened?

The embedded clause itself does not automatically require a question mark.

Which and whichever

Which often introduces an embedded question involving a limited set. Whichever can form a free relative meaning any one from that set.

Embedded question

I cannot decide which route is shorter.

I cannot answer the question.

Free relative

I will take whichever route is shorter.

I will take the route that turns out to be shorter.

Who versus whoever

Embedded question

I do not know who called.

Free relative

I will speak to whoever called.

How clauses

A clause beginning with how usually expresses an embedded question of manner or method:

Explain how the device works.

I learned how the team solved the problem.

Partitive Relative Clauses: Some of Whom and Many of Which

A partitive relative clause describes a quantity or portion of an already identified group. Common patterns include some of whom, many of which, three of whom, each of which, and none of whom.

The basic pattern

plural noun, + quantity/number + of + whom/which + clause

People

The course has twenty students, six of whom are international.

Things

The report contains ten recommendations, three of which require additional funding.

The first clause identifies the complete group. The partitive relative then comments on a portion of that group.

Whole group
twenty students
Part
six of the students
Relative form
six of whom

Common quantifiers

Some

The museum owns several paintings, some of which need restoration.

Many

We interviewed thirty applicants, many of whom had relevant experience.

Most

The department introduced new rules, most of which were unpopular.

All

The team tested five devices, all of which worked correctly.

None

She contacted several advisers, none of whom replied.

Both

The study used two methods, both of which produced similar results.

Several

The article raises important questions, several of which remain unanswered.

A few

The village has old houses, a few of which date from the eighteenth century.

Exact numbers and fractions

Number

The class has eighteen students, four of whom are absent today.

Fraction

The survey included 600 participants, half of whom were under thirty.

Percentage

The company surveyed its employees, 72 percent of whom supported the change.

Ordinal portion

The series contains four volumes, the first of which introduces the main theory.

Whom for people and which for things

Relative word choice

The students, some of which were absent, missed the announcement.

The students, some of whom were absent, missed the announcement.

The documents, several of whom were incomplete, were returned.

The documents, several of which were incomplete, were returned.

For organizations and groups, choose according to whether you are thinking of the people or the entity:

Members as people

The committee has twelve members, three of whom opposed the plan.

Committees as entities

The university formed four committees, two of which have completed their work.

Partitive relatives are supplementary

These structures add information about an already identified group. They are normally non-defining and therefore require a comma.

Comma required

The course has twenty students six of whom are international.

The course has twenty students, six of whom are international.

No that and no omission

Fixed relative form

The books, some of that were damaged, were removed.

The books, some of which were damaged, were removed.

The students, three of were absent, missed the test.

The students, three of whom were absent, missed the test.

Word order

The quantity comes before of whom or of which.

Correct order

The students, of whom three were absent, missed the announcement.

The students, three of whom were absent, missed the announcement.

The reversed order can occur in rare literary or highly formal styles, but learners should use the standard quantity + of whom/which pattern.

Agreement after the quantifier

The verb agrees with the quantity expression inside the relative clause.

Plural number

The students, three of whom are absent, must submit the form.

Each

The devices, each of which is expensive, require insurance.

Both

The proposals, both of which are practical, will be discussed.

Neither

The two solutions, neither of which is ideal, remain under consideration.

With none of whom/which, both singular and plural agreement occur. Plural is often natural when the group consists of countable people or things:

Plural agreement

Ten applicants applied, none of whom were qualified.

Singular agreement

Several explanations were offered, none of which was convincing.

Prepositions before the partitive expression

A larger prepositional phrase can contain the quantity + of whom/which structure.

For

The program supports fifty students, for many of whom the scholarship is essential.

Among

The collection includes rare manuscripts, among which are several medieval texts.

From

The researchers selected eight samples, from three of which they extracted usable DNA.

With

She interviewed ten teachers, with two of whom she later collaborated.

Partitive relative versus a new independent clause

Relative connection

We tested five devices, two of which failed.

Independent clause

We tested five devices, and two of them failed.

Avoid a comma splice

We tested five devices, two of them failed.

We tested five devices, two of which failed.

We tested five devices, and two of them failed.

We tested five devices; two of them failed.

Defining alternatives

A partitive relative comments on a known total. If the quantity itself identifies the people or things, use an ordinary defining structure.

Known group, added quantity

The class has twenty students, six of whom study engineering.

Identifying six students

The six students who study engineering are working together.

Advanced Possession and Whole–Part Relations

Part 2 introduced whose and formal the noun of which structures. Advanced English combines these patterns with numbers, quantifiers, parts, features, and longer noun phrases: several of whose employees, the roof of which, two chapters of which, and each of whose members.

Quantifier + of whose + noun

person or thing, + quantity + of whose + noun + clause
People connected to an organization

The company employs eighty people, several of whose contracts expire this month.

Several of the employees’ contracts expire.

Members of a group

The committee has twelve members, each of whose roles is clearly defined.

Parts of a place

We visited an old town, many of whose buildings need restoration.

Features of a theory

The article examines two theories, neither of whose assumptions can be tested directly.

How the structure is built

Two statements

The university has twenty departments.

Several of the university’s departments offer evening courses.

Combined

The university has twenty departments, several of which offer evening courses.

Here the departments themselves form the group, so several of which is sufficient. Compare a true possessive relationship:

Possessive combination

The university has twenty departments, several of whose buildings need renovation.

The buildings belong to or are associated with several departments.

Of which versus of whose

Several of which

The company opened ten branches, several of which later closed.

Several branches closed. The relative word directly represents the branches.

Several of whose + noun

The company opened ten branches, several of whose managers later resigned.

The managers are connected to several branches.

Numbers with whose

Two of whose

The professor supervises six students, two of whose projects involve fieldwork.

One of whose

We interviewed four designers, one of whose proposals was selected.

Half of whose

The organization supports forty schools, half of whose classrooms lack reliable internet access.

All of whose

The series contains three experiments, all of whose results support the same conclusion.

Agreement inside advanced whose structures

The verb agrees with the noun after whose, not with the earlier antecedent or the quantifier alone.

Plural possessed noun

The company has five branches, each of whose managers is experienced.

The subject is singular manager inside each branch’s description.

Plural possessed noun

The company has five branches, each of whose employees is trained locally.

The subject is plural employees.

Singular feature

We tested three methods, none of whose costs were acceptable.

More naturally: none of which had an acceptable cost. The example shows that awkward noun choices may need rewriting even when agreement is possible.

The noun + of which pattern

Formal English can place a part or feature before of which.

thing, + determiner/quantity + noun + of which + clause
Feature

The building, the roof of which was damaged, has been closed.

Several parts

The book contains ten chapters, three of which discuss pronunciation.

Part plus noun

The book, three chapters of which discuss pronunciation, is aimed at teachers.

Formal academic feature

The study, the limitations of which are discussed below, used a small sample.

Three chapters of which versus three of which

Three of which

The book contains ten chapters, three of which discuss pronunciation.

The antecedent is the ten chapters. Three chapters discuss pronunciation.

Three chapters of which

The book, three chapters of which discuss pronunciation, is aimed at teachers.

The antecedent is the book. Three chapters of the book discuss pronunciation.

Whose versus the noun of which

Natural and compact

The building, whose roof was damaged, has been closed.

Formal and feature-focused

The building, the roof of which was damaged, has been closed.

Natural

A theory whose assumptions are difficult to test

Formal

A theory the assumptions of which are difficult to test

Choose clarity, not weight

The noun of which form is useful in formal writing, but it is heavier. Use whose when it is clear and natural. Use the formal form when the part or feature deserves emphasis or when it fits the surrounding style.

Defining and supplementary forms

Advanced possessive relatives can identify a noun or add information.

Defining

We need a supplier several of whose products meet the new standard.

The clause helps identify the kind of supplier needed.

Supplementary

We selected Arman Textiles, several of whose products meet the new standard.

The company name identifies the supplier; the clause adds a fact.

Common word-order errors

Keep of before whose

The school, several whose classrooms need repairs, has requested funding.

The school, several of whose classrooms need repairs, has requested funding.

Do not add another possessive

The company, many of whose its employees work remotely, changed its policy.

The company, many of whose employees work remotely, changed its policy.

Use a noun after whose

The teams, two of whose won prizes, returned home.

The teams, two of which won prizes, returned home.

The teams, two of whose members won prizes, returned home.

Avoid excessively heavy possession

Overloaded

The organization, several of whose regional offices’ training programs have recently been redesigned, announced a review.

Clearer

The organization recently redesigned training programs in several regional offices. It has now announced a review.

Supplementary Relatives with As

The word as can introduce a supplementary clause that refers to an entire fact or situation. These clauses often present the information as expected, known, reported, visible, or typical.

Basic as-relatives

Expected

The plan was rejected, as we expected.

Previously mentioned

As I mentioned earlier, the deadline is flexible.

Visible evidence

As can be seen from the graph, sales declined in May.

Typical situation

As is often the case, the simplest solution was the most effective.

As can appear before, inside, or after the main clause

Before

As everyone knows, water expands when it freezes.

Inside

Water, as everyone knows, expands when it freezes.

After

Water expands when it freezes, as everyone knows.

The flexible position is one of the major differences between as and sentential which.

Which normally follows the fact it refers to

Position difference

Which surprised everyone, the plan was rejected.

The plan was rejected, which surprised everyone.

As everyone expected, the plan was rejected.

As presents a relationship of correspondence

An as-clause often means “in the way that,” “just as,” or “as was already known or expected.”

Expected correspondence

The results were inconclusive, as we had predicted.

The result matched our prediction.

New reaction or result

The results were inconclusive, which disappointed the researchers.

The second clause adds a new consequence or reaction.

As versus which

As: expected, known, typical, reported

The meeting lasted three hours, as we had expected.

Which: new comment or consequence

The meeting lasted three hours, which exhausted everyone.

As before the main clause

As was reported yesterday, the factory will close.

Which after the main clause

The factory will close, which was reported yesterday.

This is grammatical, but it may sound as though the closing itself was reported rather than introducing the report as shared background.

As can be the subject or object inside its clause

Subject-like role

The policy failed, as was widely predicted.

The fact that the policy failed was widely predicted.

Object-like role

The policy failed, as many experts had predicted.

Many experts had predicted that fact.

Common formal as-expressions

as noted above
as discussed earlier
as shown in Figure 2
as can be seen from the data
as might be expected
as is well known
as often happens
as was previously reported
as follows
Use with care

Expressions such as as everyone knows and as is well known claim shared knowledge. Do not use them when the fact is debatable or when the reader may reasonably not know it.

As is often the case

General pattern

As is often the case with new technology, early users experienced several problems.

The expression comments that this situation is typical.

Inserted comment

Early users, as is often the case with new technology, experienced several problems.

The initial version is usually clearer because the as-clause comments on the whole situation, not merely the users.

As follows

The expression as follows introduces a list, explanation, or exact formulation.

List introduction

The main requirements are as follows: submit the form, pay the fee, and upload a photograph.

Fixed form

The requirements are as follow.

The requirements are as follows.

The form remains follows even after a plural noun.

Such ... as

In formal English, such + noun + as can identify examples or a class of people or things.

Examples

Renewable sources such as solar and wind power are expanding.

Here such as introduces examples; it is not a full relative clause.

Formal relative pattern

Such applicants as meet the minimum requirement will be interviewed.

≈ Applicants who meet the minimum requirement. This form is formal and uncommon in everyday English.

The same ... as and the same ... that

These forms can express different meanings, although actual usage varies.

The same type or matching choice

I bought the same model as you recommended.

The model matches your recommendation.

The exact individual thing

I bought the same laptop that you tested yesterday.

The sentence points to the exact laptop you tested.

In everyday English, the same ... that is common for exact identity, while the same ... as often expresses similarity or correspondence.

Relative Connective Phrases

Advanced writing often links a whole event to its consequence, timing, condition, or next stage through phrases such as in which case, at which point, by which time, after which, and as a result of which.

In which case

In which case means “if that is the situation” or “if that happens.”

Condition

The supplier may refuse the request, in which case we will contact another company.

Possible outcome

The exam could be postponed, in which case the new date will be announced online.

The phrase refers to the whole preceding possibility, not to one noun.

At which point

At which point refers to a particular stage or moment created by the preceding event.

The server stopped responding, at which point the technician restarted it.

The discussion became increasingly heated, at which point the chair ended the meeting.

By which time

By which time sets a later deadline or reference point.

The project will finish in September, by which time all staff must complete the training.

We reached the station at midnight, by which time the last train had left.

After which and before which

These forms establish sequence between events.

After

The participants completed a short test, after which they were interviewed.

Before

The committee will review the evidence on Friday, before which no decision will be announced.

The version with before which is formal. A separate sentence or before then may sound more natural.

During which time

Duration

The laboratory will close for two weeks, during which time the ventilation system will be replaced.

The phrase refers to the two-week period.

For which reason

For which reason means “because of that.” It is grammatical but formal and often heavier than therefore, so, or a separate sentence.

Formal relative connective

The data were incomplete, for which reason the study was repeated.

More natural alternatives

The data were incomplete, so the study was repeated.

The data were incomplete. For this reason, the study was repeated.

As a result of which

Formal consequence

Several files were deleted, as a result of which the report had to be rewritten.

This pattern can be useful when the consequence is complex, but sentential which or a new sentence is often simpler.

Formal

Several files were deleted, as a result of which the report had to be rewritten.

Direct

Several files were deleted, which meant that the report had to be rewritten.

Several files were deleted. As a result, the report had to be rewritten.

From which, through which, and upon which

These may refer to a noun or to a stage in a process.

Source

The archive contains a database, from which researchers can download the records.

Means or route

The website provides a secure portal, through which applicants can upload documents.

Event trigger

The board approved the plan, upon which the director authorized the payment.

Upon which is highly formal; after which is usually clearer.

Relative determiner phrases

In expressions such as in which case and by which time, which modifies a noun inside the connective phrase.

which case
the situation just described
which point
the stage created by the previous event
which time
the time just established

Punctuation and restrictions

Supplementary connection

These phrases normally refer back to an already complete event or time expression and are separated with a comma.

The server may fail in which case the backup will activate.

The server may fail, in which case the backup will activate.

No that and no omission

The meeting may be cancelled, in that case we will email everyone.

The meeting may be cancelled, in which case we will email everyone.

The meeting may be cancelled. In that case, we will email everyone.

Choose a phrase that matches the relationship

in which case → condition or possible situation
at which point → stage or moment
by which time → deadline or completed-before relationship
after which → later event
during which time → event inside a period
as a result of which → consequence
Do not use complexity for its own sake

Relative connective phrases are useful when they express a precise relationship compactly. A simple conjunction or a new sentence is often better when the formal phrase makes the sentence heavy.

    Direct: The laboratory closed for a month. During that period, the equipment was replaced.

Practice 3: correct the connection

  1. The deadline may be extended in which case we will update the website.
  2. The server crashed, at that point the technician restarted it.
  3. The building will close until September, by that time the repairs must be complete.
  4. The participants signed the forms, after that they entered the laboratory. (one sentence required)
  5. The data were unreliable, for which the experiment was repeated.
  6. The road flooded, as a result which the village was isolated.
Show possible corrections
  1. The deadline may be extended, in which case we will update the website.
  2. The server crashed, at which point the technician restarted it.
  3. The building will close until September, by which time the repairs must be complete.
  4. The participants signed the forms, after which they entered the laboratory.
  5. The data were unreliable, for which reason the experiment was repeated. More naturally: ..., so the experiment was repeated.
  6. The road flooded, as a result of which the village was isolated.

What to remember

  • Relative connective phrases link a completed event to a condition, time, sequence, or result.
  • In which case, at which point, by which time, and after which have distinct meanings.
  • They are normally supplementary and require a comma.
  • Do not replace which with that inside these structures.
  • Use a simpler conjunction or separate sentence when it is clearer.

Relative Clauses After Pronouns and General Heads

Relative clauses often follow words such as someone, anyone, those, everything, all, the one, and the only person. These patterns allow English to refer to people or things without first naming them precisely.

Someone, anyone, everyone, and no one

Someone

We need someone who understands the software.

Anyone

Anyone who wishes to participate may register.

Everyone

Everyone who completed the survey received a code.

No one

No one who works here has access to that room.

These indefinite pronouns are grammatically singular:

Agreement

Anyone who wants a copy may take one.

Anyone who want a copy may take one.

Those who

Those who is a common formal or neutral way to mean “the people who.”

Those who need assistance should contact the office.

The policy protects those who cannot afford private insurance.

Because those is plural, the relative-clause verb is plural:

Singular

Anyone who needs assistance...

Plural

Those who need assistance...

The one who and the ones who

Singular person

She is the one who organized the event.

Plural people

They are the ones who reported the problem.

Thing in informal English

This is the one that I ordered.

Plural things

The blue files are the ones that we need.

He who and she who

Forms such as he who and she who are grammatical but literary, ceremonial, or old-fashioned in ordinary modern English.

Literary or formal

He who hesitates is lost.

Modern neutral

Anyone who hesitates may miss the opportunity.

Use the person who, anyone who, or whoever in normal communication unless a literary tone is intentional.

Everything, something, anything, and nothing

Everything

Everything that happened was recorded.

Something

I need something that works offline.

Anything

Is there anything that I can do?

Nothing

Nothing that he said changed my opinion.

These pronouns are grammatically singular:

Agreement

Everything that affects the result is recorded.

Everything that affect the result are recorded.

Object-relative omission after indefinite pronouns

Anything

Is there anything I can do?

Nothing

There is nothing we can change.

Something

She said something I did not understand.

The omitted relative word is the object inside the clause.

All that

All that means everything that or the only amount/information that.

All that matters is that everyone is safe.

That is all that I know.

We used all that was available.

All what is not standard

All what matters is safety.

All that matters is safety.

What matters is safety.

All, little, and much with omitted relative words

Formal English sometimes uses a very compact defining clause after words such as all, little, and much.

All

She gave me all I needed.

Little

There is little we can do.

Much

Much has been written about the issue.

This example has no relative clause after much; it shows why surface similarity alone is insufficient. Much is simply the subject.

Traditional preferences for that

Traditional grammar guides often recommend that after superlatives, ordinal numbers, words such as all, everything, anything, nothing, and expressions containing only.

Superlative

This is the best explanation that I have heard.

Ordinal

The first article that we read was the most difficult.

Only

The only method that worked was expensive.

Everything

Everything that could go wrong went wrong.

Preference, not an absolute law

These are strong usage tendencies, especially in defining clauses. They are not universal bans on who or which. For people, who is often natural:

She is the only person who understood the question.

She is the only person that understood the question.

After mixed human and non-human antecedents

When one relative clause describes both people and things, that is often the smoothest choice.

Mixed antecedent

The people and equipment that were needed arrived on time.

Rewriting may sound better when the two groups have very different roles:

Compact

The staff and equipment that were needed arrived.

Clearer

The necessary staff arrived, and the equipment was delivered on time.

That cannot be used in supplementary clauses

The traditional preference for that never cancels the non-defining restriction.

No that after a comma

My only laptop, that I bought last year, has stopped working.

My only laptop, which I bought last year, has stopped working.

Nested, Stacked, and Delayed Relative Clauses

Long sentences may contain several relative clauses. Some describe different nouns. Some describe the same noun. Some occur inside another clause, and some are separated from their antecedent. The key skill is finding boundaries and attachments.

Two clauses describing different nouns

Different antecedents

The professor who teaches the course recommended a book that explains the theory clearly.

The first clause describes the professor. The second describes a book.

Two clauses describing the same noun

Stacked defining clauses

We need applicants who have relevant experience and who can begin immediately.

Defining plus supplementary

The report that I submitted last week, which contained the revised figures, has been approved.

The first clause identifies the report. The second adds a fact about the now-identified report.

A relative clause inside another relative clause

Nested relative

The article that the professor who teaches our seminar recommended is difficult.

Main sentence
The article is difficult.
Outer relative
that the professor ... recommended
Inner relative
who teaches our seminar

The bracket method

Mentally place brackets around each clause and connect it to its noun.

Bracketed structure

The article [that the professor [who teaches our seminar] recommended] is difficult.

Then temporarily remove the smaller inserted clause:

The article [that the professor recommended] is difficult.
The professor [who teaches our seminar] recommended the article.

A content clause inside a relative clause

Mixed clause types

The researcher who believes that the treatment is effective will present the results.

Who believes... is a relative clause describing the researcher. That the treatment is effective is a content clause expressing the belief.

A relative clause inside a content clause

Mixed clause types

The claim that the method which the researchers used was unreliable caused concern.

The larger that-clause expresses the content of the claim. Inside it, which the researchers used describes the method.

Repeated that can be grammatical

A sentence may contain adjacent or nearby instances of that with different jobs.

Different functions

The report that the committee believes that the director altered is missing.

The first that introduces an object relative: the committee believes something about the report. The second introduces a content clause after believes. Although the structure can be analyzed, it is difficult to read and should usually be rewritten.

Dense

The report that the committee believes that the director altered is missing.

Clearer

The committee believes that the director altered a report. That report is now missing.

Center embedding

When several clauses are inserted before the main verb arrives, the reader must remember too many unfinished structures.

Heavy center embedding

The report that the committee that the university appointed last year produced was criticized.

Clearer sequence

Last year, the university appointed a committee. The report that the committee produced was criticized.

Delayed or extraposed relative clauses

A relative clause sometimes appears later than its noun, especially when the noun phrase is an indefinite subject and the relative clause is long.

Natural conversational pattern

A student came in who wanted to speak to the director.

The relative clause describes a student, although the verb came in appears between them.

Existential pattern

There are several issues that still need to be resolved.

Here the clause remains directly after issues, but existential sentences commonly introduce a noun before giving a long description.

Delayed heavy clause

A problem has emerged that may affect the entire schedule.

Why a relative clause may be delayed

The relative clause is long or heavy.
Placing it immediately after the noun would delay a short main verb awkwardly.
The noun is indefinite and newly introduced.
The attachment remains obvious because no competing noun intervenes.

When delay becomes dangerous

Ambiguous separation

A report was sent to the manager that contained several errors.

Grammatically, the nearest noun is manager, creating a ridiculous interpretation.

A report that contained several errors was sent to the manager.

Competing nouns

A student spoke to the professor who had lost her ID card.

The relative clause grammatically favors the professor, but the intended person may be the student.

A student who had lost her ID card spoke to the professor.

Stacked modifiers of different types

Layered noun phrase

The students in the evening class who submitted the optional project received extra credit.

The prepositional phrase first narrows the group to the evening class. The relative clause then selects those who submitted the project.

Reduced plus full relative

The data collected during the second trial that showed unusual variation were reexamined.

This is difficult because the second clause may seem to describe trial rather than data. A rewrite is safer.

Ambiguous

The data collected during the second trial that showed unusual variation were reexamined.

Clear

The data showed unusual variation, so the researchers reexamined the results from the second trial.

Reading strategy for long noun phrases

  • Find the main verb. Ask what completes the main sentence.
  • Find the main head noun. Determine what the long description is built around.
  • Bracket each relative or content clause.
  • Connect every clause to its antecedent or controlling word.
  • Temporarily remove inserted clauses. Read the simpler skeleton.
  • Reinsert the clauses one at a time.

    Ambiguity, Scope, and Advanced Editing

    Advanced grammar is not only the ability to build complex structures. It is the ability to see when a grammatically possible sentence allows the wrong interpretation, hides an important distinction, or asks the reader to do too much work.

    Antecedent ambiguity

    A relative clause may appear capable of describing more than one noun.

    Ambiguous

    I spoke to the assistant of the manager who was retiring.

    Was the assistant retiring or the manager?

    Assistant retiring

    I spoke to the retiring assistant of the manager.

    Manager retiring

    I spoke to the assistant of the manager who was retiring.

    Scope ambiguity

    A modifier’s scope is the exact part of the sentence that it applies to.

    Potential scope problem

    The university rejected the applications from students that were incomplete.

    Grammar suggests that the students were incomplete. The intended meaning is probably that the applications were incomplete.

    Clear

    The university rejected the incomplete applications from students.

    Comma-created meaning differences

    Only a subgroup

    The researchers who questioned the method requested another trial.

    All the identified researchers

    The researchers, who questioned the method, requested another trial.

    When the distinction matters, do not leave the reader to guess whether a missing or extra comma was accidental.

    Ambiguous sentential which

    Unclear reference

    The director rejected the recommendation from the committee, which was unexpected.

    Was the recommendation unexpected, the committee unexpected, or the rejection unexpected?

    Rejection was unexpected

    The director’s rejection of the committee’s recommendation was unexpected.

    Recommendation was unexpected

    The director rejected the committee’s unexpected recommendation.

    Ambiguous free relative or question meaning

    Potential overlap

    She remembered what he had written.

    This may focus on the written words as an entity or on remembered information about what he wrote. Usually the overlap causes no communication problem, but a more precise verb can clarify the intended meaning.

    Words as entity

    She quoted what he had written.

    Information or answer

    She recalled the answer to the question of what he had written.

    This is very explicit but heavy. Usually the original sentence is sufficient.

    Partitive relative versus independent clause

    Structure choice

    The project has four stages, two of them require fieldwork.

    The project has four stages, two of which require fieldwork.

    The project has four stages, and two of them require fieldwork.

    The project has four stages; two of them require fieldwork.

    As versus which

    Expected correspondence

    The plan failed, as we had predicted.

    New consequence

    The plan failed, which caused a financial loss.

    Using the wrong connector can distort the relationship:

    Meaning error

    The plan failed, as caused a financial loss.

    The plan failed, which caused a financial loss.

    Overloaded noun phrases

    Too much before the main verb

    The students in the evening program who completed the optional research project that the department introduced last semester and who submitted the final report on time received certificates.

    Edited

    Last semester, the department introduced an optional research project for evening students. Those who completed it and submitted the final report on time received certificates.

    Unclear pronouns inside and after relatives

    Unclear pronoun

    The professor spoke to the student who had criticized her article.

    Whose article: the professor’s or the student’s? Real-world context may help, but the sentence itself is not explicit.

    Professor’s article

    The professor spoke to the student who had criticized the professor’s article.

    Student’s article

    The professor spoke to the student who had criticized her own article.

    The nearest-noun expectation

    Readers normally connect a relative clause to the nearest reasonable noun. A writer who intends a more distant antecedent should restructure the sentence.

    Reader expectation

    The nearest-noun principle is not an absolute grammatical law, but it is a powerful processing expectation. Good editing respects the way readers naturally build meaning.

    Editing strategy 1: move the clause

    Unclear

    I sent the document to the adviser that contained the final figures.

    Moved

    I sent the document that contained the final figures to the adviser.

    Editing strategy 2: turn the clause into an adjective or noun phrase

    Relative clause

    The students who were absent must contact me.

    Adjective

    The absent students must contact me.

    Sentential which

    The committee rejected the plan, which surprised us.

    Explicit noun phrase

    The committee’s rejection of the plan surprised us.

    Editing strategy 3: repeat the noun

    Repeating a noun is sometimes better than using an unclear pronoun or relative word.

    Unclear reference

    The policy affected the program, which was later revised.

    Explicit

    The policy affected the program. The program was later revised.

    Editing strategy 4: split the sentence

    Overloaded

    The organization, many of whose regional offices that were opened after 2020 have struggled financially, is changing its strategy.

    Split

    Many regional offices opened by the organization after 2020 have struggled financially. The organization is therefore changing its strategy.

    Editing strategy 5: restore the full clause

    A reduced or compressed structure may need expansion.

    Compressed and unclear

    The employees handling complaints absent today will return tomorrow.

    Full and clear

    The employees who normally handle complaints and who are absent today will return tomorrow.

    A complete advanced editing routine

    1. Find the main sentence. Identify its subject and main verb.
    2. Mark every clause. Separate relative, content, embedded-question, and adverbial clauses.
    3. Identify every antecedent. Ask what each relative word refers to.
    4. Reconstruct hidden positions. Determine subject, object, possession, place, or time relationships.
    5. Check punctuation. Decide whether information identifies or comments.
    6. Check meaning preserved by reduction. Look for lost tense or modality.
    7. Check attachment and scope. Make sure each modifier is beside the right noun.
    8. Check reader effort. Split or reorder anything that requires rereading.

    • Advanced writing is successful when complexity remains easy to process.

    4. The machine stopped suddenly, ________ the emergency system activated.
    5. The laboratory will close for three weeks, ________ all ventilation units will be replaced.
    6. The deadline is 30 June, ________ every participant must submit the consent form.
    7. ________ can be seen in Table 3, the two groups performed similarly.
    8. The records were lost, ________ the analysis had to be repeated.
    Show the answers
    1. as
    2. which
    3. in which case
    4. at which point
    5. during which time
    6. by which time
    7. As
    8. as a result of which, or more directly: so

    Exercise 7: analyze the nested sentence

    Identify the main sentence, every clause, and each antecedent.

    1. The article that the professor who leads our seminar recommended has been revised.
    2. The claim that the policy which the council introduced reduced traffic is disputed.
    3. The students who joined the project, which was funded by a local company, will present tomorrow.
    4. The report that I submitted last week, which contained data that the laboratory had collected, was approved.
    5. The company whose director believes that the new system will reduce costs has invested heavily in training.
    Show the analysis
    1. Main: The article has been revised.
      Outer relative: that the professor ... recommended → article.
      Inner relative: who leads our seminar → professor.
    2. Main: The claim is disputed.
      Content clause: that the policy ... reduced traffic → claim.
      Relative: which the council introduced → policy.
    3. Main: The students will present tomorrow.
      Defining relative: who joined the project → students.
      Supplementary relative: which was funded by a local company → project.
    4. Main: The report was approved.
      Defining relative: that I submitted last week → report.
      Supplementary relative: which contained data... → report.
      Inner defining relative: that the laboratory had collected → data.
    5. Main: The company has invested heavily in training.
      Possessive relative: whose director believes... → company.
      Content clause: that the new system will reduce costs → content of the director’s belief.

    Exercise 8: correct the advanced errors

    1. The thing what worries me is the cost.
    2. Give the form to whomever arrives first.
    3. I wonder where does the director work.
    4. The course has twenty students, five of which are international.
    5. The company, several whose branches have closed, is restructuring.
    6. Which everyone predicted, the plan failed.
    7. The plan failed, as caused a serious delay.
    8. The meeting may be postponed in which case we will send an email.
    9. The report that the committee that the dean appointed wrote it was rejected.
    10. My two sisters who live abroad are visiting. (both sisters live abroad)
    Show the corrections
    1. What worries me is the cost, or: The thing that worries me...
    2. Give the form to whoever arrives first.
    3. I wonder where the director works.
    4. The course has twenty students, five of whom are international.
    5. The company, several of whose branches have closed, is restructuring.
    6. As everyone predicted, the plan failed.
    7. The plan failed, which caused a serious delay.
    8. The meeting may be postponed, in which case we will send an email.
    9. The report that the committee that the dean appointed wrote was rejected. Better: The dean appointed a committee, but its report was rejected.
    10. My two sisters, who live abroad, are visiting.

    Exercise 9: edit the paragraph

    The paragraph contains errors involving free relatives, embedded questions, partitives, advanced possession, as, connective phrases, punctuation, and attachment. Rewrite it clearly.

    The research team interviewed whoever the director recommended them and asked what did each participant expect from the program. The study included thirty volunteers ten of which lived outside the city. The volunteers, several whose travel costs were high received financial support. Whatever person missed an appointment had to contact the coordinator. The interviews finished in June, by that time all recordings had to be transcribed. The first analysis produced the pattern which the researchers had expected, which was shown in their original prediction. The second analysis produced a different result, as surprised everyone. The report that the assistant who worked remotely prepared it contained four recommendations, two of them required immediate action.

    Show a corrected version

    The research team interviewed whoever the director recommended and asked what each participant expected from the program. The study included thirty volunteers, ten of whom lived outside the city. The volunteers, several of whose travel costs were high, received financial support. Whoever missed an appointment had to contact the coordinator. The interviews finished in June, by which time all recordings had to be transcribed. The first analysis produced the pattern the researchers had expected, as their original prediction had indicated. The second analysis produced a different result, which surprised everyone. The report that the remote assistant prepared contained four recommendations, two of which required immediate action.

    Exercise 10: advanced controlled writing

    Write a paragraph of six to nine sentences about a research project, university policy, workplace change, public service, or community program. Include:

    • one free relative with what, whoever, whatever, whichever, or wherever;
    • one embedded question;
    • one partitive relative;
    • one advanced possessive structure with of whose or the noun of which;
    • one as-relative;
    • one sentential which or relative connective phrase;
    • one nested sentence that remains easy to understand;
    • one deliberate rewrite or split used to prevent ambiguity.
    Show a sample paragraph

    Our university recently introduced a mentoring program for first-year students. The coordinators promised to provide whatever each participant needed during the first semester. Before assigning mentors, they asked which subjects the students found most difficult. The program recruited forty senior students, twelve of whom study engineering. It also works with six academic departments, two of whose advisers provide weekly training. As the coordinators had expected, demand was highest during the examination period. Several mentors requested additional resources, in which case the program supplied online materials. The guide that the adviser who leads the training developed contains practical examples, but the sentence remains manageable because each clause has a clear antecedent. The original draft contained a final sentence with three competing relative clauses, so the editors split it into two sentences.

    The complete relative-clause system

    Across the four parts, you have moved from intuitive recognition to finite relative clauses, reductions, free relatives, partitives, complex possession, whole-clause connections, and advanced editing. The goal is not to place the most complicated structure in every sentence. The goal is to understand the available choices and select the form that communicates the relationship most clearly.

    Part 4 final checklist

    • I can distinguish ordinary relative clauses from free relatives.
    • I understand why what I need contains its own noun meaning.
    • I can use what, whatever, whichever, whoever, and formal whomever.
    • I can distinguish free-relative and concessive -ever clauses.
    • I can identify place and time uses of where, wherever, when, and whenever.
    • I know that why and how usually introduce embedded questions rather than ordinary free relatives.
    • I can distinguish a free relative from an embedded question by meaning and larger sentence role.
    • I use statement word order inside embedded questions.
    • I can form partitive relatives with numbers and quantifiers.
    • I can distinguish some of which from some of whose + noun.
    • I can build advanced possessive structures such as several of whose employees.
    • I can use formal the noun of which structures when they improve precision.
    • I can distinguish supplementary as from sentential which.
    • I can use in which case, at which point, by which time, and related phrases.
    • I understand relative clauses after anyone, those, everything, all, and the one.
    • I understand traditional preferences for that without treating them as absolute laws.
    • I can bracket nested clauses and identify their antecedents.
    • I can recognize when a delayed relative clause is safe or ambiguous.
    • I can edit attachment, scope, punctuation, and reference problems.
    • I can split a grammatically complex sentence when a simpler version communicates better.

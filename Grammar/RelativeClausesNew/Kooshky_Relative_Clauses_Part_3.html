Chapter

Omission Versus Reduction

Before learning the different reduced forms, you need one crucial distinction. Sometimes English simply leaves out an object relative word. At other times, the structure of the relative clause itself changes. These are not the same process.

Omission: only the relative word disappears

Full object relative

The book that I bought was expensive.

Omitted object relative

The book I bought was expensive.

In the second sentence, the clause still contains a normal subject and verb: I bought. Only the object relative word that has disappeared.

Omission

Omission usually happens in a defining object relative clause:

the article (that) we discussed

the person (whom) I spoke to

the software (which) the company uses

Reduction: the verb structure changes

Full relative clause

Students who live off campus must register.

Reduced structure

Students living off campus must register.

Here, more than one word has disappeared. The full verb live has changed into the -ing form living. The result is a shorter structure that still describes students.

Full passive relative

The documents that were submitted yesterday are being reviewed.

Reduced passive structure

The documents submitted yesterday are being reviewed.

The relative word that and the form of be, were, have disappeared. The past participle submitted remains.

Some reduced forms are not technically clauses

The expression reduced relative clause is useful in teaching because it shows the relationship with a full relative clause. However, the final result is not always a clause in the strictest grammatical sense.

Adjective phrase

students interested in biology

≈ students who are interested in biology

Prepositional phrase

the books on the top shelf

≈ the books that are on the top shelf

Adverb-like expression

the people outside

≈ the people who are outside

Infinitive clause

the first student to finish

≈ the first student who finished / who will finish, depending on context

Practical terminology

In this guide, reduced relative structure is used as a broad learner-friendly label. The exact grammatical category of the shortened result matters less than understanding its meaning and knowing when it sounds natural.

Why English uses reduced structures

They avoid unnecessary repetition.
They make descriptions more compact.
They are common in academic and professional writing.
They allow more information to fit smoothly inside a noun phrase.
They can make instructions, notices, and reports more direct.
Academic

The data collected during the second trial were more reliable.

Notice

Students wishing to change courses must complete this form.

Everyday

The people waiting outside have been here for an hour.

Instruction

Place the items marked with a red label in this box.

Reduction is not automatically better

A shorter sentence is not always clearer or more natural. Some verbs reduce smoothly; others do not. Some reductions erase important information about time, possibility, obligation, or completion.

Natural reduction

students who live nearby

students living nearby

Often better as a full clause

people who know the answer

?people knowing the answer

Core principle

Do not reduce a clause merely because a mechanical transformation seems possible. Use the shorter form only when it preserves the intended meaning and sounds natural.

What to remember

  • Omission removes only an object relative word.
  • Reduction changes the structure of the clause or turns it into a shorter modifier.

  • Use reduction for clarity and economy, not simply to make writing look advanced.
  • Always check whether the shorter version preserves the original meaning.

Active -ing Structures

An active relative clause can often be shortened to an -ing form when the noun before the clause performs the action.

The basic transformation

noun + who/that/which + active verb → noun + verb-ing

Full relative clause

Students who live off campus must register.

Reduced structure

Students living off campus must register.

Full relative clause

The train that leaves at 7:30 is usually crowded.

Reduced structure

The train leaving at 7:30 is usually crowded.

The noun performs the action:

Students
Students live off campus.
Train
The train leaves at 7:30.

Continuous meaning

Reduction is especially natural when the full relative clause contains a continuous form.

Ongoing action

The woman who is speaking to the manager is my aunt.

→ The woman speaking to the manager is my aunt.

Temporary activity

The researchers who are working on the project meet every Friday.

→ The researchers working on the project meet every Friday.

Simple present meaning

An -ing modifier does not always mean “happening right now.” It can describe a regular action, a general role, or a characteristic.

Regular action

People living near airports often report sleep problems.

≈ people who live near airports

Function

The machine producing these parts needs maintenance.

≈ the machine that produces these parts

Membership

Students taking the advanced course must buy a separate textbook.

≈ students who take / are taking the advanced course

Scheduled event

Passengers travelling on Flight 218 should proceed to Gate 6.

Past-time interpretation

The -ing form itself does not show tense. Context can place the action in the past.

Past context

The students attending the workshop last Saturday received certificates.

The phrase refers to students who attended the workshop in the past.

Historical context

Merchants travelling along the Silk Road carried goods and ideas between regions.

Tense comes from context

The participle travelling does not itself mean present, past, or future. The rest of the sentence and surrounding information supply the time.

Defining active reductions

A defining reduced structure identifies the noun and does not take commas.

Defining

Students requiring extra time must submit documentation.

Only those students must submit it.

Defining

The road leading to the village is closed.

The phrase identifies which road.

Supplementary active reductions

A reduced structure can also add extra information about an already identified noun. In that case, commas are required.

Extra comment

Mina, living several kilometers from campus, usually takes the bus.

≈ Mina, who lives several kilometers from campus, usually takes the bus.

Background action

The professor, speaking slowly and clearly, explained the final requirement.

This can also be understood as a supplementary participial clause describing what the professor was doing.

Common academic patterns

students participating in the study
factors affecting academic performance
companies operating in rural areas
evidence supporting the theory
changes occurring during adolescence
methods requiring specialized equipment
species living in cold climates
policies aiming to reduce pollution

Not every active verb reduces naturally

Dynamic verbs—verbs describing actions, events, movement, or processes—often reduce more naturally than verbs describing knowledge, possession, belief, or permanent relationships.

Natural

people waiting outside

vehicles approaching the bridge

researchers examining the samples

Often awkward

?people knowing the answer

?a company owning the building

?students believing the rumor

The awkward examples may become acceptable in special contexts, but the full relative clause is usually more natural:

people who know the answer
the company that owns the building
students who believe the rumor

Be careful with future meaning

Clearly future

Students who will attend the conference should register now.

Possible but less explicit

Students attending the conference should register now.

The shorter version can refer to future attendees, but the future meaning comes from context.

Active meaning only

Use an -ing reduction when the noun performs the action. If the noun receives the action, use a passive form instead.

Active

The researchers conducting the experiment wore protective clothing.

The researchers conducted the experiment.

Passive

The experiment conducted by the researchers lasted six months.

The researchers conducted the experiment.

What to remember

  • An active -ing modifier describes a noun that performs the action.
  • It may express an ongoing, regular, past, or future-related action depending on context.
  • It is especially natural with actions, processes, movement, and temporary activities.
  • It may sound awkward with verbs such as know, own, believe, and understand.
  • Defining forms take no commas; supplementary forms do.
  • The participle itself does not show tense.

Passive Past-Participial Structures

When the noun receives an action, a passive relative clause can often be shortened to a past participle. This is one of the most common structures in academic writing.

The basic transformation

noun + who/that/which + be + past participle → noun + past participle

Full passive relative

The documents that were submitted yesterday are being reviewed.

Reduced passive structure

The documents submitted yesterday are being reviewed.

Full passive relative

The method which is used in this study is inexpensive.

Reduced passive structure

The method used in this study is inexpensive.

The noun receives the action:

Documents
Someone submitted the documents.
Method
Researchers use the method.

Past participle does not always mean past time

The form is called a past participle, but its time comes from context. Its most important contribution here is passive meaning.

Present use

The software used by the department is expensive.

The department currently uses it.

Past event

The samples collected last year were reexamined.

Future interpretation

Applications received after Friday will not be considered.

This can refer to applications that will be received in the future.

General rule

Products sold without a warranty cannot be returned.

Meaning first

Do not interpret every past participle as a completed past event. Ask who performs the action, who receives it, and what time the larger sentence establishes.

Regular and irregular participles

Regular

documents submitted

methods tested

houses damaged

data collected

Irregular

books written

samples taken

items sold

problems found

With a by-phrase

The performer of the action can be included after by.

Academic

The theory proposed by Darwin transformed biology.

Administrative

The forms signed by the director are on my desk.

Leave out the by-phrase when the performer is unknown, unimportant, obvious, or already clear.

Performer matters

The mural painted by local students won an award.

Performer unimportant

The road damaged in the storm remains closed.

Defining passive reductions

Identifying

The applications received before noon were processed first.

Only those applications were processed first.

Identifying

The road blocked by the landslide has reopened.

Supplementary passive reductions

Extra information

The final report, submitted two days late, was nevertheless accepted.

Background

The building, damaged during the earthquake, remained empty for years.

The commas show that the noun is already identified and the participial phrase adds a comment.

Common academic patterns

data collected from 500 participants
methods used in previous studies
results obtained during the final trial
participants selected at random
variables measured at regular intervals
policies introduced in the 1990s
evidence presented in the report
materials required for the experiment

Participles that also behave like adjectives

Some past participles are used so commonly as descriptions that they feel adjective-like.

Interested

Students interested in the workshop should register.

Concerned

Parents concerned about the change attended the meeting.

Involved

The people involved in the project met yesterday.

Based

A decision based on incomplete evidence may be unreliable.

These forms will be examined more fully in the chapter on adjective reductions.

Do not confuse active and passive meanings

Active

The researchers collecting the data wore gloves.

The researchers collected the data.

Passive

The data collected by the researchers were stored securely.

The researchers collected the data.

Common error

The data collecting during the experiment were incomplete.

The data collected during the experiment were incomplete.

The data did not perform the collecting. They received the action.

Completed versus ongoing passive action

A simple past participle often presents the noun as the result or target of an action. If the passive action is still in progress, keep being.

Completed or viewed as complete

The patients examined this morning have gone home.

Currently in progress

The patients being examined now are in Room 4.

What to remember

  • A past-participial modifier describes a noun that receives an action.
  • The relative word and form of be disappear.
  • The participle does not automatically express past time.
  • A by-phrase may name the performer.
  • Defining forms take no commas; supplementary forms do.
  • Use being + past participle when the passive action is still in progress.

Ongoing Passive Actions with Being

A simple past participle often describes an action that is complete, habitual, or viewed as a finished result. When the passive action is happening now or is clearly in progress, English often keeps being.

The basic transformation

noun + who/that/which + be + being + past participle → noun + being + past participle

Full relative clause

The patients who are being examined are in Room 4.

Reduced structure

The patients being examined are in Room 4.

Full relative clause

The bridge that is being repaired will reopen next month.

Reduced structure

The bridge being repaired will reopen next month.

Why being remains

The form being preserves the idea that the passive action is in progress.

Action in progress

The documents being reviewed now are confidential.

Review is currently happening.

Completed or viewed as complete

The documents reviewed yesterday contained several errors.

The review happened earlier.

Current, temporary, or developing situations

Current action

The student being interviewed now applied for the scholarship.

Temporary process

The rooms being renovated this month are unavailable.

Developing project

The software being developed by the team will support several languages.

Current discussion

The proposal being discussed would change the admission process.

Being versus a simple past participle

being tested

The device being tested is a new prototype.

The testing is happening now or during the relevant period.

tested

The device tested yesterday performed well.

The testing is complete.

being built

The hospital being built near the airport will open in 2028.

built

The hospital built near the airport opened in 2020.

When being sounds heavy

Although the structure is grammatical, a full relative clause may be easier to process when the phrase is long or the action needs a clear tense.

Compact

The policy being considered by several departments would affect tuition fees.

Clearer in some contexts

The policy that is currently being considered by several departments would affect tuition fees.

The full clause makes the time and ongoing status especially explicit.

Practical advice

Use being + past participle when the ongoing passive meaning matters and the result remains easy to read. Keep the full relative clause when the reduced structure becomes dense or ambiguous.

Supplementary uses

Extra information

The west wing, being renovated this summer, will remain closed.

Background process

The proposal, being reviewed by legal advisers, cannot yet be published.

These forms are possible, but a full non-defining relative clause is often more natural:

Often clearer

The west wing, which is being renovated this summer, will remain closed.

Do not use being when no ongoing passive action exists

Common error

The reports being submitted yesterday contained errors.

The reports submitted yesterday contained errors.

The submission happened yesterday and is complete.

Common error

The method being used in most laboratories is simple.

The method used in most laboratories is simple.

Use the simple participle for a general or habitual passive meaning. The version with being used suggests a current temporary process.

What to remember

  • Being + past participle preserves an ongoing passive meaning.
  • A simple past participle usually presents the action as completed, habitual, or viewed as a result.
  • Use context words such as now, currently, this month, and at the moment to guide the choice.
  • Keep the full relative clause when the reduced form becomes heavy or when tense needs to be explicit.

Removing Be Before an Adjective

When a relative clause contains only a relative word, a form of be, and an adjective or adjective phrase, English can often remove the relative word and be.

The basic transformation

noun + who/that/which + be + adjective → noun + adjective

Full relative clause

Students who are interested in the workshop should register.

Reduced structure

Students interested in the workshop should register.

Full relative clause

The only rooms that are available are on the third floor.

Reduced structure

The only rooms available are on the third floor.

Adjectives with their complements

An adjective may need additional words to complete its meaning. Keep those words in the reduced structure.

Interested in

Students interested in environmental science may join the project.

Responsible for

The employee responsible for the error apologized.

Suitable for

We need a room suitable for group work.

Aware of

Participants aware of the risks signed the form.

Eligible for

Applicants eligible for funding will be contacted.

Similar to

Results similar to those of the first study were obtained.

Adjectives that naturally follow the noun

Some adjectives often appear after the noun, especially when they have their own complement or when words such as something, anything, nothing, and someone come before them.

Available

We used every resource available.

Possible

They examined every explanation possible.

Present

All students present signed the form.

Concerned

The people concerned have been informed.

Something useful

I need something useful for the presentation.

Anyone interested

Anyone interested may attend.

Position can affect meaning

Some adjectives have a different or more specialized meaning after the noun.

The people concerned

the people involved or affected

The concerned people

the people who are worried

The students present

the students who are there

The present students

the current students, although this wording is less natural

Meaning and position

Do not assume that moving an adjective before or after a noun preserves exactly the same meaning. Learn common post-noun patterns as complete expressions.

Enough, too, and comparison phrases

Enough

We need a room large enough for thirty people.

≈ a room that is large enough for thirty people

Too

Documents too damaged to scan were copied by hand.

Comparative

Students more experienced than the others completed the task first.

Superlative-like meaning

The option most suitable for beginners is shown first.

Defining and supplementary adjective phrases

Defining

Students eligible for financial aid must apply by Friday.

Only eligible students.

Supplementary

The students, anxious about the results, waited outside.

The students are already identified; the phrase adds their emotional state.

When the full clause is clearer

A full relative clause may be better when time, contrast, or a temporary state needs emphasis.

Compact

The rooms available today are on the first floor.

More explicit

The rooms that are available today are on the first floor.

The full clause emphasizes the temporary time limit.

What to remember

  • A relative word and form of be can often disappear before an adjective.
  • Keep any preposition or complement required by the adjective.
  • Some adjectives naturally follow nouns, especially after indefinite pronouns.
  • Adjective position can affect meaning.
  • Commas distinguish defining descriptions from supplementary comments.
  • Use a full clause when time or temporary status needs to be especially explicit.

Removing Be Before Place and Status Expressions

When a relative clause contains a relative word, a form of be, and a prepositional or adverbial expression, the relative word and be can often disappear.

Prepositional phrases after a noun

noun + who/that/which + be + prepositional phrase → noun + prepositional phrase

Full relative clause

The books that are on the top shelf are reference books.

Shorter structure

The books on the top shelf are reference books.

Full relative clause

We need something that is within walking distance.

Shorter structure

We need something within walking distance.

Common place expressions

On

The files on the desk are mine.

In

The students in Room 12 are taking the placement test.

Near

The houses near the river were evacuated.

Behind

The building behind the library is a laboratory.

Across from

The café across from the station opens early.

Under

The pipes under the floor need replacement.

Status and condition expressions

Prepositional phrases can express more than physical location.

In danger

Species in danger of extinction require protection.

Under review

The applications under review will be processed soon.

At risk

Students at risk of failing receive additional support.

In charge

The person in charge of the laboratory is absent.

Out of order

The machines out of order have been labelled.

In use

The rooms in use are marked in blue.

Adverbial expressions such as outside, inside, upstairs, and nearby

Full clause

The people who are outside have been waiting for an hour.

Shorter form

The people outside have been waiting for an hour.

Inside

The students inside cannot hear us.

Upstairs

The office upstairs belongs to the director.

Nearby

Residents nearby reported hearing an explosion.

Abroad

Students abroad can submit the form online.

Time and sequence expressions

Before a date

Applications before the committee will be reviewed today.

This formal expression means applications currently being considered by the committee.

After a noun

The week after the examination was unusually quiet.

During a period

The changes during the final stage were minor.

From a period

Documents from the nineteenth century require special handling.

The phrase may be more natural than the full clause

In many simple location descriptions, the shorter phrase is the ordinary form—not a special formal reduction.

Natural

the keys on the table

the woman at the front desk

the shops near the station

Possible but unnecessarily long

the keys that are on the table

the woman who is at the front desk

the shops that are near the station

When the full clause adds useful information

The full clause may be better when tense, temporary status, contrast, or emphasis matters.

General location

The students in the laboratory must wear goggles.

Temporary location emphasized

The students who are currently in the laboratory must wear goggles.

Compact status

The proposal under review may be revised.

Clear ongoing process

The proposal that is currently under review may be revised.

Supplementary phrases

Location comment

The old laboratory, behind the main library, is no longer in use.

Status comment

The final proposal, under review for several weeks, was approved yesterday.

Commas show that the noun is already identified. However, some supplementary prepositional phrases may sound more natural without interpreting them as reduced relatives. The important question is still what the phrase describes and whether the punctuation makes the meaning clear.

Avoid unclear attachment

Placement matters

I spoke to the assistant about the files on the second floor.

Are the files on the second floor, or is the assistant there? The sentence may be ambiguous.

On the second floor, I spoke to the assistant about the files.

I spoke to the assistant about the files that were stored on the second floor.

To-Infinitive Relative Structures

A noun can be followed by a to-infinitive that expresses an action connected to that noun. These structures are especially common after ordinal numbers, superlatives, indefinite pronouns, and nouns connected with tasks, opportunities, needs, or future actions.

The noun performs the action

Full relative clause

She was the first student who arrived.

Infinitive structure

She was the first student to arrive.

The noun before the infinitive performs the action:

Ordinal

He was the second applicant to be interviewed.

Last

Mina was the last person to leave.

Next

The next speaker to present is from Japan.

Only

She was the only student to notice the error.

After superlatives

Best

This is the best method to use in this situation.

Most important

The most important factor to consider is cost.

Easiest

The easiest route to follow goes through the park.

First available

We took the earliest train to leave the station.

The noun receives the action

In many infinitive structures, someone performs the action on the noun.

Full relative clause

I need something that I can read.

Infinitive structure

I need something to read.

Task

We have several forms to complete.

We must complete the forms.

Need

She needs a chair to use during the presentation.

Opportunity

This is a useful topic to discuss.

Choice

There are three routes to choose from.

Indefinite pronouns

Infinitive structures are extremely common after something, anything, nothing, someone, somewhere, and similar words.

Something

I need something to eat.

Nothing

There is nothing to worry about.

Someone

We need someone to manage the registration desk.

Somewhere

They are looking for somewhere to stay.

Anything

Do you have anything to add?

Nobody

There was nobody to ask for help.

Prepositions at the end

When the noun is connected to a preposition, the preposition normally remains at the end of the infinitive phrase.

Sit on

We need a chair to sit on.

Talk to

She needs someone to talk to.

Write with

Do you have a pen to write with?

Work on

We have an important project to work on.

Do not lose the preposition

We need a chair to sit.

We need a chair to sit on.

The intended meaning is that someone will sit on the chair.

Passive infinitives

Use to be + past participle when the noun needs to receive an action and the passive meaning should be explicit.

Problems

There are several problems to be solved.

Applications

The applications to be reviewed tomorrow are on my desk.

Final task

The last task to be completed is the safety check.

Future document

The report to be published next month includes new data.

Active versus passive infinitives

Active, ordinary instruction

We have several forms to complete.

We or another understood person will complete them.

Passive, formal emphasis

There are several forms to be completed.

The forms need to receive the action; the performer is not emphasized.

Stating the performer with for

Use for + person + to-infinitive when the performer is not obvious or when it is important to name them.

Person stated

We need a problem for the students to solve.

Specific task

There are several documents for you to sign.

Opportunity

This is a good topic for the class to discuss.

Place

They need a quiet room for the baby to sleep in.

Nouns commonly followed by infinitives

an opportunity to learn
a chance to improve
a decision to postpone the meeting
an attempt to solve the problem
a need to reduce costs
a plan to expand the program
the ability to communicate clearly
a request to change rooms
Not all of these are reductions

Some noun + infinitive combinations are closely related to relative clauses, while others express the content or purpose of the noun. For example, a decision to postpone the meeting tells us what the decision was. The important skill is understanding the relationship, not forcing every infinitive into one label.

Infinitives often suggest possibility, necessity, suitability, or future action

Possibility

something to read

something that one can read

Necessity

tasks to complete

tasks that must or need to be completed

Suitability

the best method to use

the best method that one can or should use

Future role

the next person to speak

the person who will speak next

Do not assume exact equivalence with one finite clause

An infinitive may combine several possible meanings. Context decides whether it means who did, who will, who can, who should, or that must be.

Context-dependent

She was the first scientist to identify the compound.

≈ the first scientist who identified it

Context-dependent

The next scientist to present is Dr. Lee.

≈ the scientist who will present next

Context-dependent

We need a method to measure small changes.

≈ a method that we can use to measure small changes

  • They are not always exactly equivalent to one specific finite relative clause.

Negative and Perfect Participial Structures

Reduced structures can be negative, and they can sometimes show that one action was completed before another. These patterns are useful, but the perfect forms are relatively formal and should not be overused.

Negative active participles

Place not before the -ing form.

Full relative clause

Applicants who do not meet the minimum requirement will not be considered.

Reduced negative structure

Applicants not meeting the minimum requirement will not be considered.

Notice

Students not wishing to participate may leave early.

Academic

Variables not affecting the outcome were removed from the model.

Administrative

Employees not attending the training must watch the recording.

General condition

Devices not operating correctly should be disconnected.

Negative passive participles

Place not before the past participle or before being.

Simple passive

Applications not received by Friday will be rejected.

≈ applications that are not received by Friday

Ongoing passive

The files not being used at the moment should be archived.

≈ files that are not being used at the moment

Condition

Items not marked clearly may be sent to the wrong department.

Formal rule

Expenses not approved in advance will not be reimbursed.

Negative adjective and prepositional forms

Not eligible

Students not eligible for funding may apply for a loan.

Not in use

Rooms not in use should remain locked.

Not available

The books not currently available can be reserved.

Not under review

Applications not under review remain in the general queue.

Perfect active participles: having + past participle

The perfect participle having + past participle can show that the action in the reduced structure was completed before the action in the main clause.

Full relative clause

Applicants who had completed the course were given priority.

Perfect participial form

Applicants having completed the course were given priority.

Prior completion

Students having passed the entrance test proceeded to the interview stage.

Prior experience

Researchers having worked with the method before completed the task more quickly.

Prior submission

Candidates having submitted all required documents received confirmation emails.

Register warning

Post-noun perfect participles such as applicants having completed the course are grammatical but formal and relatively uncommon. In most ordinary writing, a full relative clause is more natural:

Applicants who had completed the course were given priority.

Perfect passive participles

A passive perfect structure uses having been + past participle.

Full relative clause

The documents that had been reviewed twice were approved.

Perfect passive participle

The documents having been reviewed twice were approved.

This form is possible but usually sounds very formal and heavy after a noun.

Formal example

The samples, having been stored incorrectly, could not be used.

Here the phrase is supplementary and gives the reason the samples could not be used. A full clause—which had been stored incorrectly—would also be natural.

Perfect participles often work better as sentence-level clauses

The same forms are more common when they describe the subject’s earlier action and appear at the beginning of the sentence.

Post-noun modifier

Students having completed the course may apply.

Sentence-level participial clause

Having completed the course, the students may now apply.

This means: because or after they completed the course, they may apply.

Do not force the perfect form

Use a perfect participle only when the earlier completion matters and the sentence remains natural. A full relative clause is usually the clearest learner choice.

Negative perfect participles

Active

Applicants not having completed the training were not admitted.

Passive

The documents, not having been signed, were returned.

These are highly formal. Prefer a full clause in most contexts:

Applicants who had not completed the training were not admitted.
The documents, which had not been signed, were returned.

  • Place not before the participle, adjective, or phrase being negated.

Time, Modality, and Meaning Loss

Reduction makes a description shorter, but it may also remove information. Full relative clauses can clearly express tense, future time, ability, obligation, possibility, completion, and habitual meaning. Reduced forms often leave those meanings to context.

Participles do not clearly mark tense

Present or regular

students who live off campus

Past

students who lived off campus last year

Future

students who will live off campus next year

Reduced

students living off campus

The time must come from the surrounding context.

Past supplied by context

Students living off campus in 2024 paid lower housing fees.

Future supplied by context

Students living off campus next year must update their addresses.

Future meaning may become less explicit

Clearly future

Delegates who will attend next month must register.

Context-dependent

Delegates attending next month must register.

The reduced form is natural because next month supplies the future time. Without a clear future expression, the interpretation may be less certain.

Modal verbs normally disappear

A full relative clause can contain modal verbs such as can, could, should, must, may, and will. An ordinary participial reduction does not preserve them directly.

Ability

students who can solve the problem

Not equivalent

students solving the problem

This describes students actually solving it, not merely students with the ability.

Obligation

forms that must be completed today

Possible infinitive equivalent

forms to be completed today

The infinitive can preserve a requirement or scheduled action more naturally than a participle.

Possibility

methods that may reduce costs

Different strength

methods reducing costs

This sounds as though the methods actually reduce costs, not merely that they may do so.

Do not erase an important modal meaning

Keep the full relative clause when ability, possibility, permission, prediction, or obligation is central to the message.

Perfect aspect may disappear

Present perfect passive

documents that have been approved

Simple participle

documents approved

The passive result remains, but the explicit link to the present and the idea of completed approval become less prominent.

Past perfect active

students who had completed the course

Formal perfect participle

students having completed the course

The sequence is preserved, but the form is more formal and less common.

Progressive meaning may need being or a full clause

Ongoing passive

the bridge that is being repaired

Preserved reduction

the bridge being repaired

Past ongoing passive

the bridge that was being repaired when the storm began

Better kept full

A reduced form would not show the past progressive relationship as clearly.

Habitual versus temporary meaning

Habitual role

The employee who handles complaints is absent.

Possibly temporary

The employee handling complaints is absent.

This may suggest the employee currently assigned to that task rather than the person whose regular job it is.

Permanent ownership

The company that owns the land has objected.

Awkward reduction

?The company owning the land has objected.

Cause and condition may become unclear

A reduced form can sometimes imply a reason, condition, or background circumstance, especially when set off by commas.

Possible reason

The students, worried about the exam, asked for more practice.

The sentence may suggest that they asked because they were worried.

Explicit reason

Because the students were worried about the exam, they asked for more practice.

Use a full adverbial clause when the logical relationship must be unmistakable.

Infinitives can preserve some modal meanings

Necessity

tasks to be completed today

≈ tasks that must be completed today

Possibility

something to read

≈ something that one can read

Future sequence

the next person to speak

≈ the person who will speak next

Suitability

the best method to use

≈ the best method that one should or can use

A decision checklist

Does the reduced form preserve who performs or receives the action?
Is the time clear from context?
Does the full clause contain an important modal verb?
Does completion or ongoing action matter?
Could the reduced form suggest a different habitual or temporary meaning?
Would a full clause be easier to understand?

  • Participles do not clearly mark tense; context must supply it.
  • Ordinary participial reductions do not preserve modal verbs directly.
  • Reduction may weaken distinctions involving completion, ongoing action, habit, or temporary activity.
  • Infinitives can preserve some meanings of necessity, possibility, suitability, and future sequence.
  • Keep the full relative clause whenever an important meaning would be lost.

When Reduction Sounds Unnatural

Grammar is not only a question of whether a transformation can be imagined. A reduced structure must also sound like something proficient speakers would naturally use in that context.

State verbs often resist active -ing reduction

Verbs such as know, believe, understand, own, belong, contain, resemble, need, prefer, and want often describe states rather than active processes.

Natural full clauses

people who know the answer

a company that owns the land

a box that contains old records

students who prefer online classes

Usually awkward reductions

?people knowing the answer

?a company owning the land

?a box containing old records

?students preferring online classes

Important nuance

Some forms that look questionable in isolation are natural in particular contexts. For example, a box containing old records is common because containing functions as a compact descriptive relation. Naturalness depends on the verb, noun, context, and established usage—not a simple ban on state verbs.

Established descriptive patterns can be natural

Contain

A folder containing confidential documents was found.

Consist

A committee consisting of five members reviewed the case.

Include

A package including meals and transport costs less.

Belong

Documents belonging to former employees were archived.

These combinations are well established. The lesson is not “never reduce a state verb,” but “do not assume every state verb produces a natural modifier.”

Short reductions can sound incomplete

Unnatural or unclear

?the student studying

Complete description

the student studying near the window

the student studying environmental engineering

Some participles need a complement or context to create a useful description.

The reduction may attach to the wrong noun

Misplaced modifier

I saw the report on the desk containing several errors.

Does the desk contain the errors, or does the report?

I saw the report containing several errors on the desk.

On the desk, I saw the report that contained several errors.

The reduced form may accidentally describe the wrong performer

Logical subject

Walking through the laboratory, the equipment looked unfamiliar.

The grammar suggests that the equipment was walking.

Walking through the laboratory, I found the equipment unfamiliar.

The equipment that I saw while walking through the laboratory looked unfamiliar.

The example above is a sentence-level participial clause rather than a post-noun reduction, but the same principle applies: the understood performer must be logical and clear.

Long reduced phrases can become harder than full clauses

Dense

The students participating in the research project organized by the department working with the local hospital received certificates.

Clearer

The department organized a research project with the local hospital. The students who participated received certificates.

Unusual word order can sound overly formal

Natural

the problems we need to solve

Compact but formal

the problems to be solved

Natural

the people who are responsible

Compact

the people responsible

The reduced version may be perfect in a report or notice but unnecessarily formal in casual conversation.

Do not stack reductions simply to sound advanced

Overcompressed

Applicants living abroad, having completed the online module, and interested in the internship should contact the coordinator.

Clearer

Applicants who live abroad and have completed the online module should contact the coordinator if they are interested in the internship.

Naturalness tests

Does the phrase create a clear and useful description?
Is the noun clearly the performer or receiver of the action?
Does the verb commonly occur in this reduced pattern?
Has an important tense or modal meaning disappeared?
Would the full clause be easier to process?
Does the sentence sound appropriate for its context and register?
A good editing habit

Read the sentence aloud. If the reduced phrase makes you pause, reread, or search for the noun it describes, the full relative clause may be the better choice.

What to remember

  • Not every grammatically imaginable reduction sounds natural.
  • State verbs often resist active -ing reduction, though established patterns exist.
  • A reduced phrase must attach clearly to the correct noun.
  • The noun must logically perform or receive the action.
  • A long reduced phrase may be harder to read than a full clause.
  • Naturalness, clarity, and context matter more than maximum compression.

Commas with Reduced Structures

The same basic meaning distinction from full relative clauses remains important after reduction. A shortened structure may identify the noun, or it may add a separate comment about a noun that is already identified.

Defining reduced structures: no commas

A defining reduced structure tells us which person or thing is meant.

Active

Students living off campus must update their addresses.

Only students living off campus.

Passive

Applications received after Friday will be rejected.

Only late applications.

Adjective

Applicants eligible for funding will be contacted.

Only eligible applicants.

Prepositional

The files on the lower shelf are confidential.

The phrase identifies which files.

No commas for identifying information

The students waiting outside are from the evening class.

The students, waiting outside, are from the evening class.

The comma version suggests that the students are already identified and merely adds that they are waiting outside.

Supplementary reduced structures: commas

A supplementary structure adds extra information about a noun that is already clear.

Active comment

Mina, living several kilometers from campus, usually takes the bus.

Passive comment

The final report, submitted two days late, was accepted.

Adjective comment

The students, anxious about the results, waited quietly.

Prepositional comment

The old laboratory, behind the main library, is no longer used.

Meaning changes with punctuation

No commas: only some employees

The employees working from home use a different system.

Only the home-based employees.

Commas: all the identified employees

The employees, working from home, use a different system.

The employees are already identified, and the phrase adds that they are working from home. Depending on context, this may also suggest a temporary situation.

No commas: identifies the documents

The documents signed by the director are valid.

Only the signed documents are valid.

Commas: comments on identified documents

The documents, signed by the director, are valid.

The documents are already identified; the phrase adds that the director signed them.

Beginning-of-sentence participial clauses

A participial clause at the beginning of a sentence is normally followed by a comma.

Earlier action

Having completed the course, the students applied for internships.

Background action

Walking toward the library, I saw my professor.

Reason-like meaning

Concerned about the delay, the manager called the supplier.

These are not post-noun relative reductions, but they use related participial forms. The understood subject must be the subject of the main clause.

Dangling modifier

Walking toward the library, the rain began.

The grammar suggests that the rain was walking.

Walking toward the library, I noticed that it had begun to rain.

End-position supplementary phrases

A supplementary phrase at the end is separated by a comma when it comments on the whole preceding noun phrase or clause.

Added state

The students waited outside, anxious about the results.

Added action

The professor entered the room, carrying a stack of papers.

Be careful: an end-position phrase may be interpreted as describing the nearest noun or the subject of the sentence. Rewrite if the reference is uncertain.

Restrictive meaning after indefinite pronouns

Expressions after something, anything, nothing, anyone, and similar words normally appear without commas because they help define the meaning.

I need something large enough to cover the table.

Is there anyone available to help?

We have nothing to discuss.

They found somewhere within walking distance.

Do not insert commas merely because a phrase is long

Meaning controls punctuation

Students, requiring additional support during the first semester, may attend free workshops.

Students requiring additional support during the first semester may attend free workshops.

If the phrase identifies which students may attend, it takes no commas even though it is long.

Do not remove commas merely because a phrase is short

Already identified noun

Sara worried about the results called her advisor.

Sara, worried about the results, called her advisor.

The name already identifies Sara. The adjective phrase is an inserted comment.

Punctuation and cause-like interpretations

A supplementary participial phrase may imply a logical relationship such as reason, time, or accompanying circumstance.

Supplementary phrase

The students, confused by the instructions, asked for help.

This naturally suggests that their confusion led them to ask.

Explicit reason

Because the students were confused by the instructions, they asked for help.

Use this form when the cause must be unmistakable.

What to remember

  • Defining reduced structures do not take commas.
  • Supplementary reduced structures are separated by commas.
  • Punctuation can change which people or things the sentence includes.
  • Meaning—not phrase length—controls comma use.
  • Beginning sentence-level participial clauses normally take a comma.
  • Make sure the understood subject and attachment are clear.

Part 3 Comprehensive Practice

The final exercises mix all the major shortened structures. Do not reduce automatically. First decide whether the noun performs or receives the action, whether time or modality matters, and whether the result sounds natural.

Exercise 1: identify the structure

Classify the bold modifier: active -ing, passive past participle, being passive, adjective phrase, prepositional/adverbial phrase, infinitive, negative form, or perfect participle.

  1. Students living near campus often walk to class.
  2. The data collected in May were incomplete.
  3. The bridge being repaired will reopen soon.
  4. Applicants eligible for funding will be contacted.
  5. The books on the lower shelf are reference materials.
  6. She was the first person to notice the error.
  7. Applications not received by Friday will be rejected.
  8. Researchers having completed the initial trial began the second phase.
  9. The people outside cannot hear the announcement.
  10. We need a room for the children to play in.
Show the answers
  1. Active -ing
  2. Passive past participle
  3. Ongoing passive with being
  4. Adjective phrase
  5. Prepositional phrase
  6. Infinitive
  7. Negative passive participle
  8. Perfect active participle
  9. Adverbial expression
  10. For + person + infinitive

Exercise 2: reduce where natural

Rewrite each full relative clause with a shorter structure if the result is natural and clear. Otherwise, keep the full clause.

  1. The students who are waiting near the main entrance are exchange students.
  2. The company that owns the building plans to sell it.
  3. The samples that were stored at room temperature became unstable.
  4. The rooms that are available this week are on the third floor.
  5. People who know the password may enter.
  6. The proposal that is being considered by the board would reduce costs.
  7. The next researcher who will present is Dr. Sato.
  8. The forms that must be completed today are online.
  9. The office that is across from the library is closed.
  10. The applicants who had completed all three modules were given priority.
Show suggested answers
  1. The students waiting near the main entrance are exchange students.
  2. Keep the full clause: the company that owns the building.
  3. The samples stored at room temperature became unstable.
  4. The rooms available this week are on the third floor.
  5. Keep the full clause to preserve the natural state verb: people who know the password.
  6. The proposal being considered by the board would reduce costs.
  7. The next researcher to present is Dr. Sato.
  8. The forms to be completed today are online.
  9. The office across from the library is closed.
  10. Prefer the full clause. The formal applicants having completed all three modules is possible but less natural.

  4. Students who are interested in the exchange program should attend.

Exercise 4: active, passive, adjective, or location?

Choose the best form of the word or phrase in parentheses.

  1. The students ________ the survey received certificates. (complete)
  2. The surveys ________ by the students were anonymous. (complete)
  3. The students ________ for the scholarship will be interviewed. (eligible)
  4. The documents ________ the blue folder are confidential. (in)
  5. The machine ________ the samples is overheating. (analyze)
  6. The samples ________ by the machine were damaged. (analyze)
  7. The machine ________ right now cannot be used. (repair)
  8. The person ________ the laboratory is Dr. Lee. (in charge of)
Show the answers

  3. eligible
  4. in
  5. analyzing

Exercise 5: infinitive relationships

Complete each phrase with an active infinitive, passive infinitive, or for + person + infinitive.

  1. the first student __________________ (the student arrived)
  2. several tasks __________________ (we must complete them)
  3. several tasks __________________ (they must receive completion; performer unimportant)
  4. a problem __________________ (the class should solve it)
  5. a chair __________________ (someone can sit on it)
  6. the next report __________________ (it will be published)
  7. something __________________ (we can eat it)
  8. a room __________________ (the children can study in it)
Show possible answers
  1. the first student to arrive
  2. several tasks to complete
  3. several tasks to be completed
  4. a problem for the class to solve
  5. a chair to sit on
  6. the next report to be published
  7. something to eat
  8. a room for the children to study in

Exercise 6: preserve the meaning

Explain why the proposed reduction changes or weakens the meaning. Then write the clearest version.

  1. students who can solve the equation → students solving the equation
  2. methods that may reduce costs → methods reducing costs
  3. employees who will work on Saturday → employees working on Saturday
  4. documents that have been approved → documents approved
  5. the company that owns the factory → the company owning the factory
  6. the bridge that was being repaired when the storm began → the bridge being repaired when the storm began
Show suggested explanations
  1. Can solve expresses ability; solving describes actual activity. Keep the full clause.
  2. May reduce expresses possibility; reducing sounds factual. Keep the full clause.
  3. The reduced form can be natural if Saturday clearly establishes the future schedule, but the full form is more explicit.
  4. The simple participle preserves passive result but weakens the present-perfect emphasis. Choose according to context.
  5. Owning is possible in some formal contexts but often less natural. Prefer the full clause.
  6. The reduced form loses explicit past progressive tense. Keep the full clause.

Exercise 7: punctuation

Add or remove commas so that each sentence expresses the meaning in parentheses.

  1. Students living off campus must register. (only those students)
  2. My three cousins living abroad will visit this summer. (all three cousins live abroad)
  3. The reports submitted yesterday have already been reviewed. (only yesterday’s reports)
  4. Our final report submitted yesterday has already been reviewed. (extra information about one identified report)
  5. Sara worried about the result called her professor. (extra information about Sara)
  6. The machines out of order have been labelled. (only the broken machines)
Show the answers
  1. Students living off campus must register.
  2. My three cousins, living abroad, will visit this summer.
  3. The reports submitted yesterday have already been reviewed.
  4. Our final report, submitted yesterday, has already been reviewed.
  5. Sara, worried about the result, called her professor.
  6. The machines out of order have been labelled.

Exercise 8: edit the paragraph

The paragraph contains unnatural reductions, voice errors, lost prepositions, unclear attachment, and punctuation problems. Rewrite it clearly.

The students knowing the laboratory well were asked to guide the visitors arriving next week. The equipment using in the first demonstration is currently repaired. The person responsible of the safety checks, working in the department for ten years will supervise the event. Visitors wanting somewhere to sit should use the chairs placing near the entrance. The first group arriving will receive forms completing before entering. Documents not signing by a supervisor will not be accepted. The director, owning the building next door has offered a room for the guests to wait.

Show a corrected version

The students who know the laboratory well were asked to guide the visitors arriving next week. The equipment to be used in the first demonstration is currently being repaired. The person responsible for the safety checks, who has worked in the department for ten years, will supervise the event. Visitors wanting somewhere to sit should use the chairs placed near the entrance. The first group to arrive will receive forms to complete before entering. Documents not signed by a supervisor will not be accepted. The director, who owns the building next door, has offered a room for the guests to wait in.

The full clauses with know and own are more natural. Being repaired preserves the ongoing passive action. The prepositions in responsible for and wait in must remain.

Exercise 9: rewrite for two different registers

Write a clear conversational version and a compact formal version.

  1. The people who want to apply need to complete the forms that are available online.
  2. The samples that were collected from the river are the first samples that will be analyzed.
  3. The students who are interested in the program need someone whom they can speak to.
  4. There are several problems that the committee must solve before the plan can be approved.
Show sample answers
  1. Conversational: People who want to apply need to complete the forms that are available online.
    Compact: People wishing to apply must complete the forms available online.
  2. Conversational: The samples that were collected from the river are the first ones that will be analyzed.
    Compact: The samples collected from the river are the first to be analyzed.
  3. Conversational: Students who are interested in the program need someone they can speak to.
    Compact: Students interested in the program need someone to speak to.
  4. Conversational: There are several problems that the committee must solve before the plan can be approved.
    Compact: Several problems remain for the committee to solve before the plan can be approved.

Exercise 10: controlled writing

Write a paragraph of five to seven sentences about a university facility, research project, public service, workplace, or community event. Include:

  • one active -ing modifier;
  • one passive past participle;
  • one ongoing passive with being;
  • one adjective or prepositional postmodifier;
  • one infinitive modifier;
  • one full relative clause that you deliberately do not reduce because the full meaning is clearer.
Show a sample paragraph

The university is renovating a laboratory located behind the main library. The workers installing the new ventilation system expect to finish next month. Several machines purchased last year will be moved into the building. The equipment being tested at the moment will support advanced chemistry courses. Students interested in laboratory research will have more opportunities to gain practical experience. The department is also hiring technicians who can operate the new safety system. The final clause remains full because can operate expresses ability, which an ordinary participial reduction would not preserve.

Before moving to Part 4

Make sure you can distinguish omission from reduction, choose active or passive voice, understand infinitive relationships, and recognize when a full clause is clearer. Part 4 will move into free relatives, partitive structures such as many of whom, advanced possession, nested clauses, and complex-sentence editing.

Part 3 final checklist

  • I can distinguish object-relative omission from true reduction.
  • I can use an active -ing modifier when the noun performs the action.
  • I can use a past participle when the noun receives the action.
  • I can preserve an ongoing passive meaning with being.
  • I can remove be before appropriate adjectives and adjective phrases.
  • I can use prepositional and adverbial expressions after nouns.
  • I can form infinitive modifiers after ordinals, superlatives, and indefinite pronouns.
  • I can keep necessary prepositions at the end of infinitive phrases.
  • I can choose between active, passive, and for + person + infinitive structures.
  • I can form negative reduced structures.
  • I understand that perfect participial postmodifiers are formal and uncommon.
  • I know that participles do not clearly show tense.
  • I can recognize when reduction erases ability, possibility, obligation, or completion.
  • I can avoid unnatural reductions with verbs such as know and own.
  • I can place a reduced modifier next to the correct noun.
  • I can use commas to distinguish defining and supplementary meanings.
  • I can choose clarity and naturalness over unnecessary compression.

    Phrases and Nesting

    How English combines words into larger units, gives those units grammatical types, and places smaller structures inside larger ones.

    Collected and compiled by Amir Kooshky.

    Several Words Can Perform One Sentence Function

    The big black dog is running.

    The subject is not only the word dog. The complete subject is the big black dog.

    the
    determiner
    big
    adjective
    black
    adjective
    dog
    noun

    Each word has its own part of speech, but the entire group works together as one part of the sentence: the subject.

    It is running.

    The pronoun it can replace the complete group. This shows that the big black dog behaves as one grammatical unit.

    English combines words into larger units so that we can add detail without giving every word a separate sentence function.

    From Words to Phrases to Sentences

    Words can combine into phrases, and phrases can combine into sentences.

    words → phrases → sentences

    Words

    dog

    runs

    quickly

    Phrases

    the black dog

    is running

    very quickly

    The black dog is running very quickly.

    Another important structural level will be added to this model in a future lesson.

    A Phrase Can Contain One Word or Several Words

    The word phrase may sound as though it must refer to several words. In this guide, however, a phrase may contain:

    • only one word; or
    • a central word together with other words.
    Dogs bark loudly.

    Dogs = one-word noun phrase; subject

    bark = one-word verb phrase; verbal element

    loudly = one-word adverb phrase; adverbial of manner

    The large dogs have been barking very loudly.

    The large dogs = multiword noun phrase

    have been barking = multiword verb phrase

    very loudly = multiword adverb phrase

    A phrase may contain one word, but not all phrases contain only one word.

    Treating one-word units as phrases allows us to discuss sentence structure consistently. A subject may contain one word or many words, but the complete unit can still be called a noun phrase.

    Phrase Types Work Like Parts of Speech

    Parts of speech classify individual words:

    • noun
    • verb
    • adjective
    • adverb
    • preposition

    Phrase types classify complete grammatical units in a similar way:

    • noun phrase
    • verb phrase
    • adjective phrase
    • adverb phrase
    • prepositional phrase

    Individual word

    dog — noun

    nervous — adjective

    quietly — adverb

    Complete phrase

    the large dog — noun phrase

    extremely nervous — adjective phrase

    very quietly — adverb phrase

    Dogs bark. The large black dogs bark.

    A one-word noun phrase and a longer noun phrase can both function as the subject.

    The student is nervous. The student is extremely nervous.

    A one-word adjective phrase and a longer adjective phrase can both function as a subject complement.

    She spoke quietly. She spoke very quietly.

    A one-word adverb phrase and a longer adverb phrase can both function as an adverbial.

    Heads: The Central Words of Phrases

    Every phrase has a central word called its head. The head usually determines the phrase type.

    the large dog
    The head is the noun dog, so the complete unit is a noun phrase.
    extremely nervous
    The head is the adjective nervous, so the complete unit is an adjective phrase.
    very quietly
    The head is the adverb quietly, so the complete unit is an adverb phrase.
    might have forgotten
    The main verb is forgotten, and the complete verbal unit is a verb phrase.
    under the table
    The preposition under organises the complete unit, so it is a prepositional phrase.
    To identify a phrase type, first find the head or central word.

    Keep Three Classifications Separate

    1. Part of speech

    What kind of individual word is this?

    Examples: noun, verb, adjective, adverb.

    2. Phrase type

    What kind of complete grammatical unit is this?

    Examples: noun phrase, verb phrase, adjective phrase.

    3. Sentence function

    What job does the complete word or phrase perform here?

    Examples: subject, direct object, complement, adverbial.

    The nervous student answered quietly.

    Word level

    the = determiner

    nervous = adjective

    student = noun

    answered = verb

    quietly = adverb

    Phrase level

    the nervous student = noun phrase

    answered = one-word verb phrase

    quietly = one-word adverb phrase

    Sentence-function level

    the nervous student = subject

    answered = verbal element

    quietly = adverbial of manner

    Phrase type and sentence function are not the same thing.

    Noun Phrases

    A noun phrase is built around a noun or pronoun. It can perform the same sentence functions that a single noun or pronoun can perform.

    Basic noun-phrase patterns

    the dog the black dog the large black dog my new grammar book

    Noun phrase as subject

    The dog barked. The large black dog barked.

    Noun phrase as direct object

    I saw the dog. I saw the large black dog.

    Noun phrase as indirect object

    Sara gave the student a book. Sara gave the nervous new student a book.

    Noun phrase as subject complement

    Sara is a teacher. Sara is an excellent English teacher.

    Common parts of noun phrases

    Determiner + noun

    the book

    a student

    my phone

    Determiner + adjective + noun

    a difficult question

    the old building

    my new laptop

    Several adjectives + noun

    a small wooden table

    the tall nervous student

    an expensive Italian restaurant

    Noun + noun

    a school bus

    a coffee shop

    a grammar lesson

    In a grammar lesson, both grammar and lesson are nouns, but lesson is the head.

    Verb Phrases

    A verb phrase is built around a main verb. It may also contain helping verbs, modal verbs, and negative words.

    Main verb only

    Sara laughed. The dog barked.

    Helping verb + main verb

    Sara is working. They have finished. He was sleeping.

    Modal + main verb

    She can swim. They might leave. You should study.

    Several helping verbs

    She has been working. They might have forgotten. The car could have been damaged.

    Objects remain outside the verb phrase in this guide

    Sara opened the door.

    opened = one-word verb phrase

    the door = noun phrase; direct object

    Adverbs placed inside verb phrases will be explained in the nesting section.

    Adjective Phrases

    An adjective phrase is built around an adjective. Other words may strengthen it, weaken it, compare it, or complete its meaning.

    difficult very difficult extremely difficult slightly difficult

    The head is difficult.

    Adjective phrase before a noun

    a very difficult question an extremely expensive car a surprisingly simple answer

    Adjective phrase as subject complement

    The student was very nervous. The answer seems completely correct. The room became unusually quiet.

    Adjective phrase as object complement

    The news made her very nervous. We painted the wall bright blue. The journey left everyone completely exhausted.

    The way an adjective phrase can appear inside a larger phrase will be introduced in the nesting section.

    Adverb Phrases

    An adverb phrase is built around an adverb. Other words may strengthen or limit it.

    quietly very quietly extremely quietly surprisingly quietly

    The head is quietly.

    He answered very quickly. The machine moved unusually slowly. She completed the task remarkably well. They arrived quite early.

    extremely difficult

    Head: difficult

    Head type: adjective

    Phrase type: adjective phrase

    extremely quietly

    Head: quietly

    Head type: adverb

    Phrase type: adverb phrase

    Prepositional Phrases

    A prepositional phrase begins with a preposition and includes the word or group that completes the preposition.

    in the room under the table after lunch to Mina for me

    The internal roles

    under the table

    under = preposition and head of the prepositional phrase

    the table = the unit that completes the preposition

    table = noun and head of that unit

    the = determiner identifying table

    The unit after a preposition is traditionally called the object of the preposition. Some grammar books call it the complement of the preposition. In this guide, both labels refer to the same internal role.

    The whole prepositional phrase performs the sentence-level job

    When a prepositional phrase adds information to the verb or the event, this guide calls the complete phrase an adverbial. We can then state what kind of information it gives.

    Place

    The cat slept under the table.

    The complete prepositional phrase is an adverbial of place.

    Time

    We met after lunch.

    The complete prepositional phrase is an adverbial of time.

    Recipient

    He gave the money to me.

    The complete prepositional phrase is an adverbial identifying the recipient.

    Beneficiary

    She bought coffee for me.

    The complete prepositional phrase is an adverbial identifying the beneficiary.

    A complete analysis

    He gave the money to me.

    to me = prepositional phrase; adverbial identifying the recipient

    Inside the phrase, to = preposition and head

    Inside the phrase, me = pronoun and object/complement of the preposition

    Me does not separately perform another major sentence function. The whole phrase to me performs the sentence-level job; me has an internal role inside that phrase.

    A prepositional phrase can also appear inside a larger noun phrase. That pattern is introduced in the next section.

    Nesting: Phrases Inside Other Phrases

    Nesting occurs when one phrase appears inside a larger phrase. The smaller phrase has an internal role, while the larger phrase may perform a major sentence function.

    Nesting is one of the main ways English adds detail and builds longer sentences without giving every word or phrase a separate sentence-level function.

    1. Adjective phrase inside a noun phrase

    the [very nervous] student

    very nervous = adjective phrase

    It appears inside the larger noun phrase the very nervous student.

    Its internal role is to describe the head noun student.

    2. Adverb phrase inside an adjective phrase

    [extremely] tired

    extremely = one-word adverb phrase

    It appears inside the adjective phrase extremely tired.

    Its internal role is to strengthen the head adjective tired.

    3. Adverb phrase inside a larger adverb phrase

    [very] quietly

    very = one-word adverb phrase

    It appears inside the larger adverb phrase very quietly.

    Its internal role is to strengthen the head adverb quietly.

    4. Noun phrase inside a prepositional phrase

    under [the old table]

    the old table = noun phrase

    It appears inside the prepositional phrase under the old table.

    Its internal role is object/complement of the preposition under.

    5. Prepositional phrase inside a noun phrase

    the student [near the window]

    near the window = prepositional phrase

    It appears inside the noun phrase the student near the window.

    Its internal role is to identify which student is meant.

    Inside that prepositional phrase, another noun phrase appears:

    the student [near [the window]]

    6. Adverb phrase inside a verb phrase

    She has [already] finished it.

    has already finished = verb phrase

    already = one-word adverb phrase placed inside it

    has = helping verb

    finished = main verb

    it = noun phrase functioning as direct object; it remains outside the verb phrase in this guide.

    She has never seen it. They will probably arrive soon. He is still working. We have finally finished.

    A natural sentence with several levels of nesting

    The extremely nervous student near the heavy metal door has already answered the final question very quietly.
    Sentence level [The extremely nervous student near the heavy metal door] [has already answered] [the final question] [very quietly]
    Inside the subject noun phrase The [extremely nervous] student [near the heavy metal door]
    Inside the adjective phrase [extremely] nervous
    Inside the prepositional phrase near [the heavy metal door]
    Inside the verb phrase has [already] answered
    Inside the final adverb phrase [very] quietly

    The extremely nervous student near the heavy metal door = noun phrase; subject

    extremely nervous = adjective phrase inside the subject noun phrase

    extremely = adverb phrase inside that adjective phrase

    near the heavy metal door = prepositional phrase inside the subject noun phrase

    the heavy metal door = noun phrase inside the prepositional phrase

    has already answered = verb phrase; verbal element

    already = adverb phrase inside the verb phrase

    the final question = noun phrase; direct object

    very quietly = adverb phrase; adverbial of manner

    very = adverb phrase inside the larger adverb phrase

    Sentence-Level Functions and Internal Functions

    The same expression must be analysed at the correct level.

    The big dog ran.

    At the sentence level

    the big dog = noun phrase; subject

    Inside the noun phrase

    the = determiner

    big = adjective and head of a one-word adjective phrase describing dog

    dog = head noun

    The adjective big does not independently function as a subject, object, complement, or adverbial. Its role exists inside the subject noun phrase.

    He gave the money to me.

    At the sentence level

    to me = prepositional phrase; adverbial identifying the recipient

    Inside the prepositional phrase

    to = preposition and head

    me = pronoun and object/complement of the preposition

    The whole phrase performs the major sentence function. The individual words and smaller phrases perform internal functions inside that phrase.

    A Reliable Method for Analysing Phrases

    1. Find the major sentence functions.

      Identify the subject, verbal element, objects, complements, and adverbials.

    2. Put brackets around each complete phrase.

      Do not begin with individual words. First find the larger units.

    3. Name the phrase type.

      Use the head to decide whether the unit is a noun, verb, adjective, adverb, or prepositional phrase.

    4. Look inside every phrase.

      Find determiners, heads, describing phrases, strengthening phrases, and objects/complements of prepositions.

    5. Look for nesting.

      Check whether any phrase appears inside another phrase.

    6. Keep external and internal roles separate.

      The larger phrase may be the subject, while its smaller parts only perform roles inside that subject.

    A one-word phrase can have an external sentence function and also contain only one head word. A multiword phrase has the same external function as a unit, while its words and smaller phrases have internal roles.

    Final Review

    Phrase
    A grammatical unit that may contain one word or several words.
    Head
    The central word that usually determines the phrase type.
    Noun phrase
    A phrase built around a noun or pronoun.
    Verb phrase
    A phrase built around a main verb and possibly helping verbs, modal verbs, negative words, and inserted adverb phrases.
    Adjective phrase
    A phrase built around an adjective.
    Adverb phrase
    A phrase built around an adverb.
    Prepositional phrase
    A phrase headed by a preposition and completed by an object/complement.
    Object of a preposition
    The word or phrase that completes a preposition inside a prepositional phrase.

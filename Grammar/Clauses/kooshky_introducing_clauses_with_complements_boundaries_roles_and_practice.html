    Introducing Clauses

    How English builds clauses from phrases, combines clauses into larger sentences, and nests one clause inside another.

    Collected and compiled by Amir Kooshky.

    From Words to Phrases to Sentences

    We already know that individual words can combine into phrases, and phrases can combine to form a complete sentence.

    The dog near the front gate barked very loudly at the neighbors.

    This sentence is made up of several phrases:

    [The dog near the front gate] [barked] [very loudly] [at the neighbors]

    The dog near the front gate

    Phrase type: noun phrase

    Sentence function: subject

    barked

    Phrase type: one-word verb phrase

    Sentence function: verbal element

    very loudly

    Phrase type: adverb phrase

    Sentence function: adverbial of manner

    at the neighbors

    Phrase type: prepositional phrase

    Sentence function: adverbial

    Some of these phrases contain smaller phrases inside them.

    [The dog [near [the front gate]]]

    the front gate = noun phrase

    near the front gate = prepositional phrase

    the dog near the front gate = larger noun phrase; subject

    [[very] loudly]

    very = one-word adverb phrase strengthening loudly

    very loudly = larger adverb phrase; adverbial of manner

    [at [the neighbors]]

    the neighbors = noun phrase

    at the neighbors = prepositional phrase; adverbial

    Smaller grammatical units can appear inside larger grammatical units. This is the nesting principle.

    So far, our model has been:

    words → phrases → sentences

    English sentences can also contain another important kind of unit.

    A Sentence with Two Subjects and Two Verbs

    He was tired, but he continued working.

    This sentence contains two subjects:

    • he
    • he

    It also contains two separate verbal structures:

    • was
    • continued working
    According to what we have learned so far, this seems unusual. We have been analysing a basic sentence as a structure containing one subject and one main verbal element. This example appears to contain that basic structure twice.

    We can divide the sentence into two units:

    [He was tired], but [he continued working].

    First unit

    He was tired.

    he = subject

    was = linking verb

    tired = subject complement

    Second unit

    He continued working.

    he = subject

    continued working = verb phrase

    Each unit can stand as a complete sentence by itself:

    He was tired. He continued working.

    English allows us to connect these two smaller sentences and create one larger sentence:

    He was tired, but he continued working.

    The conjunction but connects the two units and shows a contrast between them.

    Combining Smaller Sentences into a Larger Sentence

    Consider another pair of sentences:

    First sentence

    Sara opened the window.

    Sara = subject

    opened = verb

    the window = direct object

    Second sentence

    David closed the door.

    David = subject

    closed = verb

    the door = direct object

    English allows us to combine them:

    Sara opened the window, and David closed the door.

    The larger sentence contains the two original sentence units:

    [Sara opened the window], and [David closed the door].

    The conjunction and connects them.

    This follows the same general nesting principle that we have already studied:

    words → phrases → sentence-like units → larger sentences

    Grammarians therefore needed a precise name for these smaller sentence-like units that can exist inside a larger sentence.

    What Is a Clause?

    We have been calling the two parts of the sentence sentence-like units:

    [He was tired], but [he continued working].

    But sentence-like unit is only an informal description. Grammarians needed a precise term for these smaller structures that could be joined together to create a larger sentence.

    Before choosing a name, they first needed to answer an important question:

    How can we recognize one of these units?

    Look again at the two parts:

    He was tired

    Subject: he

    Verb: was

    he continued working

    Subject: he

    Verb: continued

    How did we separate these units at the beginning?

    We noticed that each unit had its own subject and its own verb.

    Each sentence-like unit contains its own subject and verb.

    Grammarians call such a unit a clause.

    DEFINITION

    A clause is a group of words that contains a subject and a verb.

    Consider again:

    Sara opened the window, and David closed the door.

    We can recognize two clauses because we can find two separate subject–verb structures:

    [Sara opened the window], and [David closed the door].

    Clause 1

    Subject: Sara

    Verb: opened

    Clause 2

    Subject: David

    Verb: closed

    The conjunction and connects the two clauses, but each clause has its own subject and verb.

    Testing the Definition

    the dog near the front gate

    This is a long group of words.

    It contains determiners, nouns, an adjective, and a preposition.

    However, it does not contain a verb.

    Result: noun phrase, not a clause.

    the dog waited near the front gate

    Subject: the dog

    Verb: waited

    Result: clause.

    The important difference is not length.

    A long phrase

    the unusually large black dog near the front gate

    This group has no verb, so it is not a clause.

    A short clause

    Dogs bark.

    Subject: dogs

    Verb: bark

    Does this unit contain its own subject and verb? If it does, it is a clause.

    Phrase or Clause?

    Phrase

    after lunch

    Prepositional phrase

    No subject–verb structure

    Clause

    The students finished lunch.

    Subject: the students

    Verb: finished

    Phrase

    near the old building

    No subject–verb structure

    Clause

    The students waited near the old building.

    Subject: the students

    Verb: waited

    Phrase

    very tired

    Adjective phrase

    Head: tired

    very strengthens tired.

    Clause

    Sara was very tired.

    Subject: Sara

    Verb: was

    The phrase very tired exists inside the clause:

    Sara was [very tired].

    A clause can therefore contain several phrases.

    Phrases Inside Clauses

    A clause is normally built from phrases.

    The dog near the front gate barked very loudly.

    The entire group is a clause because it contains:

    • subject: the dog near the front gate
    • verb: barked
    [[The dog [near [the front gate]]] [barked] [[very] loudly]]

    Clause-level structure

    the dog near the front gate
    noun phrase; subject
    barked
    one-word verb phrase; verbal element
    very loudly
    adverb phrase; adverbial of manner

    Smaller phrases inside those phrases

    the front gate
    noun phrase
    near the front gate
    prepositional phrase inside the subject noun phrase
    very
    one-word adverb phrase inside the larger adverb phrase

    The clause is therefore a larger grammatical unit made from phrases.

    Clauses Inside Larger Sentences

    He was tired, but he continued working.

    The complete sentence contains two clauses:

    [He was tired], but [he continued working].

    Clause 1

    Subject: he

    Verb: was

    Subject complement: tired

    Clause 2

    Subject: he

    Verb phrase: continued working

    The conjunction but joins the two clauses and creates one larger sentence.

    Sara opened the window, and David closed the door.
    [Sara opened the window], and [David closed the door].

    Each bracketed unit is a clause because each one contains its own subject and verb.

    One Clause Can Form a Complete Sentence

    Just as a phrase can contain only one word, a sentence can contain only one clause.

    Dogs bark.

    This sentence contains one clause:

    [Dogs bark].
    • dogs = subject
    • bark = verb

    Because this clause forms a complete statement, it can stand alone as a sentence.

    Sara opened the door.

    This is both:

    • one clause; and
    • one complete sentence.
    A sentence does not need several clauses. One complete clause is enough.

    But Not Every Clause Can Stand Alone

    A phrase can sometimes contain only one word:

    dogs quietly tired

    However, being a phrase does not mean that the unit can stand alone as a complete sentence.

    the tired student

    This is a complete noun phrase, but it does not express a complete statement by itself.

    In a similar way, every clause contains a subject and a verb, but not every clause can stand alone as a complete sentence.

    he was tired

    Subject: he

    Verb: was

    Complete statement

    because he was tired

    Subject: he

    Verb: was

    The thought is unfinished by itself.

    The word because makes the reader expect more information:

    What happened because he was tired?

    This gives us two major kinds of clauses:

    1. independent clauses
    2. dependent clauses

    Independent Clauses

    DEFINITION

    An independent clause contains a subject and a verb and expresses a complete thought. It can stand alone as a complete sentence.

    He was tired. Sara opened the window. The students finished the assignment. The old machine stopped suddenly.

    Each example contains its own subject and verb, and each one makes complete sense by itself.

    Independent clauses inside a larger sentence

    An independent clause does not stop being independent when it is joined to another clause.

    He was tired, but he continued working.
    [He was tired], but [he continued working].

    Both units are independent clauses because each one could stand alone as a sentence.

    Sara opened the window, and David closed the door.
    [Sara opened the window], and [David closed the door].

    Dependent Clauses

    DEFINITION

    A dependent clause contains a subject and a verb but does not express a complete thought by itself. It depends on another clause to complete its meaning.

    because he was tired

    This is a clause because it contains:

    • subject: he
    • verb: was

    But it cannot stand alone naturally as a complete sentence. It needs an independent clause:

    He went home because he was tired.

    Independent clause

    He went home

    Complete thought

    Can stand alone

    Dependent clause

    because he was tired

    Gives the reason

    Cannot normally stand alone

    More dependent clauses

    when the class ended

    This unit contains the class as its subject and ended as its verb. It is therefore a clause, but it makes the reader expect another statement:

    The students left when the class ended.
    although she studied carefully

    This unit contains she as its subject and studied as its verb, but it also leaves the reader waiting for contrasting information:

    She made several mistakes although she studied carefully.

    What Makes a Clause Dependent?

    Words such as the following can introduce dependent clauses:

    because although when while if since before after unless

    Independent clause

    He was tired.

    This is complete.

    Dependent clause

    because he was tired

    The subject and verb remain, but because makes the clause depend on another statement.

    Independent clause

    The class ended.

    This is complete.

    Dependent clause

    when the class ended

    The word when makes the unit depend on another statement.

    The presence of a subject and verb tells us that the unit is a clause. Its ability to stand alone as a complete statement tells us whether it is independent or dependent.

    How Do We Find Clause Boundaries?

    Finding the subjects and verbs tells us how many clause structures may be present. The next question is where each clause begins and ends.

    He went to the store because he needed something.

    A learner may reasonably wonder:

    Does because belong to the first clause or the second clause?

    The correct division is:

    [He went to the store] [because he needed something].

    Because belongs with the second clause. It introduces that clause, shows its relationship to the first clause, and makes it dependent.

    USEFUL GENERAL RULE

    A word that introduces a dependent clause normally belongs at the beginning of that dependent clause.

    because

    [We stayed inside] [because it was raining].

    when

    [The students left] [when the class ended].

    if

    [Call me] [if you need help].

    although

    [Although she was tired], [she continued working].

    A Practical Method

    1. Find every subject–verb pair. Each pair may mark the centre of a clause.
    2. Look for clause-introducing words. Words such as because, when, if, although, before, after, while, since, and unless often mark the beginning of a dependent clause.
    3. Keep the introducing word with the clause it introduces. For example, use [because he needed something], not [because] [he needed something].
    4. Check whether the group can stand alone. This helps distinguish an independent clause from a dependent one.
    5. Check for nesting. One subject–verb unit may appear inside the boundaries of a larger clause.

    What About Coordinating Words?

    Words such as and, but, or, so, and yet can connect clauses that stand beside each other:

    Sara opened the window, and David closed the door.
    [Sara opened the window], and [David closed the door].

    In this kind of analysis, the conjunction sits between the two clauses and links them. It is not part of the subject–verb structure inside either clause.

    Punctuation Can Help, but It Is Not Enough

    Commas and other punctuation marks sometimes make clause boundaries easier to see:

    If the weather improves, we will leave early.

    However, many clauses are not separated by commas:

    We will leave early if the weather improves.

    Therefore, punctuation is only a clue. The more reliable method is to identify the subject–verb structures and understand the words that introduce or connect clauses.

    There is no single mechanical trick that finds every clause boundary in English.

    The general rules above are extremely useful, but some boundaries become clear only after students learn the different ways clauses are formed and connected.

    As you study more examples, you will gradually recognize common clause patterns automatically. In the next part of these notes, we will examine the different types of clauses in detail. That knowledge will make difficult clause boundaries much easier to identify.

    Phrase, Independent Clause, or Dependent Clause?

    PHRASE

    after the long class

    No subject–verb structure

    Prepositional phrase

    INDEPENDENT CLAUSE

    the long class ended

    Subject: the long class

    Verb: ended

    Can stand alone

    DEPENDENT CLAUSE

    after the long class ended

    Subject: the long class

    Verb: ended

    Cannot normally stand alone

    after the long class is a phrase because it has no verb. after the long class ended is a clause because it contains a subject and verb.

    A Clause and a Sentence Are Not Always the Same Thing

    A clause is defined by its internal structure:

    It contains a subject and a verb.

    A sentence is a complete grammatical unit used to make a statement, ask a question, give a command, or express another complete communicative purpose.

    One clause, one sentence

    Sara laughed.

    Two clauses, one sentence

    Sara laughed, and David smiled.

    A dependent clause inside a sentence

    David smiled because Sara laughed.

    • A one-clause sentence contains one independent clause.
    • A multi-clause sentence contains more than one clause.
    • Some clauses are independent.
    • Some clauses are dependent.

    Clauses Can Be Nested Too

    Just as one phrase can appear inside another phrase, one clause can appear inside another clause.

    The teacher said the test was easy.
    [The teacher said [the test was easy]].

    The inner clause

    the test was easy

    Subject: the test

    Verb: was

    This is a clause because it has its own subject and verb.

    The larger clause

    The teacher said the test was easy

    Subject: the teacher

    Verb: said

    The inner clause the test was easy appears inside the larger clause and completes the meaning of said by telling us what the teacher said.

    This is genuine clause nesting: [The teacher said [the test was easy]].

    More Examples of a Clause Inside a Clause

    [I think [the answer is correct]].

    Inner clause: the answer is correct. It tells us what I think.

    [Mina knew [the door was locked]].

    Inner clause: the door was locked. It tells us what Mina knew.

    [We noticed [the lights were off]].

    Inner clause: the lights were off. It tells us what we noticed.

    [The manager believed [the machine was safe]].

    Inner clause: the machine was safe.

    [He discovered [the window was open]].

    Inner clause: the window was open.

    In every example, the inner clause has its own subject and verb, but it exists inside a larger clause.

    Several Levels of Clause Nesting

    I know Sara thinks the plan will work.
    [I know [Sara thinks [the plan will work]]].
    Innermost clause the plan will work — subject: the plan; verb phrase: will work
    Middle clause Sara thinks the plan will work — contains the inner clause
    Largest clause I know Sara thinks the plan will work — contains the middle clause

    More Multiple-Level Examples

    [The teacher said [Mina believed [the test was easy]]].

    Innermost: the test was easy. Middle: Mina believed the test was easy.

    [We knew [David thought [the meeting was cancelled]]].

    Innermost: the meeting was cancelled. Middle: David thought the meeting was cancelled.

    [I heard [the manager said [the office was closing]]].

    Innermost: the office was closing. Middle: the manager said the office was closing.

    [Sara remembered [her teacher had explained [the rule was important]]].

    Innermost: the rule was important. Middle: her teacher had explained the rule was important.

    A Dependent Clause Inside Another Clause

    Sara left because the store closed when the power failed.
    [Sara left [because the store closed [when the power failed]]].

    when the power failed is nested inside because the store closed when the power failed.

    The larger dependent clause is itself nested inside the complete sentence.

    More Examples

    [We stayed inside [because the roads froze [after the storm ended]]].

    after the storm ended is inside the larger dependent clause.

    [The children cheered [when the teacher announced [school was closed]]].

    school was closed is inside when the teacher announced school was closed.

    [He called me [after the doctor confirmed [the results were normal]]].

    the results were normal is inside the clause introduced by after.

    [Mina relaxed [when the manager said [the problem was solved]]].

    the problem was solved is inside the clause introduced by when.

    [The workers left [because the supervisor believed [the building was unsafe]]].

    the building was unsafe is inside the clause introduced by because.

    Phrases and Clauses Can Be Nested at the Same Time

    The nervous student near the door said the final question was extremely difficult.
    [The nervous student near the door said [the final question was extremely difficult]].

    the nervous student near the door = noun phrase; subject of the larger clause

    near the door = prepositional phrase inside that subject noun phrase

    the final question was extremely difficult = nested clause

    the final question = noun phrase; subject of the nested clause

    extremely difficult = adjective phrase; subject complement

    extremely = adverb phrase inside the adjective phrase

    Every Nested Clause Has an External Role and an Internal Structure

    When a clause is nested inside another clause, we can analyse it at two different levels.

    EXTERNAL LEVEL

    The whole nested clause performs a grammatical role inside the clause that contains it.

    INTERNAL LEVEL

    Inside the nested clause, its own words and phrases perform roles such as subject, verb, object, complement, and adverbial.

    The Broader Meaning of “Complement”

    Earlier, we studied two specific kinds of complements:

    • subject complements, which describe or identify the subject;
    • object complements, which describe or identify the object.

    However, grammar also uses complement in a broader sense.

    BROADER DEFINITION

    A complement is a word, phrase, or clause that completes the meaning of another word or structure.

    For example, some verbs feel incomplete unless something follows them:

    Incomplete meaning

    The teacher said …

    The reader naturally asks: said what?

    Completed meaning

    The teacher said the test was easy.

    The clause the test was easy completes the meaning of said.

    This broader use of complement includes many structures that complete the meaning of verbs, adjectives, nouns, or other grammatical units. It is wider than the two special categories subject complement and object complement.

    Example 1: A Nested Clause Functioning as an Adverbial

    He went home because he was tired.
    [He went home [because he was tired]].

    EXTERNAL ROLE OF THE NESTED CLAUSE

    The whole clause because he was tired functions as an adverbial of reason in the larger clause. It explains why he went home.

    INTERNAL STRUCTURE OF THE NESTED CLAUSE

    he = subject

    was = linking verb

    tired = subject complement

    Example 2: A Nested Clause Functioning as the Subject

    That the team won surprised everyone.
    [[That the team won] surprised everyone].

    EXTERNAL ROLE OF THE NESTED CLAUSE

    The whole clause that the team won functions as the subject of the larger clause. It identifies what surprised everyone.

    INTERNAL STRUCTURE OF THE NESTED CLAUSE

    the team = subject

    won = verb

    Example 3: Object or Complement?

    The teacher said the test was easy.
    [The teacher said [the test was easy]].

    EXTERNAL ROLE OF THE NESTED CLAUSE

    The whole clause the test was easy completes the meaning of said. It tells us what the teacher said.

    INTERNAL STRUCTURE OF THE NESTED CLAUSE

    the test = subject

    was = linking verb

    easy = subject complement

    TRADITIONAL SCHOOL GRAMMAR

    Direct object

    Many traditional grammar books call the whole clause the test was easy the direct object of said.

    The usual question is: The teacher said what?

    MANY NEWER GRAMMAR FRAMEWORKS

    Complement of the verb

    Many newer approaches call the same clause a complement of said.

    They often reserve the term object mainly for noun phrases and use complement for a clause that completes a verb’s meaning.

    You may therefore see the same structure described in two ways:

    the direct object of said

    or

    a clause functioning as the complement of said

    This difference should not matter much to you at this stage. Both analyses recognize the same important fact: the entire nested clause completes the meaning of said.

    In these notes, we may sometimes use the clearer description the clause completes the meaning of the verb, while also mentioning the traditional object label when it is useful.

    Several Levels of Roles at Once

    I know Sara thinks the plan will work.
    [I know [Sara thinks [the plan will work]]].

    the plan will work

    External role: completes the meaning of thinks.

    Internal roles: the plan = subject; will work = verb phrase.

    Sara thinks the plan will work

    External role: completes the meaning of know.

    Internal roles: Sara = subject; thinks = verb; the plan will work = nested clause completing thinks.

    I know Sara thinks the plan will work

    This is the largest clause. Internally, I is its subject, know is its verb, and the clause Sara thinks the plan will work completes the meaning of know.

    A nested clause is one complete unit from the outside, but it also has its own grammatical structure on the inside.

    The Main Principle

    To find a clause nested inside another clause:

    1. Find every subject–verb structure.
    2. Place brackets around the smallest complete clause units.
    3. Check whether one clause appears inside the boundaries of another clause.

    Clauses beside each other

    [The teacher spoke], and [the students listened].

    One clause inside another

    [The teacher said [the students were ready]].

    [I know [Sara thinks [the plan will work]]].

    This sentence contains several levels of clause nesting.

    The Expanded Structure

    Our earlier model was:

    words → phrases → sentences

    We can now add the missing level:

    words → phrases → clauses → sentences

    WORDS COMBINE INTO PHRASES

    the dog

    very loudly

    near the gate

    PHRASES COMBINE INTO CLAUSES

    The dog barked very loudly.

    ONE CLAUSE CAN FORM A SENTENCE

    The dog barked.

    CLAUSES CAN FORM LARGER SENTENCES

    The dog barked, and the neighbors complained.

    The neighbors complained because the dog barked.

    This sentence contains an independent clause and a dependent clause.

    A sentence may contain words inside phrases, phrases inside clauses, and clauses inside the complete sentence.

    Simple, Compound, Complex, and Compound-Complex Sentences

    Once we can identify independent and dependent clauses, we can also classify complete sentences according to the clauses they contain.

    KEY IDEA

    These labels describe the clause structure of the complete sentence.

    SIMPLE SENTENCE

    One independent clause

    The students waited outside.
    [The students waited outside].

    A simple sentence contains one independent clause. It may still contain many words and phrases.

    COMPOUND SENTENCE

    Two or more independent clauses

    The students waited outside, and the teacher opened the door.
    [The students waited outside], and [the teacher opened the door].

    A compound sentence joins independent clauses. Each clause could stand alone as a complete sentence.

    COMPLEX SENTENCE

    One independent clause plus at least one dependent clause

    The students entered when the teacher opened the door.
    [The students entered] [when the teacher opened the door].

    A complex sentence contains an independent clause and at least one dependent clause.

    COMPOUND-COMPLEX SENTENCE

    Two or more independent clauses plus at least one dependent clause

    The students entered when the teacher opened the door, and the lesson began.
    [The students entered [when the teacher opened the door]], and [the lesson began].

    A compound-complex sentence combines the structures of compound and complex sentences.

    The Length of a Sentence Does Not Determine Its Type

    A long simple sentence

    The extremely nervous students near the front entrance waited very quietly for several minutes.

    This is still a simple sentence because it contains only one independent clause.

    A short compound sentence

    I called, but she left.

    This is compound because it contains two independent clauses.

    Simple: one independent clause

    Compound: two or more independent clauses

    Complex: one independent clause plus at least one dependent clause

    Compound-complex: two or more independent clauses plus at least one dependent clause

    Final Summary

    Phrase
    A grammatical unit built around a head word.
    Clause
    A group of words containing its own subject and verb.
    Independent clause
    A clause that expresses a complete thought and can stand alone as a sentence.
    Dependent clause
    A clause that contains a subject and verb but cannot normally stand alone as a complete sentence.
    Clause nesting
    A structure in which one clause appears inside another clause.

    Phrase

    the old dog

    very quietly

    near the gate

    Clause

    the old dog barked

    Sara opened the door

    the students were waiting

    An independent clause can stand alone:

    The students left.

    A dependent clause cannot normally stand alone:

    when the class ended

    First ask: Does the unit contain its own subject and verb?

    If yes, it is a clause.

    Then ask: Can it stand alone as a complete statement?

    If yes, it is independent. If no, it is dependent.

    Practice: Find and Classify the Clauses

    This final practice reviews the entire guide. For each sentence, identify every clause, decide whether it is independent or dependent, and check whether any clause is nested inside another.

    YOUR METHOD

    1. Underline or circle every subject.
    2. Find the verb connected to each subject.
    3. Use those subject–verb pairs to mark the clause boundaries.
    4. Ask whether each clause can stand alone as a complete statement.
    5. Check whether one clause appears inside another clause.
    A clause may be dependent because it begins with a word such as because, if, when, although, before, or after. A clause may also be dependent because it is embedded inside another clause and completes its meaning.

    Worked Sample

    The students left when the class ended.

    STEP 1 · FIND THE SUBJECT–VERB PAIRS

    the students + left

    the class + ended

    STEP 2 · MARK THE CLAUSES

    [The students left] [when the class ended].

    STEP 3 · CLASSIFY THEM

    Clause 1 Independent

    [The students left]

    It contains a subject and verb and can stand alone as a complete statement.

    Clause 2 Dependent

    [when the class ended]

    It contains its own subject and verb, but when makes it depend on the first clause.

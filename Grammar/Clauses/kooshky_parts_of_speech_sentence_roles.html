    Parts of Speech and Sentence Functions

    A practical guide to recognizing what kind of word something is and what job it performs inside a sentence.

    Collected and compiled by Amir Kooshky.

    Telegram · @KooshkyTOEFL Instagram · @kooshkytoefl Personal Telegram DM

    1. Two Different Ways to Describe a Word

    A word can be described by its part of speech and by its sentence function. These are related, but they are not the same classification.

    Part of speech

    Answers: What kind of word is this?

    Examples: noun, verb, adjective, adverb.

    It describes the word’s grammatical category and helps determine where it can go and how it can behave.

    Sentence function

    Answers: What job is this word doing here?

    Examples: subject, direct object, subject complement.

    It only makes sense when the word appears inside a sentence and has a relationship with the other words.

    Sara called David.
    Sara = noun; subject
    David called Sara.
    Sara = noun; direct object

    Sara remains a noun in both sentences, but its sentence function changes.

    The same word form can have different parts of speech

    The room is clean.
    clean = adjective
    Please clean the room.
    clean = verb

    The spelling is the same, and the meanings are related, but the word behaves differently. Examine its meaning, position, nearby words, and possible forms.

    Why “verb” appears in both systems

    Birds fly.

    As a part of speech, fly is a verb: that is its category. As a sentence function, it is the main verb: that is its job here.

    Keep the two questions separate.1. What kind of word is it?
    2. What job does it perform in this sentence?

    2. The Main Parts of Speech

    These categories describe individual words. A word may have more than one possible category, so always examine how it is used.

    Noun

    Names a person, place, thing, event, state, or idea.

    teacher · city · book · meeting · happiness

    It can often follow a, the, this, or my.

    Pronoun

    Replaces a noun.

    I · she · they · me · her · them

    She often appears as a subject; her often appears as an object.

    Verb

    Expresses an action, event, change, state, or relationship.

    run · break · become · know · seem · be

    It can often change form: work, works, worked, working.

    Adjective

    Describes or classifies a noun or pronoun.

    a difficult question
    The question is difficult.

    Adverb

    Can describe a verb, adjective, another adverb, or the whole statement.

    spoke quietly
    very difficult

    Not every adverb ends in -ly.

    Determiner

    Comes before a noun and identifies, limits, counts, or specifies it.

    the student · my phone · several questions

    Preposition

    Shows a relationship involving place, time, direction, method, or connection.

    in class · after lunch · with her · under the table

    A noun or pronoun after it is its object.

    Conjunction

    Connects equal words or other grammatical elements.

    tea and coffee
    difficult but fair

    Interjection

    Expresses a sudden reaction, emotion, greeting, or response.

    Wow! · Ouch! · Oh! · Hey!

    It is often separate from the core grammar.

    Some grammar books use additional labels such as particle. At this stage, the nine categories above are the most important ones to recognize.

    3. The Main Sentence Functions

    A sentence function is the job performed by a word or complete grammatical unit inside a sentence. The most important functions are not merely labels to memorize: each one has a different relationship with the verb and with the rest of the sentence.

    At this stage, focus on the major sentence-level jobs: subject, verbal element, direct object, indirect object, subject complement, object complement, and adverbial.

    The Basic Sentence Skeleton

    Every complete statement needs a subject and a finite verbal element.

    Sara laughed.
    • Sara = subject
    • laughed = main verb

    Some verbs can stand alone. Other verbs need or allow additional elements.

    Sara opened it.
    • Sara = subject
    • opened = main verb
    • it = direct object
    Sara gave me money.
    • Sara = subject
    • gave = main verb
    • me = indirect object
    • money = direct object

    The number and type of elements in a sentence depend largely on the verb.

    The Predicate

    In traditional grammar, the predicate is everything that the sentence says about the subject. It normally includes the verb and any objects, complements, or adverbials connected to it.

    Sara gave me money yesterday.
    • Sara = subject
    • gave me money yesterday = predicate

    Because the predicate can contain several different jobs, this guide usually analyses its parts separately rather than treating it as one undivided block.

    1. Subject

    The subject is usually the person, thing, idea, or situation that the statement is about.

    Sara laughed.
    Rain stopped.
    Prices increased.

    The subject is often the person or thing performing an action, but this is not always true.

    Sara fell.

    Sara did not deliberately perform the action, but Sara is still the subject because the statement is about her.

    The glass broke.

    The glass is not an active “doer,” but it is still the subject.

    Useful clues for finding the subject

    • Ask what person, thing, or idea the statement is mainly about.
    • Look for the word that controls agreement with the present-tense verb: The student works, but The students work.
    • Do not assume that the first noun is always the subject.
    Yesterday, Sara arrived.

    Yesterday comes first, but it tells us when the event happened. The subject is Sara.

    2. The Verbal Element: Main Verbs and Helping Verbs

    The verbal element tells us what happens, what someone does, what state exists, or what connection is being made.

    Main verb

    The main verb carries the central meaning.

    Sara laughed.
    I understand.
    The situation changed.

    A main verb does not have to express a physical action. Verbs such as know, understand, own, seem, and exist express states or relationships.

    Helping verb

    A helping verb works with the main verb to express tense, time, completion, continuation, ability, possibility, obligation, or another grammatical meaning.

    Sara has finished.
    • has = helping verb
    • finished = main verb
    They are working.
    • are = helping verb
    • working = main verb
    She can swim.
    • can = modal helping verb
    • swim = main verb
    “Verb” is used in two ways. As a part of speech, it names a word category. As a sentence function, it refers to the verbal element in a particular sentence.

    3. Direct Object

    A direct object normally follows an action verb and identifies the person or thing directly affected by the action, transferred by the action, perceived, understood, created, or discussed.

    Sara opened it.

    It is the thing directly affected by the action.

    David bought coffee.

    Coffee is the thing bought.

    I understand you.

    You completes the meaning of understand: I understand whom? You.

    Not every sentence has a direct object

    Sara laughed.

    Nothing receives the action. The verb laughed does not take a direct object here.

    The baby slept.

    Slept is complete without an object.

    Do not confuse a subject complement with a direct object

    Sara became angry.

    Angry does not receive an action. It describes Sara, so it is a subject complement, not a direct object.

    A word that appears after a verb is not automatically a direct object. First identify what kind of verb it follows and what relationship it has with the subject.

    4. Indirect Object

    An indirect object usually identifies the person who receives something or the person who benefits from an action.

    It normally appears in this pattern:

    subject + verb + indirect object + direct object
    Sara gave me money.
    • money = direct object: the thing transferred
    • me = indirect object: the recipient

    To distinguish the two objects, first find the thing transferred, shown, sent, told, or bought. That is usually the direct object. Then find the person who receives it or benefits from it. That is usually the indirect object.

    David sent her flowers.
    • flowers = direct object
    • her = indirect object
    The teacher showed us the answer.
    • the answer = direct object
    • us = indirect object
    My brother bought me coffee.
    • coffee = direct object
    • me = indirect object because I benefit from the action

    Not every verb allows an indirect object

    Sara opened the door.

    The sentence has a direct object, the door, but no recipient or beneficiary. Therefore, it has no indirect object.

    Find the transferred thing first. If there is no separate thing being given, sent, shown, told, bought, or made, there is usually no indirect object.

    5. Linking Verbs

    A linking verb connects the subject to information that describes or identifies it. It does not send an action from the subject toward an object.

    Sara is tired.

    The verb is links Sara to the description tired.

    Mina became a doctor.

    The verb became links Mina to the identity doctor.

    Common linking verbs include:

    • forms of be: am, is, are, was, were
    • seem
    • become
    • appear
    • remain
    • feel
    • look
    • sound
    • smell
    • taste

    The same verb may be linking in one sentence and an action verb in another

    The soup smells delicious.

    Smells is linking because it connects soup to the description delicious.

    Sara smelled it.

    Smelled is an action verb here, and it is the direct object.

    6. What Is a Complement?

    A complement completes a grammatical relationship by describing, identifying, or completing another element. In this guide, the two most important kinds are:

    • subject complement: refers back to the subject
    • object complement: refers back to the direct object

    The most important question is:

    Which earlier element does this word describe or identify?

    Subject complement

    Sara became angry.

    Angry describes the subject Sara.

    Object complement

    The news made Sara angry.

    Angry describes the direct object Sara.

    7. Subject Complement

    A subject complement comes after a linking verb and describes or identifies the subject.

    Adjective as subject complement

    Sara is tired.

    Tired describes Sara.

    The problem seems serious.

    Serious describes the problem.

    The soup smells delicious.

    Delicious describes the soup.

    Noun as subject complement

    Mina is a doctor.

    Doctor identifies Mina. Mina and the doctor are the same person.

    David became captain.

    Captain identifies the role David entered.

    A useful equality test

    A subject complement often creates a description or identity relationship:

    Mina = doctor
    Sara = tired

    This does not mean the two words are grammatically identical. It means the complement refers back to the subject.

    Compare a complement and an object

    Sara is a doctor.

    A doctor identifies Sara, so it is a subject complement.

    Sara called a doctor.

    A doctor is a different participant receiving the action of called, so it is a direct object.

    After a linking verb, look for a subject complement—not a direct object.

    8. Object Complement

    An object complement comes after a direct object and describes it, identifies it, or states the result of the action.

    The basic pattern is:

    subject + verb + direct object + object complement
    The news made her angry.
    • her = direct object
    • angry = object complement describing her
    They elected him president.
    • him = direct object
    • president = object complement identifying him
    We painted it blue.
    • it = direct object
    • blue = object complement describing its resulting condition

    How to recognize an object complement

    Check whether the final word refers back to the object.

    her = angry
    him = president
    it = blue

    Do not confuse an object complement with an indirect object

    Sara gave him money.

    Him is the indirect object and money is the direct object. Money does not describe or identify him.

    Sara called him foolish.

    Him is the direct object and foolish is the object complement because it describes him.

    Two words after a verb do not automatically mean “indirect object + direct object.” Check whether the second word is a transferred thing or a description of the object.

    9. Adverbial

    An adverbial gives additional information about the event, situation, or complete statement. It often tells us:

    • when
    • where
    • how
    • how often
    • the speaker’s attitude toward the whole statement

    Adverbial of time

    She arrived yesterday.

    Yesterday tells us when she arrived.

    Adverbial of place

    She waited outside.

    Outside tells us where she waited.

    Adverbial of manner

    She answered carefully.

    Carefully tells us how she answered.

    Adverbial of frequency

    She often complains.

    Often tells us how frequently the event happens.

    Adverbial commenting on the whole sentence

    Fortunately, nobody fell.

    Fortunately expresses the speaker’s attitude toward the complete situation.

    Adverb and adverbial are not the same label

    Adverb is a part of speech. Adverbial is a sentence function.

    She answered carefully.
    • part of speech: adverb
    • sentence function: adverbial of manner

    However, not every adverb functions as a separate adverbial.

    The test was very difficult.

    Very is an adverb, but it only strengthens the adjective difficult. It is not a separate sentence-level adverbial.

    She spoke very quietly.
    • quietly = adverb; adverbial of manner
    • very = adverb strengthening quietly
    Do not give every adverb a major sentence function. Some adverbs function inside a larger unit by strengthening another word.

    10. Determiners and Adjectives Inside Larger Sentence Parts

    Determiners and adjectives usually do not form separate major sentence functions. They work inside a larger subject, object, or complement.

    The careful student answered.
    • the careful student = complete subject
    • the = determiner identifying student
    • careful = adjective describing student
    • student = central noun

    The words the and careful do not independently function as the subject. They contribute to the larger subject.

    The next guide explains how several words can work together as one sentence part and how such groups are classified.

    4. A Simple Analysis Method

    1. Identify each part of speech. Ask what kind of word it is and how it behaves.
    2. Find the subject and verbal part. Decide what the statement is about and what action, event, state, or connection is expressed.
    3. Look for objects or complements. Decide whether a word receives an action, receives something, or describes the subject or object.
    4. Identify extra information. Determiners identify nouns, adjectives describe nouns, and adverbials tell when, where, how, or how often.
    Sara opened it carefully.
    Sara
    proper noun; subject
    opened
    verb; main verb
    it
    pronoun; direct object
    carefully
    adverb; adverbial of manner
    Do not group words yet.In these exercises, analyse every word separately. Later lessons can explain how groups of words work together.

    6. Final Review

    Part of speech
    The grammatical category of a word: noun, verb, adjective, and so on.
    Sentence function
    The job a word performs in a particular sentence: subject, direct object, complement, and so on.
    Adverb
    A part of speech.
    Adverbial
    A sentence function giving information such as time, place, manner, or frequency.
    Indirect object
    A recipient or beneficiary before the direct object without a preposition.
    Subject complement
    Describes or identifies the subject after a linking verb.
    Object complement
    Describes or identifies the direct object.

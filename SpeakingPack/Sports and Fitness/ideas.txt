# 1. Choosing Between Different Types of Sports

### 1. Individual sports give people greater control over their progress.

**Use in context:** In an individual sport such as swimming or running, a person can choose their own training schedule and pace. Their improvement also depends less on the availability or performance of teammates.

### 2. Team sports suit people who enjoy shared goals.

**Use in context:** Some people exercise more enthusiastically when they feel responsible to a group. Training and competing together can make the activity feel more meaningful than exercising alone.

### 3. Recreational sports can be more sustainable than highly competitive ones.

**Use in context:** A person who plays mainly for enjoyment may feel less pressure to perform perfectly. This can make it easier to continue the activity for many years.

### 4. Competitive sports provide clear goals but may create unnecessary pressure.

**Use in context:** Competitions give athletes something specific to prepare for and can motivate them to train consistently. However, people who focus only on winning may lose their enjoyment of the sport.

### 5. Indoor sports are more dependable in places with difficult weather.

**Use in context:** Activities such as basketball, badminton, or indoor swimming can continue throughout the year. Outdoor sports may be interrupted by extreme heat, snow, rain, or poor air quality.

### 6. Outdoor sports offer exercise and contact with nature at the same time.

**Use in context:** Hiking, cycling, and rowing allow people to leave crowded indoor spaces and enjoy fresh air. The natural environment can make the activity feel less repetitive than a gym workout.

### 7. Low-impact sports are often easier to continue as people age.

**Use in context:** Swimming and gentle cycling place less pressure on the joints than running or jumping. They may therefore be more suitable for older adults or people recovering from injuries.

### 8. Extreme sports attract people who value excitement more than convenience.

**Use in context:** Activities such as rock climbing or skydiving can create a powerful sense of adventure and achievement. However, they usually require specialist training, equipment, travel, and greater acceptance of risk.

# 2. Describing Sports and Physical Activities

### 9. Some sports are physically simple but mentally demanding.

**Use in context:** An activity may not require continuous running, yet it can demand intense concentration and careful planning. Archery, golf, and some racket sports depend heavily on patience and precise decision-making.

### 10. Fast-paced sports reward quick reactions.

**Use in context:** In games such as basketball or table tennis, players have very little time to decide what to do. A brief loss of concentration can immediately give the opponent an advantage.

### 11. Endurance sports test patience as much as physical fitness.

**Use in context:** Long-distance runners and cyclists must continue even when progress feels slow and the body becomes tired. They need to control their pace instead of using all their energy at the beginning.

### 12. Technically difficult sports can be frustrating for beginners.

**Use in context:** New participants may understand the basic rules but still struggle to perform the movements correctly. They often need repeated practice before the activity begins to feel natural.

### 13. Visually exciting sports are easier for casual spectators to enjoy.

**Use in context:** People who know little about a sport may still enjoy dramatic jumps, high speeds, or sudden changes in the score. Slower and more strategic sports may require more knowledge before they become interesting to watch.

### 14. Repetitive exercise can become calming or boring depending on the person.

**Use in context:** Some people enjoy repeating the same movement because it allows them to clear their minds. Others need variety, music, changing routes, or new challenges to remain interested.

### 15. A sport may look effortless when performed by an expert.

**Use in context:** Skilled athletes often make difficult movements appear smooth and natural. This can cause beginners to underestimate the amount of practice, strength, and coordination involved.

### 16. Sports that combine several abilities can remain interesting for longer.

**Use in context:** An activity that requires speed, balance, strategy, and teamwork offers several areas for improvement. A participant can continue developing even after mastering the basic rules.

# 3. Sports Participation, Habits, and Personal Routines

### 17. A fixed exercise schedule reduces the need to make daily decisions.

**Use in context:** If someone always trains on Monday, Wednesday, and Friday, they do not have to decide each day whether to exercise. The routine can gradually make physical activity feel like a normal responsibility.

### 18. Short workouts are practical for people with limited time.

**Use in context:** A student or employee may not have an hour for the gym but can still exercise for fifteen or twenty minutes. Short sessions are easier to fit between other responsibilities and are better than doing nothing.

### 19. Exercising at home removes several practical barriers.

**Use in context:** Home workouts do not require travel time, membership fees, or special clothing. However, some people find it harder to stay focused when they are surrounded by household distractions.

### 20. Exercising with another person creates accountability.

**Use in context:** People are less likely to cancel a workout when a friend is expecting them. A partner can also provide encouragement when motivation is low.

### 21. Variety prevents a routine from becoming mentally tiring.

**Use in context:** Someone can alternate between walking, swimming, strength training, and team sports. Changing the activity keeps the week more interesting and develops different physical abilities.

### 22. Starting with an unrealistic routine often leads to early failure.

**Use in context:** A beginner who plans to train intensely every day may soon become tired or discouraged. A smaller routine that can be maintained is usually more effective in the long term.

### 23. Daily movement can matter even when it is not formal exercise.

**Use in context:** Walking to work, taking the stairs, cleaning, and playing with children all increase physical activity. These habits are especially useful for people who dislike gyms or organized sports.

### 24. Participation often changes during different stages of life.

**Use in context:** A person may play team sports at school, stop exercising during a busy period, and return to fitness later. Time, money, family duties, health, and access to facilities can all affect participation.

### 25. Enjoyment is one of the strongest predictors of consistency.

**Use in context:** People are more likely to continue an activity when they genuinely look forward to it. Choosing the theoretically “best” workout is not useful if the person repeatedly avoids doing it.

# 4. Skills, Techniques, Rules, and Performance

### 26. Correct technique is more valuable than speed in the early stages.

**Use in context:** Beginners who rush may repeat the same movement incorrectly and develop bad habits. Learning slowly with proper form creates a stronger base for later improvement.

### 27. Small technical improvements can produce major performance gains.

**Use in context:** A slight change in posture, timing, or foot placement may make a movement more efficient. The athlete can then perform better without necessarily becoming much stronger.

### 28. Recording performance makes hidden mistakes easier to notice.

**Use in context:** A player may feel that a movement is correct until they watch a video of themselves. Video allows them to compare their actual technique with what they intended to do.

### 29. Understanding strategy separates experienced players from beginners.

**Use in context:** Beginners often concentrate only on hitting, running, or scoring. More experienced players also think about positioning, timing, energy management, and the likely actions of their opponents.

### 30. Rules make competition fair but can discourage casual participants.

**Use in context:** Clear rules are necessary when people compete seriously and compare results. In informal games, simplifying the rules may help beginners participate without feeling confused.

### 31. Performance under pressure is a separate skill.

**Use in context:** A person may perform perfectly during practice but make simple mistakes during an important match. Learning to control nerves requires experience as well as technical ability.

### 32. Consistency is usually more valuable than occasional brilliance.

**Use in context:** A player who performs reliably helps the team more than someone who is excellent one day and careless the next. Consistency makes results easier to predict and builds trust.

### 33. Natural talent gives a useful start but does not replace practice.

**Use in context:** A talented beginner may learn quickly and perform better than others at first. Without discipline and regular training, less talented but more committed athletes can eventually pass them.

# 5. Strength, Endurance, Flexibility, and Overall Fitness

### 34. Different sports develop different kinds of physical ability.

**Use in context:** Weightlifting mainly develops strength and power, while distance running develops cardiovascular endurance. A person’s training should depend on the physical demands of the activity they want to perform.

### 35. Strength is useful in everyday life, not only in competition.

**Use in context:** Strong muscles make it easier to carry objects, maintain good posture, and avoid injury. This becomes increasingly important as people grow older.

### 36. Cardiovascular fitness improves a person’s ability to sustain effort.

**Use in context:** Someone with good cardiovascular endurance can walk, run, or climb stairs without becoming tired immediately. It also helps athletes recover more quickly between periods of intense activity.

### 37. Flexibility without strength may not protect the body.

**Use in context:** A flexible joint still needs strong surrounding muscles to remain stable. Effective fitness programs should therefore combine mobility work with controlled strength training.

### 38. Balance and coordination are often overlooked parts of fitness.

**Use in context:** These abilities help people control their bodies, react to unexpected movement, and avoid falls. They are important in sports but also in ordinary activities and healthy aging.

### 39. Visible muscles are not a complete measure of fitness.

**Use in context:** A muscular person may still have poor stamina, limited flexibility, or weak coordination. Overall fitness includes several abilities that cannot always be seen from appearance.

### 40. Core strength supports many unrelated movements.

**Use in context:** The muscles around the abdomen and back help stabilize the body during running, lifting, throwing, and balancing. A weak core can make other movements less efficient and place more strain on the body.

### 41. Fitness goals should reflect the person’s real needs.

**Use in context:** An office worker may benefit more from better posture and general endurance than from maximum strength. A competitive athlete, by contrast, may need a highly specialized program.

# 6. Training, Preparation, and Recovery

### 42. Gradual progression reduces injury risk.

**Use in context:** The body needs time to adapt to heavier weights, longer distances, or more intense workouts. Increasing the difficulty too quickly can cause pain, fatigue, or loss of motivation.

### 43. Rest days are part of training rather than a break from it.

**Use in context:** Muscles and the nervous system need time to recover after demanding exercise. Without enough rest, performance may decline even when the person is training harder.

### 44. Sleep strongly affects athletic performance.

**Use in context:** A tired athlete may react more slowly, make poor decisions, and recover less effectively. Regular training cannot fully compensate for consistently poor sleep.

### 45. Warm-ups prepare both the body and the mind.

**Use in context:** Gentle movement increases blood flow and allows the athlete to practise important movement patterns. It also marks the transition from ordinary activities to focused training.

### 46. Training should become more specific as a goal approaches.

**Use in context:** General fitness is useful at the beginning of a program, but later sessions should resemble the real demands of the sport. A runner preparing for hills, for example, should eventually practise on similar terrain.

### 47. Tracking progress can reveal improvement that is difficult to feel.

**Use in context:** A person may think they are making no progress because changes happen slowly. Records of distance, time, repetitions, or weight can show gradual improvement more clearly.

### 48. Training through pain can turn a small problem into a serious injury.

**Use in context:** Athletes sometimes ignore pain because they do not want to miss practice or appear weak. Continuing without identifying the cause may greatly extend the recovery period.

### 49. Nutrition supports training but cannot replace it.

**Use in context:** Suitable food provides energy and helps the body recover. However, expensive products or strict diets will not create fitness without consistent physical activity.

### 50. Repeating exactly the same workout can lead to a plateau.

**Use in context:** Once the body becomes used to a routine, the same level of exercise may stop producing improvement. Adjusting the intensity, volume, or type of activity can create a new challenge.

# 7. Motivation, Discipline, Goals, and Barriers

### 51. Clear goals make progress easier to recognize.

**Use in context:** “Become fit” is difficult to measure, while “walk five kilometers comfortably” is more specific. A clear target helps a person choose suitable training and notice improvement.

### 52. Small achievements build motivation more reliably than distant goals.

**Use in context:** Reaching a weekly target gives the person an immediate sense of progress. A goal that is many months away may feel too abstract to influence daily behavior.

### 53. Motivation naturally rises and falls.

**Use in context:** People often feel enthusiastic when they begin a new routine but less interested after several weeks. A stable schedule is useful because it allows them to continue even when the initial excitement disappears.

### 54. Lack of time is sometimes a planning problem rather than a true barrier.

**Use in context:** A person may not have time for a long gym session but may still have time for a walk or brief home workout. Reducing the size of the activity can make participation more realistic.

### 55. Embarrassment prevents many beginners from exercising publicly.

**Use in context:** People may compare themselves with experienced athletes and worry that others are judging them. Beginner-friendly classes and supportive instructors can make the environment less intimidating.

### 56. Immediate comfort often competes with long-term health.

**Use in context:** Staying on the sofa feels easier than exercising after a tiring day. The benefits of exercise appear gradually, while the effort is felt immediately.

### 57. Social support can compensate for weak self-discipline.

**Use in context:** A friend, coach, or group can provide reminders and encouragement. This external structure is especially helpful for people who struggle to maintain routines alone.

### 58. Excessive goals can turn a healthy activity into a source of stress.

**Use in context:** Someone may begin exercising for enjoyment but become obsessed with missing workouts or failing to reach targets. Goals should guide behavior without controlling the person’s entire life.

### 59. Removing barriers is often more effective than demanding more willpower.

**Use in context:** Preparing clothes in advance, choosing a nearby facility, or scheduling a session can make exercise easier to begin. A convenient environment reduces the number of decisions the person must make.

# 8. Personal Experiences, Competition, Achievement, and Failure

### 60. Early sporting experiences can shape lifelong attitudes.

**Use in context:** A supportive coach or enjoyable school team may create a lasting interest in exercise. A humiliating or overly competitive experience can make someone avoid sports for years.

### 61. Losing can reveal weaknesses that winning hides.

**Use in context:** A defeat forces players to examine mistakes in strategy, preparation, or communication. Success may feel better, but it does not always encourage the same level of reflection.

### 62. Personal records provide competition without direct comparison.

**Use in context:** A runner can try to improve their own time instead of beating another person. This approach creates motivation while reducing pressure and unhealthy rivalry.

### 63. A comeback can be more memorable than an easy victory.

**Use in context:** Recovering after falling behind requires persistence and emotional control. The struggle often gives the final result greater meaning.

### 64. Participating can be an achievement even without winning.

**Use in context:** Completing a difficult race or entering a first competition may require months of preparation. For a beginner, finishing can be more important than the final position.

### 65. Competition brings out different sides of a person’s character.

**Use in context:** Some people become more focused and determined when the result matters. Others become aggressive, nervous, or unwilling to accept responsibility for mistakes.

### 66. Memorable sporting experiences are often social rather than purely athletic.

**Use in context:** People may remember travelling with teammates, celebrating together, or receiving encouragement from family more clearly than the score. The relationships surrounding the event can give it lasting value.

### 67. Returning after failure builds confidence differently from immediate success.

**Use in context:** When someone fails, changes their approach, and later succeeds, they gain evidence that improvement is possible. This can create deeper confidence than succeeding easily on the first attempt.

# 9. Teamwork, Coaching, Leadership, and Social Connection

### 68. Team sports teach people to depend on others.

**Use in context:** A player cannot control every part of the game and must trust teammates to perform their roles. This can be difficult for people who prefer complete independence.

### 69. Shared physical effort creates strong social bonds.

**Use in context:** Training, struggling, and improving together give people common experiences. Teammates often become friends because they regularly support one another through difficult situations.

### 70. Good communication prevents avoidable mistakes.

**Use in context:** Players need to share information quickly about positioning, danger, or changes in strategy. Silence or unclear instructions can cause confusion even when everyone is individually skilled.

### 71. A coach should adapt to different personalities.

**Use in context:** Some athletes respond well to direct criticism, while others need calm explanation and encouragement. Using the same approach with everyone may reduce confidence or motivation.

### 72. Leadership is shown through behavior, not only authority.

**Use in context:** A captain earns respect by remaining calm, working hard, and taking responsibility after mistakes. Simply giving orders does not make someone an effective leader.

### 73. Team success sometimes requires personal sacrifice.

**Use in context:** A player may need to accept less attention, a different position, or fewer opportunities to score. This can be frustrating, but it may improve the group’s overall performance.

### 74. Team conflict can be useful when handled respectfully.

**Use in context:** Disagreement may reveal problems that people were previously avoiding. If teammates discuss the issue honestly rather than attacking one another, the conflict can improve cooperation.

### 75. Good sportsmanship matters most after emotional results.

**Use in context:** It is easy to behave respectfully when winning comfortably. Accepting defeat, respecting officials, and congratulating opponents show stronger character.

# 10. Contact Sports, Safety, Injuries, and Risk

### 76. Contact sports can develop courage but may normalize unnecessary aggression.

**Use in context:** Players learn to remain calm during physical pressure and recover after being challenged. However, coaches must clearly separate legal contact from behavior intended to hurt or intimidate others.

### 77. Noncontact sports are not automatically safe.

**Use in context:** Running, gymnastics, and tennis can still cause falls, overuse injuries, or joint problems. The absence of direct contact only removes one category of risk.

### 78. Protective equipment reduces harm but may create false confidence.

**Use in context:** Helmets and padding can lower the seriousness of certain injuries. Players may still behave more dangerously if they believe the equipment makes them completely safe.

### 79. Rule enforcement is as important as written safety rules.

**Use in context:** A rule against dangerous contact has little value if referees rarely apply it. Consistent penalties change behavior more effectively than warnings alone.

### 80. Children may need stricter protection than adults.

**Use in context:** Young athletes may not understand long-term health risks and may continue playing to please adults. Coaches and parents should remove them from activity when symptoms appear.

### 81. Overuse injuries are often caused by repetition and poor recovery.

**Use in context:** Repeating the same movement every day can gradually damage muscles, tendons, or joints. The problem may develop slowly rather than through one dramatic accident.

### 82. Athletes sometimes hide injuries because they fear losing their position.

**Use in context:** A player may worry that a teammate will replace them or that a coach will see them as unreliable. This pressure can encourage unsafe decisions and longer-term damage.

### 83. Recovery requires patience as well as medical treatment.

**Use in context:** A person may feel almost normal before the injured area has fully regained strength. Returning gradually reduces the chance of suffering the same injury again.

# 11. Equipment, Facilities, Cost, and Accessibility

### 84. Low-cost sports are easier to practise regularly.

**Use in context:** Walking or running requires little equipment and can be done in many locations. Sports that need specialist facilities, coaching, and protective gear are less accessible.

### 85. The initial cost of equipment can discourage experimentation.

**Use in context:** People may hesitate to try skiing, cycling, or racket sports because they do not know whether they will continue. Rental services allow beginners to test an activity before making a large purchase.

### 86. Convenient facilities strongly affect participation.

**Use in context:** A person is more likely to exercise when a gym, court, or park is near home or work. Long travel times turn a short workout into a much larger commitment.

### 87. Public facilities can reduce inequality in access to sport.

**Use in context:** Affordable pools, fields, and community centers give lower-income families opportunities that private clubs may not provide. Governments may therefore treat sports facilities as part of public health infrastructure.

### 88. Poor maintenance can make an affordable facility unsafe.

**Use in context:** Damaged equipment, slippery surfaces, and inadequate lighting increase the risk of injury. Low prices are not enough if the environment is not properly maintained.

### 89. Expensive equipment may improve comfort more than performance.

**Use in context:** High-quality shoes or clothing can be lighter, more durable, or more comfortable. However, beginners often gain more from consistent practice than from buying professional-level products.

### 90. Rural communities face different access problems from cities.

**Use in context:** People in large cities may have many facilities but struggle with high fees and crowding. Rural residents may have space for outdoor activity but fewer coaches, clubs, and specialized facilities.

### 91. People with disabilities may need adaptation rather than a completely separate activity.

**Use in context:** Modifying rules, equipment, entrances, or playing surfaces can make many sports accessible. Small changes may allow participants with different abilities to join the same program.

### 92. Schools can expand community access without building new facilities.

**Use in context:** Courts, fields, and gyms often remain unused after school hours. Allowing supervised community use can create more opportunities at a lower public cost.

# 12. Children, Education, Community Programs, and Inclusion

### 93. Children should experience several sports before specializing.

**Use in context:** Trying different activities develops a wider range of physical skills and helps children discover what they enjoy. Early specialization can cause boredom, pressure, and repeated strain on the same parts of the body.

### 94. School sports should serve beginners as well as talented athletes.

**Use in context:** Competitive teams usually focus on students who already perform well. Physical-education programs should also help less confident students develop basic skills and positive attitudes toward movement.

### 95. Enjoyment should be the main goal of children’s early participation.

**Use in context:** Children who associate sport with friendship and fun are more likely to remain active later. Excessive focus on winning can make them feel that participation has no value without success.

### 96. Parents can encourage effort without controlling the experience.

**Use in context:** Supportive parents attend events, provide transportation, and praise improvement. Problems arise when adults criticize every mistake or use the child’s performance to satisfy their own ambitions.

### 97. Community sports create relationships across social groups.

**Use in context:** Local programs bring together people of different ages, occupations, and backgrounds. Regular interaction around a shared activity can strengthen trust within a neighborhood.

### 98. Inclusive programs benefit participants without disabilities as well.

**Use in context:** Adapting activities teaches patience, cooperation, and respect for different abilities. Inclusion can improve the social environment rather than simply helping one group.

### 99. Teenage participation often declines when sport becomes too serious.

**Use in context:** Young people may stop when training requires too much time or when only the strongest players receive attention. Offering recreational options can keep them active without demanding professional-level commitment.

### 100. Free programs still need suitable scheduling and transportation.

**Use in context:** Removing the fee does not solve every access problem. Children may still be unable to attend if sessions are held far away or at times when parents cannot take them.

### 101. Volunteer coaches expand access but require proper training.

**Use in context:** Volunteers make many local programs financially possible. They still need guidance in safety, communication, inclusion, and working responsibly with children.

# 13. Spectators, Professional Sports, Culture, and Ethics

### 102. Supporting a team gives people a shared identity.

**Use in context:** Fans may feel connected to a city, school, workplace, or country through sport. The team also gives strangers an easy topic of conversation and a reason to gather.

### 103. Watching sports provides emotional excitement without physical participation.

**Use in context:** Spectators experience tension, hope, disappointment, and celebration as the result changes. This makes sports valuable as entertainment even for people who rarely exercise.

### 104. Rivalries increase interest but can encourage hostility.

**Use in context:** A long history between teams makes matches feel more meaningful. Problems arise when competition leads to insults, violence, or hatred toward ordinary supporters.

### 105. Professional athletes are paid for rarity as well as effort.

**Use in context:** Their salaries reflect public demand, broadcasting revenue, sponsorship, and the small number of people able to perform at that level. High pay may still seem unfair when compared with socially essential professions.

### 106. A short career can partly justify high professional earnings.

**Use in context:** Many athletes have only a few successful years and face a real risk of injury. However, this argument applies more strongly to ordinary professionals than to the very richest stars.

### 107. Commercial support improves sport but can distort its priorities.

**Use in context:** Sponsorship pays for facilities, events, salaries, and media coverage. Too much commercial influence may cause organizations to value revenue more than athletes, fans, or fair competition.

### 108. Famous athletes become role models whether they choose to or not.

**Use in context:** Children often copy their behavior, clothing, and attitudes. Athletes deserve privacy, but their public actions can still influence millions of people.

### 109. Cheating damages trust beyond a single result.

**Use in context:** Doping or deliberate rule-breaking makes spectators question whether other performances are genuine. Once trust is lost, even honest athletes may be viewed with suspicion.

### 110. National pride can unite people temporarily.

**Use in context:** International competitions give people from different backgrounds a shared experience. Pride becomes harmful when supporters treat other nations as enemies rather than competitors.

# 14. Fitness Culture, Body Image, and Social Pressure

### 111. Fitness goals can become too closely connected to appearance.

**Use in context:** People may begin exercising to improve health but judge success only through weight, muscle size, or photographs. This can hide real improvements in energy, strength, sleep, or endurance.

### 112. Social media often presents unrealistic physical standards.

**Use in context:** Images may be carefully selected, edited, or taken under ideal conditions. Viewers may compare their ordinary bodies with professional or artificial images and feel unnecessarily dissatisfied.

### 113. Visible progress receives more attention than functional improvement.

**Use in context:** Losing weight or gaining muscle is easy to show in a photograph. Better balance, stronger joints, improved stamina, and reduced pain may be more valuable but less noticeable to others.

### 114. Fitness communities can provide support or create comparison.

**Use in context:** Group members may share advice, celebrate progress, and encourage consistency. The same environment can become unhealthy if everyone constantly compares results, bodies, and routines.

### 115. Rest can create guilt in an achievement-focused culture.

**Use in context:** Some people feel lazy whenever they miss a workout, even when they are sick or exhausted. Treating recovery as part of progress creates a more balanced relationship with exercise.

### 116. One body type cannot represent fitness for every sport.

**Use in context:** A marathon runner, swimmer, weightlifter, and gymnast need very different physical qualities. Judging all athletes by the same appearance ignores the purpose of their training.

### 117. Exercise should support life rather than control it.

**Use in context:** A healthy routine improves mood, energy, and physical ability without destroying relationships or other responsibilities. The routine becomes harmful when a person cannot adjust it for illness, work, family, or rest.

### 118. Health messages should avoid humiliating inactive or overweight people.

**Use in context:** Shame may cause people to avoid gyms, doctors, and public exercise spaces. Respectful support and practical opportunities are more likely to create lasting change.

# 15. Technology, Modern Fitness, and Future Developments

### 119. Fitness trackers make gradual progress more visible.

**Use in context:** Daily information about steps, distance, heart rate, or sleep can show patterns that people would otherwise miss. This feedback may encourage them to make small changes.

### 120. Tracking can reduce enjoyment when every activity becomes a number.

**Use in context:** A person may stop enjoying a walk because the device shows a slower speed or fewer calories than expected. Data should support the activity rather than determine its entire value.

### 121. Online workouts improve access for busy or isolated people.

**Use in context:** Participants can exercise at home and choose sessions that fit their schedules. However, beginners may receive less correction and could repeat unsafe movements.

### 122. Video analysis gives ordinary athletes tools once limited to professionals.

**Use in context:** A phone camera can help a runner, swimmer, or weightlifter examine technique. This makes detailed feedback cheaper and easier to access.

### 123. Automated coaching cannot fully replace human judgment.

**Use in context:** Software can analyze data and suggest training changes. A human coach is better able to understand fear, pain, motivation, personality, and unusual circumstances.

### 124. Technology can make sports decisions fairer but less spontaneous.

**Use in context:** Video review can correct serious mistakes by referees. Frequent delays may interrupt the rhythm of the game and reduce the emotional excitement of immediate decisions.

### 125. Advanced technology may widen the gap between wealthy and poorer athletes.

**Use in context:** Rich teams can afford better equipment, medical monitoring, analysis, and recovery systems. Talent alone may become less important when access to technology strongly affects preparation.

### 126. Future fitness programs will probably become more personalized.

**Use in context:** Devices may combine information about sleep, stress, movement, and recovery to adjust workouts automatically. The main challenge will be using personal data accurately and protecting privacy.

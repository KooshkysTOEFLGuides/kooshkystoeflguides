## Prompt

You are an expert designer of vocabulary materials for the TOEFL Speaking Interview task.

I am developing a topic pack about:

MAIN TOPIC: Sports and Fitness

My current seed subtopics are:

SEED SUBTOPICS:
    types of sports
    descriptions of sports
    sports participation
    types of physical strength
    experiences with sports
    contact vs. noncontact sports

Treat these subtopics as **minimum coverage requirements, not as a complete or closed taxonomy**.

Before producing the vocabulary, silently examine the main topic and identify additional useful subtopics, question angles, situations, contrasts, and speaking needs that I may have overlooked. Add them whenever they genuinely belong under the main topic.

Possible dimensions to consider include, when relevant:

* personal habits and routines;
* preferences and personality;
* past experiences and memories;
* descriptions of people, objects, places, activities, or processes;
* benefits and disadvantages;
* motivations and obstacles;
* costs, convenience, and accessibility;
* skill development and personal growth;
* family, friendship, and social life;
* children, teenagers, and education;
* workplace or professional contexts;
* technology and social media;
* culture, identity, and generational differences;
* environmental or ethical concerns;
* community action or public policy;
* current changes and future developments;
* comparisons between traditional and modern alternatives.

Do not force every dimension into every topic. Include only angles that naturally create useful TOEFL interview questions.

## Main task

Create a comprehensive lexical plan for discussing this topic in spoken English.

Prioritize vocabulary that B1–B2 learners can realistically move into their **active speaking vocabulary**. Most of the material should be natural, flexible, and easy enough to use smoothly under time pressure.

Include some advanced vocabulary, but do not allow rare, literary, excessively formal, or essay-only language to dominate the pack.

As a general balance:

* most items should be high-utility B1–B2 language;
* some should be strong upper-B2 language;
* a smaller number may be useful C1-level upgrades.

Do not confuse “advanced” with “unnatural.” A simple expression that students can use accurately is more valuable than a sophisticated expression they are unlikely to use.

## Organize the material

Divide the topic into clear thematic sections. Expand, combine, rename, or reorganize my seed subtopics when that produces a more complete and logical structure.

Under each thematic section, provide two separate parts.

### A. Headwords and their collocations

For each useful headword, give:

**headword** — collocation 1; collocation 2; collocation 3; collocation 4; etc.

Include a useful mixture of:

* nouns;
* verbs;
* adjectives;
* adverbs when genuinely useful;
* phrasal verbs;
* common idiomatic expressions that are transparent enough for speaking.

Give several natural collocations for each headword. Include different grammatical patterns when useful, such as:

* adjective + noun;
* verb + noun;
* noun + noun;
* verb + preposition;
* adjective + preposition;
* common clause patterns;
* comparative or contrastive patterns.

Do not manufacture collocations merely to reach a number.

### B. Useful phrases, sentence parts, and free collocations

After the headwords in each section, provide a separate list of useful multi-word chunks that should be learned as complete speaking units.

These may include:

* sentence openings;
* preference structures;
* ways to describe experiences;
* cause-and-effect chunks;
* comparison structures;
* concession structures;
* phrases for discussing advantages and disadvantages;
* phrases for explaining personal habits;
* phrases for recommendations and solutions;
* natural phrasal verbs;
* common conversational collocations;
* flexible sentence frames;
* topic-specific expressions that do not need to be treated as headwords.

Do not force every useful expression into a headword entry. Keep simpler multi-word language in the phrase bank when that is more pedagogically sensible.

## Selection standards

Include language that helps students:

* answer personal questions;
* describe concrete experiences;
* explain reasons;
* compare alternatives;
* discuss positive and negative effects;
* give advice;
* suggest solutions;
* discuss changes over time;
* express balanced opinions;
* talk about children, society, institutions, or the future when relevant.

Avoid:

* duplicate headwords appearing in several sections;
* multiple phrases that differ by only one insignificant word;
* obvious inflectional variations presented as separate entries;
* highly technical terminology with little speaking value;
* empty academic expressions that could apply to every topic;
* unnatural collocations;
* expressions that are mainly useful in formal essays;
* excessive repetition of words such as good, bad, important, interesting, and useful without better alternatives.

Where overlap between sections is unavoidable, place the item in the section where it is most teachable.

## Output rules

Return plain text only.

Do not give:

* definitions;
* example sentences;
* explanations;
* teaching notes;
* an introduction;
* an HTML file;
* an idea bank;
* interview questions.

Use this exact general structure:

# Expanded Coverage Map

1. [Subtopic]
2. [Subtopic]
3. [Additional subtopic you identified]
   ...

# 1. [THEMATIC SECTION]

## Headwords and Collocations

* **word** — collocation; collocation; collocation
* **word** — collocation; collocation; collocation

## Useful Phrases, Sentence Parts, and Free Collocations

* phrase
* phrase
* phrase

# 2. [THEMATIC SECTION]

Continue until the topic has been covered thoroughly.

At the end, include:

# Coverage Notes

* **Seed subtopics fully covered:** [list]
* **Additional subtopics added:** [list]
* **Potential overlap with other topic packs:** [brief list]

Do not ask follow-up questions. Make reasonable decisions yourself.

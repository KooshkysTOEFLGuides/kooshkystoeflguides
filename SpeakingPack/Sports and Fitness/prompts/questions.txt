# Core Principle: Broad Topical Connection

Each set must contain exactly four questions about the same broad main topic.

The questions should feel like four turns in one short research interview, but they do **not** need to form a tightly chained discussion of one narrow subtopic.

In authentic-style sets, the connection is often simply the broad research topic.

For example, a sports interview might move across:

* current sports participation;
* a sport the participant would like to learn;
* a memorable physical-activity experience;
* an opinion about children and contact sports.

These questions belong together because they concern sports, even though each examines a different angle.

Do not design every set as a four-stage investigation of one microtopic.

Avoid structures such as:

* four questions entirely about sports injuries;
* four questions entirely about athletic leadership;
* four questions entirely about early specialization;
* four questions entirely about fitness technology;
* four questions entirely about professional-sports ethics.

A narrower angle may appear in one question, especially Question 3 or Question 4. It should not normally dominate all four questions.

Each set should usually sample **three or four distinct but naturally related subtopics** from the broader topic.

---

# Coverage Principle

Treat my seed subtopics as minimum coverage requirements, not as a closed taxonomy.

Include useful additional subtopics identified in the approved coverage material.

However, distribute these subtopics **across the complete question bank**. Do not assume that every subtopic deserves its own four-question set.

Do not create one set for every item in the coverage map.

Several minor or related subtopics may be:

* combined;
* represented by one question;
* distributed across different sets;
* omitted when they would produce questions that are too technical, repetitive, or unlike the corpus.

Prefer fewer authentic sets over a larger number of narrow, artificial sets.

For a broad topic, create approximately **12–18 alternative interview sets**. A narrower topic may need fewer.

This is not a quota. Do not pad the collection or divide natural subject matter into increasingly specialized sets merely to reach a number.

Some overlap between sets is acceptable. Realistic alternative tests may revisit broad functions such as personal habits, preferences, experiences, comparisons, and social opinions from different angles.

# Typical Four-Question Shape

Use the following as a corpus-based tendency, not an inflexible formula.

## Question 1 — Accessible Entry Point

Question 1 should usually establish the participant’s personal relationship with the broad topic.

Common functions include:

* current habits;
* frequency;
* participation;
* personal experience;
* familiar preferences;
* past experience;
* the experience of a friend or family member;
* an immediately accessible opinion.

Question 1 should normally be answerable without specialized knowledge or extensive reflection.

It may ask one or more tightly connected subquestions.

Examples of suitable functions include:

* whether the person currently does something;
* how often they do it;
* what kind they prefer;
* whether they have experienced it;
* how important it is in their daily life;
* what they or people close to them normally do.

Do not routinely begin with an abstract social, institutional, ethical, or professional question.

## Question 2 — Concrete Development

Question 2 should commonly ask for something concrete, descriptive, personal, or hypothetical.

Suitable functions include:

* describing a routine;
* describing an activity;
* giving a personal example;
* recalling an experience;
* choosing between familiar alternatives;
* imagining a realistic situation;
* describing what the participant, a friend, or a family member would do;
* explaining an accessible benefit or disadvantage.

This question does not always need to follow directly from the participant’s answer to Question 1.

It should remain independently understandable.

## Question 3 — Flexible Bridge

Question 3 may move somewhat beyond direct description.

Suitable functions include:

* a memorable experience;
* a realistic hypothetical;
* a personal change;
* an obstacle;
* a motivation;
* a cause or effect;
* an accessible comparison;
* a recommendation;
* a future possibility;
* a moderately broader opinion.

Question 3 may remain personal, or it may begin widening the discussion.

Do not force it to be more abstract than the topic naturally supports.

## Question 4 — Broader Opinion

Question 4 should usually ask for a broader judgment connected to the main topic.

Common forms include:

* “Some people believe that …”
* “Some people prefer X, while others prefer Y.”
* “Do you agree or disagree?”
* “Which approach do you think is better?”
* “Do you think this will become more or less common?”
* “Should schools, families, employers, or communities …?”
* “What do you think about this issue?”

The final question may concern:

* children;
* education;
* society;
* work;
* the future;
* a general benefit or disadvantage;
* a familiar social choice;
* an accessible institutional decision.

Use only one broader angle at a time.

Do not turn Question 4 into a technical policy, medical, legal, scientific, or professional decision that an ordinary test taker would not be expected to understand.

Not every final question must use “Some people believe.” Vary the form while preserving the general level and function found in the corpus.

# Breadth and Narrowness

The subject matter must remain accessible to an ordinary university-age test taker regardless of academic major, employment, athletic ability, cultural background, or technical knowledge.

Questions may touch on:

* health;
* education;
* family;
* children;
* community life;
* technology;
* social effects;
* the future;
* cost and convenience.

However, these angles must remain broad and familiar.

Avoid requiring knowledge of:

* sports medicine;
* professional coaching;
* exercise physiology;
* detailed injury rehabilitation;
* return-to-play protocols;
* athletic administration;
* sponsorship economics;
* specialized training theory;
* technical equipment;
* detailed public policy;
* professional ethics specific to an industry.

For example, prefer:

* “What can make it difficult for someone to exercise regularly?”

over:

* “What should someone change if harder training is no longer improving their performance?”

Prefer:

* “Do you think children should participate in contact sports?”

over:

* “Who should make the final return-to-play decision after a young athlete’s injury?”

A specialized term may be used only when:

* it is common enough for ordinary speakers;
* its meaning is clear from context;
* or the interviewer briefly explains it with an example.

---

# Language and Sophistication

Use natural, accessible spoken English.

The intellectual level should allow meaningful reasoning, but the wording itself should not sound like:

* an academic seminar;
* a professional consultation;
* a policy hearing;
* a sports-science examination;
* a formal essay prompt.

Prefer familiar wording over compressed technical phrasing.

Do not make questions artificially sophisticated through expressions such as:

* institutional responsibility;
* performance optimization;
* return-to-play decisions;
* commercial value to stakeholders;
* specialized intervention;
* data-driven athletic development.

A moderately advanced concept is acceptable when expressed clearly and supported by context.

When a topic or contrast may be unfamiliar, briefly explain it or give examples inside the interviewer’s turn.

For example:

* “Contact sports, such as rugby or hockey, involve physical contact between players.”
* “Fitness technology can include exercise apps, online classes, or wearable trackers.”

Do not overexplain familiar ideas.

---

# Question Construction

Each question should have one central response direction.

However, it may contain two or three **tightly connected** subquestions, as real Interview questions often do.

Acceptable:

* “Can you describe a sports experience you remember well? What made it memorable?”
* “How often do you exercise, and what kinds of activities do you usually do?”
* “Which sport would you like to learn, and why would you choose it?”

Avoid combining unrelated tasks:

* “Describe your exercise routine, explain whether schools should require sports, and predict how technology will change fitness.”

The participant should be able to understand the complete task after hearing it once.

The question should support a natural response of approximately 45 seconds.

Do not make every question the same length.

Do not make every question a single short sentence merely for neatness.

Use enough context to make the task clear, but avoid long lectures before the question.

---

# Prerecorded Interview Logic

Treat the interview as prerecorded.

The interviewer does not know what the participant actually said.

Use generic transitions such as:

* Thank you.
* Interesting.
* I see.
* That is helpful.
* Moving on.
* Great.
* For my final question …

Do not use reactions that assume specific content from the participant’s response.

Avoid:

* “That was a difficult injury.”
* “I agree that swimming is the best choice.”
* “Your experience with your coach is interesting.”

A later question may refer to an earlier question only when the earlier question established a fixed scenario.

For example:

* Question 2 asks the participant to create an exercise plan for a friend.
* Question 3 asks what challenge that same friend might face.

Do not make frequent use of this structure. Most questions should be independently understandable.

---

# Interview Framing

Give every set a short research-interview setup similar in function to the corpus.

The setup should ordinarily say that the participant has:

* volunteered for a university research study;
* agreed to take part in a short online interview;
* or been invited to answer questions for a research project.

Keep the setup brief and natural.

Question 1 should include a full interviewer opening, such as:

“Thank you for agreeing to participate. I’d like to ask you some questions about sports and physical activity. First, do you currently play any sports or exercise regularly?”

Questions 2–4 should normally include brief generic transitions.

Do not overuse exactly the same transition sequence in every set.

The editorial set title is not spoken by the interviewer.

# Set Variety

Across the complete collection, vary the order and combination of question functions.

Possible authentic-style combinations include:

### Pattern A

1. current habit;
2. hypothetical preference;
3. memorable experience;
4. broader social opinion.

### Pattern B

1. personal preference;
2. concrete description;
3. advantage or obstacle;
4. comparison between two general approaches.

### Pattern C

1. recent experience;
2. usual behavior;
3. future possibility;
4. broader consequence.

### Pattern D

1. experience of the participant or someone they know;
2. descriptive example;
3. cause or motivation;
4. agree–disagree statement.

### Pattern E

1. simple personal opinion;
2. realistic recommendation;
3. challenge and possible response;
4. another accessible judgment connected to the scenario.

These are models, not mandatory templates.

Do not mechanically apply the same progression to every set.

Do not make all Question 1 items personal routines, all Question 2 items descriptions, all Question 3 items obstacles, and all Question 4 items agree–disagree statements.

Preserve corpus-like variation.

---

# Avoid Overengineering

Do not attempt to demonstrate every item in the idea bank through the questions.

Do not design the question bank as a complete academic taxonomy of the main topic.

The vocabulary and idea bank may be exhaustive. The Interview questions should be **selective, ordinary, and test-like**.

The questions should look simpler and broader than the supporting teaching material.

Do not add a separate set merely because the idea bank includes:

* leadership;
* accessibility;
* technology;
* ethics;
* body image;
* age differences;
* professional contexts;
* environmental effects.

Use such material only when it produces a natural, accessible Interview question.

* Question 1 is normally accessible and personal or familiar;

* Question 3 provides flexible development;

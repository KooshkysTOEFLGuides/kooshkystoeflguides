## Prompt

You are an expert writer of reusable idea banks for the TOEFL Speaking Interview task.

Create a comprehensive idea bank for:

**MAIN TOPIC:** [INSERT TOPIC]

My original seed subtopics were:

[INSERT SEED SUBTOPICS]

Here is the approved expanded coverage map and vocabulary plan:

[PASTE APPROVED STEP 1 MATERIAL]

The approved structure should guide your organization, but do not assume it covers every possible idea. My original subtopics and the approved sections are **minimum coverage requirements, not strict boundaries**.

Add another idea category when you notice a genuinely useful TOEFL interview angle that is still missing. Do not add categories merely to make the bank look larger.

## Purpose of the idea bank

The ideas should help B1–B2 students answer many different questions without memorizing one complete answer for each question.

Each idea must be:

* reusable across several possible questions;
* specific enough to produce meaningful development;
* simple enough to explain naturally in spoken English;
* suitable for a response of approximately 30–45 seconds;
* distinct from the surrounding ideas;
* realistic for students and ordinary adults;
* adaptable to personal, social, educational, professional, or public contexts when relevant.

The idea bank should not merely repeat facts about the topic. It should give students useful **answer angles**.

## Coverage

Include a broad and balanced mixture of ideas involving, when relevant:

* personal preferences;
* habits and routines;
* memorable experiences;
* convenience and practicality;
* enjoyment and emotional effects;
* physical or mental benefits;
* skill development;
* identity and self-expression;
* confidence and independence;
* social connection;
* family relationships;
* children and education;
* costs and accessibility;
* obstacles and disadvantages;
* risks and unintended consequences;
* fairness and social inequality;
* environmental or ethical concerns;
* traditional versus modern approaches;
* individual responsibility;
* institutional or government responsibility;
* short-term versus long-term effects;
* present conditions and future developments.

Do not force irrelevant political, environmental, technological, or child-related ideas into the topic. Use them only when they create natural speaking content.

Include positive, negative, and balanced ideas. Do not make the entire bank promotional or one-sided.

## Idea quality

Avoid generic claims such as:

* “It is good for people.”
* “It has many advantages.”
* “It makes life better.”
* “People can learn many things.”

Replace them with precise, reusable claims, such as:

* the activity creates structure in a person’s week;
* the habit reduces decision fatigue;
* the option is accessible to people with limited time;
* the experience gives people a shared topic of conversation;
* the policy may help one group while creating costs for another.

Avoid repeating the same underlying idea with slightly different wording.

For example, “reduces stress,” “helps people relax,” and “makes people feel calmer” should not become three separate ideas unless each one presents a genuinely different mechanism or context.

## Relationship to the vocabulary plan

Use the approved vocabulary and collocations naturally where they fit, but do not turn the idea bank into a vocabulary demonstration.

Do not force advanced vocabulary into every item.

The ideas should remain understandable to B1–B2 students.

## Output format

Return plain text only.

Organize the ideas into numbered thematic sections.

For every idea, use this format:

### [NUMBER]. [Concise reusable idea]

**Use in context:** [Two or three natural sentences showing how the idea could be developed in a TOEFL speaking answer.]

Example of the required level of development:

### 1. A regular activity can create structure in a person’s week.

**Use in context:** If someone attends a class every Saturday morning, the activity gives the week a clear rhythm. It can also motivate the person to organize other responsibilities around it.

The heading should express the reusable idea clearly. The “Use in context” section should explain, illustrate, qualify, or apply the idea rather than simply repeat it.

## Quantity

For each major category, normally provide approximately 8–12 strong ideas.

For a broad topic, the complete bank will usually contain approximately **80–140 ideas**. A particularly broad topic may justify more, while a narrow one may justify fewer.

Do not pad the bank with weak or repetitive ideas merely to reach a target.

## Additional requirements

* Make the ideas varied in grammatical form.
* Use concrete examples from ordinary life.
* Include some nuanced ideas with limitations or conditions.
* Include useful contrasts.
* Include both individual and broader social angles when appropriate.
* Do not write complete 45-second sample answers.
* Do not generate interview questions.
* Do not reproduce the vocabulary lists.
* Do not produce HTML.
* Do not include instructions addressed to students.
* Do not ask me follow-up questions.

At the end, include:

# Coverage Audit

For each thematic section, briefly state which seed or added subtopics it covers. Also identify any new category you added beyond the approved Step 1 structure.
